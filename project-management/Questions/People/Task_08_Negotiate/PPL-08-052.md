# Question

Two organisations have signed an MoU to deliver a joint product, but the document is silent on IP ownership of anything created jointly. Development starts in two weeks. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Begin development on the agreed schedule and capture the IP position in a later amendment once the shape of the joint output is clearer.
- **B**: Assume joint IP ownership by default, document that position in the project charter, and proceed unless the other party formally objects in writing.
- **C**: Pause kick-off and negotiate a written IP schedule — ownership, licensing, and background carve-outs — before either team produces disputed deliverables together.
- **D**: Ask the legal teams of both organisations to negotiate directly while the project proceeds, and merge their outcome into the MoU retrospectively afterwards.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

PMI treats ambiguity in foundational agreements as a risk to resolve before execution, and IP is non-negotiable to sort out up-front because it becomes adversarial once artefacts exist. (A) creates joint artefacts with no ownership rule, which is precisely the conflict trigger. (B) makes a unilateral assumption on a legally material question. (D) lets execution and negotiation run in parallel and accumulates deliverables that may have to be unwound.
