# Question

On a hybrid project, technical specialists default to dense jargon during workshops with business stakeholders, who in turn give vague high-level statements that the engineers then describe as "not real requirements." Both sides quietly believe the other can't communicate. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Run all communication through a senior business analyst who can translate between the two languages.
- **B**: Build a shared glossary, use visual artefacts (mockups, flowcharts) in workshops, and facilitate sessions where each side restates the other's points.
- **C**: Split future workshops by audience so technical and business sessions can run in parallel and aggregate at the end.
- **D**: Send technical specialists to a stakeholder communication course before the next workshop.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

PMI's preferred response is to build bridges between the parties — shared vocabulary, visuals that transcend jargon, and dialogue patterns that force translation — rather than to insulate them from each other. (B) does all three. (A) creates a permanent dependency on one person and never builds direct understanding. (C) saves short-term friction but cements the divide and pushes the integration problem to the worst possible moment. (D) is a long-term capability investment that doesn't address the active workshops; the PM should change the operating model now and let training compound later.
