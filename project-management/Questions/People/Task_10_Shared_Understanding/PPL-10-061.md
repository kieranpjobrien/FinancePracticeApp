# Question

The sponsor believes the next release will contain 12 key features because that was the list in the business case. The team, based on current velocity, believes it can deliver six to eight of them. Neither has explicitly discussed the gap. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Let the release proceed on the team's current plan and address any shortfall at the release retrospective if fewer than 12 features are ultimately delivered.
- **B**: Push the team to commit to all 12 features since the sponsor's expectation is grounded in the approved business case and the team must find a way.
- **C**: Rework the business case silently to reduce the planned scope and seek approval for the reduction at the next steering committee without surfacing it beforehand.
- **D**: Facilitate an explicit release-planning conversation with the sponsor using velocity data, confirm the prioritised subset, and update the business case and roadmap to match.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Shared understanding of release scope is built by bringing velocity data and the business case into the same conversation and agreeing the prioritised truth — not leaving the two sides with different mental models. (A) lets a gap become a broken promise. (B) forces unsustainable commitment and either kills the team or the quality. (C) updates governance artefacts without the conversation that gives them meaning, which is a transparency failure.
