# Question

Your 8-person integration team has 23 in-flight tickets across the current sprint board. Engineers are visibly busy, yet only two stories have closed in the last week. The team tells you "everything is nearly done." Your product owner is frustrated. What should the PM do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Increase sprint length from two to three weeks so that the many nearly-done stories have time to cross the line before the end of each sprint cadence.
- **B**: Reassign ticket ownership so that each engineer has only one in-flight story at a time and the remaining tickets are redistributed across other available team members.
- **C**: Agree a work-in-progress limit with the team — say two tickets per engineer — and hold it rigorously so stories finish before new ones start across the entire sprint board.
- **D**: Add capacity to the team with one or two additional contractors so that the 23 in-flight tickets can be progressed to completion without reducing what any individual engineer is already handling.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

(C) is the PMI flow-efficiency pattern: WIP limits are the proven intervention when "everything is nearly done" — they force completion before starting, which shortens cycle time and reduces context switching. (A) extends the container rather than fixing the flow and will fill up just as quickly. (B) stops on a point in time but does not agree the discipline that prevents recurrence. (D) adds capacity to an already-unfinished system, which almost always raises WIP further and makes completion rates worse.
