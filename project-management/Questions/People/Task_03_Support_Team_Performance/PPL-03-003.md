# Question

A high-performing team has been working together for over a year. Recently, the team has become complacent - they consistently meet but never exceed commitments, resist trying new practices, and retrospectives have become routine with few improvements identified. How should the project manager re-energize the team?

## Options

- **A**: Increase performance targets to challenge the team
- **B**: Introduce new challenges such as stretch goals, learning opportunities, or process experiments, and celebrate innovation attempts regardless of outcome
- **C**: Accept that the team has reached its optimal performance level
- **D**: Rotate team members to other projects to bring in fresh perspectives

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The correct answer is B. The team shows signs of stagnation - competent but not growing. Introducing new challenges (stretch goals, learning opportunities, experiments) addresses the need for growth and novelty. Importantly, celebrating innovation attempts regardless of outcome creates psychological safety for experimentation, which is key to breaking complacency.

Option A (higher targets) adds pressure without addressing the underlying lack of challenge and growth. More of the same work at higher volume isn't energizing.

Option C accepts premature stagnation. Teams can continue improving, but it requires intentional effort to break out of comfortable routines.

Option D (rotating members) destroys the team that took time to build. Some knowledge transfer is healthy, but wholesale rotation should be a last resort and doesn't address the root cause - the environment allows complacency.
