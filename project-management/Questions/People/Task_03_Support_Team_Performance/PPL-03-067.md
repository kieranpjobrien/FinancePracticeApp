# Question

Your 11-person research software team has never used peer feedback. Delivery is adequate but career growth conversations feel empty, and three engineers have told you in 1-1s they "don't know what they need to work on." You are considering introducing structured peer feedback. What should the PM do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Co-design a lightweight peer-feedback practice with the team — purpose, frequency, format, confidentiality — and pilot it for one quarter with a review at the end before adopting it fully.
- **B**: Launch a full 360-degree feedback programme for all 11 team members immediately so that career conversations have rich data from next quarter's performance review onwards for everyone.
- **C**: Ask HR to run their standard 360 template for your team so that the organisation's existing process is used consistently and the team's feedback data is comparable across other teams.
- **D**: Start providing detailed PM-to-engineer feedback in your 1-1s and leave peer feedback to happen naturally as the team matures, rather than formalising another process right now.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

(A) is the PMI tailoring-and-participation pattern: co-design, pilot, review — feedback practices introduced to a team that has never had them fail when imposed top-down. (B) launches the heaviest option without any co-design, which often lands as surveillance. (C) adopts a generic organisational template that may not fit a research team and removes the team's voice. (D) leaves an identified growth gap to chance and misses the opportunity to build a shared learning loop the team needs.
