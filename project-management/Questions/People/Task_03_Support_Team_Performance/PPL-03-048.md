# Question

Your agile team runs retrospectives every two weeks, but the same three action items keep reappearing unchanged sprint after sprint. The team has stopped engaging meaningfully with retrospectives and morale is beginning to slide. What should the PM do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Replace retrospectives with a lighter-weight written reflection template so that people can opt in when they have something genuinely useful to contribute each sprint.
- **B**: At the next retrospective, name the pattern honestly, ask the team why actions are not landing, and co-design a smaller number of owned experiments with clear accountability.
- **C**: Take ownership of the three recurring actions yourself so that they finally get done and the team can move on to newer issues at the next retrospective they run.
- **D**: Skip retrospectives for the next two sprints to break the habit and restart them afresh once the team has some distance from the current pattern of disengagement.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

(B) uses the retrospective to inspect the retrospective itself — the most PMI-aligned inspect-and-adapt move — and converts vague actions into owned experiments. (A) removes the structure before fixing the behaviour and loses a core agile feedback loop. (C) reinforces the PM as the doer of actions, which is why the team has disengaged in the first place. (D) pauses the learning loop rather than repairing it and does nothing to explain why the restart will be any different.
