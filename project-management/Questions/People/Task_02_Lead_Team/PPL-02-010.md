# Question

A project manager notices that decisions made in team meetings are frequently not implemented. Team members agree during meetings but don't follow through afterward. The PM has confirmed that workload isn't the issue. What is the MOST likely cause and appropriate response?

## Options

- **A**: Team members are being insubordinate; implement stricter accountability measures
- **B**: Team members may not genuinely agree with decisions; create space for authentic discussion and ensure true consensus before concluding
- **C**: The PM should send written follow-ups after each meeting to document commitments
- **D**: Assign a team member to track action items and report on completion

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The correct answer is B. The pattern suggests "false consensus" - team members appear to agree in meetings but don't truly buy in. This often happens when team members don't feel safe disagreeing, when dominant voices override others, or when meetings are rushed. The solution is better facilitation that surfaces genuine concerns and builds real commitment, not compliance tracking.

Option A assumes bad intent when the problem is likely structural (meeting dynamics). Stricter accountability doesn't address why people aren't committing and may reduce psychological safety further.

Option C (documentation) addresses memory/clarity issues, which isn't the problem here. People remember the decisions - they just don't implement them because they never truly agreed.

Option D (tracking) might surface non-completion faster but doesn't address why it happens. Adding tracking overhead without fixing the root cause creates bureaucracy without solving the problem.
