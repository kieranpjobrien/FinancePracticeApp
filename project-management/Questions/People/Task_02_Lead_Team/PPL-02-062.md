# Question

Four new graduates have joined your embedded-systems team of six mid-career engineers mid-way through a hardware product build. The seniors are resistant to pairing because "they slow us down" and the delivery milestone is eight weeks away. What leadership approach should the PM take?

## Options

- **A**: Explain to the seniors that pairing grows the team's long-term capability, agree a structured rotation with time-boxed pairing sessions, and protect delivery commitments by adjusting the plan accordingly.
- **B**: Defer the pairing until after the hardware milestone and have the graduates shadow reviews in read-only mode so the seniors can focus on delivery without interruption.
- **C**: Assign each graduate an independent stream of small tickets off the critical path so they learn by doing without drawing mid-career engineer time away from the delivery work.
- **D**: Offer the mid-career engineers a small delivery bonus for hitting the milestone with full pairing in place so their delivery concern is financially offset by the additional effort.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

(A) holds both truths — pairing is the right long-term move and delivery must still happen — and adjusts the plan honestly rather than pretending pairing is free. This is the PMI leadership pattern for capability-versus-delivery tension. (B) optimises for the near term and ships delivery-first culture that will punish graduates on every project. (C) isolates graduates in ghetto work and fails the learning investment PMI expects of the PM. (D) pays for a behaviour the seniors should show anyway and does not change their underlying objection.
