# Question

A high-performing team member privately tells the project manager they are bored and considering leaving the project. They feel their skills are underutilized on routine tasks. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Remind them of their commitment to the project and the importance of completing assigned work
- **B**: Explore opportunities for them to take on more challenging work, mentor others, or lead improvement initiatives
- **C**: Offer a salary increase to retain them on the project
- **D**: Begin planning for their replacement while searching for new challenges for them

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The correct answer is B. This addresses the root cause - the team member needs growth and challenge. Exploring options like challenging assignments, mentoring, or leading improvements satisfies higher-level needs (Maslow's self-actualization, Herzberg's motivators) while benefiting the project. This is proactive leadership that develops people and prevents turnover.

Option A focuses on obligation rather than motivation and ignores the underlying issue. This approach likely accelerates their departure and damages the relationship.

Option C addresses extrinsic motivation when the problem is intrinsic (boredom, underutilization). According to Herzberg's theory, salary is a hygiene factor - it prevents dissatisfaction but doesn't create motivation. The team member needs motivators like growth and meaningful work.

Option D is premature and negative. The PM should first attempt to address concerns before assuming departure is inevitable. Planning replacement without genuine effort to retain signals the PM has given up.
