# Question

A project manager takes over a troubled project. The previous PM used a command-and-control style, and the team is demoralized. Team members do only what they're explicitly told and never volunteer ideas or raise concerns. The project manager wants to shift to a more collaborative approach. What is the BEST way to begin this transition?

## Options

- **A**: Announce the new leadership philosophy and immediately implement self-organizing team practices
- **B**: Hold a team meeting to criticize the previous approach and promise things will be different
- **C**: Gradually build trust through consistent actions - listening actively, acting on feedback, celebrating contributions, and protecting the team
- **D**: Replace team members who seem resistant to the new approach

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The correct answer is C. Trust must be rebuilt through consistent actions over time, not words or announcements. The team has learned that speaking up isn't safe. The PM must demonstrate through repeated behaviors - listening, acting on input, protecting the team - that the environment has genuinely changed. This takes patience and consistency.

Option A moves too fast. Announcing a philosophy change means little to a team that's been burned. Immediately implementing self-organization may overwhelm a team that's been conditioned to wait for direction. Trust comes before empowerment.

Option B criticizes the predecessor, which is unprofessional and may make the team defensive (some may have adapted to that style). Promises mean nothing without actions to back them up.

Option D punishes team members for adapting to their previous environment. The behavior is a symptom of the system they worked in, not a character flaw. Removing people signals the new PM is just as threatening as the old one.
