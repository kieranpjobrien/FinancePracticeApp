# Question

A PM has been asked by a senior executive to "mentor" them on how younger staff perceive leadership, essentially a reverse-mentoring arrangement. The PM is much more junior and worries about candour. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Decline the invitation on the basis that reverse mentoring with a much more senior leader rarely produces useful candour or safe conversations in practice.
- **B**: Accept but commit to sharing only views that are safe to share, carefully avoiding any observations that could be perceived as direct criticism of leadership.
- **C**: Accept, and in the first session jointly agree explicit ground rules around confidentiality, purpose, candour, and what both parties hope to get from it.
- **D**: Accept and act as a conduit by anonymously channelling observations from other junior staff to the executive, instead of sharing the PM's own personal views.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

PMI's mentoring approach starts with explicit agreements — confidentiality, purpose and candour ground rules make reverse mentoring safe and productive. (A) refuses a development opportunity the PM's feedback could benefit from. (B) pre-limits candour before the relationship has even tested its safety. (D) makes the PM a pipeline for others' views rather than a mentor offering their own.
