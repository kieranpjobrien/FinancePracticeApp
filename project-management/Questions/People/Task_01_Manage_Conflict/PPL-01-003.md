# Question

A project manager is leading an agile transformation project. The operations manager openly criticizes the project in meetings, claiming it will disrupt stable processes and is unnecessary. Private conversations reveal the operations manager fears losing team members to agile squads. The project has strong executive sponsorship. What approach would BEST address this situation?

## Options

- **A**: Document the resistance and inform the executive sponsor to address the operations manager
- **B**: Acknowledge the operations manager's concerns, explore how the transformation can address their underlying interests, and involve them in shaping the transition plan
- **C**: Present data and case studies showing successful agile transformations to convince the operations manager
- **D**: Proceed with the transformation as planned since executive support ensures success

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The correct answer is B. This approach addresses the root cause of the conflict - the operations manager's underlying fear of losing resources and relevance. By acknowledging concerns (showing empathy), exploring mutual interests, and involving them in shaping solutions, the PM uses a collaborative approach that can transform a resistor into a supporter. This aligns with effective stakeholder engagement and conflict resolution principles.

Option A immediately escalates without attempting resolution. While documentation is good practice, jumping to executive intervention may damage the relationship and create a more adversarial dynamic.

Option C focuses only on logical persuasion but ignores the emotional and political dimensions. Data rarely changes minds when the underlying concerns are about power and job security.

Option D ignores the conflict and relies on positional power. Even with executive support, ongoing resistance from operations can undermine implementation and team morale. Sustainable change requires broader buy-in.
