# Question

A vendor providing critical components has consistently delivered late, causing schedule slippage. The project manager has documented the issues and held multiple discussions with the vendor, but performance hasn't improved. The vendor relationship is managed by the procurement department, and the contract includes penalty clauses. The project is now at risk of missing a regulatory deadline. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Invoke the contract penalty clauses immediately to motivate the vendor
- **B**: Work with procurement to formally escalate performance issues, explore contractual remedies, and simultaneously develop contingency plans
- **C**: Replace the vendor with a new supplier to avoid further delays
- **D**: Accept the delays and adjust the project schedule since vendor management is outside PM control

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The correct answer is B. This takes a comprehensive approach: formal escalation through proper channels (procurement), exploring all contractual options, AND developing contingencies. The PM must work within organizational processes while protecting the project. This acknowledges the PM doesn't have direct contract authority but has responsibility for project success and must actively manage this risk.

Option A bypasses procurement, who manages the vendor relationship. The PM cannot unilaterally invoke contract clauses - this must go through proper channels. Also, penalties alone may not solve the delivery problem.

Option C (replacing vendor) is a major decision with cost and schedule implications that requires procurement involvement. It may not be feasible given the regulatory deadline and time needed to onboard a new vendor.

Option D is passive and abandons the PM's responsibility. While vendor management involves procurement, the PM must actively escalate risks and drive resolution to protect the project. "Outside PM control" doesn't mean "not PM's problem."
