# Question

During a retrospective, a team member brings up that certain colleagues consistently arrive late to meetings and interrupt others. The team doesn't have documented ground rules. What should the Scrum Master do?

## Options

- **A**: Address the specific individuals privately after the meeting
- **B**: Facilitate the team in creating ground rules together, including expectations about meetings and communication
- **C**: Create and distribute ground rules for the team to follow
- **D**: Remind the team about professional behavior expectations

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The correct answer is B. This creates an opportunity for the team to establish their own norms collaboratively. Ground rules created by the team have more buy-in than imposed rules. The retrospective is an appropriate forum for this discussion. By facilitating rather than dictating, the Scrum Master supports team ownership.

Option A addresses individuals but not the systemic issue - without shared norms, the behavior isn't clearly wrong. Individual coaching is appropriate after norms exist.

Option C imposes rules without team input, reducing buy-in and ownership. Ground rules are more effective when the team creates them together.

Option D is vague and doesn't create actionable expectations. "Professional behavior" means different things to different people - specific ground rules are more useful.
