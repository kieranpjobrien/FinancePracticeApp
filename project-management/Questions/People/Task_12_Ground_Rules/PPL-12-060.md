# Question

An agile team has a ground rule requiring all user stories to meet a detailed Definition of Ready before they enter sprint planning. A major regulatory deadline now forces the team to take in stories that are only partially ready in order to meet the date. The Scrum Master is uncomfortable. What should the Scrum Master do?

## Options

- **A**: Hold firm to the existing Definition of Ready and refuse to accept partially-ready stories, escalating the regulatory deadline risk directly to the sponsor for a decision.
- **B**: Silently accept the partially-ready stories and retain the existing Definition of Ready unchanged as the team's formal ground-rule standard for all other work.
- **C**: Suspend the Definition of Ready entirely for the duration of the regulatory work and reinstate it automatically once the deadline has passed for the team.
- **D**: Work with the team to agree a time-boxed exception with explicit entry conditions, risks accepted, and a commitment to return to the standard.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

PMI's tailoring and risk-management principles allow time-boxed, explicit exceptions with accepted risk — this preserves the rule's meaning and the team's discipline. (A) is rigid in a situation that genuinely warrants tailoring. (B) is the worst combination — breach without acknowledgement, eroding the rule. (C) suspends a rule without defining the exception's edges, making return harder.
