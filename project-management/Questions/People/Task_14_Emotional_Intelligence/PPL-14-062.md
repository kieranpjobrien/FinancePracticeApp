# Question

In a governance meeting, the PM confidently stated a fact that turned out to be wrong, and a decision was taken based on it. The error was only discovered privately later, after the meeting closed. No one else has noticed yet, and revisiting the decision will be awkward and costly. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Raise the error promptly with the meeting chair and affected stakeholders, correct the record, and work with them on adjusting the decision if necessary.
- **B**: Leave the decision in place and quietly compensate for the wrong data in subsequent project work so that the original decision is never publicly revisited at all.
- **C**: Raise the error only if it begins to affect delivery in a visible way, since flagging it now would damage the PM's credibility with the governance group unnecessarily.
- **D**: Correct the record only with the sponsor privately, rely on the sponsor's judgement on whether to reopen the decision, and avoid a broader disclosure to other stakeholders.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

PMI's integrity and transparency principles require proactive correction — credibility is built by owning mistakes openly, not by concealing them or hoping they do not surface. (B) lets a decision stand on known bad information. (C) weighs PM reputation against stakeholder information rights. (D) hides the error from parties who relied on the original statement to make the decision.
