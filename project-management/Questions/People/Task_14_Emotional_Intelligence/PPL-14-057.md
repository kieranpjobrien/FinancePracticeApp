# Question

In a global kickoff, one team member has publicly disagreed with a colleague's proposal twice, using very direct language. The colleague has visibly withdrawn since. The PM knows the direct member considers their style honest, and the withdrawn colleague considers it rude. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Tell the direct member privately that their style is unacceptable in a global context and must change immediately to match the rest of the international team.
- **B**: Tell the withdrawn colleague privately that direct challenge is a normal part of business meetings and to build resilience to different communication styles over time.
- **C**: Accept that both styles are legitimate and let the two individuals adapt to each other naturally as the kickoff and subsequent delivery work progresses over time.
- **D**: Talk with both members separately, build each one's awareness of the other's frame, and facilitate a shared norm the team can genuinely work with together.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

PMI's cross-cultural empathy approach is to build mutual awareness and co-create a shared norm — neither style is wrong, but the team needs one agreement. (A) labels one style as unacceptable without dialogue. (B) puts the burden on the withdrawn colleague to tolerate behaviour that silenced them. (C) leaves a damaged relationship to "work itself out," which rarely happens.
