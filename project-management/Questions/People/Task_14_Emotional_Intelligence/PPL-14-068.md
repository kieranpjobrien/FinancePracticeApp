# Question

The organisation has just announced a restructure that will affect thirty percent of staff. Decisions have not yet been made. The PM's team is visibly anxious, productivity has dropped, and no one has concrete information yet. The PM has little more information than the team does. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Reassure the team by downplaying the likely impact on the project team members, so the group's anxiety eases and productivity can recover more quickly.
- **B**: Tell the team the PM has no information, ask them to keep their heads down on delivery, and wait for formal announcements from the executive before addressing it.
- **C**: Acknowledge the anxiety openly, share honestly what is and is not known, commit to timely updates, and adjust near-term expectations to the team's actual current capacity.
- **D**: Continue working normally without raising the restructure topic until the organisation makes formal announcements, since speculation from the PM might make things worse.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

PMI's authentic-leadership and psychological-safety principles expect honest acknowledgement of reality plus sustainable expectations — this is what the team actually needs under this kind of uncertainty. (A) manufactures a reassurance the PM cannot honestly give. (B) dismisses the team's emotional state and returns to transactional direction. (D) pretends silence protects the team when it actually isolates them.
