# Question

The PM realises they have been consistently scoring one team member lower in informal performance judgements than their actual output warrants. On reflection, the PM recalls the member reminds them of a colleague from an earlier job who underperformed. Formal ratings are due next month. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Assume self-awareness is enough and make sure to score the member fairly next month, being careful to set aside the association in the moment of rating.
- **B**: Discuss the realisation with the member and ask them to bring forward evidence of their performance to help the PM produce a rating that is genuinely fair.
- **C**: Refer the rating decision to a peer PM who has not worked closely with the member, to reduce the impact of the original PM's earlier biased judgement.
- **D**: Ground the rating in documented observations and artefacts, calibrate with peers on the same scale, and invite specific self-assessment from the member.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

PMI's bias-mitigation approach is structural — evidence-based ratings, peer calibration and the member's own self-assessment produce fairness that self-awareness alone cannot reliably deliver. (A) relies on intent in the moment against a known bias. (B) puts the burden on the member to compensate for the PM's bias. (C) outsources the rating rather than correcting the process the PM owns.
