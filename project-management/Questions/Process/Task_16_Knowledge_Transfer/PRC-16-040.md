# Question

A year after a successful platform handover, a major outage occurs. The post-mortem finds operations had quietly modified several runbook procedures because they seemed inefficient, not realising the steps existed to satisfy a regulatory control. What was the most important gap in the original knowledge transfer?

## Options

- **A**: The runbooks lacked sufficient step-by-step detail for the operations team.
- **B**: Operations did not receive enough hours of formal training before go-live.
- **C**: The transfer captured the procedures but not the rationale, risks, or compliance constraints behind them.
- **D**: The project did not impose a change control process on operations after handover.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

PMI emphasises transferring both explicit procedures and the contextual knowledge that makes them defensible, including the why and the risks they mitigate. (C) addresses the root cause: operations had no way to know which steps were load-bearing, so well-intentioned simplification removed a control. (A) adds detail to the same content that was already failing. (B) is more of the same training and would not have prevented the deviation, since the issue was missing context, not missing repetition. (D) treats the symptom by adding governance rather than equipping operations to make informed decisions themselves.
