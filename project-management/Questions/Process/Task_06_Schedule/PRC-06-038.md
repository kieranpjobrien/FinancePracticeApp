# Question

Three days into a two-week sprint, the team realises one user story they pulled in is at least three times bigger than they estimated. The other seven stories are progressing as expected, but the sprint goal is now at risk. What should the team do?

## Options

- **A**: Mandate overtime for the rest of the sprint so the original eight stories can all be delivered as committed.
- **B**: Freeze the sprint backlog because the Scrum framework does not allow changes after sprint planning has finished.
- **C**: Convene with the product owner to either split the oversized story and return the remainder to the backlog or swap out a lower-priority story to keep the sprint goal achievable.
- **D**: Drop the oversized story without further discussion and pick up a smaller backlog item in its place to keep the story count at eight.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Scrum allows scope renegotiation within a sprint when the product owner is engaged and the sprint goal is not jeopardised. (C) follows that practice — collaborate with the PO and protect both the goal and sustainable pace. (A) burns the team and is unsustainable. (B) is wrong about Scrum; the sprint backlog is owned by the developers and can be renegotiated with the PO. (D) makes a unilateral scope change without involving the PO who owns prioritisation.
