# Question

The client contract carries a liquidated-damages clause of $10,000 per day late. Current forecasts show the project finishing five days late. The PM has confirmed with engineering that crashing the remaining critical-path work is feasible at $8,000 per day saved. What should the PM recommend?

## Options

- **A**: Accept the five-day delay and the $50,000 in liquidated damages because crashing introduces additional execution risk.
- **B**: Crash the schedule to recover the five days because the $40,000 crash cost is lower than the $50,000 penalty and protects client relationship value.
- **C**: Negotiate a contract amendment to extend the delivery date so the liquidated-damages clause does not apply to the slip.
- **D**: Crash three of the five days and accept the penalty for the other two days because crash costs typically rise sharply over time.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A straight cost-benefit shows crashing ($40,000) is cheaper than the penalty ($50,000) and avoids the relationship damage of late delivery. (B) is correct. (A) accepts a more expensive option and a reputational hit. (C) seeks a contract change to escape an obligation rather than meeting it; this also depends on client willingness. (D) is a plausible-looking distractor but the question states the rate is a flat $8,000 per day, so partial crashing leaves $10,000 of avoidable cost on the table.
