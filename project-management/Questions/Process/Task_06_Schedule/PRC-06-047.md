# Question

Your Scrum team's velocity has varied between 28 and 44 points across the last eight sprints, averaging 36. A stakeholder demands you "commit" to delivering all 360 remaining backlog points in 10 sprints. The team is uneasy with the commitment framing. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Commit to 10 sprints since the arithmetic matches and the team should aim to stabilise velocity at the average level.
- **B**: Communicate a forecast range based on velocity history, not a point commitment, so expectations reflect empirical uncertainty.
- **C**: Extend the release to 12 sprints to build a buffer and commit to the longer window to reduce risk to delivery.
- **D**: Split the backlog into must-have and nice-to-have items and commit only to the must-haves within the 10-sprint window.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Empirical process control calls for forecast ranges using velocity history, not fixed commitments that ignore variance. (B) educates the stakeholder and protects the team. (A) ignores variance and pressures the team. (C) pads without explaining and still commits to a fixed window. (D) is useful scoping but does not address the commitment framing that is the core issue.
