# Question

A project spans three business units, each with their own VP who wants decision-making authority over aspects affecting their unit. The project sponsor sits above all three VPs. Decisions are being delayed because each VP wants to approve anything affecting their area. How should governance be structured?

## Options

- **A**: Let each VP have veto power over their unit's involvement
- **B**: Escalate all decisions to the sponsor to override the VPs
- **C**: Establish a governance framework that defines decision domains for each VP, shared decisions requiring consensus, and escalation to the sponsor for unresolved conflicts
- **D**: Remove the VPs from project governance

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The correct answer is C. Multi-stakeholder governance requires clear decision domains. Each VP may have authority over unit-specific decisions, but shared decisions need a defined process (consensus, majority, etc.). The sponsor serves as the escalation point for conflicts. This structure respects each VP's legitimate authority while enabling progress.

Option A creates gridlock since any VP can block decisions.

Option B overloads the sponsor with decisions that should be made at lower levels.

Option D ignores legitimate stakeholder authority and would likely face resistance.
