# Question

During a sprint retrospective, the team identifies that they've been discovering significant defects late in each sprint, causing rushed fixes and incomplete testing. What type of risk response should the project manager implement?

## Options

- **A**: Accept the risk and allocate buffer time for defect fixes
- **B**: Avoid the risk by extending sprint duration
- **C**: Mitigate the risk by implementing earlier testing practices such as test-driven development or continuous integration
- **D**: Transfer the risk by hiring additional QA staff

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The correct answer is C. This is a mitigation strategy - reducing either the probability of late-found defects (through earlier testing) or the impact (smaller defects found earlier are easier to fix). TDD and CI are proven practices for shifting defect discovery earlier in the development cycle, addressing the root cause.

Option A (acceptance) doesn't address the problem - it just accommodates it. While some buffer is realistic, acceptance shouldn't be the strategy when better options exist.

Option B (avoidance) doesn't solve the root cause. Longer sprints still have late-discovered defects; they're just found later in a longer cycle.

Option D (transfer) isn't really risk transfer - hiring QA doesn't transfer risk to another party. More QA might help, but without process changes, defects will still be found late.
