# Question

A government agency is modernising a 30-year-old core system. It must stay live throughout, documentation is sparse, and requirements include replicating existing behaviour plus adding new features. What approach is MOST appropriate?

## Options

- **A**: Reverse-engineer the old system fully into a complete specification before building anything new.
- **B**: A big-bang predictive replacement so cutover happens once and risk is concentrated in a planned window.
- **C**: A greenfield agile build of the new system, with the old system replaced once the new one is ready.
- **D**: A phased approach: discovery iterations to learn current behaviour, incremental migration of functions, parallel running of old and new during cutover.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The combination of unknown legacy behaviour, must-stay-live operations, and new requirements rules out anything monolithic. (D) layers in the right techniques: discover, migrate slice by slice, and run both systems while validating. (A) is impractical — full reverse engineering of a 30-year-old system rarely converges. (B) concentrates risk dangerously for a system processing millions of transactions a day. (C) ignores the obligation to replicate behaviours that may not even be documented.
