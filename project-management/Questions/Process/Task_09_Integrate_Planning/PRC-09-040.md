# Question

At a steering committee, one executive demands contingency be added to every individual activity in the integrated plan; another insists no contingency at all should be needed if the estimates are sound. How should the project manager respond?

## Options

- **A**: Comply with both by adding activity-level contingency in one plan version and removing it in another version.
- **B**: Explain that PMI practice uses project-level contingency reserves from risk analysis, with separate management reserves.
- **C**: Add per-activity contingency to satisfy the more senior executive and avoid further steering committee conflict.
- **D**: Strip all contingency from the plan entirely to satisfy the executive who challenged the reserve approach.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The right move is education, not capitulation. PMI's contingency model uses risk-based reserves at the project level (and management reserves above that) rather than padding individual activities, which encourages local consumption and obscures variance. (A) creates two competing plans. (C) and (D) trade rigour for politics in opposite directions.
