# Question

A manufacturing line for medical sensors shows control charts that are stable and within statistical limits, yet 3% of finished units fall outside the customer's tolerance specification of less than 1% defects. The team has already retrained operators and replaced suspect sensors. What should the project manager do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Tighten the control limits on the chart so defects trigger alerts sooner.
- **B**: Add a 100% inspection step at the end of the line to screen out defective units.
- **C**: Treat the gap as common cause variation and initiate a process improvement review.
- **D**: Issue a corrective action to the operators on the shifts producing the most defects.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

A stable in-control process producing consistent defects is producing common cause variation that is built into the process design. Per PMI quality philosophy, that requires management-led process change (equipment, materials, methods), not local fixes. (A) misunderstands control limits, which are calculated from the process and not arbitrary thresholds. (B) inspects in quality rather than building it in, leaving the underlying defect rate unchanged. (D) blames operators for variation that is systemic, which violates Deming's principle that most variation is owned by the system, not the worker.
