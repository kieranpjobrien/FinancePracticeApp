# Question

A construction project manager notices that concrete pours are consistently failing quality tests. Investigation reveals that the supplier is meeting contract specifications, but those specifications were based on outdated standards. Correcting this mid-project would require change requests and additional budget. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Continue using the current supplier since they meet contract specifications
- **B**: Document the issue as a lesson learned for future projects
- **C**: Initiate a change request to update specifications and source compliant materials, even though it impacts cost
- **D**: Negotiate with the supplier to provide higher-quality materials at the same price

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The correct answer is C. Quality failures that affect the end product must be addressed, even if they require change requests and budget impacts. Using materials that fail quality tests creates long-term liability, safety risks, and potentially larger costs for remediation later. The PM should follow the change control process to address the specification gap.

Option A (continue as-is) prioritises contract compliance over actual quality. Meeting outdated specifications doesn't ensure fitness for purpose.

Option B (lesson learned only) doesn't address the current problem. Lessons learned are valuable but don't fix defective concrete in this project.

Option D (negotiate same price) is unrealistic - better materials cost more. Even if successful, this avoids the necessary change control process.
