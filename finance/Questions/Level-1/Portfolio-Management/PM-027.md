# Question

An analyst issues a "buy" recommendation on a stock at $50. Six months later, despite deteriorating fundamentals and a price drop to $35, she maintains her recommendation, citing only data that supports her original thesis while dismissing negative information. She is most likely exhibiting:

## Options

- **A**: Anchoring and loss aversion
- **B**: Confirmation bias and anchoring
- **C**: Representativeness and availability bias

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The analyst exhibits two cognitive biases:

**Confirmation Bias:**
- Seeking out information that confirms her existing belief (buy recommendation)
- Dismissing or underweighting contradictory evidence (deteriorating fundamentals)
- Processing information in a biased manner to support her original conclusion

**Anchoring:**
- Fixating on the original $50 price and initial thesis
- Insufficiently adjusting views despite new information
- The original analysis serves as an "anchor" that biases subsequent judgments

These biases often work together, making it difficult to change positions even when evidence suggests the original thesis is wrong.

**Common mistakes:**
- Option A: While the analyst may have loss aversion (reluctance to admit being wrong), the primary behaviors described are cognitive processing errors
- Option C: Representativeness (judging by stereotypes) and availability (overweighting recent/memorable info) don't fit the described behavior
