# Question

An investor keeps her "retirement money" in conservative bonds and her "play money" in speculative stocks, treating each account separately rather than as a unified portfolio. This behavior best illustrates:

## Options

- **A**: Regret aversion
- **B**: Mental accounting
- **C**: Representativeness

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Mental accounting is a cognitive bias where investors:

- Treat money differently based on subjective categories
- Segregate wealth into separate "buckets" with different purposes
- Fail to consider the total portfolio's risk-return profile
- May result in suboptimal asset allocation

In this example:
- "Retirement money" and "play money" are artificial mental categories
- Each bucket is managed independently
- The overall portfolio may not be optimal from a risk-return perspective
- Modern portfolio theory suggests evaluating risk at the total portfolio level

**Common mistakes:**
- Option A (Regret aversion): Fear of making decisions that might later prove wrong
- Option C (Representativeness): Judging probability based on how similar something is to a stereotype
