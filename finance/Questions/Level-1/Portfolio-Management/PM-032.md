# Question

A risk manager reports that the portfolio's 99% daily VaR is $2 million. The CFO asks what the maximum possible daily loss is. The most appropriate response is:

## Options

- **A**: The maximum daily loss is $2 million
- **B**: VaR does not indicate the maximum loss; losses beyond $2 million are possible 1% of the time
- **C**: The maximum loss can be estimated by doubling the VaR to $4 million

## Answer

B

## Explanation

VaR has important limitations:

**What VaR tells us:**
- 99% of the time, losses will not exceed $2 million
- There is a 1% probability of losses exceeding $2 million

**What VaR does NOT tell us:**
- The maximum possible loss (tail risk)
- The expected loss when losses exceed VaR
- The distribution of losses beyond the VaR threshold

This is why VaR is often supplemented with:
- **Conditional VaR (CVaR/Expected Shortfall):** Expected loss given that losses exceed VaR
- **Stress testing:** Evaluates portfolio under extreme scenarios
- **Scenario analysis:** Tests specific adverse conditions

**Common mistakes:**
- Option A: VaR is a threshold, not a maximum; extreme events can cause losses far exceeding VaR
- Option C: There is no simple multiplier to estimate maximum losses; tail risks can be many times VaR
