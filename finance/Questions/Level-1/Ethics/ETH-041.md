# Question

A portfolio manager's composite returned 12% last year. When preparing marketing materials, she considers showing only the top-performing accounts which returned 18%. This practice would most likely:

## Options

- **A**: Be acceptable if she discloses that not all accounts are included
- **B**: Violate standards against misrepresentation by showing unrepresentative performance
- **C**: Be required to show the most relevant performance data to prospective clients

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Cherry-picking top-performing accounts to present misleading performance figures violates standards against misrepresentation. Performance presentations must be fair, accurate, and complete. Showing only the best-performing accounts gives a false impression of the manager's typical results and misleads prospective clients about expected outcomes.

**Common mistakes:**
- Option A is incorrect because disclosure does not cure the fundamental misrepresentation; composite performance should be shown
- Option C mischaracterizes selective presentation as a requirement when it actually constitutes misleading marketing
