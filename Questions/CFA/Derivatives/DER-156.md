# Question

An interest rate cap is essentially a series of:

## Options

- **A**: Call options on interest rates (or put options on bonds)
- **B**: Put options on interest rates (or call options on bonds)
- **C**: Forward contracts on interest rates

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Interest rate caps:

**Structure:**
- Cap = Series of caplets
- Each caplet covers one period
- Payoff if rate > cap rate

**Caplet payoff:**
If reference rate > cap rate:
Payment = Notional × (Rate - Cap) × (days/360)

**Option analogy:**
- Call on interest rates
- Benefits when rates rise above cap
- Buyer pays premium upfront

**Bond equivalent:**
- Put on bond prices
- Bond prices fall when rates rise
- Equivalent exposure

**Uses:**
- Floating-rate borrowers
- Protect against rising rates
- Insurance on interest costs

**Floors (opposite):**
- Put options on rates
- Call options on bonds
- Protect against falling rates

**Collar:**
- Long cap + Short floor
- Or vice versa

**Common mistakes:**
- Option B: This describes floors
- Option C: Caps are contingent claims, not commitments
