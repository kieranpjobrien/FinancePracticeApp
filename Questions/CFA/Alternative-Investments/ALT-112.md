# Question

The "denominator effect" in alternative investment allocations occurs when:

## Options

- **A**: Alternative investments increase in value faster than public markets
- **B**: Public market declines increase the relative weight of illiquid alternatives
- **C**: All investments perform equally

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Denominator effect explained:

**The mechanism:**
- Public markets decline sharply
- Private assets valued with lag
- Total portfolio (denominator) shrinks
- Alternatives become overweight

**Example:**
- $100 portfolio: 10% alternatives ($10)
- Public drops 30%: Portfolio now $73
- Alternatives still valued at $10
- New allocation: 13.7%

**Consequences:**
- Policy limits exceeded
- Forced to reduce alternatives
- Sell at bad time (secondary market)
- Liquidity stress

**Why it happens:**
- Stale private valuations
- Mark-to-market vs appraisal
- Timing differences
- Illiquidity of alternatives

**Management strategies:**
- Dynamic allocation ranges
- Commitment pacing models
- Liquidity stress testing

**Common mistakes:**
- Option A: Opposite effect described
- Option C: Differential performance causes it
