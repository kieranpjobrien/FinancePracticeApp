# Question

Naive diversification (1/n rule) refers to:

## Options

- **A**: Optimizing portfolio weights using mean-variance analysis
- **B**: Equally dividing investments across available options
- **C**: Investing entirely in the lowest-risk option

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Naive diversification:

**Definition:**
- Divide equally among available options
- Also called "1/n" or "equal weighting"
- Simple heuristic, no optimization

**Example:**
- 401(k) with 10 funds → 10% in each
- Ignores risk, return, correlation
- "Mental shortcut" for allocation

**Research findings:**
- Often used in retirement plans
- Number of options affects stock/bond split
- More stock funds → higher equity allocation

**Comparison to optimal:**
- May not be efficient
- Ignores useful information
- But: Simple and avoids estimation error

**Common mistakes:**
- Option A: Naive avoids optimization complexity
- Option C: This is extreme risk aversion, not diversification
