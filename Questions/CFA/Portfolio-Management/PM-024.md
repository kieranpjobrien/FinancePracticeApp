# Question

An investor who refuses to sell a losing position because "it's not a loss until I sell" is most likely exhibiting:

## Options

- **A**: Loss aversion
- **B**: Confirmation bias
- **C**: Anchoring

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Loss aversion is an emotional bias where investors:

- Feel losses more intensely than equivalent gains (typically 2-2.5x more painful)
- Hold losing positions too long, hoping to "break even"
- Avoid realizing losses to prevent the psychological pain
- Rationalize with phrases like "it's only a paper loss"

This stems from prospect theory (Kahneman and Tversky), which shows people evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point and weigh losses more heavily than gains.

**Common mistakes:**
- Option B (Confirmation bias): A cognitive bias where investors seek information that confirms existing beliefs
- Option C (Anchoring): A cognitive bias where investors fixate on irrelevant reference points (like purchase price) when making decisions
