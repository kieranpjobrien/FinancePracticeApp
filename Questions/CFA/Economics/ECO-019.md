# Question

Quantitative easing (QE) differs from conventional open market operations primarily because QE:

## Options

- **A**: Involves only short-term government securities
- **B**: Typically involves large-scale purchases of longer-term assets when policy rates are near zero
- **C**: Reduces the central bank's balance sheet

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Quantitative easing is an unconventional monetary policy used when policy rates approach zero (the "zero lower bound"). Unlike conventional open market operations focused on short-term rates, QE involves large-scale purchases of longer-term government bonds and sometimes other assets like mortgage-backed securities. This aims to lower long-term interest rates and stimulate the economy when conventional rate cuts are exhausted.

**Common mistakes:**
- Option A describes conventional operations, not QE
- Option C is backwards—QE expands the central bank's balance sheet
