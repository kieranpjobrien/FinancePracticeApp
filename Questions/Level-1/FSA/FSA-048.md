# Question

Compared to straight-line depreciation, double-declining balance depreciation in the early years of an asset's life results in:

## Options

- **A**: Lower depreciation expense and higher net income
- **B**: Higher depreciation expense and lower net income
- **C**: Equal depreciation expense and net income

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Double-declining balance (DDB) is an accelerated method that front-loads depreciation expense. DDB rate = 2 × (1/useful life), applied to declining book value. Early years have higher expense, reducing net income and asset values faster. Total depreciation over the asset's life is the same; only the timing differs.

**Common mistakes:**
- Option A describes later years of DDB, not early years
- Option C is incorrect—methods produce different patterns
