# Question

An investor believes a company with innovative products and charismatic leadership must be a good investment, without analyzing its financials. This reflects:

## Options

- **A**: Representativeness bias
- **B**: Self-attribution bias
- **C**: Framing bias

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Representativeness bias:

**Definition:**
- Judging probability based on stereotypes or similarity
- Assuming something that looks like a good investment is a good investment
- Ignoring base rates and statistical probabilities

**In this example:**
- Innovation + charisma = "good company" stereotype
- Investor ignores actual financial analysis
- Company characteristics don't guarantee stock returns

**Forms of representativeness:**
- Sample size neglect: Drawing conclusions from small samples
- Base rate neglect: Ignoring underlying probabilities
- Hot hand fallacy: Believing streaks will continue

**Common mistakes:**
- Option B (Self-attribution): Taking credit for successes, blaming failures on external factors
- Option C (Framing): Decisions affected by how choices are presented
