# Question

An investor who selects the first "good enough" investment option rather than evaluating all alternatives is demonstrating:

## Options

- **A**: Unbounded rationality
- **B**: Satisficing behavior due to bounded rationality
- **C**: Loss aversion

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Bounded rationality and satisficing:

**Bounded rationality (Herbert Simon):**
- Humans have limited cognitive capacity
- Cannot process all available information
- Time and attention are scarce resources
- Leads to simplified decision-making

**Satisficing:**
- Choosing option that is "good enough"
- Stops search once threshold met
- Contrast with optimizing (finding best option)
- Rational response to cognitive limits

**Investment implications:**
- May not find optimal portfolio
- Easier decisions may be preferred
- Status quo maintained to avoid analysis
- Heuristics used for complex decisions

**Not necessarily irrational:**
- Cost of perfect analysis may exceed benefits
- Practical response to complexity

**Common mistakes:**
- Option A: Bounded rationality is opposite of unbounded
- Option C: Loss aversion is about avoiding losses, not decision shortcuts
