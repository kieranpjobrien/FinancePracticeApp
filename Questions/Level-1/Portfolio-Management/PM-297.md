# Question

Mental accounting most likely leads to:

## Options

- **A**: Optimal portfolio construction based on total wealth
- **B**: Treating money differently based on source or intended use
- **C**: Ignoring investment gains and losses

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Mental accounting:

**Definition:**
- Cognitive bias
- Segregating money into mental "accounts"
- Different rules for different buckets
- Violates fungibility principle

**Manifestations:**
- "House money" effect (treat gains differently)
- Separate vacation vs. retirement funds
- Spending inheritance differently
- Income vs. capital distinction

**Investment impact:**
- Suboptimal asset allocation
- Multiple accounts not integrated
- Ignoring total portfolio view
- Behavioral portfolio theory layers

**Example problems:**
- Safe bonds in one account, risky stocks in another
- Not considering all wealth together
- Inefficient overall portfolio

**Can be helpful:**
- Goal-based planning
- Budgeting assistance
- Forced savings discipline

**Common mistakes:**
- Option A: Leads to suboptimal, not optimal allocation
- Option C: Still considers gains/losses, just separately
