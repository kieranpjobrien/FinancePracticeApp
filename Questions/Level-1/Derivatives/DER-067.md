# Question

A "long" position in a derivative typically means the party:

## Options

- **A**: Has sold the derivative and has obligations
- **B**: Has bought the derivative or benefits from price increases
- **C**: Has the shortest time until expiration

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Long vs. short positions:

**Long position:**
- Buyer of the contract
- Benefits from price increases
- In options: pays premium, gets rights
- In forwards/futures: agrees to buy

**Short position:**
- Seller of the contract
- Benefits from price decreases
- In options: receives premium, has obligations
- In forwards/futures: agrees to sell

**Examples:**

*Long call:*
- Right to buy
- Profits if price rises

*Long put:*
- Right to sell
- Profits if price falls (but "long" the put)

*Long forward:*
- Obligated to buy
- Profits if spot > forward price

**Note on puts:**
- "Long put" = Bought a put
- Still benefits from price decline of underlying
- "Long" refers to position, not direction

**Common mistakes:**
- Option A: This describes a short position
- Option C: Unrelated to position direction
