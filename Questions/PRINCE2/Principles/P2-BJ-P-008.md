# Question

A water utility is initiating a leak-detection sensor rollout. The benefits comfortably outweigh the costs (viable) and the business genuinely needs the outcome (desirable), but the project manager judges that the chosen technology cannot realistically be delivered with the available skills and timescale. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Proceed, because a project that is desirable and viable has a sound enough justification
- **B**: Flag to the project board that the project may not be achievable, so the justification is incomplete and must be addressed before authorisation
- **C**: Start delivery and treat the delivery doubts purely as a risk to be managed later
- **D**: Reduce the cost tolerance to compensate for the delivery uncertainty

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A justifiable project must be desirable, viable and achievable; failing achievability means the justification is not sound, and the board needs to know before authorising. (A) is the near-miss — two of three tests passing is not enough when the project cannot actually be delivered. (C) buries a fundamental viability problem inside routine risk management. (D) adjusts a tolerance that does nothing to make an undeliverable approach achievable.
