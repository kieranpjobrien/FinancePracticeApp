# Question

A national museum's exhibition-app project remains viable overall, but the executive privately tells the project manager to keep the Business Case showing the original, now-unachievable benefits, because cancelling would be politically embarrassing. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Comply, because the executive owns the Business Case and has given a direct instruction
- **B**: Keep the optimistic figures but record a private note of disagreement for personal protection
- **C**: Maintain an honest Business Case reflecting the true expected benefits, and escalate through project assurance or corporate management if the executive refuses
- **D**: Revise the benefits down silently without telling the executive or the project board

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The principle demands an honest, valid justification; the project manager must keep the Business Case truthful and, if the executive insists otherwise, escalate via project assurance or the commissioning corporate or programme management. (A) is the near-miss — executive ownership does not license a knowingly false Business Case. (B) preserves the falsehood while merely protecting the individual. (D) bypasses the executive and board, breaching the governance the principle relies on.
