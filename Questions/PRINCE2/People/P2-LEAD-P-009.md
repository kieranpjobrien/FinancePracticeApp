# Question

A pharmaceutical project team has matured into a capable, motivated unit that consistently delivers high-quality work and proposes sound improvements. The project manager, used to directing closely, continues to specify exactly how each task should be done. Engagement is now starting to dip. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Maintain the detailed directive style, as it has produced good results so far
- **B**: Withdraw completely from the team and cease all involvement to maximise their independence
- **C**: Introduce stricter individual reporting to keep standards high as autonomy increases
- **D**: Shift to a delegating style, granting the team greater autonomy within agreed tolerances while remaining available to support

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Situational leadership requires the style to evolve with the team; a competent, committed team performs best under a delegating approach with autonomy inside agreed tolerances, supporting both performance and engagement. (A) clinging to a directive style with a mature team is precisely what is now demotivating them. (B) is the tempting near-miss: delegating is right, but complete withdrawal removes the support and direction-setting the leader should still provide. (C) tightening reporting as autonomy grows sends a contradictory signal of distrust and further erodes engagement.
