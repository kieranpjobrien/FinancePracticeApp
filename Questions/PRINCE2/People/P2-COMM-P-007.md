# Question

A local-government office-relocation project faces growing resistance from a department head who has high influence but was, until now, treated as a low-interest stakeholder receiving only routine bulletins. Their opposition is starting to threaten delivery. What is the BEST response?

## Options

- **A**: Continue the routine bulletins, since the engagement approach was agreed at initiation and should not change
- **B**: Escalate the individual to the project board as a formal risk and otherwise leave engagement unchanged
- **C**: Reduce communication to the department head to avoid giving the resistance a platform
- **D**: Reassess the stakeholder's position, recognise their now-high interest and influence, and move to active two-way engagement to understand and address their concerns

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Stakeholder positions change, so the engagement approach should be revisited and upgraded to active, two-way engagement that seeks to understand and influence the now-critical stakeholder. (A) treating the initiation-stage plan as fixed ignores that the stakeholder's interest has shifted materially. (B) logging a risk is the tempting near-miss, and may be warranted, but doing so without changing the engagement leaves the root cause unaddressed. (C) cutting off communication with an influential opponent is likely to harden resistance.
