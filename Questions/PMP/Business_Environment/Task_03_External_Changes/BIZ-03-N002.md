# Question

Your agile team is building a connected home thermostat. A global shortage of a specific microcontroller is reported across the industry, and your hardware supplier warns that lead times have tripled and that uncommitted stock is being allocated first-come. The component is central to several backlog items the product owner had ranked near the top; the team also has groomed items that need no scarce hardware. The current sprint is unaffected and ends in four days, and sprint planning for the next sprint is the day after. The product owner is available now. How should the project manager proceed?

## Options

- **A**: Ask procurement to place a stock-reservation order today for enough microcontrollers to cover the affected items, then bring the secured position and its cost into the next planning session.
- **B**: Bring the supplier warning to the product owner now and let them decide whether to commit to the hardware items at their new cost and risk or pivot the next sprint toward the hardware-independent work.
- **C**: Reorder the backlog to pull the hardware-independent items to the top for the next sprint, keeping the team productive while the supply picture settles.
- **D**: Add a spike to the next sprint to qualify an alternative component and scope the redesign, so a second source is in hand before the shortage bites.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Every option is a reasonable response to a supply shock, so the discriminator is what most needs to happen *before* the imminent planning session, and that is a value decision the product owner owns. The shortage has changed the cost and risk profile of the top-ranked items; only the product owner can decide whether they remain worth pursuing or the sprint should pivot, and they are available to make that call before planning (B). Reserving stock (A) is a defensible no-regret move, but it spends money to preserve features whose value the product owner has not yet re-confirmed at the new cost — it pre-buys one answer to the question B is asking. Reordering toward hardware-independent work (C) is sensible flow management but quietly makes the prioritisation decision for the product owner rather than informing it. Qualifying a second source (D) is prudent contingency, but it commits next-sprint capacity to one branch before the product owner has weighed whether these features still warrant that investment. A, C and D are all candidate outcomes of the conversation B convenes — doing any of them first lets the PM pre-empt a call that belongs to the product owner.
