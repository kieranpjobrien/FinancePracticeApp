# Question

An agile team is six iterations into launching a premium subscription tier and is delivering working increments on schedule. A sharp rise in interest rates and a consumer downturn have cut the discretionary spending the business case assumed. The sponsor has seen the same economic news, agrees the original benefit forecast now looks optimistic, and has asked the project manager what to bring to the next steering committee. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Prepare a revised benefits projection under current economic assumptions and present options for the committee to decide on continued investment.
- **B**: Demonstrate a leaner minimum viable subscription tier the team can ship sooner, so value is proven before the committee weighs the downturn.
- **C**: Propose pausing the project until interest rates and consumer confidence stabilise, so investment is not sunk into eroded demand.
- **D**: Reframe the benefits case around customer retention and lifetime value rather than new-subscription revenue, to show the tier still pays back.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The sponsor already accepts the forecast has weakened and is asking what to put in front of governance, so the value the project manager adds is an evidence-based re-projection of the benefits under today's assumptions plus framed options, letting the body that owns the investment decide with current facts (A). Showing a leaner MVP (B) is a sensible response the committee might choose, but presenting it as the move pre-selects a scope cut before the benefits have been re-evaluated and the decision-makers have weighed alternatives. Proposing a pause (C) likewise pre-empts the governance call and may forfeit a tier that is still viable or strategically wanted. Re-framing the case around retention and lifetime value (D) may be valid, but advancing one favourable narrative risks steering the committee with a re-cut justification rather than an honest reassessment of whether the original benefits still hold.
