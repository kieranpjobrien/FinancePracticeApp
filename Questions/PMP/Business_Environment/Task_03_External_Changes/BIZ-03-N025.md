# Question

Six months into a two-year predictive project to build a new distribution warehouse, a competitor opens a regional automated facility expected to undercut your client's delivery prices. The original business case rested on a cost advantage that this development largely erodes, though the warehouse still supports other stated benefits such as capacity and resilience. The project is on schedule and on budget. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Document how the competitor's move affects the business case benefits, and bring that analysis to the sponsor to decide whether and how to proceed.
- **B**: Ask the sponsor to convene the benefits owners and reassess the business case now that a key assumption has changed.
- **C**: Raise the threat in the next steering committee report and recommend the project continue, since most benefits remain intact.
- **D**: Initiate a feasibility study into adding automation so the facility can compete on cost, and present it to the sponsor.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The PM's accountability is to connect delivery to value, so the most complete first move is to analyse exactly how the competitor's move affects each benefit and hand the sponsor a clear basis to decide whether and how to proceed (A). Asking the sponsor to reassess the case (B) is right in spirit, but pushing the reassessment to the sponsor without first supplying the impact analysis leaves them to start cold—the PM should bring the evidence. Reporting the threat while recommending continuation (C) is defensible because other benefits survive, yet pre-judging the recommendation before the benefits owners have weighed in pre-empts a governance call that is not the PM's to make. Launching an automation feasibility study (D) presumes a solution before the sponsor has even decided the case needs solving, committing effort prematurely. The margin is that A equips the decision-maker with analysis without pre-selecting the outcome.
