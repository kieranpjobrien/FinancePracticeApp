# Question

A hybrid programme runs for three years in a sector exposed to frequent regulatory, economic and competitive shifts. The team already holds regular risk reviews, and stakeholders raise external developments ad hoc when they happen to notice them. The sponsor asks the project manager to make the programme more reliably aware of external change so nothing material is missed and responses start earlier. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Build environmental scanning into the cadence—assign owners to track defined external categories and feed findings into the existing risk reviews.
- **B**: Add a standing "external environment" agenda item to the regular risk review so the team discusses developments together each cycle.
- **C**: Commission a periodic external-environment analysis from a specialist function and circulate it to the programme ahead of each governance checkpoint.
- **D**: Expand stakeholder engagement so the people closest to regulators, customers and the market report relevant changes to the programme as they arise.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The sponsor's concern is reliability—nothing material missed and responses started early—which needs scanning that is owned, categorised and routed into action, not just discussed (A). Adding a standing agenda item (B) improves the conversation but a discussion slot with no one accountable for actively scanning defined categories still depends on whoever happens to bring something, which is the gap the sponsor identified. Commissioning a periodic specialist analysis (C) is valuable input, yet a report delivered each checkpoint can lag fast-moving shifts and concentrates awareness in one external source rather than embedding continuous vigilance in the programme. Broadening stakeholder reporting (D) widens the inbound signal and is genuinely useful, but relying on stakeholders to notice and forward changes recreates the ad hoc pattern that is already failing. The margin is that A assigns explicit ownership over named external categories and connects it to the decision cadence, turning scanning into a dependable process where B, C and D each strengthen one input without closing the reliability gap.
