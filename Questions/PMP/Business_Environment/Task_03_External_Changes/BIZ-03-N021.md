# Question

Midway through a 14-month predictive programme to build a consumer lending platform, the national regulator publishes draft rules that, if enacted unchanged, would require affordability checks the product does not support. The rules enter a 90-day consultation before becoming binding, and industry bodies are expected to lobby for amendments. The architecture team says retrofitting affordability checks later would be far costlier than designing for them now. The sponsor wants a recommendation. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Commission an impact assessment of the draft against scope, schedule and cost, and present response options to the sponsor.
- **B**: Add the draft requirement to the risk register, assess likelihood and impact given the expected lobbying, and define triggers for action.
- **C**: Have the architects build in extension points now so affordability checks can be added cheaply once the final text is known.
- **D**: Submit the project's position to the consultation and seek to shape the rule toward what the platform can already support.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The rule is uncertain because the final text may shift through consultation and lobbying, so the first move is to treat it as a risk: capture it, assess likelihood and impact, and set triggers that tell you when to act (B). A full scope/schedule/cost impact assessment (A) is premature against text that is not yet stable and can over-invest analysis in a moving target before the risk is even characterised. Building extension points now (C) is a sensible *response* the risk analysis may recommend, but committing design effort before assessing the risk pre-selects a mitigation. Engaging the consultation (D) is also a legitimate influence tactic, yet acting to shape the rule before understanding its probable impact reverses the natural order—assess first, then decide whether to mitigate, influence, or both.
