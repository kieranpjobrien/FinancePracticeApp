# Question

Fresh research shows a sharp rise in interest rates has made your agile product's planned premium subscription tier far less attractive to the price-sensitive segment it targets. The tier is the centrepiece of the next two sprints. The product owner has reviewed the research, accepts it, and has reprioritised the backlog—deferring the premium-tier stories and elevating other work—while pricing is rethought. The sponsor, who championed the premium tier to the executive committee, hears about the reprioritisation secondhand and is annoyed it happened without their input. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Arrange for the product owner to brief the sponsor on the research and the reprioritisation rationale, reaffirming that backlog priority sits with the product owner while keeping the sponsor informed.
- **B**: Ask the product owner to restore the premium-tier stories to their original priority until the sponsor has agreed to the change, to respect the sponsor's stake.
- **C**: Escalate the conflict to the steering committee to rule on whether the sponsor or the product owner should set the priority of the premium tier.
- **D**: Reassure the sponsor that the deferral is temporary and re-add the premium-tier work to a near-term sprint to demonstrate continued commitment to it.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Responding to validated market evidence by reprioritising the backlog is squarely the product owner's authority, and they have exercised it correctly; the gap is communication, not the decision, so the fix is to have the product owner brief the sponsor on the rationale while reinforcing who owns priority and keeping the sponsor engaged (A). Restoring the original priority until the sponsor agrees (B) hands the sponsor an effective veto over backlog ordering, inverting the product owner's role and re-committing the team to work the evidence shows is now lower value. Escalating to the steering committee to adjudicate ownership (C) treats a settled accountability—priority belongs to the product owner—as an open question, and turns a communication lapse into a governance dispute. Re-adding the premium work to a near-term sprint to mollify the sponsor (D) reverses a sound, evidence-based decision for relationship reasons, optimising for the sponsor's feelings over the product's value. The margin is that A repairs the real problem—the sponsor was surprised—without surrendering the product owner's legitimate decision rights, where B, C and D all respond to the sponsor's annoyance by undoing or relitigating a correct call.
