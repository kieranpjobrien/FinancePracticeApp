# Question

A predictive programme in a volatile sector faces three enacted regulatory changes in one reporting period. All three are now legally binding—none is a draft—and each compels some response. They differ in how the obligation lands on the current plan. Match each scenario to the project manager's most appropriate immediate action.

## Items

- **1**: The new rule mandates a feature the baseline omits, the required change is well understood, and the compliance deadline sits comfortably within the current schedule.
- **2**: The new rule mandates a change, but how it applies to this product is genuinely ambiguous; complying the wrong way could cost as much as not complying, and a regulator clarification is expected in two months.
- **3**: The new rule does not change any deliverable, but its compliance costs erode the savings the approved business case was built on, putting the project's justification in question.

## Matches

- **A**: Raise a change request to bring the mandated requirement into the scope baseline and schedule the work.
- **B**: Hold off on a baseline change, engage legal to interpret the obligation, and design to keep options open until the clarification arrives.
- **C**: Escalate to the sponsor to reassess the project's benefits and continued justification.

## Answer

1-A, 2-B, 3-C

## Explanation

All three obligations are enacted and binding, so "certainty of the law" cannot be the discriminator—what differs is whether the *required response* is clear, ambiguous, or value-threatening. Item 1 is a known requirement that fits the schedule, so it is a straightforward scope change to formalise through a change request and plan (1-A). Item 2 is binding but its application is unclear and acting wrongly is as costly as inaction, so the response is to avoid locking in a baseline change, get legal interpretation, and keep design options open until the imminent clarification resolves the ambiguity (2-B). Item 3 mandates no deliverable change at all; its effect is financial, undermining the business case, which is a governance question for the sponsor about whether the project still earns its justification (3-C). The trap is matching on the word "mandated": both 1 and 2 are mandates, but only 1 has a clear response to baseline now—2's correct move is to hold and interpret precisely because committing prematurely to the wrong interpretation carries the same cost as non-compliance. Reading 3 as just another scope change misses that nothing to build has changed; only the economics have.
