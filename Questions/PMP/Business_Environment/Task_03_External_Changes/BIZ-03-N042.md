# Question

On a predictive plant-upgrade project, a government announces import tariffs taking effect in six weeks that will raise the landed cost of specialised equipment from your fixed-price supplier, who has built the units but not yet shipped them. The contract is silent on tariff changes, the equipment is on the critical path, and your procurement specialist has already estimated the likely tariff cost. The sponsor asks the project manager to recommend a course of action at a review in two days. What should the project manager do next?

## Options

- **A**: Ask procurement to open talks with the supplier on shipping ahead of the tariff date and on how the contract allocates the new cost.
- **B**: Build a short options analysis comparing early shipment, absorbing the cost, and alternative sourcing on cost, schedule and contractual risk.
- **C**: Raise a change request to lift the cost baseline by procurement's estimated tariff figure ahead of the sponsor review.
- **D**: Run a quantitative risk analysis on the tariff to size the cost and schedule exposure with confidence ranges.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Procurement has already produced a cost estimate, so the gap the sponsor needs filled is a comparison of the realistic responses and their trade-offs, which is exactly what an options analysis delivers and is the natural input to a recommendation in two days (B). Opening supplier talks on early shipment and cost allocation (A) is a sound action and will likely follow, but it pursues one avenue before the options have been weighed and may not be the route the sponsor chooses. A change request to the baseline (C) commits to absorbing the cost before alternatives or who bears it under the contract have been settled. A quantitative risk analysis (D) over-engineers a largely known, near-certain cost into a probabilistic model and still would not, on its own, tell the sponsor which course to take.
