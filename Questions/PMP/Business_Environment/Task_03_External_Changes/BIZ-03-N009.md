# Question

Halfway through an agile product build, an economic downturn forces the organisation to cut discretionary budgets. The sponsor tells you the programme's funding will fall by a third from next quarter but still wants something valuable in market. With a third less funding, the team can keep its current size for a shorter runway or reduce its size and run longer. The product owner has a prioritised backlog. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Reduce the team's size now to fit the new budget, then have the product owner re-plan the backlog around the smaller team's velocity.
- **B**: Keep the full team and target the nearest releasable increment, accepting that the runway ends sooner.
- **C**: Ask the sponsor to define exactly which features to drop so the scope clearly matches the reduced funding.
- **D**: Work with the product owner to agree what valuable outcome can realistically be delivered for the reduced funding, before deciding how to reshape the team to deliver it.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The funding cut is a constraint on outcomes, and in agile the product owner owns what gets delivered, so the PM first works with them to agree the valuable target achievable within the new budget, which then drives how the team is reshaped (D). Cutting the team first (A) reverses that order, fixing capacity before the value goal is set and risking a team mis-sized for the agreed outcome. Keeping the full team for a shorter runway (B) is one viable funding shape, but committing to it now pre-empts the outcome conversation that should determine which shape fits. Asking the sponsor to choose the features to drop (C) pushes backlog prioritisation onto the wrong person; the sponsor sets the funding envelope and value expectation, while the product owner decides scope.
