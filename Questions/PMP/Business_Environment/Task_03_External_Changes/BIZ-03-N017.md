# Question

On a predictive fit-out project, a bout of high inflation pushes the price of timber and steel well above the rates assumed in the cost baseline; commentators expect prices to keep climbing for months. The materials have not yet been procured, and the project's contingency reserve could absorb the increase at today's prices but not if they rise much further. What is the most appropriate first action for the project manager?

## Options

- **A**: Procure the timber and steel now to lock in today's prices before they climb further.
- **B**: Submit a change request to draw down contingency to cover the forecast increase.
- **C**: Update the cost forecast using current and projected prices, and review the exposure against the budget and reserves before deciding on a response.
- **D**: Ask the design team whether specifying alternative materials could offset the price rise.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The first step is to quantify the exposure, including the projected trajectory, against the budget and reserves so any response is based on the real picture (C). Procuring now to lock in prices (A) is a credible hedge but commits funds before the exposure is sized and before alternatives are weighed, and it may not be the best use of cash. Drawing down contingency (B) presumes absorbing the rise is the chosen response before the forecast shows whether reserves can in fact cover a still-rising cost. Exploring alternative materials (D) is a reasonable mitigation to consider, but it is one option to evaluate after the impact is understood, not the first move before the size of the problem is known.
