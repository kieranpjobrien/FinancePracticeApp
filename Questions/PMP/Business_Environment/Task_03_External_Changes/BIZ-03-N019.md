# Question

A predictive renewable-energy project was justified largely on a government grant covering 40% of capital cost. Following an election, the incoming administration announces the grant scheme will be wound down; your project's tranche is at risk but not yet formally cancelled, and the wind-down detail is due within a month. Construction is due to start in six weeks, and mobilising the main contractor commits a large, non-refundable payment. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Recommend the sponsor pause mobilisation until the grant wind-down detail is published and the project's tranche is confirmed either way.
- **B**: Work with the sponsor and finance to model the business case under reduced and withdrawn-grant scenarios, and present options ahead of the mobilisation commitment.
- **C**: Raise a change request to defer the construction start so the non-refundable payment is not committed while funding is uncertain.
- **D**: Engage the funding body and industry association to seek clarity on whether projects already approved will retain their grant tranche.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The threat is to the business case, and the impending mobilisation payment is the decision that matters, so the PM first equips the sponsor and finance with modelled scenarios and options before that money is committed (B). Recommending a pause (A) or raising a change request to defer the start (C) are both plausible protective moves, but each pre-selects "delay" before the scenarios show whether the project remains viable, perhaps with reduced grant, and might still proceed. Seeking clarity from the funding body and association (D) is a useful input and could be done in parallel, but it depends on an external party's timing and does not, on its own, ready the sponsor to make the imminent mobilisation decision.
