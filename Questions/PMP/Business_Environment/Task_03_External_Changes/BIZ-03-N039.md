# Question

On an agile programme, three regulatory developments land in the same week—all touching how the product handles customer data, all potentially affecting scope. They differ in how settled they are and in who must act. Match each development to the project manager's most appropriate immediate response.

## Items

- **1**: A data-protection regulator publishes guidance clarifying that an *existing* enacted law already requires a data-export feature the product lacks; enforcement begins in 90 days.
- **2**: A regulator opens consultation on a *proposed* rule that, as drafted, would require new consent screens; the text may change and would not bind for at least a year.
- **3**: An enacted law's interpretation is being tested in a court case whose ruling—expected within months—will determine whether the product's current data-retention design is compliant.

## Matches

- **A**: Have the product owner add the feature to the backlog as mandatory scope and schedule it to be delivered before enforcement, confirming detail with legal.
- **B**: Log it as a risk, assess likelihood and impact with legal, and monitor the proceeding before committing any build.
- **C**: Identify low-regret design choices that keep the current approach defensible under either outcome, and track the proceeding as a high-impact risk.

## Answer

1-A, 2-B, 3-C

## Explanation

The discriminator is not the topic—all three concern data handling—but how certain the obligation is and what response that certainty warrants. Item 1 is a confirmed, already-binding requirement with a hard enforcement date, so it is mandatory scope to add and schedule now, with legal confirming detail (1-A). Item 2 is an unsettled proposal that may change and is far off, so the proportionate response is to log and monitor it as a risk without committing build effort to draft text (2-B). Item 3 is the subtle middle case: the law is already in force, but its application to the product is genuinely uncertain pending a ruling, so neither building a fix (premature—the design may already comply) nor merely monitoring (insufficient—the impact could be severe) fits; the right move is to steer toward low-regret, defensible design while tracking it as a high-impact risk (3-C). Swapping B and C is the trap: both 2 and 3 are "uncertain," but only 3 carries a live, high-impact exposure to an in-force law that warrants active design hedging rather than passive monitoring.
