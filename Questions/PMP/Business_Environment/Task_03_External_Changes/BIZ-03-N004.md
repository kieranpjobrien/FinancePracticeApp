# Question

You run a hybrid programme delivered partly by an offshore vendor paid in a foreign currency. Over two months that currency strengthens by 18% against your home currency, and the remaining vendor invoices now threaten to exhaust the contingency reserve. The contract is fixed-fee in the foreign currency and roughly half the vendor's scope is still outstanding. Which three actions are most appropriate for the project manager to take now? (Choose three.)

## Options

- **A**: Quantify the forecast cost overrun at current and projected exchange rates and update the cost forecast.
- **B**: Engage the sponsor and finance about treasury and funding responses such as currency hedging or releasing management reserve.
- **C**: Review the contract with procurement to see whether any clause shares, caps or allows renegotiation of the currency exposure.
- **D**: Reduce the vendor's remaining scope to the items that must be done offshore, moving the rest to lower-cost or home-currency delivery where feasible.
- **E**: Accelerate the vendor's remaining invoices to pay them now, on the assumption the home currency will weaken further.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

The sound moves are to size the exposure by reforecasting against current and projected rates (A), bring treasury and funding levers such as hedging or management reserve to the sponsor and finance who control them (B), and check with procurement whether the contract already allocates or allows renegotiation of currency risk (C). Re-scoping the vendor's remaining work (D) is a plausible mitigation but is premature as an action "now": it should follow the forecast and contractual review, since the fixed-fee terms and offshore dependencies may make it ineffective or costly to change. Accelerating payments on a currency bet (E) speculates on exchange-rate direction with project funds, paying earlier than required for an uncertain gain, which is not the PM's role.
