# Question

A predictive infrastructure project depends on specialised pumps imported from a single overseas manufacturer. A newly announced trade tariff, effective in 60 days, will add 25% to the landed cost of those pumps, which have not yet been ordered. The PM's team has already modelled the realistic options: ordering now to beat the tariff (accepting an earlier, less favourable payment milestone that strains project cash flow), qualifying an alternative supplier (adds schedule risk but caps exposure), or absorbing the tariff from contingency. Each has a material but different cost and schedule profile, and the 60-day clock is running. What should the project manager do next?

## Options

- **A**: Place the order now to beat the tariff, capturing the certain saving on the pumps before the window closes and resolving the largest cost line.
- **B**: Begin qualifying the alternative supplier in parallel now, so a viable second source exists whichever option is later chosen and the 60-day window is not lost to inaction.
- **C**: Present the modelled options, their trade-offs, and a recommendation to the sponsor and finance, so the funding and cash-flow decision is taken by those who carry it.
- **D**: Submit a change request to draw down contingency to absorb the tariff, locking in the least disruptive option administratively.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The analysis is done; what remains is a choice among options that trade project cash flow, schedule and contingency against each other — and those are funding levers the sponsor and finance carry, so the PM's next step is to put the trade-offs and a recommendation in front of them while 60 days still allow a real decision (C). Ordering now (A) secures the cheapest pump price but silently accepts the cash-flow strain of the earlier milestone, which is precisely the trade-off the decision-makers must weigh, not one the PM should settle for them. Qualifying the second source in parallel (B) is the strongest distractor — it looks like a no-regret hedge against the clock — but it still commits schedule and spend to one branch before the direction is set, and the schedule risk it carries may be exactly what the sponsor would decline. Drawing down contingency (D) pre-selects the absorb option and consumes reserve the decision-makers may want for a different response. The fine margin is B versus C: both respect the 60-day window, but only C keeps the decision with its owners instead of the PM quietly committing the project down one path.
