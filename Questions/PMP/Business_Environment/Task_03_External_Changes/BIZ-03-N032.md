# Question

A sudden shipping crisis has tripled lead times for imported electronic components essential to your predictive product assembly. The affected path currently has three months of float. Which actions should the project manager take to address this external disruption? (Choose three.)

## Options

- **A**: Re-analyse the schedule network to determine whether the longer lead times consume the available float.
- **B**: Investigate qualified alternate or domestic suppliers, even at a higher unit cost.
- **C**: Engage procurement and the supplier to explore expedited freight or partial early shipments.
- **D**: Re-baseline the schedule now to assume the components arrive at the new tripled lead time.
- **E**: Place a forward order for the full remaining quantity immediately to lock in supply at current prices.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

The PM should first test whether the float absorbs the delay (A), pursue alternate sourcing as a hedge (B), and work procurement and the supplier on expediting or staged delivery (C)—proportionate responses that preserve options. Re-baselining to the tripled lead time now (D) bakes a worst-case assumption into the plan before the float analysis or mitigations have been tried, potentially conceding schedule that need not be lost. Committing to a full forward order at once (E) is a single response chosen before alternatives are assessed, tying up capital and inventory against a disruption whose duration is unknown. D and E are both legitimate tools in some circumstances, but each pre-commits before the situation is analysed; the appropriate set assesses and hedges before locking in a costly or irreversible move.
