# Question

A hybrid programme relies on a third-party API from a small specialist provider for a core capability. News breaks that the provider has been acquired by your organisation's largest competitor. The integration works today and the current contract has 14 months to run, but the long-term availability and terms of the API are now uncertain. The PM has logged the threat as a risk. What should the project manager do next?

## Options

- **A**: Ask procurement to seek confirmation from the new owner of continued support and pricing under the existing contract.
- **B**: Have architecture add an abstraction layer now so the application is not tightly coupled to this provider, whatever the new owner decides.
- **C**: Brief the sponsor on the dependency threat and recommend that switching away from the provider be funded as a priority.
- **D**: With architecture and procurement, assess the realistic responses, abstracting the integration, lining up an alternative provider, or building in-house, and their cost and effort, to inform a contingency plan.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The risk is logged but the response is not yet shaped, so the next step is to work with architecture and procurement to evaluate the realistic options and their costs as the basis for a contingency plan (D). Seeking assurances from the new owner (A) is worth doing and may form part of the response, but a competitor-owner's word is weak protection and gathering it is only one input to the broader assessment. Building an abstraction layer now (B) commits engineering effort to one mitigation before the options have been weighed and may be more or less than the situation warrants. Briefing the sponsor to fund a switch as a priority (C) recommends a specific, costly course before the alternatives and their costs are understood.
