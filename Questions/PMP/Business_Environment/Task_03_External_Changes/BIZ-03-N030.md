# Question

A predictive infrastructure project receives notice that a revised environmental regulation now requires an additional habitat survey before ground can be broken. The survey can only run during a breeding season that begins in two months and lasts eight weeks; results take a further two weeks to certify. Ground-breaking was scheduled for next month and sits on the critical path. The project manager has analysed the impact: the survey pushes the earliest possible ground-breaking out by about four months, and several follow-on activities have no float. What should the project manager do next?

## Options

- **A**: Re-sequence the schedule to pull forward any survey-independent work that can proceed during the survey window, then take a revised plan with the recovered time to the sponsor.
- **B**: Submit the four-month delay to the change control board as a schedule baseline change for formal approval before adjusting any work.
- **C**: Approach the regulator to ask whether a provisional or phased early start is permissible while the survey is in progress.
- **D**: Fast-track the follow-on activities by overlapping them so the four months lost to the survey can be recovered downstream.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The impact is already analysed and the survey window is a fixed external constraint, so the most valuable next move is to mine the schedule for survey-independent work that can run *during* the otherwise-idle window, recovering time before anything is escalated or rebaselined (A). Submitting the raw four-month delay to change control first (B) is premature: the PM should bring a plan that already reflects achievable recovery, not hand the board the worst-case slip before mitigation has been explored. Asking the regulator for an early start (C) is a legitimate avenue, but an environmental prerequisite tied to a breeding season is rarely waivable, and pinning the response on a concession the regulator is unlikely to grant risks losing the window while waiting; it is a long shot to pursue alongside, not the first action. Fast-tracking the follow-on work (D) overlaps activities that cannot begin until ground is broken—work downstream of the survey cannot recover time lost upstream of it, and overlapping critical activities adds risk without addressing the real gap. The margin is that A acts on the idle window the constraint creates, which is the recoverable time actually available, where B escalates too early, C bets on an unlikely waiver, and D fast-tracks the wrong part of the network.
