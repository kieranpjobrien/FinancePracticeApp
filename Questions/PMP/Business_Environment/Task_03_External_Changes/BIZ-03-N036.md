# Question

Halfway through building a custom machine-learning recommendation engine in an agile delivery, a cloud vendor releases a managed service. Its marketing claims comparable accuracy to a bespoke model at a fraction of the build cost, and lists an integration connector for your stack. The bespoke engine the team has built so far performs well against the product's data in testing. The product owner and sponsor have seen the vendor's headline figures, think the option could be attractive, and have asked the project manager to come back with something they can base a keep-or-switch decision on. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Run a time-boxed spike that measures the managed service's accuracy on the product's own data and exercises the integration connector end to end.
- **B**: Build a weighted comparison of the managed service against the bespoke build across cost, accuracy, lock-in and support, and present it to the product owner.
- **C**: Pause further investment in the bespoke engine while the managed-service option is evaluated, to avoid sinking cost into a path that may be dropped.
- **D**: Raise the potential switch as a change to the product's technical approach and route it through change control for authorisation.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The two figures that would actually swing a keep-or-switch decision—whether the service matches the bespoke engine's accuracy on *this product's* data, and whether the advertised connector works for this stack—are vendor claims, not yet observed in this environment. The first move is therefore to generate that evidence directly with a time-boxed spike (A). A weighted comparison matrix (B) is a sound way to present a conclusion, but filling it in now means populating the two decisive cells with the vendor's unproven marketing numbers; it becomes meaningful only once the spike supplies real figures, so it follows rather than precedes the spike. Pausing the bespoke build (C) treats the managed service as the likely winner before any of its claims are tested, conceding a working asset on the strength of headline numbers. Routing a switch through change control (D) is the right mechanism only after a decision to switch—raising a change request before the gating evidence exists asks governance to authorise a direction no one has chosen. The margin is that A produces precisely the evidence the decision hinges on, where B presents an answer built on unverified inputs, and C and D both act on a switch before it has been justified.
