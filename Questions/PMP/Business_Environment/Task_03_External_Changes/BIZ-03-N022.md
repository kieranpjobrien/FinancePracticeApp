# Question

An agile team is one sprint from releasing a mobile payments app. Mid-sprint, a well-funded competitor launches a near-identical product whose headline feature is instant peer-to-peer transfers—a capability the team ranked low and has not built. The product owner, after talking with the sponsor, decides the release should still go ahead on schedule without peer-to-peer transfers, treating it as a fast-follow. Two senior engineers disagree strongly, arguing the launch will look obsolete on day one, and want the sprint reopened to attempt the feature. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Support the product owner's prioritisation call, ensure the engineers' obsolescence concern is captured as input to the fast-follow decision, and keep the sprint goal intact.
- **B**: Reopen the sprint scope discussion with the team and product owner so the engineers' objection can be weighed against the release date before work continues.
- **C**: Escalate the disagreement to the sponsor, since the sponsor co-owns the release decision and can settle whether the date or the feature takes precedence.
- **D**: Ask the product owner to add a peer-to-peer transfer story to the backlog for the very next sprint so the team has a concrete fast-follow commitment to rally around.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Prioritising scope against a release date is the product owner's accountability, and they have made that call in consultation with the sponsor; the PM's job is to uphold that decision-rights boundary while making sure the dissent is heard as input, not as grounds to reopen a settled question (A). Reopening the sprint scope discussion (B) relitigates a decision the role that owns it has already made, undermining the product owner and the team's commitment over a disagreement about priority rather than new information. Escalating to the sponsor (C) is tempting because the sponsor is involved, but the sponsor already weighed in and the question—what to build—is the product owner's to decide; routing it upward invites the sponsor to override the very role they delegated it to. Adding the fast-follow story now (D) is sound backlog hygiene the product owner will likely do anyway, but doing it to placate the engineers sidesteps the actual issue, which is whether the PM respects the prioritisation decision or treats every strong objection as a trigger to reverse it. The margin is that A protects legitimate decision ownership while still valuing the technical concern, where B and C erode that ownership and D dodges it.
