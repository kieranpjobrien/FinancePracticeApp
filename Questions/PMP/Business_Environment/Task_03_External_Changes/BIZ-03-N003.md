# Question

A predictive project to replace a government agency's citizen-facing web portal is in its design phase. New accessibility legislation is enacted requiring conformance to a stricter standard than the current requirements, with a compliance deadline one month after the planned go-live. The agency's legal team has confirmed the portal is in scope, and the sponsor agrees the standard must be met. The design baseline is under change control. What should the project manager do next?

## Options

- **A**: Submit a change request to adopt the new standard in full and rebaseline the schedule for the additional design and testing work, so the board authorises the rework promptly.
- **B**: Direct the design team to begin reworking the affected components to the new standard in parallel, protecting the schedule while the paperwork follows.
- **C**: Incorporate the new standard into the requirements baseline directly as a mandatory legal constraint, since a binding obligation is not a discretionary change for the board to weigh.
- **D**: Have the design authority and a compliance specialist assess exactly which design elements and test criteria the standard affects, then bring a change request with costed options to the change control board.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The destination is fixed — the portal must conform — but the cost, schedule and design route to get there are not yet known, and that is what the board needs in order to authorise the rework responsibly (D). Submitting a request to adopt the standard "in full" (A) honours change control but asks the board to approve work nobody has scoped, and "in full" may commit more than the standard actually requires. Starting rework in parallel (B) protects the schedule and is tempting given the deadline, but it builds to an un-scoped reading of the standard and spends effort the board has not authorised. Folding it straight into the baseline (C) is the subtle trap: it is true that compliance is non-negotiable, but "mandatory outcome" does not mean "no change decision" — the impact on cost, schedule and design still has to be assessed and authorised, and skipping that bypasses the very control that keeps the baseline trustworthy. The fine margin is C versus D: both accept the obligation, but only D produces the scoped impact the board must see before the work is real.
