# Question

During an agile CRM rebuild, a new data-protection law takes effect in 60 days granting customers the right to have their personal data deleted on request. The backlog has no story covering deletion-on-request, and the team is mid-way through a high-value sales-automation epic the sponsor is eager to ship. What should the product owner and project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Have the product owner add a "right to erasure" story and slot it for delivery before the law takes effect, confirming the legal interpretation with counsel.
- **B**: Add the "right to erasure" story to the backlog at the top and pull it into the current sprint ahead of the sales-automation work.
- **C**: Raise a risk that the product will be non-compliant at the deadline and ask the sponsor to decide between the compliance work and the sales epic.
- **D**: Ask legal to confirm the exact obligations first, then have the product owner prioritise the resulting work against the deadline.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A binding law with a fixed deadline creates mandatory scope, and the product owner—who owns the backlog—should add the story and schedule it to land before the deadline, validating the legal detail in parallel rather than blocking on it (A). Pulling it into the current sprint immediately (B) disrupts a committed sprint when the 60-day window gives room to sequence it deliberately; speed beyond what the deadline requires is not free. Escalating a compliance-versus-sales choice to the sponsor (C) frames a false trade-off—legal compliance is not optional, so presenting it as a sponsor decision misplaces accountability. Gating all prioritisation on a full legal opinion first (D) is reasonable diligence, but serialising prioritisation behind legal confirmation wastes part of the runway when the obligation's existence is already clear and detail can be refined as the story is built. The margin is that A treats the requirement as non-negotiable scope, sequences it to the real deadline, and confirms detail concurrently.
