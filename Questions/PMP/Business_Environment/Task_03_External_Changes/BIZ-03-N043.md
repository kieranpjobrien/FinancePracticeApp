# Question

You are leading a hybrid medical-device firmware project. Mid-execution, the governing international safety standard is revised; your organisation has 18 months to bring new products into compliance. The current release ships in four months and is expected to remain on sale well past the 18-month deadline. Your quality lead has reviewed the revision and confirmed that two of its changes touch requirements already verified in this release. What should the project manager do next?

## Options

- **A**: Add the two affected requirements and their re-verification to the backlog and plan them into the remaining iterations before release.
- **B**: Raise a change request to bring the affected requirements and verification scope into the release under change control.
- **C**: Work with the quality lead to size the rework and decide, with the sponsor, whether to comply in this release or a fast-follow within the window.
- **D**: Schedule the two changes for the next planned release, since the 18-month window leaves time after this one ships.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The quality lead has confirmed the standard applies and which requirements it hits, so the open question is no longer whether to act but how much rework this release can absorb against the four-month date and whether full compliance belongs here or in a fast-follow that still meets the window; that is a sizing and sponsor-level trade-off (C). Adding the work straight to the backlog (A) or raising a change request to pull it in (B) both pre-commit to complying in this release before the cost and schedule hit are weighed or the sponsor has chosen the path. Deferring to the next release on the 18-month window (D) ignores that this release stays on sale past the deadline, so a later fix may leave a non-compliant product in market. The decision turns on balancing a confirmed obligation against delivery, not on whether the standard applies.
