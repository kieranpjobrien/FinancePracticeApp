# Question

A two-year predictive programme to build a chain of retail showrooms was approved when borrowing was cheap. Eight months in, the central bank raises the base rate sharply for the third time. The finance director privately tells you her draft figures show the project's internal rate of return now sits below the corporate hurdle, but asks you to "sit on it" until she has refined the numbers, as the sponsor reacts badly to bad news. The next stage gate, which authorises the largest capital tranche, is in two weeks. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Work with the finance director to firm up the revised business case quickly, then ensure the position reaches the sponsor before the stage gate authorises the next tranche.
- **B**: Respect the finance director's request to hold the information, giving her time to refine the figures before anyone is alarmed prematurely.
- **C**: Log the viability concern as a high-priority risk and recommend the steering committee defer the gate decision until the revised business case is complete.
- **D**: Inform the sponsor immediately that the finance director's draft figures show the project is below the hurdle rate.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The pending stage gate is the constraint: authorising the largest tranche on a business case that may no longer be viable is the real exposure, so the PM partners with the finance director to firm up the numbers fast and makes sure the sponsor has them before the gate decision (A). Honouring the request to hold (B) lets a major funding commitment proceed on possibly false assumptions and subordinates the project's interests to the finance director's comfort. Recommending the gate be deferred (C) is a defensible instinct, but it pushes for a delay before the revised numbers exist to justify it, and the two-week window is enough to bring a firm position rather than stall governance on a draft. Relaying unrefined draft figures straight to the sponsor (D) is the strongest trap — it satisfies the duty not to withhold, but it hands the decision-maker an unverified number that could misinform the very call it is meant to protect, and cuts across the finance director when the figures can be firmed up with her inside the window. The fine margin is A versus D: both refuse to suppress the information, but only A ensures what reaches the sponsor is accurate enough to decide on.
