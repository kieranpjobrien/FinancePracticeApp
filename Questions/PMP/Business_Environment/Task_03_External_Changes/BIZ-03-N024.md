# Question

Your agile data-analytics product depends on a third-party social-media API. The provider announces that its free tier ends in exactly 90 days, after which stricter usage terms apply and pricing jumps sharply. The product owner has already assessed the roadmap, confirmed that four upcoming features rely on the data, and decided the team should migrate to the provider's paid tier rather than drop the features. Engineering believes the migration itself—new authentication, rate-limit handling, contract sign-off—could take most of the 90 days if it is left until the affected features come up in the normal backlog order. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Treat the migration as a time-bound dependency with a hard external deadline and work with the product owner to schedule it ahead of the features that need it, not alongside them.
- **B**: Have the team run a time-boxed spike against the paid tier to confirm the migration effort before committing it to a position in the backlog.
- **C**: Raise the looming cut-off as a risk, assess the probability of overrunning the 90 days, and define a trigger date for escalation.
- **D**: Begin the commercial process now by engaging procurement to negotiate and sign the paid-tier contract, since sign-off can be the longest pole.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The value question is already settled—the product owner has decided to migrate—so this is no longer about assessing impact; it is about sequencing a migration with a fixed external deadline so it completes before the dependent features need it (A). A spike to size the work (B) is useful, but the team already has an engineering estimate that the migration is large and deadline-threatening; inserting a validation step before scheduling spends part of a tight runway confirming a risk that is already clear enough to act on. Logging it as a risk with a trigger date (C) is reasonable monitoring, yet treating a known, dated, mandatory dependency as a probabilistic risk to be watched understates it—the cut-off will happen, so the response is to plan around it, not to wait for a trigger. Engaging procurement on the contract now (D) is genuinely one of the longest poles and will form part of the plan, but jumping straight to the commercial thread before sequencing the whole migration optimises one strand while the technical work could still miss the date. The margin is that A reorders the backlog to respect a hard external deadline, which is the move the situation demands once the decision to migrate has been made; B, C and D each touch one piece without securing the deadline.
