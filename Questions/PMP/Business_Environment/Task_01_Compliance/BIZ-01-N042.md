# Question

During an internal audit of a commercial high-rise, the auditor finds the project file is missing the mill test certificates for three structural-steel deliveries that are already installed and on the critical path. The client's contract requires full material traceability for every structural element and gives the client the right to reject any element that cannot be substantiated. The supplier's quality manager insists the certificates were issued and emailed months ago and that the steel met the specified grade; the site team recalls receiving them but cannot locate the files. The next client audit is in two weeks. How should the project manager proceed?

## Options

- **A**: Have the supplier reissue the certificates and confirm each one maps to the specific heat numbers and delivery records of the steel actually installed, before deciding whether any further action is needed.
- **B**: Reconstruct the traceability chain from the project's own goods-received and inspection records to re-link each installed element to its delivery, then obtain the matching certificates against that chain.
- **C**: Log a traceability nonconformance against the three deliveries now and run certificate recovery as its corrective action, so the gap is formally tracked and visible to the client at the next audit regardless of outcome.
- **D**: Treat the certificates as recoverable and use the two weeks to prepare a fallback in-situ testing plan in case they cannot be produced, holding the plan in reserve.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The contract's traceability requirement is satisfied by certificates that demonstrably belong to the steel installed, and the supplier says those certificates exist, so the move that most directly closes the contractual gap is to have them reissued and verified against the specific heats and deliveries on the building — if they map and conform, traceability is restored at source and nothing further is required (A). Reconstructing the chain from the project's own goods-received and inspection records (B) is sound practice and will likely be needed to prove the mapping, but on its own it re-links elements to deliveries without yet producing the certificates the contract actually demands; it is a supporting step toward A, not a substitute for obtaining the documents, and treating it as the answer stops one step short of the obligation. Logging a nonconformance now and recovering the certificates as its corrective action (C) is the right mechanism the moment the documents cannot be produced or do not match, but raising a formal nonconformance — and exposing it at the client audit — before testing whether the certificates simply exist commits the project to a recorded finding, and a client rejection right, over a gap that may close quietly within days. Preparing a fallback in-situ testing plan (D) is prudent contingency and worth doing in parallel, but as the primary response it invests two weeks in the destructive, costly fallback while the cheapest and most likely resolution — the certificates themselves — sits untried. The fine line is A versus B: both work the documentary route in good faith, but A pursues the certificates the contract requires while B rebuilds only the chain that links to them.
