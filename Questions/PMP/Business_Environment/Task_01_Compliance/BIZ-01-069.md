# Question

Midway through a predictive construction project, an external regulator announces a compliance audit of the project's welding records to begin in three weeks. The records span eighteen months of work across several subcontractors, and the project manager has never had them independently checked. With three weeks available, how should the project manager prepare?

## Options

- **A**: Obtain the regulator's audit scope and assessment criteria, then review the records against those specific criteria and close any gaps found.
- **B**: Commission a full independent re-inspection of every welded joint to regenerate the underlying records from physical evidence.
- **C**: Have each subcontractor self-certify that its own welding records are complete and submit the certifications as the audit pack.
- **D**: Compile and index the existing records into a single submission so they can be handed to the auditors in good order on the day.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Three weeks is enough to verify a paper record but not to re-do the work, so the highest-value preparation is to learn what the regulator will actually test and review the records against those criteria, closing gaps while time remains (A). A full physical re-inspection (B) targets the welds rather than the records the audit concerns, cannot realistically complete in three weeks, and treats an untested belief about completeness as if the engineering were in doubt. Subcontractor self-certification (C) relies on the very parties whose records are unverified and substitutes assertion for the independent check the situation calls for. Compiling and indexing the records (D) presents them tidily but never tests them against the requirement, so any gap surfaces as a live finding during the audit rather than being caught and fixed beforehand; the discriminator is verifying content against the audit criteria, not merely organising it for handover.
