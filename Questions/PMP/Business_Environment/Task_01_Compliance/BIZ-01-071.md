# Question

On a pharmaceutical packaging project, the project manager discovers that the quality manager has been signing off batch-release records using a digital signature delegated to a junior technician who actually performs the checks. Internal policy and the governing good-manufacturing-practice code both require the named signatory to perform the review personally. The quality manager argues the technician is fully competent and the practice has been routine for years. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Accept the arrangement given the technician's competence, and record it as an approved deviation in the project's quality file.
- **B**: Report the non-conforming practice through the organisation's quality and compliance escalation route so it can be formally assessed and corrected.
- **C**: Instruct the quality manager to stop the practice immediately and treat the matter as closed without further escalation.
- **D**: Ask the technician to obtain the qualification needed to sign in their own right, allowing the practice to continue lawfully thereafter.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

This is a recorded breach of a regulated control with potential product-safety and legal consequences, and the project manager's duty is to escalate it through the proper quality and compliance channel where its scope, affected batches, and remediation can be formally assessed (B). Approving it as a deviation (A) tries to legitimise a practice that contradicts the governing code and the project manager has no authority to bless. Simply instructing the quality manager to stop (C) addresses the future but leaves potentially non-compliant historical releases unexamined and unreported. Getting the technician qualified (D) addresses only the forward arrangement and again ignores the existing records that may already be compromised; the obligation is to surface the breach, not to quietly fix the going-forward signatory.
