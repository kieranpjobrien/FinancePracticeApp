# Question

A hybrid infrastructure project is laying fibre across a coastal corridor. After the baseline was set, an environmental authority designates part of the route a protected nesting habitat, prohibiting groundworks there during a four-month breeding season that overlaps the planned trenching window. The project manager confirms the designation is legally binding. What is the most appropriate first action?

## Options

- **A**: Submit a change request to assess and incorporate the rescheduling or rerouting needed to meet the restriction, and update the affected baselines.
- **B**: Continue trenching on the original schedule while applying for an exemption from the seasonal restriction.
- **C**: Reroute the affected section immediately on the project manager's own authority to avoid losing time.
- **D**: Re-plan the trenching sequence within the team to fit around the closed window using available float, and report the recovered schedule at the next governance review.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A legally binding designation that invalidates a baselined plan is an external constraint that must flow through integrated change control, where its schedule, cost, and routing impacts are assessed and the baselines formally updated (A). Continuing to trench while seeking an exemption (B) risks breaching the prohibition during the very period it protects, exposing the project to enforcement before any exemption exists. Rerouting unilaterally (C) bypasses change control, may cross other constrained land, and commits cost and scope changes without authorisation. Quietly re-planning within float and only reporting afterwards (D) is the closest distractor — it at least respects the restriction — but it treats a legally driven change to a baselined plan as an internal scheduling tweak, commits to absorbing it before the impact is properly assessed (it may well exceed available float once knock-on works are considered), and presents stakeholders with a fait accompli rather than a decision; the binding external change still belongs in integrated change control.
