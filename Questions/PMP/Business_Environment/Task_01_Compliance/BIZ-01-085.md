# Question

While planning a hybrid project to refurbish a chemical storage facility, the project manager identifies that periodic third-party inspections of the bunds and containment systems are legally required at fixed intervals throughout the project. The exact inspection dates are set by regulation, but the fee each accredited inspector will charge varies and is not yet confirmed. The sponsor has set an aggressive cost target. How should the project manager handle the inspection costs in the budget?

## Options

- **A**: Place the entire inspection cost in contingency reserve, releasing it only once each inspection is actually booked, so the baseline stays low against the sponsor's target.
- **B**: Baseline the planned inspections at an estimated fee and hold a separate reserve sized to the range of possible quoted fees.
- **C**: Baseline a single rough allowance for "inspections" as one line, to be reconciled against actual invoices as they arrive.
- **D**: Defer all inspection cost from the baseline until the inspector quotes are confirmed, then add the agreed figures to the baseline in one change.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The inspections themselves are a legally fixed certainty, so the activity and its estimated cost belong in the baseline as planned work; the genuine uncertainty is only the variable fee, which is what contingency reserve is for — hence baselining the estimate and reserving for the fee range (B). Putting the whole cost in contingency (A) misclassifies a certain, scheduled obligation as a possible event and understates the true baseline to flatter the cost target, the opposite of sound estimating. A single rough allowance (C) does baseline the cost but as an unstructured lump that cannot be tracked per inspection or separate the certain activity from the uncertain fee, weakening control. Deferring the cost until quotes arrive (D) knowingly publishes a baseline that omits a required activity and pushes a foreseeable cost into a later change, when nothing about the obligation is actually unknown except a price that can be estimated now; the fine line is splitting the certain activity (baseline) from the uncertain fee (reserve), not banishing the whole cost to contingency or out of the baseline altogether.
