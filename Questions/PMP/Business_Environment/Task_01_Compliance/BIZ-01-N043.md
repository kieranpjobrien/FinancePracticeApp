# Question

A hybrid programme is launching a consumer-lending product in two countries whose consumer-credit rules overlap in most areas but diverge sharply on affordability checks and disclosure wording. To control cost, the sponsor mandates one product backlog and one delivery team. The project manager wants to prevent conflicting jurisdictional rules from surfacing late and forcing rework, while keeping the single-team model the sponsor requires. What is the best approach during planning?

## Options

- **A**: Build a compliance requirements register that ties each obligation to its jurisdiction and to specific backlog items, and use it to set acceptance criteria and backlog order so divergences are designed for from the start.
- **B**: Engage compliance specialists from both jurisdictions to jointly define a single superset of requirements that satisfies the stricter rule wherever the two regimes differ.
- **C**: Run a regulatory gap analysis across the two regimes up front, then write the divergent rules into the team's Definition of Done as jurisdiction-specific checks.
- **D**: Establish a compliance change-control trigger so any rule difference discovered mid-delivery is logged and routed through the programme's change process before it affects a release.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The risk is late-surfacing divergence forcing rework, and the most complete preventive control is a register that traces every obligation to its jurisdiction and to concrete backlog items, then drives both acceptance criteria and backlog order — this makes the divergences visible and sequenced before code is written, within the single-team constraint (A). Building a single stricter superset (B) is a tempting simplification but is often non-compliant in practice: "stricter" disclosure wording or an affordability rule in one country can violate the other's requirements, so collapsing the regimes can manufacture breaches rather than prevent them. An up-front gap analysis feeding the Definition of Done (C) is a genuinely good practice and partially correct, but a point-in-time analysis plus DoD checks lacks the living obligation-to-item traceability that keeps an evolving backlog aligned, so divergence can still drift back in. A change-control trigger (D) handles differences once they are discovered mid-delivery — it is a reactive safety net, not the planning-phase mechanism that stops them surfacing late in the first place.
