# Question

A predictive project is delivering a new effluent-treatment plant for a brewery. The discharge consent, the pressure-vessel certification regime, and the regional noise ordinance will all apply once the plant is commissioned. The sponsor wants a single budget line labelled "regulatory" and a single named lead, to keep the cost baseline and accountability simple. The cost baseline is being built this week. How should the project manager treat these obligations in the plan?

## Options

- **A**: Record one regulatory provision under a single accountable lead as the sponsor asks, and capture any differences between the obligations in the risk register as they surface.
- **B**: Separate the obligations into distinct plan items, each with its own owner, verification point, and baselined cost, accepting the extra lines and multiple owners that creates.
- **C**: Place the obligations under the operations team that will run the plant long-term, since they will inherit the permits and are best placed to maintain them.
- **D**: Plan in detail only the obligations with the earliest commissioning deadlines now, and treat the remainder once the design is frozen and their cost is firmer.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The three obligations arise from different sources, attach at different lifecycle points, and demand different evidence, so each needs its own owner, verification activity, and baselined cost — merging them loses that structure no matter how the single owner is chosen (B). One provision under a single lead (A) is attractive for a clean baseline and clear accountability, but a permit, a certification regime, and an ordinance cannot share one verification route or one owner without one being missed, and the risk register is not a substitute for a compliance plan. Handing them to the operations team (C) is reasonable for long-run permit stewardship yet still collapses three differently-verified obligations under one group during delivery and offloads work the project must plan and demonstrate itself. Planning only the earliest-due now (D) sounds disciplined but defers obligations that should be shaping the design — discharge limits drive the process design — past the point they can influence it. The discrimination is A versus B: both give the obligations a home, but only B preserves the separate handling each source requires.
