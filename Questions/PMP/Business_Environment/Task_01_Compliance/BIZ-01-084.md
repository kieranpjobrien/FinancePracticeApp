# Question

A predictive project to install a hospital radiation-therapy unit must, by regulation, retain a complete commissioning and calibration record demonstrating the unit met dose-accuracy limits at handover. Two weeks before handover, the project manager finds that several calibration results were recorded only in a technician's personal notebook, not in the controlled project record. The values themselves appear correct. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Accept the notebook entries as sufficient, since the calibration values themselves are correct.
- **B**: Transcribe and formally verify the missing results into the controlled record before handover, addressing the record-keeping gap.
- **C**: Re-run every calibration from scratch to generate fresh controlled records.
- **D**: Hand over with the gap noted, leaving the receiving department to reconcile the records later.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The regulation requires a complete controlled record, so the gap is in the record-keeping, not necessarily the engineering, and the proportionate fix is to bring the missing results into the controlled record with proper verification of their provenance before handover (B). Accepting personal-notebook entries as-is (A) leaves the regulated record incomplete and the entries unverified within the quality system. Re-running every calibration (C) is disproportionate when the underlying results exist and can be verified, wasting time and unit availability. Handing over with the gap unresolved (D) transfers a known compliance defect to the clinical user and risks the unit being used without the legally required complete record.
