# Question

An agile team building a mobile payments app has a backlog containing a high-value loyalty feature the business wants for an imminent marketing campaign, and a card-data-security control from the payment-industry standard the app must certify against before launch. Both are sized at roughly one sprint, and only one will fit the current sprint. The product owner, focused on the campaign date, has placed the loyalty feature first. The campaign launch and the product launch are several months apart. How should the project manager respond?

## Options

- **A**: Take the product owner's ordering as it stands, since sequencing the backlog is the product owner's accountability and either item can be built next sprint.
- **B**: Walk the product owner through how the security control gates the eventual product launch, so the ordering reflects that constraint as well as the campaign date.
- **C**: Ask the team whether both items can be split so a slice of each fits the sprint, giving the product owner an option that serves the campaign without deferring the control.
- **D**: Add the certification dependency to the risk register with the launch date as the trigger, so it is tracked and surfaces well before it becomes critical.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The product owner has ordered against the campaign date alone, and the decisive fact they may be weighing too lightly is that the security control gates the product launch — so the project manager's most useful move is to make that dependency and its timing explicit, leaving the re-ordering with the product owner (B). Accepting the order as-is (A) is legitimate deference but leans on "either can go next sprint" without checking the product owner has registered the gate, which is exactly the information at issue. Proposing to split both items (C) is a reasonable facilitation once the constraint is understood, but engineering a clever compromise before the product owner even has the full picture solves the wrong problem and may not be technically sound for a security control. Logging it as a risk with the launch as trigger (D) is sensible tracking, yet the dependency is a known certainty rather than an uncertainty, and recording it does not inform the decision the product owner is making right now; the fine line is between surfacing the constraint for the decision-maker and quietly managing around it.
