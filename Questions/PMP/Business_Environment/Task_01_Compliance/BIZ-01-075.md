# Question

During a sprint review for a warehouse-automation project, a developer mentions that a guard interlock on a robotic arm was disabled to speed up integration testing and has not been re-enabled, though the cell is in use right now by warehouse staff running trials. Site safety regulations require the interlock to be active whenever people are present. The site has a safety officer and a documented incident-reporting procedure. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Report the disabled interlock to the site safety officer immediately and ask them to determine and direct the appropriate corrective action.
- **B**: Stop the staff trials in the cell now, before anything else, and keep it out of use until the interlock is restored and verified.
- **C**: Log the disabled interlock as a safety non-conformance under the site's incident-reporting procedure so it is investigated and corrected through the proper channel.
- **D**: Have the developer re-enable and test the interlock straight away so the control is restored at source as quickly as possible.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

People are in the cell with the guard defeated right now, so the overriding first action is to remove them from the line of fire by halting the trials and keeping the cell out of use until the control is restored and verified (B). Reporting to the safety officer (A) is correct and necessary, but it hands off the decision and introduces delay while staff remain exposed; notification follows the stop, it does not replace it. Logging a non-conformance (C) is also required for the record and investigation, yet a procedure entry does nothing to end the immediate exposure and must not come before it. Having the developer re-enable the interlock (D) is closer — it attacks the hazard at source — but reaching into a live cell to work on the safety system while people are present and trials run is itself unsafe; you make the area safe by stopping use first, then restore and verify the interlock. All four actions belong in the response; the discriminator is sequence — protecting people now takes precedence over notifying, recording, or fixing.
