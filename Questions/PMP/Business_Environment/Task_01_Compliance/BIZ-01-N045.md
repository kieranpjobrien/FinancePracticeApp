# Question

A project is delivering a customer-loyalty mobile app that collects personal and location data, subject to data-protection law. The team works in two-week iterations and releases to production frequently. A data-protection impact assessment was completed during initial planning and signed off by legal. The project manager wants compliance to remain demonstrably current as the product evolves, rather than drifting between releases. Which THREE actions best sustain compliance through ongoing delivery?

## Options

- **A**: Add data-protection acceptance criteria (consent, retention, access controls) to relevant user stories so compliance is verified within each increment's Definition of Done.
- **B**: Appoint the legal reviewer who signed off the assessment as an approver on every production release, so each deployment carries a fresh compliance sign-off.
- **C**: Keep a compliance obligations log and review its status as a standing item in iteration reviews and risk reviews.
- **D**: Revisit and update the data-protection impact assessment whenever an iteration introduces or materially changes data-processing behaviour.
- **E**: Hold a single consolidated privacy review covering all accumulated changes once per quarter, timed to the organisation's compliance reporting cycle.

## Answer

A, C, D

## Explanation

Sustained compliance in frequent-release delivery comes from embedding checks in the cadence so each change is covered as it ships: privacy acceptance criteria inside the Definition of Done (A), a standing compliance-log review within iteration and risk reviews (C), and an impact assessment that is revisited whenever data processing changes (D) keep the obligations current release-by-release and keep the team accountable for them. Making the legal reviewer a mandatory approver on every release (B) is tempting because it sounds rigorous, but it routes compliance through a single external gatekeeper on every deployment — it throttles the team's release cadence, makes legal the bottleneck and owner rather than building the capability into the team, and a rubber-stamp under release pressure adds little real assurance; the team-owned checks in A/C/D are the stronger pattern. A single quarterly consolidated review (E) is a real governance practice and not negligent, but with frequent releases it lets weeks of changes go unverified between reviews, which is exactly the drift the PM wants to prevent; it complements but cannot substitute for per-increment verification.
