# Question

A Scrum team is building a patient-scheduling feature for a hospital network. Two days into a two-week sprint, the product owner learns that a regional health-data regulation takes effect in roughly eight weeks and will require consent-capture and audit-logging behaviour the current design does not include. The current sprint goal does not depend on that behaviour, and the work has not yet been described in any detail. How should the project manager proceed?

## Options

- **A**: Get the obligation onto the product backlog and scheduled for the team's next refinement, where it can be broken down and sized before the product owner decides where it falls against the eight-week date.
- **B**: Split a thin consent-and-logging slice into the current sprint now, while the deadline is still distant and spare capacity exists, so regulatory work starts before pressure builds.
- **C**: Have the obligation written up as a fully specified backlog item with acceptance criteria now, so the team can pull a complete, ready item the moment the current sprint ends.
- **D**: Take the requirement to the programme's compliance authority for a formal interpretation of the eight-week date before the team spends any refinement capacity on it.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The obligation is real but not yet understood, and there are roughly eight weeks before it bites, so the proportionate move is to put it on the backlog and into the next refinement where the team can break it down and size it — only then can its true position against the deadline be judged, and that ordering decision sits with the product owner working from real information rather than assumption (A). Splitting a slice into the current sprint now (B) is genuinely available in Scrum and tempting because capacity exists, but it disturbs a sprint goal that is on track and commits the team to undescribed, unsized regulatory work the eight-week horizon does not yet demand; acting early is not the same as acting before the work is understood. Writing it up as a fully specified, acceptance-criteria-complete item immediately (C) front-loads detailed specification onto a requirement the team has not yet collaboratively examined, pre-empting the refinement conversation that is where size, slices and dependencies actually surface — it looks thorough but inverts the order in which the work should mature. Seeking a formal interpretation of the date from a compliance authority first (D) can be a reasonable input, but treating it as a precondition before any refinement stalls the team's own analysis and assumes the date, not the design impact, is the open question; the size and shape of the change are discoverable now and should not wait on an external ruling. The fine line is A versus B: both engage the work early, but A lets the team understand and size it before committing, while B commits capacity to work it cannot yet responsibly scope.
