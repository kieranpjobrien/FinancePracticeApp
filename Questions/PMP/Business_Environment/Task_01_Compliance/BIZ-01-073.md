# Question

A project will export a satellite-imaging subsystem that falls under national export-control law, with licensing executed by the organisation's corporate trade-compliance function. The project manager is building the compliance approach during planning and wants it to be both sufficient and proportionate. Which actions belong in a sound compliance plan for this obligation? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Identify the specific export-control classifications that apply to the subsystem and the licences they require, working with the trade-compliance function.
- **B**: Name a single point of accountability within the project for the licensing obligation, even though the corporate function does the filing, so it has an owner inside the project.
- **C**: Build the licence lead times and conditions into the schedule as dependencies of shipment, and define where the licensing evidence will be held.
- **D**: Treat the corporate function's involvement as discharging the project's accountability, and track the obligation only through that function's own status updates.
- **E**: Set the licensing milestones to the optimistic processing times the authority publishes, on the basis that adding buffer for a corporate-run activity would pad the schedule.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

A sound plan establishes which classifications and licences actually apply, drawing on the specialist function rather than guessing (A); gives the obligation a named owner inside the project so it does not fall into the gap between project and corporate function (B); and ties licence lead times and conditions into the schedule as real shipment dependencies with defined evidence custody (C). Treating the corporate function as discharging the project's accountability and tracking only through its updates (D) is the plausible trap — delegating execution is fine, but accountability for an obligation that gates shipment stays with the project, and passive reliance on another team's reporting is how the constraint gets missed. Planning to optimistic published processing times to avoid "padding" (E) sounds like schedule discipline but builds the baseline on best-case licensing that routinely slips, leaving no margin for the queries and conditions export licences attract. The discrimination across D and E is between owning and resourcing the obligation realistically versus assuming a specialist function makes it someone else's risk.
