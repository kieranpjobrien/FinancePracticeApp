# Question

On an aerospace component project, a design engineer proposes a manufacturing change that cuts unit cost by 12% and shortens lead time. The change is technically sound, but the component is governed by an airworthiness certification basis and the proposed process differs from what was qualified. The sponsor is enthusiastic and wants it in the next production lot, which starts in three weeks. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Route the proposal through the certification and change-control process to determine what re-qualification or regulatory approval is required, and let that path govern when the change can enter production.
- **B**: Have the engineer prepare the substantiation and re-qualification data package now so it is ready for the certification authority, then adopt the change as soon as approval is granted.
- **C**: Submit the change to the project's Change Control Board with the cost and schedule benefits so the CCB can authorise it against the next lot.
- **D**: Confirm with the certification authority whether the change qualifies as a minor change that can be self-certified under the existing basis before deciding the approval route.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A change to a qualified process on a certification-controlled component must enter through the certification and change-control process, which determines the re-qualification or approval required and gates production on that outcome — this is the correct, complete first move and it neither blocks nor rubber-stamps the idea (A). Preparing the substantiation package now (B) is a sensible activity, but it presumes the answer is re-qualification and skips the step that decides what evidence and what regulatory route actually apply; it is an action taken inside the process, not the act of invoking it. Taking it to the project CCB (C) is a real and tempting move, but a project Change Control Board cannot authorise a deviation from a certification basis on its own authority — the airworthiness route governs, and a CCB sign-off without it gives false assurance. Asking the authority whether it is a self-certifiable minor change (D) is plausible and may even be the eventual finding, but pre-classifying the change as "minor" before the formal process has characterised it pre-empts that determination; classification is an output of the process in A, not a precondition for entering it.
