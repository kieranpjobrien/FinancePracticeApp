# Question

A project manager is assessing the potential consequences of failing to meet a binding emissions-reporting obligation on an energy project, to decide how much effort to invest in compliance controls. Which of the following are legitimate consequences of non-compliance that the assessment should weigh? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Regulatory fines or sanctions imposed on the organisation.
- **B**: Suspension or revocation of the operating permit needed to run the asset.
- **C**: Reputational damage that reduces the organisation's ability to win future work.
- **D**: The cost the project will incur to design and operate the emissions-reporting controls themselves.
- **E**: Lower-than-forecast emissions in a reporting period, allowing the organisation to report comfortably under its permitted threshold.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

A compliance-consequence assessment weighs the exposures that flow from failing to meet the obligation: financial penalties and sanctions (A), loss of the permit or licence to operate that can halt the asset (B), and reputational harm that damages future business prospects (C) — all of which scale the case for investing in controls. The cost of building and running the controls (D) is the price of compliance, not a consequence of non-compliance; folding it into this assessment double-counts it, since the very purpose of the assessment is to judge how much of that cost is justified by the exposures in A–C. Reporting comfortably under the threshold (E) is an outcome of compliance, not a consequence of breaching it, and is in fact the desired result rather than a risk to weigh. Only A, B, and C represent genuine consequences of non-compliance.
