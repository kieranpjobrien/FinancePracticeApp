# Question

An agile team building a commercial logistics platform pulls an open-source mapping library into the product to hit a sprint commitment. During the review, a developer notes the library is published under a copyleft licence that, the organisation's policy states, may not be used in distributed commercial products without legal clearance. The feature works, an internal stakeholder demo is tomorrow, and the release that would ship this code to customers is several sprints away. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Let the feature ship in the next customer release as planned and run the licence clearance in parallel, on the basis that the code already works and clearance will likely come through in time.
- **B**: Hold the component from any customer release until the licence is cleared against policy or an approved alternative is substituted, while allowing tomorrow's internal demo to go ahead.
- **C**: Have the team immediately swap in a permissively licensed mapping library now so the copyleft component never reaches a release branch.
- **D**: Ask the product owner to accept the licence risk as a documented decision, so the feature can proceed without waiting on legal clearance.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The policy exists because a copyleft licence in a distributed commercial product can impose obligations on the whole codebase, so the component must be kept out of any customer release until it is cleared or replaced — and since the breach only crystallises on distribution, the internal demo can still proceed (B). Shipping in the planned release while clearing in parallel (A) bets the release on an outcome that may not land and risks distributing a breach that is far costlier to unwind afterwards. Swapping in an alternative immediately (C) pre-empts a clearance decision that might well approve the original library, discarding working code and burning capacity before anyone has checked whether the existing component is in fact acceptable. Letting the product owner accept the risk (D) treats a legal-clearance requirement as a product trade-off the product owner can waive, but licence compliance is not within the product owner's authority to sign off; the discriminator is gating the right milestone (distribution) while preserving lower-risk activity, not eliminating the component or routing the decision to someone who cannot make it.
