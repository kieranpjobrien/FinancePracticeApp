# Question

A government agency deployed a new case-management system across 40 offices. Go-live was clean and training is complete. The project is in its final month before transition to operations, and the project manager has already traced the missing benefit: staff are entering cases into the system but maintaining their old spreadsheets in parallel because local supervisors still ask for the spreadsheet reports they have always used, so processing time has risen rather than fallen by the promised 25%. The operations function will own the system after closure but has no plan addressing this behaviour. The sponsor asks whether the project can close on schedule. What is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: Document the parallel-working issue, the root cause, and a recommended reinforcement plan in the closure and transition package, and hand it to operations to execute after closure.
- **B**: Brief the supervisors directly on the reports now available in the new system and ask them to stop requesting the spreadsheets, so the demand driving parallel working is removed at source.
- **C**: Work with the sponsor and operations leaders to retire the duplicate spreadsheet reporting and equip supervisors to pull what they need from the new system, then close once the benefit is on a credible path.
- **D**: Add the parallel-working issue and its 25% benefit shortfall to the risk and issue register, raise it at the closure review, and let the governance board decide how it is carried into operations.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The root cause is already known and it is not a handler-skill or system problem — supervisors are still demanding the old spreadsheet reports, so the parallel working will persist until that demand is removed and supervisors can self-serve from the new system. Stopping the duplicate reporting changes what supervisors are accountable for and how a whole layer of management gets its information — that is an operating-model decision the sponsor and operations leaders must own, and the project manager is best placed to drive it now while leadership attention is on the project; doing so puts the benefit on a credible path and makes closure responsible (C). Writing it up and handing it to operations (A) is the textbook "document and transition" move, but operations has no plan and the project still holds the relationships and momentum to fix a sustainment problem that is squarely within reach — transferring it risks it dying in the backlog. Briefing the supervisors and asking them to stop (B) targets the right root cause but has the project manager attempt it alone: supervisors report into operations, not the project, and absent visible backing from the sponsor and their own leaders a request to abandon reporting they rely on will not hold — the demand has to be retired by the people who own it, which is what C arranges. Routing the issue to the governance board for a carry-into-operations decision (D) is procedurally correct and ensures visibility, but it converts a fixable sustainment problem into a logged item awaiting a future ruling and surrenders the leverage the project has right now to resolve it before closure.
