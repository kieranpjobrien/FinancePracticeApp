# Question

A university replaced its student-records platform across registry, finance, and faculty admin, delivered incrementally and now four sprints past the final release. Adoption looked healthy until the term's first peak: a respected faculty admin lead who is fully capable on the platform has begun telling peers across departments that it is "an IT failure waiting to happen," and several admins who had been coping are now hesitating on harder cases and phoning the project team instead of working them through. The project manager has spoken with the lead and found her objection is concrete — a reconciliation step she relies on takes far more clicks than before and she believes that proves the platform was rushed. The product owner agrees the step is clumsy and can be improved, but not for two or three sprints given committed work. The sponsor wants peak handled now. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Stand up on-the-job coaching and quick-reference aids for the harder cases now, so the hesitating admins can work peak through unaided regardless of the lead's commentary.
- **B**: Sit with the lead to work through exactly where the reconciliation step costs her, agree a near-term improvement and an interim workaround, so the most influential voice has a concrete answer rather than a grievance.
- **C**: Ask the product owner to pull the reconciliation fix forward ahead of the committed sprint work, since it is the specific trigger feeding the lead's narrative.
- **D**: Show the admins concrete evidence of the benefit the platform is already delivering, to rebuild belief in its value before the narrative hardens further.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Two things are true at once: a single influential lead is shaping a spreading narrative, and her objection is grounded in one real, fixable irritation. The hesitation rippling through her peers is downstream of her voice, so the highest-leverage first move is to engage her directly — understand the reconciliation pain precisely, agree a credible near-term fix and an interim workaround so she has something concrete to hold rather than a grievance to broadcast. That converts the person most able to harden or defuse the narrative and is what makes the other supports land (B). Coaching and job aids for the hesitating admins (A) is a genuine and probably necessary action, but it treats the symptom rippling outward while leaving the source — the lead's "failure waiting to happen" message — unaddressed, so the doubt keeps being reseeded. Pulling the fix forward (C) is part of the answer and may well result from the conversation, but committing the product owner to re-sequence before sitting with the lead pre-empts what an interim workaround might make unnecessary and skips the engagement that actually turns her; it also trades against committed work on the strength of one person's framing. Showing benefit evidence (D) suits people who doubt the value, but this lead does not doubt the value — she has a specific functional complaint, and generic benefit messaging will read as dismissing it, hardening her further rather than answering her.
