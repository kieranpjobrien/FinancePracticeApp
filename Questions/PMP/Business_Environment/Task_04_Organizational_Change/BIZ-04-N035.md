# Question

Midway through an agile programme to digitise a council's housing-repairs service, a new regulation changes the information tenants must be shown before booking a repair. The product owner wants to slot the regulatory work into the next two sprints. The project manager realises this also changes the call-centre agents' scripts and the contractor portal — areas owned by other teams who are unaware. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Map who else is affected by the regulatory change and bring those stakeholders in to plan the work and their own transition together.
- **B**: Let the product owner add the regulatory items to the backlog and address the call-centre and contractor impacts once the software is built.
- **C**: Raise a change request through a formal control board before the team touches any of the regulatory work.
- **D**: Build the tenant-facing changes first since they are the legal priority, then notify the other teams when development is complete.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The regulatory change is not only a software item — it alters how agents and contractors work — so the organisational-change task is to identify everyone affected and engage them to plan the change and their transitions together (A). Building the software first and dealing with the human impacts afterwards (B and D) treats people as an afterthought and risks a compliant system that the wider operation is unprepared to use on day one. Routing it through a formal control board (C) suits a predictive change-control regime, but in an agile context the team can absorb the work via the backlog; the pressing gap is coordinating the affected groups, not paperwork.
