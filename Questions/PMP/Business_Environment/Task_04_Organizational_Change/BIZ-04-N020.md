# Question

A media company is shifting from project-based funding to long-lived product teams — a major change in how work is organised, funded, and prioritised. Three months in, the change has stalled. The product teams exist on paper, but funding still flows through the old project gates, the executives sponsoring the shift are sending inconsistent messages about how committed they are, and staff say they do not see why the change matters to them. The project manager must decide where to focus first. What is the most important thing to address first?

## Options

- **A**: Get the sponsoring executives aligned on the change and consistent in what they say and reward, so the organisation stops receiving mixed signals from the top.
- **B**: Redirect funding from the old project gates to the product teams, removing the structural mechanism that actively pulls work back to the previous model.
- **C**: Define and communicate a clear, compelling rationale for the change so staff understand why it matters and what it means for them.
- **D**: Document each product team's mandate, decision rights, and ways of working, so people have a concrete operating model to follow rather than an idea.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Several real problems are in play, but they are not independent: the inconsistent messages from the sponsoring executives sit upstream of the rest. While leaders are themselves unaligned, any rationale communicated to staff lacks credibility, and no one has the authority to redirect funding or ratify a new operating model — so aligning the sponsors and making their commitment consistent is the precondition that unblocks everything else (A). Redirecting the funding (B) is essential and removes a genuine structural contradiction, but it is a decision the sponsors must own and drive; attempting it before they are aligned puts the mechanism ahead of the mandate. Communicating the rationale (C) is necessary too, yet a message pushed out while leaders are visibly sending mixed signals will be undercut by their behaviour — actions speak louder than the narrative. Documenting team mandates and decision rights (D) gives people a concrete model, but codifying how teams should work inside a change leadership has not yet got behind formalises something the organisation has not committed to. Leadership alignment comes first.
