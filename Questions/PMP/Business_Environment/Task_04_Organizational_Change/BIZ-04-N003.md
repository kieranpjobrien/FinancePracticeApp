# Question

A manufacturing firm is implementing a new shop-floor quality system that changes how line supervisors record defects. Midway through, the executive sponsor has stopped attending steering meetings, no longer references the change in town halls, and has redirected two key supervisors to an unrelated cost-cutting initiative. Frontline adoption is slowing. The project manager wants to act before the next increment. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Brief the steering committee on the adoption data and ask them to formally direct the sponsor to re-engage.
- **B**: Identify a second senior leader in the quality function and ask them to co-sponsor and provide the visible backing the project is missing.
- **C**: Meet the sponsor privately to learn whether priorities have genuinely shifted, and on that basis agree either renewed visible support or a change to scope.
- **D**: Replan the rollout around the reduced support — slowing the pace and narrowing scope — so the change can still progress without leaning on the sponsor.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The sponsor's behaviour is ambiguous: it could be a deliberate reprioritisation or simply competing demands, and the project manager does not yet know which. A private conversation surfaces the real cause and lets the sponsor either recommit visibly or signal a genuine change that should reshape scope (C). Going to the steering committee first (A) escalates and pressures the sponsor before their intent is even understood, risking the relationship over what might be a fixable misunderstanding. Recruiting a co-sponsor (B) can be a sound move, but doing it before confirming the original sponsor's position works around the owning leader rather than re-engaging them and may read as undermining. Replanning around the gap (D) accepts diminished sponsorship as fixed and quietly erodes the benefits case rather than testing whether the support can be restored — which is the cheaper and more direct first step.
