# Question

A national retailer is rolling out a new workforce-scheduling system to store managers. Head-office communications have been clear and frequent, store managers have been trained, and the system works. Adoption is patchy, and the project manager traces the pattern to the area managers — the layer between head office and stores — who are sending mixed signals, several telling their stores the old system is "fine for now." Probing further, the project manager learns the area managers are not hostile to the system; they are sceptical it will survive, having seen two head-office initiatives launched and quietly dropped in the past three years. What is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the area managers together for a working session to align them on consistent messaging and equip them with a toolkit to roll the system out to their stores.
- **B**: Meet the area managers to surface and address the "this will be dropped too" belief — with evidence of leadership's commitment and the decommissioning of the old system — and agree how they will lead the change with their stores.
- **C**: Ask the sponsor and retail director to make a visible, unambiguous commitment to the system — including a firm date to retire the old one — so the doubt about permanence is removed from the top.
- **D**: Have store managers who are using the system well share their results in the area-manager forum, so peer evidence shows the change is real and working.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The diagnosis is specific: the area managers' mixed signals come from a belief, grounded in two abandoned initiatives, that this change will not last. The decisive move addresses that belief directly with the people holding it — pairing evidence of genuine commitment with a concrete plan for how they will lead their stores — because nothing shifts until the "it'll be dropped" story does (B). A messaging-and-toolkit session (A) equips them to communicate but assumes they are already willing; it skips the scepticism that is the actual cause, so polished messaging would be delivered by unconvinced leaders. A top-down commitment from the sponsor (C) supplies exactly the evidence the area managers need and is a necessary ingredient, but broadcasting it without engaging them leaves the influential layer as passive recipients rather than committed leaders — the commitment lands best inside the conversation that B holds. Peer evidence from successful stores (D) is persuasive but partial; early success can be dismissed as "too soon to tell" by managers who have watched promising starts fade before, so it supports the case without resolving the doubt about permanence at its root.
