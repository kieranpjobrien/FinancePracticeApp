# Question

A bank is two weeks from cutting over its branches to a new core banking platform. Technically the system is ready and tested. However, the change readiness dashboard shows three of forty branches with low confidence scores, unresolved questions from frontline staff, and a customer-facing reconciliation process those branches have not yet practised. The steering committee wants to proceed on the planned date to avoid slipping a board commitment. What is the project manager's best recommendation?

## Options

- **A**: Recommend delaying the entire cutover for all forty branches until the three lagging branches reach full readiness.
- **B**: Recommend a phased cutover that proceeds for the ready branches on schedule while giving the three unready branches a short, targeted readiness sprint before they follow.
- **C**: Recommend cutting over all forty branches on the planned date to keep the board commitment, with the change and support teams surged into the three lagging branches through go-live week.
- **D**: Recommend proceeding on the date and assigning extra staff to the three branches to fix issues reactively as customers encounter them.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A phased approach honours both the board commitment and people-readiness: the ready branches go live on time, and the three unready ones get focused preparation before following, so the commitment is met without exposing customers to an unpractised, customer-facing financial process (B). Delaying everything (A) penalises thirty-seven ready branches and the wider commitment for the sake of three. Surging support around an all-branch go-live (C) is the strongest distractor — it does try to protect the commitment and the lagging branches — but extra hands on the day cannot manufacture readiness that needs practice beforehand, and the board date can still be honoured for the ready branches without taking that risk. Fixing issues reactively at the counter (D) leans even harder on after-the-fact support and exposes customers to errors that a short readiness sprint would have prevented.
