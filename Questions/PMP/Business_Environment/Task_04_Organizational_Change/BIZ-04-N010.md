# Question

A council is deploying new procurement software. Staff have received an awareness campaign explaining why the change is happening and are willing to use it, but in the first week the help desk is flooded with calls from people who simply do not know how to complete basic tasks in the new tool. What does this situation most clearly indicate the change effort is missing?

## Options

- **A**: A stronger case for change to convince staff the new tool is worthwhile and worth persevering with.
- **B**: Better quick-reference guides and embedded help text so staff can find answers without calling the help desk.
- **C**: Adequate hands-on training so willing users gain the capability to perform tasks in the new tool.
- **D**: More visible executive sponsorship to reinforce that the change is mandatory and here to stay.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Staff already understand and accept the change — willingness is present — but they cannot perform the tasks, which is an ability gap that hands-on training is designed to close (C). A stronger case for change (A) builds desire, which is not what is missing here. Better job aids and help text (B) is the closest distractor and would deflect some calls, but reference material assumes a baseline competence the staff do not yet have; people who have never been shown the basic tasks need to be taught them, not handed a lookup guide. More sponsorship (D) reinforces commitment, which is also already present. The skill being tested is recognising which element of readiness is genuinely deficient — here, capability — rather than reaching for whichever lever is most familiar.
