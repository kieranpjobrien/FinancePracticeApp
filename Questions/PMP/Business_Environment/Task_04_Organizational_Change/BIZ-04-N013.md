# Question

A multinational is rolling out a new HR system to 12,000 employees across 30 countries with varied languages, labour practices, and local cultures. The small central change team cannot personally reach every site, and a uniform global message has fallen flat in early markets. What approach will best support adoption at this scale?

## Options

- **A**: Translate and localise the central campaign into each language, adapting examples and tone for each market, and broadcast it from the centre to every site.
- **B**: Require every employee to complete a standardised global e-learning module by a fixed deadline to guarantee consistent exposure to the change.
- **C**: Centrally segment the markets and run tailored campaigns market-by-market, the central team focusing on one cluster at a time in priority order.
- **D**: Build a network of local change champions, equip them with the rationale and materials, and have them lead adoption in their own market while the centre coordinates.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The central team's reach is the binding constraint, and the early failure shows that messaging crafted at the centre does not land locally however it is dressed. A champion network solves both: it multiplies on-the-ground capacity and puts adaptation in the hands of people who understand each context, with the centre keeping the effort coherent (D). Localising the campaign (A) is the strongest distractor and fixes more than mere translation — it adapts tone and examples — but it is still a campaign pushed from the centre to passive recipients, so it lacks the local ownership and two-way engagement that drive adoption. A mandated e-learning module (B) guarantees exposure, not adoption, and ignores local nuance entirely. Sequencing tailored central campaigns cluster by cluster (C) improves relevance but still funnels everything through a team that has already proven too small to reach 30 countries, so most markets wait and the capacity gap remains.
