# Question

A manufacturer is moving its three plants from a legacy ERP to a cloud platform, changing daily routines for shop-floor supervisors, planners, and procurement. The three plants run different product lines, were acquired at different times, and have very different histories with past system changes — one adopted a previous rollout smoothly, another fought it for a year. The steering committee has approved the business case and set the go-live date; the project manager is building the change approach. A capable change analyst is available for roughly two weeks before the engagement and training design must be locked. How should the project manager use that window?

## Options

- **A**: Have the analyst profile each plant's impact and readiness separately, so the engagement and training are shaped to each site rather than applied uniformly.
- **B**: Have the analyst build one strong engagement and training design based on the plant that adopted the last change smoothly, then roll the same proven design out to all three.
- **C**: Have the analyst stand up the communications channel and start a steady drumbeat of messages to all three plants now, so awareness is building while the design is finalised.
- **D**: Have the analyst concentrate the two weeks on the plant that resisted last time, since that is where the change is most at risk, and treat the two cooperative plants as lower priority.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The plants differ in product line, history, and prior change experience, so the impact and the readiness genuinely vary by site; spending the scarce window on a per-plant profile is what lets the design fit each one rather than a single template stretched across all three (A). Reusing the smooth plant's design everywhere (B) assumes the other two are like it — the very assumption the differing histories contradict — and bakes in a fit that may fail at the harder sites. Starting the communications drumbeat now (C) commits to messaging before knowing what each plant needs to hear and spends the analyst on broadcast rather than diagnosis. Pouring the whole window into the resistant plant (D) is tempting because that is where risk concentrates, but it leaves the other two unexamined and presumes their readiness rather than confirming it; a balanced read across all three is what tells you where to weight the effort.
