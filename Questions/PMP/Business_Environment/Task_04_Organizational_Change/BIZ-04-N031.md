# Question

A software firm is rolling out a new internal design system to its product teams in iterations. After the second release, a feedback session reveals that several teams find the components themselves good but stall during migration: the guide assumes prior knowledge they lack, and each team is hitting the same wall independently. The next increment is being planned, the component backlog is full, and the product teams are waiting on those components. The project manager must decide how to handle the migration blocker. What is the best course?

## Options

- **A**: Keep the increment's component work intact and have a couple of the design-system team members pair directly with each stalled team to unblock them hands-on.
- **B**: Bring a scoped piece of migration-guide and onboarding improvement into the upcoming increment alongside the component work, sized so both progress.
- **C**: Treat the recurring gap as its own work item and dedicate the upcoming increment to rewriting the migration guide properly, deferring new components by one cycle.
- **D**: Capture the migration problems in a shared FAQ that all teams can consult, so the knowledge is available without diverting the increment's capacity.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The blocker is systemic — every team hits the same wall — and an iterative cadence exists so feedback shapes the very next increment; folding a scoped guide-and-onboarding fix into the upcoming work removes the shared obstacle while components keep flowing (B). Pairing experts with each team (A) unblocks people but solves the same problem repeatedly, team by team, and consumes specialist time without fixing the artefact that is failing everyone. Dedicating the whole increment to a full guide rewrite (C) over-invests in documentation and stalls the components teams are waiting on, swapping one bottleneck for another when a targeted slice would do. A shared FAQ (D) is cheap and helps, but it patches over a guide that assumes missing knowledge rather than closing that gap, so teams keep stalling on the underlying onboarding deficit; the durable fix is to improve the guide and onboarding in-increment.
