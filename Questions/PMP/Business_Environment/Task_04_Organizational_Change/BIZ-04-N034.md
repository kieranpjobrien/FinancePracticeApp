# Question

A bank delivered a new mortgage-origination workflow through a hybrid programme: the platform was built iteratively, and a fixed organisational cut-over moved all branches onto it. Adoption is now steady and the programme is winding down. The project manager wants the change to sustain after the team disbands. Which actions support sustainment? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Transfer ownership of the workflow, its KPIs, and its support materials to the mortgage operations line.
- **B**: Agree who will reinforce the new behaviours and respond to issues once the project team is gone.
- **C**: Capture and communicate the benefits realised so far to reinforce conviction in the change.
- **D**: Retain a few project change specialists indefinitely as the permanent help desk for the new workflow, so expert support is always on hand.
- **E**: Treat the steady adoption as proof the change is now self-sustaining and close out without assigning ongoing reinforcement, freeing operations from extra overhead.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Sustainment depends on a permanent owner taking on the process, measures, and materials (A); a clear answer to who reinforces behaviour and handles issues after handover (B); and visible evidence of benefits that keeps people convinced the change was worth it (C). Retaining project specialists as the standing help desk (D) is superficially supportive but keeps the workflow dependent on a temporary structure and on people whose knowledge should have been transferred into the operating line, so capability never truly lands where it belongs. Reading steady adoption as self-sustaining and closing without assigning reinforcement (E) is the most tempting trap — current adoption is real — but newly embedded behaviour decays without an owner to reinforce it and respond when issues surface, so removing that overhead is exactly what lets the change erode.
