# Question

A retailer is transitioning its head-office teams from a traditional waterfall delivery model to agile ways of working. The project manager is championing the change. Several middle managers are anxious because the shift to self-organising teams blurs their traditional command-and-control roles, and they fear becoming redundant. Their cooperation matters because they control whether their staff get time to attend ceremonies. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Show the managers evidence from other organisations that agile transformations rarely eliminate management roles, to ease the redundancy fear so they stop obstructing.
- **B**: Ask the transformation sponsor to direct the managers to release their staff for ceremonies, removing the dependency on each manager's goodwill.
- **C**: Work with the managers to define what their role becomes in the agile model and how they will contribute, involving them in shaping it rather than presenting a finished answer.
- **D**: Move the at-risk managers into the first product teams as members, so they experience agile directly and the structural question resolves itself over time.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The managers' resistance is rooted in a genuine identity threat — their existing role really is changing — so the durable fix is to give them a credible, valued place in the new model and to involve them in defining it, which converts a threat into a stake (C). Citing reassuring evidence (A) addresses the surface fear but leaves the managers without a concrete answer to "what do I actually do now?"; generic comfort rarely outlasts the first self-organising team. Having the sponsor mandate staff release (B) can unblock ceremony attendance, but it forces compliance over a person whose buy-in you need long-term and does nothing about the underlying anxiety, so resistance resurfaces elsewhere. Dropping them into teams as members (D) gives exposure but effectively dissolves their role without discussion — the very redundancy they fear — and is more likely to deepen resistance than resolve it.
