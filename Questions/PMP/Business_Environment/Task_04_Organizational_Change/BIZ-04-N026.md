# Question

A logistics company has rolled out new warehouse-scanning devices across its sites and the project is in its final weeks. Adoption is high and error rates have dropped as expected. The warehouse operations function will own the devices and the new process after closure. Its operations manager has accepted the handover in principle but is stretched, and the project manager has time for essentially one substantial handover activity before the team is released. Which will best protect the gains once the project is gone?

## Options

- **A**: Compile a complete handover pack — process maps, device guides, and training materials — and lodge it where operations staff can find it.
- **B**: Run a full lessons-learned workshop with the project team and operations so the knowledge gained is captured before people disperse.
- **C**: Agree with the operations manager who will own the new process and its performance measures day to day, and confirm they will track those measures and act when they slip.
- **D**: Deliver a final round of refresher training to all warehouse staff so the new routines are firmly bedded in at the point of handover.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

All four are reasonable closure activities, but gains decay unless a named owner is accountable for the new process and watches the measures that show whether it is holding; securing that ownership and follow-through is what keeps the change alive after the team leaves (C). A handover pack (A) is necessary but inert — documents on a shared drive sustain nothing if no one is responsible for using them. A lessons-learned workshop (B) serves future projects more than this change and does not put anyone in charge of maintaining it. Refresher training (D) reinforces behaviour at the moment of handover but fades without ongoing ownership; whoever owns the process can commission more training if the measures call for it, which is why establishing that ownership is the higher-leverage move.
