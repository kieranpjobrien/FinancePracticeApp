# Question

A health authority is beginning a project that will change how hundreds of community nurses across many geographically dispersed sites schedule and record home visits. The sites vary widely — some are large urban teams, others are small rural ones with very different visit patterns and quite different appetites for change. The project manager has a fixed go-live date, a trained pool of super-users ready to deploy, and a polished communications plan from a similar recent project. Planning must be signed off within two weeks, and there is pressure to show early momentum. With everything seemingly ready to launch, what should the project manager prioritise in that window?

## Options

- **A**: Run a change-impact and readiness assessment across the varied nurse groups to learn how much each one's work changes and where resistance is likely, then shape the engagement and training around what it finds.
- **B**: Adapt the recent project's communications plan to this workforce and begin a steady cadence of updates so nurses hear about the change early and often.
- **C**: Deploy the super-users to the sites now so a trusted on-the-ground presence is building familiarity before the formal rollout begins.
- **D**: Convene the site managers to agree adoption targets and a reporting rhythm so the steering committee can see readiness improving as go-live approaches.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

It is tempting to spend a ready-to-launch window on visible action, but the sites differ sharply in how the change lands and how willing they are, so acting before that variation is understood risks aiming the effort at the wrong places. The assessment is what tells you which messages, how much super-user presence, and which targets each group actually warrants, which is why it comes first (A). Adapting the recent communications plan (B) imports assumptions from a different workforce and commits to messaging before knowing what these varied groups need to hear. Deploying super-users now (C) spends a scarce resource before knowing where the deepest impact and resistance sit, so the presence may land where it is least needed. Agreeing targets and reporting (D) tracks progress toward an adoption goal not yet grounded in how ready each site is — measuring a number before understanding it. Diagnose the variation, then design the interventions around the findings.
