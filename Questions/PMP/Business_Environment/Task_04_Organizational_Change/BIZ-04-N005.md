# Question

A logistics company is rolling out new route-optimisation software to its depots using an agile delivery approach. After three increments, depot managers in two of the eight depots are actively undermining the change, telling drivers the old method is faster and refusing to display the new performance dashboards. These two managers are well respected across the network. The project manager has confirmed the software works and saves time elsewhere. Which THREE actions best support adoption in this situation? (Choose three.)

## Options

- **A**: Meet the resistant managers one-to-one to understand the specific concerns driving their behaviour before deciding how to respond.
- **B**: Share concrete, depot-level evidence from the successful depots that the resisting managers find credible and relevant.
- **C**: If their concerns can be addressed, equip and position the respected managers as advocates whose endorsement will sway drivers across the network.
- **D**: Ask the executive sponsor to issue a network-wide directive mandating dashboard use and the new method at every depot.
- **E**: Accelerate the rollout at the six willing depots to build a clear majority before returning to address the two holdouts.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Resistance from respected influencers is a leverage point, not just a problem, and the best actions diagnose it and turn it around. Understanding the actual concerns first (A) prevents you from solving the wrong issue. Credible, locally relevant evidence (B) addresses scepticism about whether the change genuinely helps. Converting respected resisters into advocates (C) uses their network influence to accelerate adoption far beyond their own depots. A top-down mandate (D) is tempting because it looks decisive, but forcing compliance on credible influencers without addressing their concerns typically buys grudging conformity and squanders the advocacy they could provide. Racing ahead at the willing depots (E) postpones the real problem and lets two respected voices keep arguing the old way is better across the network, so the resistance spreads rather than resolves.
