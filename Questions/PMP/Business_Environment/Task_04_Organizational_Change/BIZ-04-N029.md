# Question

A utility introduced mandatory mobile time-logging for field crews several weeks ago to replace weekly paper timesheets. Crews can clearly operate the app — logs are coming in correctly — but completion is grudging and inconsistent, and supervisors report crews muttering that head office is "watching where we are all day." The project manager wants completion to become reliable and willing rather than enforced. Which approach is most likely to achieve that?

## Options

- **A**: Sit with a sample of crews to confirm exactly what they object to before adding anything to the change approach, since the "surveillance" talk may be a proxy for a different grievance.
- **B**: Recognise the crews and supervisors with the most consistent logging in the weekly operations briefing, making willing completion visible and valued.
- **C**: Have the crews' own supervisors, rather than head office, own and chase daily completion, so the request comes from a trusted local voice rather than a remote one.
- **D**: Work through crew supervisors to explain what the data is and is not used for and the concrete benefits to crews — quicker, more accurate pay and fewer disputed hours — addressing the belief driving the reluctance.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The crews can use the app, so the barrier is meaning, not mechanics: they have read the change as covert monitoring. The most direct way to convert grudging completion into willing completion is to replace that narrative with an accurate account of what the data does and the personal benefits it brings, delivered through the supervisors they trust (D). Confirming the objection first (A) is a defensible instinct, but the supervisors have already surfaced a clear and specific belief; further diagnosis mostly delays acting on a cause that is already visible. Recognising consistent loggers (B) rewards the behaviour without touching the suspicion underneath, so it tends to produce compliance among the willing rather than shifting the doubters. Shifting ownership to local supervisors (C) improves who delivers the message but not its content; a trusted voice repeating a request the crews still believe is surveillance changes the messenger, not the meaning that is driving the reluctance.
