# Question

A project manager is preparing to deploy a new procurement system to a shared-services centre. While planning the rollout, they learn that the same teams will simultaneously absorb a finance-system upgrade and a major office relocation, both led by other project managers. Staff are already describing themselves as "drowning in change." What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Raise the concurrent change load with the sponsor and the other initiative leads so the competing demands on these teams can be sequenced or paced.
- **B**: Accelerate the procurement rollout to land before the other two changes hit, getting it done while the teams still have capacity.
- **C**: Add more intensive training and on-floor support so the procurement change cuts through the noise of the other initiatives.
- **D**: Proceed as scheduled and monitor adoption closely, intervening only if procurement-specific metrics start to slip.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The core issue is change saturation across overlapping initiatives, which no single project can solve alone, so the right first step is to surface it to the sponsor and peer leads and coordinate the pacing and sequencing of the combined load (A). Accelerating procurement (B) adds to the very pile-up that is overwhelming staff and competes with the other changes rather than easing the burden. More training and support (C) treats one stream in isolation and ignores that the teams' total capacity for change is the binding constraint. Proceeding and watching only procurement metrics (D) misses the cross-initiative cause and lets predictable overload damage all three.
