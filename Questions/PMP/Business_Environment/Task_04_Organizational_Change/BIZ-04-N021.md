# Question

A retail bank is rolling out a new lending-decision platform across 200 branches over six months. The technical delivery is on track and branch staff have been trained region by region. Three regions in, the change manager reports that adoption is strong where the regional executive personally opened the launch event, and weak where that executive sent a deputy or a recorded message. The remaining regions are scheduled next quarter. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Work with the sponsor to secure visible, in-person commitment from each remaining regional executive at their launch, and follow up where engagement has lagged.
- **B**: Standardise the launch by replacing all executive appearances with a single polished video from the chief executive so every region receives the same message.
- **C**: Add extra refresher training in the lagging regions, since uneven adoption usually reflects gaps in capability.
- **D**: Document the adoption variance in the risk register and continue the rollout as planned, reviewing results at the end.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The evidence ties adoption directly to visible, in-person leadership sponsorship, so the highest-leverage move is to secure that same active commitment for the regions still to come and shore up the ones lagging (A). A single recorded message (B) is exactly the lower-engagement pattern that correlated with weak adoption. More training (C) assumes a capability gap the data does not support — the difference tracks sponsorship, not competence. Merely logging the variance and pressing on (D) wastes a known, actionable lever and accepts avoidable underperformance.
