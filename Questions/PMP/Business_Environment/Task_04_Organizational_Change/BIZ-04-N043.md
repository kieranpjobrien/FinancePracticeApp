# Question

A manufacturing firm is introducing a warehouse-management system that changes how 150 staff across day, night, and weekend shifts pick, pack, and record stock. Delivery is incremental, with usable slices released to pilot users first. The project manager's readiness assessment found the day shift broadly positive, while the night and weekend shifts are sceptical: their workflows differ in ways the design has not yet been tested against, and the experienced leads those shifts follow are openly unconvinced the system will hold up on a busy night. The sponsor wants a single firm-wide go-live and one standard training package for all shifts. How should the project manager shape the rollout to best support the change?

## Options

- **A**: Pilot first with the receptive day shift so a proven, working slice and credible champions exist before the sceptical shifts are asked to adopt.
- **B**: Bring the night and weekend shift leads in to co-shape the pilot and the rollout sequence for their teams, so the people their shifts follow help define how the change lands.
- **C**: Proceed with the sponsor's single firm-wide go-live, but train the night and weekend leads first as super-users who then support their own shifts through the change.
- **D**: Run a pilot on a night shift specifically, so the workflow differences and the leads' reliability concerns are surfaced and resolved on the hardest case before wider rollout.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Two things make the sceptical shifts hard: their workflows are genuinely untested against the design, and the leads those shifts trust doubt the system will cope — and a shift takes its cue from its leads. The highest-leverage move addresses both at once by giving those leads real influence over how the change is piloted and sequenced for their teams, converting the people who currently set a sceptical norm into co-owners of the rollout while their hands-on knowledge surfaces the workflow gaps early (B). Piloting with the willing day shift first (A) is sound sequencing and builds proof and champions, but a clean day-shift result does not prove the system handles the night/weekend workflows that are the actual risk, and it leaves the sceptical leads as spectators to someone else's success. Training the leads as super-users under a firm-wide go-live (C) uses their standing and is a real option, yet it casts them as deliverers of a design they were not part of shaping and accepts a big-bang over the incremental approach already chosen — super-users cannot vouch for a workflow fit that was never tested with them. Piloting on a night shift (D) rightly targets the hardest case and would expose the workflow and reliability concerns, but running the pilot *at* the sceptics is not the same as giving their leads a hand in shaping it; it tests the system on them rather than building their ownership of the change, so it surfaces problems without converting the resistance. Engaging the trusted leads as co-designers is what turns the norm-setters, which is the barrier the assessment actually found; the others act on the symptoms around it.
