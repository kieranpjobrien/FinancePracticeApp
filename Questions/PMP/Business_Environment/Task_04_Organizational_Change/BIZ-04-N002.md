# Question

An agile team delivered a self-service expenses portal in increments, and the product owner is satisfied it meets the acceptance criteria. Two sprints after release, finance reports that most employees still email scanned receipts to the old shared inbox instead of using the portal. The team has moved on to the next feature. The project manager is accountable for the benefits case, which depends on retiring the shared inbox, and has confirmed only that usage is low—not why. What is the project manager's best course of action?

## Options

- **A**: With the product owner, raise adoption-focused backlog items and run a short investigation with employees to find why the portal is being bypassed, then target the specific obstacles found.
- **B**: Announce a firm date to close the shared inbox and pair it with a refreshed communications and training push, so employees have a clear deadline and the support to meet it.
- **C**: Add the most-requested enhancements from the original portal feedback to the backlog, on the basis that closing functionality gaps is what will pull users across.
- **D**: Have finance route any receipts still arriving at the inbox back to the sender with a reminder to use the portal, nudging the behaviour change without removing the channel yet.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A product can meet its acceptance criteria yet still fail to deliver the benefit if people do not adopt it, and the project manager owns that gap. Critically, the cause of low usage is unknown—only the symptom is confirmed—so the move that fits is to make adoption visible work in the backlog *and* diagnose the real obstacle with users before choosing a remedy (A). Setting a hard cut-off with a comms-and-training push (B) is a credible adoption play, but launching it before the cause is known assumes the barrier is awareness or habit; if the portal is genuinely failing users—say it rejects valid receipt formats—a deadline forces them onto a broken path and risks lost claims. Adding enhancements (C) assumes a functionality gap with no evidence; the obstacle may be trust, awareness or workflow fit, none of which more features address. Routing receipts back with a reminder (D) is a gentler nudge but still presumes the problem is reluctance rather than a real impediment, applying pressure before understanding what it is. The margin is that B, C and D each commit to a specific intervention—deadline, features, or nudge—on an unverified theory of the cause, while A establishes the cause first so the intervention actually removes the obstacle that exists.
