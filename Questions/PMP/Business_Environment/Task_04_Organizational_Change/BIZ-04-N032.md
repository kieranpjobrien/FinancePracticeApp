# Question

A professional-services firm deployed a new CRM eight weeks ago. Login statistics are excellent — nearly every consultant signs in daily — and the sponsor is ready to declare the change a success. The project manager, however, finds that opportunities and client notes are still being kept in personal spreadsheets, and the CRM is used mainly to look up contact details. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Reframe the measure of success around the behaviours that deliver the benefits — pipeline and notes maintained in the CRM — and investigate why consultants keep using spreadsheets.
- **B**: Accept the login data as evidence of adoption and move the project toward closure as the sponsor suggests.
- **C**: Mandate that personal spreadsheets be deleted so consultants have no alternative to the CRM.
- **D**: Add advanced CRM features such as forecasting dashboards to make the system more attractive than spreadsheets.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Logins measure access, not adoption of the behaviours the CRM was meant to change, so the project manager should shift the success measure to the value-bearing actions and find out what is keeping consultants on spreadsheets (A). Accepting login data and closing (B) declares victory on a vanity metric while the intended benefits remain unrealised. Deleting spreadsheets (C) removes the workaround without understanding why it persists — likely trust, effort, or a genuine gap — and invites a backlash. Adding advanced features (D) assumes the barrier is capability when the real obstacle is unknown; diagnose the behaviour first, then decide whether features, support, or trust is the lever.
