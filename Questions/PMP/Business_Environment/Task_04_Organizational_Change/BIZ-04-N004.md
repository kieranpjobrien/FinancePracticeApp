# Question

A university is introducing a new student records system that will change administrative routines for faculty staff. The design is only part-complete, so the exact impact on each department's workflow and the go-live date are not yet settled. Early on, several department heads say they have heard about the change from colleagues at other institutions but nothing official from the project, and unease is spreading. How should the project manager respond?

## Options

- **A**: Communicate now what is known and decided — the reason for the change, the broad scope, and the process for keeping staff informed — and be explicit about what is still being worked out.
- **B**: Hold formal communications until the design is finalised, so that everything shared is accurate and the project does not have to retract details staff may rely on.
- **C**: Send a short factual notice confirming a project exists and asking staff to disregard external rumours until official detail is available.
- **D**: Route communication through the department heads, briefing them privately so each can decide how and when to inform their own staff.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The damaging vacuum is not the absence of final detail but the absence of any credible source, and that can be filled now without inventing specifics: communicate the rationale, the known scope, and the route for ongoing updates while being candid about open questions (A). Waiting for a finalised design (B) is the most tempting trap — accuracy is a real concern — but it leaves the rumour mill as the only voice for weeks and lets misinformation set, when honest "here is what we know and don't yet know" messaging avoids that without risking retractions. A bare "disregard rumours" notice (C) tells people what not to believe while giving them nothing to believe instead, which rarely settles unease. Briefing only the department heads (D) can be part of the approach but, left as the whole response, makes the message dependent on each manager's willingness and timing — recreating the inconsistent, partial flow that caused the problem.
