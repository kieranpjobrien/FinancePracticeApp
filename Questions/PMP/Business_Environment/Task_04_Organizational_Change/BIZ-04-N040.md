# Question

A logistics company built a new driver-dispatch platform with an agile team and is choosing how to move 12 depots onto it. The change will significantly alter dispatchers' daily routines. The sponsor, under pressure to show benefits, wants a single big-bang cut-over of all depots in one weekend. The platform itself is stable. From a change-support perspective, what should the project manager advise?

## Options

- **A**: Recommend a phased rollout across depots, using early depots to refine the change approach and build proof and advocates before the rest go live.
- **B**: Support the big-bang cut-over since the platform is stable and a single transition realises the benefits fastest.
- **C**: Run the big-bang but double the training budget so every dispatcher is fully prepared before the weekend.
- **D**: Delay any rollout until a complete, organisation-wide readiness assessment guarantees all 12 depots are equally prepared.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Technical stability is not the constraint — the risk is the scale of behavioural change hitting all depots at once with no chance to learn — so a phased rollout that uses early depots to refine support, generate evidence, and create advocates is the sounder change approach (A). A big-bang cut-over (B) maximises simultaneous disruption and forfeits the learning a staged approach provides, even if the software is reliable. Doubling training for a big-bang (C) still concentrates the human change on one weekend and cannot substitute for staged adoption and reinforcement. Delaying for a guarantee of equal readiness (D) is unrealistic and sacrifices momentum; phasing manages readiness as you go rather than waiting for perfect conditions.
