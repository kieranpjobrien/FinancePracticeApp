# Question

A project manager is about to launch a major new CRM and the supporting change campaign for a regional sales force. While preparing, the project manager learns the same sales force is simultaneously absorbing a reorganisation, a new commission structure, and a separate compliance system rollout this quarter. Early soundings suggest the teams feel overwhelmed and several changes are competing for the same people's attention. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Raise the cumulative load with the sponsor and the other change leads, and work jointly to sequence or coordinate the competing changes so each remains adoptable.
- **B**: Within the CRM project's own control, phase its change activities to land in the quieter pockets of the quarter and lighten the demands the campaign places on the teams.
- **C**: Proceed with the CRM launch as planned; each change has its own approved business case and sponsor, and reopening that is beyond this project's remit.
- **D**: Reinforce the CRM change effort with extra coaching and support so the sales force can absorb it despite the competing demands on their attention.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Too many changes converging on the same people degrades adoption of all of them, and no single project can fix that in isolation — it requires the sponsor and the other change leads to see the combined load and coordinate or re-sequence across initiatives (A). Phasing only the CRM's own activities (B) is a sensible thing to do within the project's authority, but it optimises one change while the aggregate overload — and the three other initiatives — remains unaddressed, so the saturation persists. Proceeding because each case was individually approved (C) is the trap of treating changes in isolation: every business case can be sound on its own yet collectively unachievable, and noticing that is precisely the project manager's responsibility to raise. Pouring more support into the CRM (D) treats the symptom for one initiative and actually adds to the demands on already-overwhelmed teams rather than relieving the cross-programme pressure.
