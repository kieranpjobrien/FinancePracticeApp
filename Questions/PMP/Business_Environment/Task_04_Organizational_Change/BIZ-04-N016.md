# Question

A project manager is leading a significant change to how a finance department closes its monthly accounts, affecting the controller's teams across three regions. The project manager will plan the communications, training, and transition. Several senior figures are willing to lend their names to the effort. With limited time to cultivate leadership backing, whose active and visible involvement should the project manager invest most in securing throughout?

## Options

- **A**: The Chief Financial Officer, who commissioned and funds the change, owns the closing outcome, and can visibly champion it across all affected teams.
- **B**: The financial controller, who runs the day-to-day close and whose teams must adopt the new process for it to work.
- **C**: The head of the project management office, who chairs governance and can keep the project on track through the standard controls.
- **D**: The regional finance managers, who are respected by the affected staff and can encourage adoption within their own teams.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The single strongest contributor to change success is active, visible sponsorship from the leader who owns the business outcome and has the authority and funding behind it — here, the CFO, who can champion the change across every affected team in a way no one below them can (A). The controller (B) is a critical stakeholder whose people must adopt the change and whose support matters greatly, but the controller operates within the mandate the sponsor sets; they reinforce sponsorship rather than substitute for it. The PMO head (C) governs process and controls, useful for delivery but not the source of the leadership credibility that moves people to change. The regional managers (D) are valuable local change agents and should be engaged, yet their influence is confined to their own teams and lacks the enterprise authority and resourcing power the sponsor brings. The distinction being tested is recognising the primary sponsor among several genuine leadership figures.
