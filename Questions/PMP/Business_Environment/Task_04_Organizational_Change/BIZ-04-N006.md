# Question

A government agency is consolidating five regional case-management teams onto a single shared platform. The project manager built the delivery plan from the platform's standard deployment template. As cutover nears, team-specific issues surface one by one: one team's statutory reporting cycle clashes with the cutover date, another relies on a bespoke approval step the platform does not support, and a third will lose a role entirely. Two regions are still to be planned. What should the project manager do for the remaining regions?

## Options

- **A**: Add a contingency buffer to each remaining region's schedule so there is slack to absorb whatever issues surface during their cutover.
- **B**: Work through each remaining team's actual roles, processes, and local variations against the platform, and build what that surfaces into their plan.
- **C**: Convene the change control board to review the issues found so far and apply the resulting lessons as standing constraints on the remaining regions.
- **D**: Run a combined workshop with the remaining teams to walk them through the platform early, so they can raise concerns before their cutover.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The issues are not random bad luck; they are real differences between each team's way of working and a generic template that was never checked against the specifics. Examining each remaining team's roles, processes, and local variations against the platform surfaces those clashes while there is still time to plan for them (B). A schedule buffer (A) cushions the impact of issues but never identifies them, so the team is still blindsided at cutover. Taking the issues found so far to change control (C) is useful for what is already known, but turning past surprises into generic constraints will miss whatever is unique to the two unexamined regions. An early walkthrough workshop (D) is genuinely helpful and may prompt some teams to speak up, but it relies on staff spontaneously recognising and voicing every conflict — a deliberate examination of each team's processes is more reliable than hoping the right concerns get raised on the day.
