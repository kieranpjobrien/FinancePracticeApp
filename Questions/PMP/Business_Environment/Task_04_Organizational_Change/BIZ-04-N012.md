# Question

A traditional engineering firm has decided to adopt agile delivery enterprise-wide, and all teams were moved to two-week sprints, daily stand-ups, and full ceremonies within a month. Two months later the events are running on schedule and attendance is high, yet how the teams actually make decisions, escalate, and prioritise looks much as it did before, and some staff have started calling the ceremonies a box-ticking exercise. The project manager is asked to advise on what to address first. What is the most likely root cause?

## Options

- **A**: The mandated one-month adoption window was too aggressive, so the practices were imposed before teams could absorb them and should be re-introduced more gradually.
- **B**: The rollout installed the events and mechanics but did not shift the underlying mindset, behaviours, and decision-making that give those events meaning.
- **C**: Senior leaders adopted the ceremonies for their teams but continue to direct work and approvals the old way, so staff take their cue from what leaders do rather than what the process prescribes.
- **D**: The teams were assembled without the cross-functional skills agile needs, so they fall back on old hand-offs because they cannot complete work within the team.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The diagnostic clue is that the visible mechanics are fully in place while the actual ways of working are unchanged — events are happening, but the thinking and decision-making behind them did not move. That points to a mindset-and-behaviour gap rather than a missing practice (B). The aggressive timeline (A) is a real contributing factor, but reintroducing the same events more slowly still only addresses pace and mechanics; going slower would not, on its own, change behaviour that the mechanics never reached. Leaders modelling the old way (C) is the strongest competing answer and a genuine cause of stalled transformations — but the scenario gives no signal that leaders specifically are the block, whereas it broadly describes practices adopted without the accompanying shift in behaviour, so B is the better-supported root cause and C an unevidenced inference. A cross-functional skills gap (D) would show up as work physically unable to finish inside a team, which is not what is described — the teams are running the ceremonies, just not changing how they operate.
