# Question

On a hybrid programme modernising a manufacturer's planning processes, a vocal group of experienced planners is resisting the new approach. They have made general complaints in meetings but have not set out exactly what troubles them, and the project has a benefits case it has not yet walked them through. The project manager needs to decide how to handle the resistance. What is the most useful first step?

## Options

- **A**: Walk the planners through the benefits case in detail, so the value of the new approach is clearly understood and the objections have something concrete to engage with.
- **B**: Meet the resisting planners to draw out the specific reasons behind their objections before deciding how to respond.
- **C**: Put the resistance on the agenda of the next team forum so the concerns are aired openly and the wider group can weigh in.
- **D**: Bring the sponsor in to reaffirm the rationale and the decision to proceed, giving the change visible authority the planners can align behind.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The planners' objections are still vague, and experienced staff may be signalling genuine flaws, risks, or unmet needs that the change should account for — so the most useful first step is to understand the specific reasons before choosing any response (B). Presenting the benefits case (A) is reasonable and may eventually be needed, but doing it first assumes the problem is that people do not grasp the value, which pre-judges the cause and risks talking over a legitimate concern, hardening the resistance. Airing it in an open forum (C) can be useful later, yet surfacing half-formed objections in a group before they are understood can amplify and entrench them rather than resolve them. Invoking the sponsor's authority (D) may secure outward compliance but buys conformity rather than commitment and forgoes the chance to learn what the resistance is actually about. Understanding the why first keeps every later option open.
