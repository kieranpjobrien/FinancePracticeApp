# Question

A media company is shifting three product groups from project-based delivery to long-lived agile product teams. Two groups have adapted well. The third, led by a long-serving director, still runs heavyweight stage gates and treats its Scrum Master as a junior coordinator; delivery is slipping and the group is confused about who decides what. The project manager has met the director privately and understands the resistance: the director fears losing the release-quality control the old gates gave them and believes the Scrum Master cannot safeguard it. The director is respected and not obstructive in bad faith. The sponsor is pressing for the group to operate the new way. What should the project manager do next?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the director to see how the two successful groups run their agile model, so they can judge for themselves that quality holds without the old gates.
- **B**: Ask the sponsor to reaffirm the new operating model and decision rights to all three groups, giving the change clear authority the director must work within.
- **C**: Agree a time-boxed transition for the group that keeps lighter gate checkpoints initially and phases them out as confidence in the new quality controls grows.
- **D**: Facilitate a working session for the director, Scrum Master, and product owner to design how the new model will assure the release quality the director is anxious about, clarifying decision rights in the process.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The resistance is understood and specific — the director fears the new model cannot protect release quality and does not trust the Scrum Master to do so — so the next step must convert that named concern into a concrete, jointly owned answer. A working session that designs how quality will be assured under the new model, with the director, Scrum Master, and product owner together, addresses the actual fear and settles the decision-rights confusion at the same time (D). Showing the director the successful groups (A) is persuasive but relies on inference from others' contexts and does not give this director a quality mechanism they helped shape for their own group. Having the sponsor reassert the model and decision rights (B) supplies authority the director must comply with but leaves the underlying quality concern unanswered, so it buys grudging conformance, not embedded change. A time-boxed taper of lighter gate checkpoints (C) is a sensible tactic and may emerge from the session, but adopting it now — before agreeing what the new model's quality controls actually are — concedes the mechanism before designing it; the controls must be defined first for the taper to phase out toward anything real.
