# Question

A government department is launching a new digital permitting service that changes how case officers process applications. The sponsor, a director-general, is genuinely committed and has cleared real time in their diary for the change. They ask the project manager to name the one thing they should personally do with that time that the project team cannot do for them. Which should the project manager name?

## Options

- **A**: Speak to staff in their own forums about why the change matters and keep showing up to reinforce it as the rollout proceeds, so the workforce repeatedly sees their leader backing it.
- **B**: Hold the other directors and middle managers accountable for actively supporting the change with their own teams, rather than quietly tolerating the status quo.
- **C**: Personally walk the case-officer floor during go-live week to demonstrate visible commitment at the moment of cut-over.
- **D**: Stand ready to clear obstacles and release additional resources quickly if adoption proves harder than the team expects.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A sponsor's scarcest and most distinctive contribution is the authentic, repeated voice of leadership — telling staff why the change matters and being seen to back it over time; no one on the project team can supply that legitimacy, which is why it should claim the sponsor's reserved time (A). Holding peer leaders accountable (B) is a genuine and important sponsor act, but it works through the management chain rather than directly with the workforce and is most powerful once the sponsor has first set the why; alone it can read as enforcement without conviction. A go-live floor walk (C) is visible commitment compressed into one week, whereas durable change needs presence sustained across the rollout, not a single appearance. Standing ready to remove blockers and fund gaps (D) is valuable but reactive and resource-focused; it addresses problems if they arise rather than supplying the ongoing advocacy that shapes whether they arise at all.
