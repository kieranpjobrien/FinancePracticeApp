# Question

An insurance company is moving claims handlers from a legacy desktop tool to a new cloud platform, released incrementally by an agile team. Handlers log in and use the platform for routine claims but switch back to the legacy tool for anything non-routine. The project manager wants to close the gap and embed the platform for all claim types. Which THREE actions are most likely to move non-routine work onto the new platform? (Choose three.)

## Options

- **A**: Have experienced handlers and a coach work alongside the team to add the non-routine claim paths handlers most often fall back to the legacy tool for.
- **B**: Create a safe practice environment with realistic non-routine cases so handlers build confidence before handling them live with customers.
- **C**: Identify and engage the senior handlers whom peers follow, understand why they avoid the platform for complex work, and enlist them to model and endorse it.
- **D**: Announce a date after which the legacy tool will be retired, and publish the remaining gaps the team will close before then so handlers can plan for the switch.
- **E**: Track each handler's share of non-routine claims processed on the new platform and review the trend with team leads to target where fallback persists.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Handlers default to the legacy tool for complex claims for three reinforcing reasons the actions must address: the new platform may not yet handle those paths well, handlers lack confidence to attempt them live, and influential peers set the norm of avoiding it. Closing the functional gaps on the very paths driving fallback removes the practical reason to switch back (A); a safe space to rehearse hard cases builds the confidence to do them live rather than retreat (B); and converting the senior handlers peers imitate changes the social norm that sustains avoidance (C). Setting a legacy-retirement date with a transparent gap list (D) is a legitimate and often necessary step, but as a forcing function it pressures handlers toward a deadline rather than building the capability and confidence that make non-routine work succeed, and pushing it before the gaps in (A) are closed risks customer-facing errors. Measuring the non-routine share by handler (E) is a genuinely useful diagnostic that beats a vanity login metric, yet measurement alone embeds nothing — it tells you where fallback persists without doing anything to remove it, so it supports the three actions rather than substituting for one.
