# Question

A hospital network is replacing paper triage forms with a tablet intake app, built by an agile team in two-week iterations. The emergency department adopted it over three releases and relies on it daily. The two outpatient clinics scheduled next reverted to paper after a half-day, their lead nurse saying it "fits emergency, not us." The product owner has walked the clinic floor, confirmed the flow genuinely differs, and refined clinic-specific stories into the backlog with the clinic leads. The team's next two sprints are fully committed to ED enhancements the ED is actively waiting on. The sponsor wants the clinics live within the quarter but has not been told the clinic work and the ED work cannot both fit that window. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Quantify the capacity conflict — the clinic stories against the committed ED enhancements within the quarter — and bring it to the sponsor so the priority call is made by the person who owns the deadline.
- **B**: Ask the product owner to sequence the refined clinic stories against the committed ED enhancements in the backlog, then take the resulting plan to the sponsor.
- **C**: Have the product owner negotiate directly with the clinic leads and the ED's representative to agree a shared ordering they can all live with, then schedule to that.
- **D**: Have the team forecast how much of the clinic work could be absorbed into the coming sprints without displacing the ED enhancements, then propose that slice to the sponsor.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The diagnostic work is done and the backlog is refined; what is now true is that two legitimate demands — clinic adaptation and committed ED enhancements — cannot both fit the quarter, and the sponsor is making a deadline promise without knowing that. The project manager's first move is to make the conflict explicit and route the priority decision to the sponsor, who owns the quarter commitment and the relative business value of the two groups (A). Asking the product owner to sequence the backlog (B) is a sound and necessary step — the product owner owns ordering — but ordering within a fixed capacity does not resolve a constraint where the total exceeds the window; the trade-off across ED value and clinic adoption is the sponsor's to weigh, and surfacing it is what unblocks the product owner to sequence against a real target. Having the product owner broker a shared ordering directly among the clinic and ED representatives (C) seeks consensus among the people closest to the work, but they do not own the quarter commitment and have no standing to trade ED business value against clinic adoption against a deadline the sponsor set; pushing that call down produces a negotiated order that may still breach the window and leaves the sponsor's promise unexamined. Forecasting an absorbable slice (D) is a constructive option to bring to that decision, but it pre-supposes the answer is "fit some clinic work in now" and quietly trades away ED commitments the ED is waiting on — a value judgement that should be named for the sponsor, not embedded in a forecast, before any capacity is reshuffled.
