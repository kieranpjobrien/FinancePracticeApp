# Question

A scrum team has released the first increment of a new self-service expenses tool to one department. Usage telemetry shows employees open the tool, start a claim, then abandon it and email the finance team as before. The product owner is keen to add the receipt-scanning feature from the backlog, believing richer functionality will pull people in. The next sprint review is in two days. What should the project manager encourage the team to do first?

## Options

- **A**: Investigate why users abandon the flow — through their feedback and the telemetry — before committing the next sprint to new features.
- **B**: Prioritise the receipt-scanning feature, as a more capable tool is more likely to displace the old email habit.
- **C**: Agree a date with the finance team to stop accepting emailed claims, pairing the cutover with extra support so employees complete claims in the tool.
- **D**: Extend the pilot to two more departments to gather a larger sample before drawing conclusions about the abandonment.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The signal is behavioural — people start the claim and abandon it — so understanding what is driving the drop-off, whether friction, confusion, or a trust barrier, must come before deciding what to build (A). Adding receipt scanning (B) assumes the problem is missing capability, but users are not finishing the flow they already have, so more features may not help and could burn a sprint on the wrong thing. Setting a date to withdraw the email fallback (C) is the strongest distractor — eventually removing the old channel is often necessary to cement a change, and the added support softens it — but doing so before the obstacle is understood risks forcing people into a flow that genuinely does not work, converting abandonment into frustration and escalations. Widening the pilot (D) gathers more data but simply multiplies the same unexplained abandonment, when there is already enough signal in hand to investigate the cause.
