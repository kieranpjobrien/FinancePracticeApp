# Question

A start-up's agile team has built a minimum viable product for a peer-to-peer equipment-rental marketplace and released it to a single city. After eight weeks, transaction volume is far below forecast, but interviews reveal that the few users who do transact are highly satisfied and renting repeatedly. The founder, who funds the project, wants to know what value the MVP has delivered so far. What is the most accurate framing for the project manager to give?

## Options

- **A**: The MVP has delivered little value, since transaction volume is well below the forecast the business case set.
- **B**: The MVP has delivered full value, because a working marketplace now exists and users are transacting on it.
- **C**: Value cannot be assessed until the product is rolled out to additional cities and reaches forecast scale.
- **D**: The MVP's primary value so far is validated learning: it has tested key assumptions cheaply and shown strong retention in a niche, informing the next investment decision.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The purpose of an MVP is to generate validated learning at low cost — testing whether the core assumptions hold before committing further investment — and discovering strong retention in a niche despite low overall volume is exactly the kind of insight that has real value for the next decision (D). A judges the MVP solely on a volume forecast, missing that the experiment's chief output is learning, not scale, at this stage. B overclaims "full value" from the mere existence of a working product and some transactions, ignoring that the business outcome is unproven. C refuses to recognise any value until forecast scale is reached, which both denies the learning already gained and would have the founder invest in a wider rollout before understanding whether the model works.
