# Question

A health-tech company is part way through delivering a patient-scheduling platform whose business case promises shorter appointment wait times and fewer missed appointments. The product is released feature by feature, and the sponsor has asked the PM to make sure the project can both *deliver* and *prove* those benefits. The team already has a definition of done, a groomed backlog, and regular stakeholder demos. Which THREE actions most directly strengthen the project's ability to evaluate and deliver the intended value? (Choose three.)

## Options

- **A**: Record a pre-release baseline for wait times and no-show rates so post-release movement in those metrics can be attributed to the platform.
- **B**: Agree with the sponsor which leading indicators — such as online-booking uptake — will be read between releases as early signals before the headline metrics move.
- **C**: Re-sequence the backlog so the features modelled to shift the wait-time and no-show numbers most are released ahead of lower-impact ones.
- **D**: Expand the stakeholder demo into a formal sign-off gate so each feature is approved by the business before it is considered done.
- **E**: Stand up a delivery dashboard tracking burndown, cycle time, and escaped-defect rate so the team's throughput and quality stay visible across releases.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Proving and delivering benefit turns on measurement and value-driven sequencing: a baseline lets later change be attributed rather than assumed (A); agreed leading indicators read benefit trajectory between releases before the lagging outcome moves (B); and ordering the backlog by modelled benefit impact pulls the targeted outcomes forward (C). Adding a business sign-off gate (D) improves delivery governance and acceptance but measures that a feature is *wanted and built*, not that the wait-time and no-show benefits are *materialising* — it speaks to output acceptance, not outcome. A throughput-and-quality dashboard (E) is genuinely useful project hygiene, yet burndown, cycle time, and defect rate describe how efficiently the team ships, not whether patients wait less or miss fewer appointments. The trap in D and E is that both are good practices that sound value-adjacent but track delivery, not benefit.
