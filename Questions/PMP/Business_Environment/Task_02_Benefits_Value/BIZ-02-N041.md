# Question

A predictive project to consolidate three regional warehouses into one was justified by an annual logistics saving of £2.4m, with the freight contract assumed flat. Halfway through fit-out — on schedule and on budget — the carrier announces a tariff change that, on first read, would erode a large part of that saving. The PM's procurement lead believes part of the loss may be recoverable by re-tendering freight, but firm numbers are several weeks away. The sponsor has asked the PM for a recommendation at next week's steering committee, which falls before that analysis can complete. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Take an interim position to the committee built on the carrier's headline tariff figure, clearly flagged as a worst-case pending the freight re-tender, so the sponsor has a basis to decide at the meeting as requested.
- **B**: Recommend in writing that the committee pause fit-out spend until the freight position is renegotiated, so no further capital is committed against an unproven benefit.
- **C**: Brief the committee that a material benefit assumption has moved, present the range bounded by the headline erosion and procurement's recoverability estimate, and recommend the decision be confirmed once the re-tender firms the number.
- **D**: Keep building to hold the schedule and ask the committee to treat the tariff change as a post-implementation risk to be managed once the warehouse is live.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

A benefit assumption underlying the approved case has moved, and the sponsor has asked for a recommendation at a meeting that lands before firm numbers exist — so the PM's job is to give governance the most complete *current* picture and let it decide with eyes open, not to wait for perfect data nor to hand over a single distorted figure. C does exactly that: it surfaces the change, presents the realistic range (headline erosion to procurement's recoverable estimate), and recommends governance confirm once the re-tender firms the number — honouring the sponsor's timeline while flagging the uncertainty. A is the trap: it respects the deadline but goes in on the worst-case headline alone, discarding the recoverability signal the team already holds and biasing the committee toward a worse-than-real number. B pre-empts the committee's call and may forfeit schedule and contractor availability over a loss that is not yet sized. D reclassifies a realised change to the value case as a future possibility and ducks a decision the sponsor explicitly wants now. The fine margin is A versus C: both meet the deadline with the headline figure on the table, but only C also carries the recoverable-portion information forward, so the committee weighs a range rather than a one-sided number.
