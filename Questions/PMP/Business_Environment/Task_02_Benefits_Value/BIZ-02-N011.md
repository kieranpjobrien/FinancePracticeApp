# Question

Three sprints after a sales team's new CRM features shipped, telemetry shows reps still logging deals in the old spreadsheet and barely touching the new system. The build works as specified and passed UAT. The sales director, who sponsored the work, says reps simply need to be told to switch and offers to mandate it at the next team meeting. The projected pipeline-visibility benefit depends on the data living in the CRM. What is the most effective next step for the product owner?

## Options

- **A**: Investigate with the reps why they are staying on the spreadsheet, and shape the next response around whatever the barrier turns out to be, since the benefit depends on real usage.
- **B**: Take up the director's offer to mandate the switch and pair it with disabling the spreadsheet workflow, using sponsor authority to force the behaviour change the benefit needs.
- **C**: Prioritise a short backlog of usability and workflow fixes for the next sprint to close the gap between the CRM and the spreadsheet reps prefer, then re-measure adoption.
- **D**: Add CRM data-entry completion to the reps' performance measures with sales-management agreement, so the system that drives the benefit is reinforced by how reps are assessed.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Benefits flow from use, not from a working build, and when a delivered product sits unused the productive first move is to find out why before committing to a remedy — the cause could be usability, trust, training, or workflow fit, and each implies a different fix, so diagnosis precedes solution (A). Accepting the mandate and disabling the spreadsheet (B) is tempting because the sponsor is offering authority and a mandate can lift usage figures, but forcing the switch before understanding the resistance risks compliance on paper with workarounds and resentment underneath, and may entrench rather than resolve the real barrier. Committing a sprint to usability and workflow fixes (C) is a credible response and might well be where the investigation lands, but jumping to build fixes presumes the problem is the product when it may be training or incentives; spending a sprint on an unverified diagnosis is exactly the step A guards against. Wiring CRM completion into performance measures (D) is a legitimate, well-supported adoption lever and could form part of the answer, but applied before the cause is known it leans on accountability to fix a problem that might be the tool itself, and measuring reps on a system they avoid for unaddressed reasons can backfire. The fine line is A versus C: both take the gap seriously, but A diagnoses the barrier first while C commits build capacity to a fix the diagnosis has not yet confirmed.
