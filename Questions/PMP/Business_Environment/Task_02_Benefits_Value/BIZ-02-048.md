# Question

Midway through a two-year warehouse automation project for a grocery retailer, a major competitor exits the region and the retailer's logistics volumes are projected to grow far beyond the assumptions in the original business case. The project is on schedule and within budget. The sponsor is delighted and tells the project manager to "keep going exactly as planned." What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Revisit the business case with the sponsor to test whether the original scope still maximises value under the new volume assumptions.
- **B**: Continue as instructed, since the project is on track and the change is favourable rather than a threat.
- **C**: Raise a change request to expand the automation scope to match the higher projected volumes.
- **D**: Note the development in the assumption log and review it at the next scheduled phase gate.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A material shift in the conditions the business case rests on is a trigger to re-validate that case, even when the project is healthy and the news is good — the question is whether the planned scope still represents the best available value, not merely whether it is deliverable (A). B treats "on track" as the only test and ignores that the definition of the right target may have moved. C jumps to a specific solution before confirming that more automation is actually the value-maximising response; the higher volumes might be better served differently. D defers a potentially significant value decision to a routine gate, when the sponsor and PM can and should assess it now while there is still runway to act.
