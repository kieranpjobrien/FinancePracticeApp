# Question

A retailer's loyalty-app project went live six months ago, justified by a forecast 5% lift in repeat-purchase rate. The post-implementation review shows repeat purchases are up 7%. The sponsor wants to declare the benefit exceeded and close the benefits register. The PM is aware that, during the same period, the marketing department ran an unrelated and heavily funded discount campaign, and a competitor's store in the region closed. The sponsor argues the 7% speaks for itself. How should the PM respond?

## Options

- **A**: Record the 7% as achieved against the 5% target, since the measured outcome exceeds the forecast and the review window matches the plan.
- **B**: Before attributing the lift to the app, isolate its contribution from the concurrent discount campaign and competitor closure, so the benefit claimed reflects what the app actually drove.
- **C**: Lower the recorded benefit to the 5% target, treating any excess as unreliable given the other factors, and close the register at plan.
- **D**: Extend the measurement window by six months so the discount campaign and competitor effects wash out, then read the steady-state figure.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A credible benefit claim attributes the outcome to the project, and here two large confounders — a funded discount campaign and a competitor closure — plausibly drove part of the 7%, so the figure cannot be booked to the app without separating its contribution (B). A takes the headline number at face value and credits the project with a lift it may not have caused, which overstates the benefit and corrupts the organisation's basis for funding similar work. C is arbitrary: capping the result at the 5% target neither measures the app's true effect nor reflects the confounding — it just substitutes the forecast for evidence. D assumes the confounders are temporary and will simply disappear, but a competitor closure is a lasting market change, so waiting does not isolate the app's effect; it conflates a permanent shift with the project's contribution. The fine margin is B versus D: both reject the naive 7%, but only B addresses attribution directly, whereas D relies on the false hope that time alone will untangle a structural change from the project's impact.
