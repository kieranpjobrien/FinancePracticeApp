# Question

A manufacturer must choose between two automation initiatives within one capital envelope. Option X returns most of its benefit in year one and little thereafter. Option Y returns a larger total but spread across five years. The organisation funds projects from borrowed capital and applies a cost of capital to investment decisions, and the sponsor wants a single comparison that both can be ranked on defensibly. Which technique should the project manager lead the recommendation with?

## Options

- **A**: Net present value, ranking each option on its future benefits discounted to today at the organisation's cost of capital.
- **B**: Internal rate of return, ranking each option by the percentage return it generates so the more efficient use of the borrowed capital is selected.
- **C**: Payback period, ranking each option by how quickly it recovers the outlay so exposure on the borrowed capital is minimised soonest.
- **D**: Total undiscounted benefit over each option's full horizon, ranking on the larger gross return the capital ultimately produces.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

With capital that carries a cost and two options whose benefits fall in very different years, net present value is the technique that ranks them on a like-for-like basis — it discounts each future benefit to today at the cost of capital, so an early-returning option and a larger-but-later one can be compared as single present-day figures (A). Internal rate of return (B) is a legitimate and tempting capital-efficiency measure that also uses discounting, but as the lead ranking it can mislead between mutually exclusive options of different scale and timing — a higher-percentage but smaller-value option can out-rank the one that adds more present value, which is the question actually being asked. Payback (C) speaks directly to minimising exposure on borrowed funds and is a reasonable secondary lens, but it ignores all benefit after the outlay is recovered and would mechanically favour the fast-returning Option X regardless of total value created. Undiscounted total benefit (D) captures the full horizon but treats a dollar in year five as equal to a dollar today, which is exactly the assumption a cost of capital rules out, biasing the ranking toward the later-returning option. The discriminator is that all four are real appraisal techniques; only NPV ranks mutually exclusive options on discounted value, which is what a cost-of-capital decision requires.
