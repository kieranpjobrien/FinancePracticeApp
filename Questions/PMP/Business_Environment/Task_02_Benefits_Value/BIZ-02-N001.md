# Question

A product team is three sprints into building a logistics platform. A low-effort warehouse dashboard sits low in the backlog, below several larger integration features. The dashboard would let one operations team retire a daily manual report next sprint; the integration features are prerequisites for onboarding three new client warehouses the sponsor has already signed. The sponsor questions why the cheap, quick win is not being pulled forward. How should the product owner respond?

## Options

- **A**: Keep the current order, explaining that the integration features unlock contracted revenue the dashboard does not, so the higher-value work is sequenced ahead despite the dashboard's lower cost.
- **B**: Pull the dashboard into the next sprint, since its value-to-effort ratio is high and it begins returning benefit immediately while the larger features continue in parallel.
- **C**: Reorder the backlog by weighted shortest job first, so the dashboard's short duration moves it ahead of the longer integration items.
- **D**: Defer the ranking decision to the sponsor, since the trade-off between a quick operational win and contracted onboarding is a business judgement the sponsor owns.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Prioritisation maximises benefit delivered for the value at stake, and here the integration features are gating contracted revenue from three signed warehouses while the dashboard saves one team a daily report — the larger features carry the greater value even though the dashboard is cheaper, so holding the order and explaining the value basis is the defensible call (A). Pulling the dashboard because its value-to-effort ratio looks high (B) is the tempting trap: a strong ratio favours small wins, but a high ratio on modest absolute value should not displace work that releases far larger contracted benefit, and "quick and cheap" is not the same as "most valuable now." Reordering by weighted shortest job first (C) is a real economic-sequencing technique, but applied mechanically it rewards the dashboard purely for its short duration and ignores that the integration features unlock revenue an order of magnitude larger; WSJF would itself score the integration work higher once cost of delay is included. Deferring entirely to the sponsor (D) is defensible since the sponsor owns value trade-offs, but the product owner owns backlog ordering and is expected to bring a value-based recommendation rather than hand the decision back; abdicating the ranking is weaker than explaining it. The fine line is A versus B: both are value arguments, but A weighs absolute benefit while B over-weights the effort ratio.
