# Question

A development team demonstrates completed features at the end of each sprint. Attendance is good and the demos run smoothly, but the product owner finds the reviews confirm that work was finished without ever testing whether it is delivering value. The product owner wants to change the review so it genuinely informs the value question. Which change would do most to achieve that?

## Options

- **A**: Have key business stakeholders work with the increment against their real tasks and react to its usefulness, rather than watch the team present finished features.
- **B**: Open each review by restating the sprint's value goal and the benefit metrics, then judge each demonstrated feature against whether it moved those measures.
- **C**: Replace the feature walkthrough with a release of the increment to a slice of real users and bring their early usage data into the next review.
- **D**: Add a structured stakeholder feedback round after the demonstrations, capturing reactions and feeding them into backlog re-prioritisation.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A review informs value most when the people who stand to benefit actually engage with the increment against their own needs and respond to it, because passive viewing of finished features confirms completion but never exercises the product against real use — turning the demo into hands-on inspection is the change that directly surfaces whether the work is useful (A). Framing the review around the value goal and benefit metrics (B) is a genuine improvement and sharpens the value lens, but discussing features against metrics is still largely a presentation; without stakeholders interacting with the increment it can confirm intent more than realised usefulness. Releasing to a slice of users and bringing usage data back (C) is arguably the strongest evidence of value of all, but it shifts the learning out of the review and into the next cycle and is not always feasible every sprint; as a change to the review itself it partly answers a different question than the in-session one posed. Adding a structured feedback round that feeds re-prioritisation (D) is sound practice and closes the loop to the backlog, but feedback gathered after watching a demonstration still rests on what stakeholders saw rather than used, so it inherits the same gap A is meant to close. The fine line is A versus B: both make the review about value, but A has stakeholders test the increment while B keeps it a metric-framed presentation.
