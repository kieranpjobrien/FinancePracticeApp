# Question

A project manager is helping a manufacturing client structure the benefits register for a new plant-floor IoT programme. To choose the right measurement approach for each, the PM must first classify each expected benefit. Match each expected benefit to the category that best describes it.

## Items

- **1**: A £400,000 annual reduction in energy spend from optimised machine scheduling.
- **2**: Improved employee perception of the company as a technology-forward employer.
- **3**: Avoiding an anticipated regulatory fine by meeting new emissions-reporting rules.
- **4**: A 15% increase in units produced per shift, monetised through additional sales.

## Matches

- **A**: Intangible benefit, assessed through proxy measures such as engagement surveys.
- **B**: Tangible benefit realised as a direct, quantifiable cost saving.
- **C**: Tangible benefit realised as additional revenue from increased output.
- **D**: Tangible benefit realised as a cost avoidance rather than a saving or a gain.

## Answer

1-B, 2-A, 3-D, 4-C

## Explanation

Energy spend falling by a fixed annual figure is a direct, measurable cost saving against an existing outlay (1-B), whereas the production uplift converted into extra sales is new revenue, a different category of tangible gain (4-C). Avoiding a fine the organisation would otherwise have incurred is a cost avoidance — a tangible benefit, but one measured against a counterfactual rather than a reduction in current spend (3-D). Improved perception as an employer has no direct monetary meter and must be tracked through proxies such as engagement or reputation surveys, marking it as intangible (2-A). The fine margins are saving versus avoidance (1 versus 3) and saving versus revenue (1 versus 4): all three are tangible, but they realise through distinct mechanisms that drive different measurement approaches.
