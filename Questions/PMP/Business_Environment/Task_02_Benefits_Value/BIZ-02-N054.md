# Question

Two competing initiatives are bidding for the same capital. Initiative A returns £500,000 in year one and nothing thereafter. Initiative B returns £150,000 a year for four years (£600,000 undiscounted). Both cost the same to deliver, and the organisation discounts future cash flows at a meaningful rate to reflect the time value of money and the uncertainty of later years. A steering-committee member argues B is clearly superior because £600,000 beats £500,000. As the PM advising the committee, how should you frame the comparison?

## Options

- **A**: Agree that B is superior, since its total undiscounted return of £600,000 exceeds A's £500,000.
- **B**: Compare the two on discounted (present-value) terms, because B's later cash flows are worth less today and carry more uncertainty, which may reverse the ranking the raw totals suggest.
- **C**: Recommend A outright, because front-loaded returns are always preferable to deferred ones regardless of the discount rate.
- **D**: Treat the two as equivalent, since the difference between £500,000 and £600,000 is within the normal margin of forecasting error.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

With a meaningful discount rate, future cash flows are worth less than present ones, so the correct comparison is on a present-value basis — and B's stream, spread over four uncertain years, may discount below A's single near-term £500,000, meaning the raw-total ranking can flip (B). A compares undiscounted totals and ignores the time value of money the organisation has explicitly chosen to apply, which is the precise error the committee member is making. C reaches a defensible-sounding conclusion but for an absolute reason that is wrong: front-loading is not *always* better — whether A beats B depends on the discount rate and could go either way, so asserting A wins regardless overstates the case. D waves the difference away as forecast noise, abandoning the analysis entirely when a present-value calculation would give a defensible answer. The fine margin is B versus C: both suspect A may win, but only B grounds the decision in the actual discounted comparison rather than an unconditional rule of thumb.
