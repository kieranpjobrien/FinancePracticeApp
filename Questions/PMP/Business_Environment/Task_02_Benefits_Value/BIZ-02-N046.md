# Question

A 12-month product-development project is at month 9, with £900,000 of its £1.2m budget spent. A competitor has just launched an equivalent product, and the revised forecast shows the remaining £300,000 of work would return only about £150,000 in directly attributable lifetime benefit — though the product also underpins a platform capability the organisation's strategy depends on. The sponsor, conscious of the £900,000 already committed, has asked the PM to prepare the continuation case for the steering committee. How should the PM frame that case?

## Options

- **A**: Weigh the £300,000 still to spend against the £150,000 forecast benefit *plus* the strategic platform value, presenting the £900,000 already spent as sunk and excluded from the forward decision.
- **B**: Net the £150,000 forecast benefit against the full £1.2m project cost so the committee sees the project's true end-to-end return before deciding whether to finish.
- **C**: Weigh the £300,000 still to spend against only the £150,000 directly attributable benefit, and record the strategic platform capability separately as a qualitative consideration rather than in the figures.
- **D**: Compare the £300,000 still to spend against the £150,000 attributable benefit and an estimate of what completing the project would cost to replicate later if stopped now and the capability is needed.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A sound continuation decision weighs cost *still to be incurred* against *all* the value still to be gained, and the strategic platform capability is a legitimate forward benefit — so excluding it from the economics understates the case for continuing (A). B re-imports the £900,000 sunk cost into a forward decision; it guarantees the project reads as a loss and is exactly the distortion the sunk-cost discipline exists to prevent. C correctly treats the £900,000 as sunk but relegates the platform value to a footnote, so the quantified comparison (£300k against £150k) is structurally biased toward stopping even though a material benefit has been pushed out of the numbers — a subtle but real way to mis-frame the trade-off. D introduces a replacement-cost figure that is neither a benefit of *this* project nor a cost the committee is being asked to commit; it muddies the forward comparison with a hypothetical avoided cost rather than sizing the value the £300,000 actually buys. The fine margin is A versus C: both keep the sunk cost out, but only A puts the strategic value *inside* the comparison the committee will actually weigh, rather than leaving the decisive benefit alongside it as colour.
