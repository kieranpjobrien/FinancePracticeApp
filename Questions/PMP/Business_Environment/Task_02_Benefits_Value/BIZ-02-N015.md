# Question

A PM is drafting the benefits management plan for a new self-service HR portal expected to reduce the HR team's administrative workload. The sponsor wants assurance that the plan will actually allow the benefit to be evaluated after delivery. Which elements are essential for the plan to support credible benefit evaluation? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: A baseline measurement of the current administrative effort before the portal goes live.
- **B**: A named owner accountable for tracking and realising the benefit after handover.
- **C**: A defined metric and target that quantify what "reduced workload" means.
- **D**: A complete list of every screen and field the portal development team will build.
- **E**: The total development cost of the portal, recorded so the spend can be reported at go-live.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Credible evaluation needs a pre-change baseline (A), an accountable owner to track realisation (B), and a quantified metric and target defining the benefit (C) — without these the benefit cannot be measured or attributed. The detailed build inventory (D) describes the solution, not how its value is judged. The development cost (E) is a real and necessary figure for the business case, but on its own it sizes the *investment*, not whether the administrative-workload *benefit* is realised — the plan still needs a measurable outcome to set that cost against, which is what A and C provide. The trap in D and E is that both are legitimate project artefacts that sound relevant, yet neither lets anyone judge whether the workload actually fell.
