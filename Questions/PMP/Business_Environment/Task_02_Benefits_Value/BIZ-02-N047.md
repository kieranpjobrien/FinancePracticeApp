# Question

A digital-banking squad has built a minimum viable payments feature over three sprints. It passes all acceptance tests, and analytics from a small beta cohort of self-selected early adopters show customers completing payments faster than the legacy flow. The product owner is keen to ship to the full customer base immediately to start booking the projected fee uplift. Two items from the original scope remain unbuilt: a fraud-screening check and a low-balance warning. What should the PM advise the product owner to do?

## Options

- **A**: Release to all customers now and add the fraud-screening and low-balance items in the next sprint, since the feature already demonstrates the speed benefit.
- **B**: Hold general release until the fraud-screening and low-balance items ship, since both protect customers from losses the live feature would otherwise expose them to.
- **C**: Expand the beta to a larger, representative cohort to confirm the speed benefit holds beyond early adopters before deciding on the unbuilt items.
- **D**: Re-baseline the scope to drop the two unbuilt items, then release, on the basis that the measured speed gain already justifies the feature.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Fraud screening and a low-balance warning are protective controls on a payments feature, so releasing to the full base without them exposes customers to fraud losses and overdrafts the live product would actively cause — a payments-money disbenefit that outweighs an unproven fee uplift (B). A treats those controls as routine backlog and books the uplift while customers are unprotected, which is exactly the harm the controls exist to prevent. C is a reasonable validity concern about the early-adopter cohort, but the binding constraint here is the missing protection, not the strength of the speed signal; confirming the benefit holds more widely does not make it safe to ship money-movement without controls. D drops the controls to clear the path to release, discarding risk protection to suit the desire to ship rather than because the risk has gone away. The fine margin is B versus C: both pause general release, but only B addresses the actual blocker — unprotected customers on a live payments flow — rather than gathering more evidence for a benefit that is not what is holding the release.
