# Question

Six months after a customer-service transformation went live at a telecoms company, the headline benefit metric — average call handling time — has improved by the targeted 18%. However, the benefits owner notices that customer satisfaction has fallen and repeat-call rates have risen over the same period. The sponsor is ready to declare the benefit realised on the strength of the handling-time figure. What should the project manager raise?

## Options

- **A**: Nothing; the project committed to handling time, that target is met, and satisfaction is outside the agreed benefit scope.
- **B**: That a new project should be chartered to address the satisfaction decline as a separate initiative.
- **C**: That the handling-time gain may be illusory if it has been achieved by cutting calls short, and the benefit should be assessed against the underlying outcome it was meant to proxy.
- **D**: That the satisfaction and repeat-call figures are likely seasonal noise and the realisation declaration should proceed.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

A single metric improving while related outcomes deteriorate is a signal that the metric may be gamed rather than the real benefit realised — shorter handling time was a proxy for more efficient, effective service, and rising repeat calls suggest the underlying outcome has not improved (C). A hides behind the letter of the target and ignores that the metric was only ever a stand-in for value; declaring success on a gamed measure realises no real benefit. B leaps to chartering work before the existing benefit claim has even been properly examined, and treats a symptom of the same change as an unrelated problem. D dismisses correlated, directionally consistent signals as noise without evidence, which is a convenient way to wave the declaration through rather than test it.
