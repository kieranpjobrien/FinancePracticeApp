# Question

A PM inherits a stalled programme to roll out telematics across a haulage fleet. Reviewing the approved business case, the PM finds the fuel-saving benefit was forecast at the top of the vendor's published range and assumed 100% driver compliance from day one — assumptions that comparable rollouts in the sector did not achieve. The programme is otherwise viable, and the sponsor is attached to the headline benefit figure that secured funding. What is the most appropriate action for the PM?

## Options

- **A**: Re-forecast the benefit using evidence-based assumptions — a realistic saving within the range and a phased compliance ramp — and take the revised case to governance with the rationale.
- **B**: Proceed against the approved business case as signed off, since changing an agreed benefit figure mid-programme undermines the sponsor and the original approval.
- **C**: Keep the headline figure but add a contingency note that actual savings may be lower, so the approved benefit is preserved while the risk is visible.
- **D**: Quietly manage delivery to the realistic figure without amending the business case, to avoid a difficult conversation with the sponsor about the original numbers.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A benefit forecast built on optimistic, evidence-contradicted assumptions misstates the value the organisation can expect, and the PM's duty is to give governance a realistic basis for decisions — so the case should be re-forecast on defensible assumptions and re-presented with the rationale (A). B defers to the original sign-off even though the assumptions are demonstrably unrealistic, perpetuating a figure the sector's experience says will not be met. C keeps the inflated headline and merely footnotes the risk, which preserves a number everyone now has reason to doubt rather than correcting it — the benefit baseline stays wrong. D manages to the right number privately but leaves the official case overstated, so governance, reporting, and any benefits review still run off a false baseline and the variance will look like underperformance. The fine margin is A versus C: both acknowledge the optimism, but only A corrects the recorded benefit so future tracking measures against a credible target, whereas C leaves the misleading figure in place with a caveat.
