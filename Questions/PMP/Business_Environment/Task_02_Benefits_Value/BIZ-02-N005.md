# Question

Midway through an agile transformation programme, a competitor launches a feature that erodes the value of a major epic still in the backlog; delivering it would now produce far less benefit than the business case assumed. A different, smaller epic already in the backlog has become disproportionately valuable as a response. Two of the four teams are mid-build on the diminished epic, and the release budget is fixed. What should the product owner do next?

## Options

- **A**: Re-rank the backlog so the next increments flow to the now higher-value epic, and take the changed value picture to the sponsor to revisit the business case.
- **B**: Finish the in-progress slice of the diminished epic to a coherent release point, then redirect capacity to the higher-value epic once the teams reach a clean stopping line.
- **C**: Stop work on the diminished epic at the current sprint boundary and reassign both teams to the higher-value epic immediately, treating further investment in low-value work as waste.
- **D**: Hold the current plan for this release and absorb the re-prioritisation into the next planning cycle, so the change is made deliberately rather than mid-flight.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

When market conditions move value, agile prioritisation re-points investment toward what now delivers the most benefit and keeps governance informed by revisiting the business case, so re-ranking the backlog for the next increments and surfacing the shift to the sponsor is the decision that both acts on value and respects oversight (A). Finishing the in-progress slice to a coherent stopping point first (B) is genuinely reasonable — abandoning partly built work mid-slice can waste effort and leave the product incoherent — but it subordinates the value pivot to tidiness and can sink another increment into an epic the market has already devalued; re-ranking now does not forbid a clean cut-over, it just stops treating completeness as the priority. Halting both teams immediately at the sprint boundary (C) is the aggressive value play and sometimes right, but reassigning everyone with no transition risks discarding nearly finished work and ignores that some of the diminished epic may still hold residual value or dependencies; "stop now" is a reflex unless the in-flight work is checked. Deferring the whole change to the next planning cycle (D) prizes process stability over a time-sensitive opportunity and lets a known value shift go unactioned for a full cycle while the competitor's advantage compounds. The fine line is A versus B: both honour value, but A re-ranks on value immediately while B lets the current build finish before pivoting.
