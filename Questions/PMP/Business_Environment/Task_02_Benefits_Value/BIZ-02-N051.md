# Question

A subscription streaming service has shipped a redesigned onboarding flow whose ultimate benefit — improved twelve-month subscriber retention — cannot be confirmed for a year. The product owner does not want to wait twelve months to learn whether the change is working. The PM is identifying leading indicators that could signal, early, whether the retention benefit is on track. Which of the following are appropriate leading indicators for this benefit? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: The proportion of new subscribers who complete onboarding within the first session.
- **B**: Early-life engagement, such as the number of titles watched in the first two weeks.
- **C**: The twelve-month retention rate itself, read at the twelve-month point.
- **D**: The thirty-day cancellation rate of the newly onboarded cohort.
- **E**: The total engineering effort spent building the redesigned onboarding flow.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

Leading indicators are early, measurable signals plausibly linked to the eventual outcome: onboarding completion (A), early-life engagement (B), and the thirty-day cancellation rate (D) all move well before the twelve-month mark and are credibly connected to retention, so they let the team course-correct early. The twelve-month retention rate (C) is the *lagging* outcome itself, not a leading indicator — reading it at twelve months defeats the purpose of an early signal. Engineering effort (E) measures the cost of building the flow, not whether subscribers are behaving in ways that predict retention; it is an input, not an indicator of benefit. The traps are C, which is the real benefit dressed up as an early signal, and E, which is a genuine project metric that says nothing about the outcome.
