# Question

An online lender is building a new loan-origination workflow. The product owner has identified a thin slice — application capture plus automated credit decisioning — that would let the business start writing loans next month, with the remaining features following each sprint. Marketing has booked a paid acquisition campaign to coincide with launch and argues a partial product will waste the spend and draw complaints about missing features. Compliance notes the credit-decisioning logic is the highest-risk component and would carry the most weight if exercised on real applicants early. Funding for the whole programme is approved. What should the project manager advocate?

## Options

- **A**: Release the thin slice next month to a limited segment without the paid campaign, begin earning real origination data and revenue, and align the marketing push with a later, fuller release.
- **B**: Release the thin slice next month and run the booked campaign against it, accepting some early friction as the cost of starting benefit flow at scale during the planned window.
- **C**: Hold the release until the credit-decisioning logic has been exercised and hardened on a larger pilot, since launching the riskiest component thin could turn early benefit into early loss.
- **D**: Keep the slice-by-slice build but gate every feature behind flags, switching the full set on for the campaign date so delivery stays incremental while the launch lands complete.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Incremental delivery captures value early by shipping a genuinely useful slice and learning from real use, and the strongest version of that here is a limited release that starts revenue and origination data while deliberately decoupling the paid campaign from a thin product — it realises benefit now without spending acquisition budget driving traffic to an incomplete experience or stress-testing the riskiest logic at full volume (A). Pairing the slice with the booked campaign (B) is a real and defensible way to maximise the launch window, but it points paid demand at a partial product and exercises the high-risk decisioning component at scale before it has proven out, trading early learning for early exposure — the same slice, released quietly first, gets most of the benefit at far less risk. Holding for a hardened pilot (C) respects the compliance concern and is not unreasonable, but it defers all revenue and real-world learning to de-risk a component that a limited live release would itself help validate; it is more cautious than the value case requires. Gating everything behind flags for a big-bang switch-on (D) incurs the full cost and delay of building the complete set before any value is realised while only appearing incremental, capturing none of the early benefit that motivates slicing. The fine line is A versus B: both release the slice next month, but A protects the marketing spend and limits risk exposure while B couples them to a thin launch.
