# Question

Midway through an agile programme to build a field-service mobile app for an energy utility, the team has delivered two of five planned epics. A regulatory change suddenly makes a sixth, unplanned epic — automated safety-isolation logging — capable of avoiding an estimated £400,000 in annual compliance penalties, more than any remaining planned epic is forecast to deliver. The product owner wants to know how to respond. There is no spare capacity; adding the new epic means dropping or deferring planned work. What should the PM facilitate?

## Options

- **A**: Re-prioritise the backlog with the product owner so the highest-value items — now including the safety-isolation epic — are scheduled next, deferring lower-value planned work.
- **B**: Complete the original five epics as approved first, then add the safety-isolation epic in a follow-on release, to honour the agreed scope.
- **C**: Add the safety-isolation epic on top of the existing plan and extend the timeline and budget to accommodate all six epics.
- **D**: Escalate to the steering committee for a formal scope-change decision before allowing the product owner to touch the backlog.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Agile delivery maximises value by continuously re-prioritising the backlog as new information arrives, and a newly available epic worth more than any remaining planned item should move up, displacing lower-value work — which is the product owner's prerogative to decide (A). B rigidly preserves the original sequence and delays a now-higher-value, penalty-avoiding benefit behind work that delivers less, sacrificing value to honour a plan that the new regulation has overtaken. C avoids the prioritisation decision by simply doing everything, expanding time and cost rather than trading lower-value scope for higher — the opposite of value-based sequencing. D over-escalates: re-ordering the backlog within the existing envelope is exactly the product owner's role, and routing a routine reprioritisation through the steering committee adds delay that itself erodes the avoided-penalty benefit. The fine margin is A versus C: both bring the high-value epic in, but only A funds it by deferring lower-value work, whereas C inflates the programme to dodge the trade-off.
