# Question

A retailer's product owner has an increment ready that meaningfully improves checkout speed. Releasing now captures the peak-season sales window but defers a planned accessibility enhancement to the next increment; that enhancement also carries a regulatory obligation with a compliance deadline two increments away. Releasing later bundles both but misses the seasonal window. Both items are valuable and both are committed in the roadmap. How should the product owner decide the release sequence?

## Options

- **A**: Weigh the time-bound value and obligations on each side — the seasonal sales window against the accessibility deadline — and choose the sequence that secures the most benefit while keeping the compliance date safe.
- **B**: Release the checkout improvement now to capture the seasonal value, since the accessibility deadline is still two increments out and the revenue window will not recur.
- **C**: Release the bundled increment later so the accessibility obligation ships as early as possible, treating the regulatory commitment as the constraint that should anchor the sequence.
- **D**: Release the checkout improvement now and pull the accessibility enhancement forward into the very next increment, committing the team to both windows rather than choosing between them.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

When two valuable items compete for a release slot and each carries its own time-bound element — a non-recurring sales window on one side, a regulatory deadline on the other — the decision turns on weighing both clocks together and choosing the sequence that maximises benefit without putting the compliance date at risk, which is precisely the trade-off A makes explicit (A). Releasing checkout now on the logic that the deadline is still distant (B) captures the seasonal value and is plausible, but it reasons from only one of the two clocks and quietly assumes the accessibility work will comfortably fit before its deadline; if the next increments are uncertain, "still two out" can erode into a compliance breach. Anchoring on the regulatory obligation and bundling later (C) safely protects the deadline but over-weights it: shipping accessibility as early as possible by missing the seasonal window forfeits revenue that the deadline does not actually require be sacrificed this increment. Committing to both windows by promising accessibility in the very next increment (D) is an attractive have-both answer, but it pre-commits future capacity without testing whether the next increment can absorb it, converting a sequencing decision into a capacity bet that may break one window or the other. The fine line is A versus B: both lean toward releasing checkout now, but A first confirms the accessibility deadline stays safe under that sequence while B infers it.
