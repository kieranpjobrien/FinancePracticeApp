# Question

Two competing proposals are on the table to spend a fixed innovation budget. Proposal X has a payback period of 14 months; Proposal Y pays back in 22 months. Over a five-year horizon, however, Proposal Y's discounted net present value is roughly double Proposal X's, because its benefits are larger and more durable. The finance director favours X for its faster payback; the CEO has asked the project manager for a recommendation on which creates more value. On what basis should the PM frame the recommendation?

## Options

- **A**: Recommend Y, on the basis that net present value measures the total discounted value created, whereas payback only measures how quickly the outlay is recovered.
- **B**: Recommend X, because a shorter payback period returns the organisation's capital sooner and reduces exposure to forecast error.
- **C**: Recommend whichever proposal the finance director prefers, since payback is the organisation's stated investment screen.
- **D**: Recommend running both proposals at half budget each to diversify and capture some value from each.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The CEO asked which option creates more value, and net present value is the measure of total value created over the horizon, capturing the size and durability of benefits that payback ignores — Y's higher NPV is the relevant answer to that question (A). B states a genuine virtue of payback (speed of capital recovery and lower forecast risk), but it answers a different question — liquidity and risk — not value creation, and so is the well-reasoned distractor. C defers to a screening heuristic rather than the decision criterion actually posed, letting a rule of thumb override the value comparison. D dilutes both investments below the scale at which their business cases were built, likely destroying value in both rather than choosing the higher-value option.
