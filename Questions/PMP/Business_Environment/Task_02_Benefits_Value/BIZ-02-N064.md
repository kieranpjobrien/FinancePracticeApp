# Question

An agile team building a B2B marketplace has spent six of a planned ten sprints. Validated learning from recent releases shows the core monetisation assumption is weaker than expected, and the realistic remaining value is now well below the cost of the four remaining sprints. The product owner is weighing whether to continue, pivot, or stop. As the PM facilitating the decision, which considerations should legitimately inform it? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: The cost of the four remaining sprints relative to the realistic value they would now create.
- **B**: Whether a pivot could redirect the remaining capacity toward a more valuable outcome.
- **C**: The six sprints of effort already invested, which would be wasted if the team stops now.
- **D**: The current evidence on the monetisation assumption from recent releases.
- **E**: The proportion of the originally committed scope the team has already completed.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

A sound continue/pivot/stop decision is forward-looking: it weighs remaining cost against realistic remaining value (A), considers whether a pivot would redeploy capacity more productively (B), and grounds itself in the latest evidence on the failing assumption (D) — all concern what happens from here. The six sprints already spent (C) are sunk — gone whether the team continues, pivots, or stops — so framing them as "wasted if we stop" is the sunk-cost fallacy and must not sway the decision. The percentage of original scope completed (E) is the same sunk effort expressed as a completion figure; how far through the plan the team is says nothing about whether finishing is worth it now, and treating "we're 60% done" as a reason to continue is the sunk-cost trap in disguise. The traps are C and E: both dress past investment up as a reason to proceed, the genuinely relevant items being only those about future cost, value and evidence.
