# Question

A hybrid programme to digitise a regional council's permit service is six iterations into a planned twelve. The business case promised a 30% reduction in average processing time, but that outcome can only be measured once the new service has been live and adopted for a full quarter — well after the programme ends. The steering committee is uneasy because they have no read on whether the benefit is on track. What is the most useful thing the project manager can give them now?

## Options

- **A**: A revised forecast of the 30% reduction extrapolated from the team's current velocity against the remaining backlog.
- **B**: A set of leading indicators already observable in the released increments, such as per-step handling time and rework rates, tracked against thresholds that would predict the final outcome.
- **C**: A commitment to measure the processing-time reduction at the first quarterly review after go-live, with no interim metric until then.
- **D**: A benefits realisation report confirming the 30% target, since the increments delivered so far implement the features the business case relied on.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

When the headline benefit is a lagging outcome that cannot be measured until after adoption, the right move is to instrument leading indicators that are observable now and are causally linked to that outcome, so the committee can steer rather than wait (B). A dresses up delivery velocity as a benefit forecast, but throughput of backlog items says nothing about whether processing time will actually fall once citizens use the service. C is honest about the lag but leaves the committee blind for the rest of the programme, which is precisely the gap they are worried about. D is the most tempting trap: shipping the features the business case named is necessary but not sufficient — the benefit is realised through adoption and changed handling times, not the mere presence of functionality, so claiming realisation now overstates what is known.
