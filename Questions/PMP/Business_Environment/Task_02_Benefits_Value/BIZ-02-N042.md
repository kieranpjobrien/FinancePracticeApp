# Question

A scrum team can start only one of two ready epics in the next two-month window. Epic X unlocks a recurring £40,000-per-month revenue stream from the day it ships and is unaffected by timing after that. Epic Y must be live before a regulator's fixed deadline that falls in exactly four months; missing it triggers a one-off £150,000 penalty, but shipping Y earlier than the deadline adds no value. Each epic is about the same size and takes roughly two months, and the estimates carry the usual uncertainty. The product owner asks the PM how to sequence them. What is the strongest recommendation?

## Options

- **A**: Start Epic X now and Epic Y second, because X forfeits £40,000 for every month it waits while Y still lands before the deadline if begun in two months, capturing both benefits.
- **B**: Start Epic Y now and Epic X second, so the compliance deadline is met with a two-month buffer that absorbs any overrun, accepting two months of forgone X revenue as the price of that protection.
- **C**: Start Epic Y now, then reassess at the sprint review whether Epic X can still be sequenced after it, rather than committing to both now.
- **D**: Ask the product owner to split Y into a minimal compliant slice plus enhancements, ship the slice first to retire the penalty risk early, then start X.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Sequencing by cost of delay favours X: each month X waits permanently forfeits £40,000, whereas Y's £150,000 value is fully preserved as long as it ships by the deadline, which it does if started second (A). Front-loading Y for a schedule buffer (B) is a genuine risk-management argument, but here it spends two months of certain, unrecoverable revenue (£80,000) to buy protection against an overrun the estimates do not indicate — a real but second-best trade. Starting Y and deferring the X decision (C) needlessly delays X's revenue and leaves value on the table while the team waits to "reassess" a sequencing question the numbers already answer. Splitting Y (D) is a sensible pattern when an early compliant slice is cheap, but it adds slicing and integration overhead to de-risk a deadline that is not actually tight, again delaying X. The fine margin is between A and B: both finish Y in time, but only A also captures the revenue that B knowingly forgoes.
