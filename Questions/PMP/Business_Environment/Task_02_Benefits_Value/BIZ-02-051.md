# Question

A predictive maintenance system for a fleet of regional trains is being handed over to the operations directorate at project closure. The expected benefit — a 20% reduction in unplanned downtime — depends on maintenance crews acting on the system's alerts and on a data-quality routine being run weekly. The operations director has accepted the system but says nothing about who will track the downtime reduction or run the routine. What should the project manager ensure happens before closing?

## Options

- **A**: Extend the project until the 20% reduction is demonstrated, so the project can be closed against an achieved benefit.
- **B**: Record the downtime target as a residual risk in the closure report and hand it to the PMO to monitor.
- **C**: Confirm a named benefits owner in operations, the measurement approach, and the ongoing routine are formally accepted as part of the transition.
- **D**: Train every maintenance crew on the alert workflow, then close, since adoption is what determines the benefit.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Benefits that realise after closure must transition to an accountable owner in the receiving organisation, together with how they will be measured and any operational routines they depend on — securing that handover is the PM's responsibility before closing (C). A keeps a delivery project open to operate a benefit, which confuses delivery with realisation and indefinitely defers closure. B reduces an ownership and measurement gap to a logged risk, which records the problem without resolving who acts on it. D is necessary but partial: training enables adoption, but without a named owner and a measurement and maintenance routine, there is no one accountable for sustaining the benefit or detecting when it slips.
