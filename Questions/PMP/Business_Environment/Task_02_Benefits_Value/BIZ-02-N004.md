# Question

A predictive infrastructure project is nearing closure. The deliverables are accepted, but most of the projected cost savings will accrue over the following two years as new equipment progressively replaces ageing assets. The operations group that will run the equipment has not been involved in the project and has its own targets. The project manager wants the savings tracked and actually pursued after the team disbands. What is the most appropriate action before closing?

## Options

- **A**: Confirm a named benefits owner in operations who accepts accountability for realising and reporting the savings, and record it with the tracking mechanism in the benefits management plan.
- **B**: Document the savings, metric, baseline and tracking method in the benefits management plan and hand the plan to the PMO to monitor against the portfolio after closure.
- **C**: Establish the measurement mechanism and a post-closure review schedule in the benefits plan, leaving operational line management to act on the reports as the savings come due.
- **D**: Brief the sponsor on the realisation timeline and forecast and have them carry the benefit into their own governance reporting once the project closes.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Benefits that mature after closure need a specific owner who accepts accountability for pursuing them, not just a record that they exist, and naming that owner in operations — the group whose actions actually deliver the savings — closes the gap between tracking a number and someone being answerable for it (A). Handing the documented plan to the PMO (B) is sound and the PMO is a legitimate monitor, but monitoring is not ownership: the PMO can report a shortfall without anyone being responsible for acting on it, and the operations group that drives the saving stays disengaged. Setting up the measurement mechanism and review schedule while leaving line management to react (C) builds the instrumentation but assigns no accountable individual, so a real risk remains that reports are produced and no one owns the outcome — especially given operations has competing targets. Briefing the sponsor to carry it in their governance (D) keeps executive visibility and is worth doing, but the sponsor oversees rather than realises the benefit; without a named realisation owner in the delivering group the saving still depends on goodwill. The fine line is A versus B/C: all record the benefit properly, but only A fixes accountability on a person positioned to make the saving happen.
