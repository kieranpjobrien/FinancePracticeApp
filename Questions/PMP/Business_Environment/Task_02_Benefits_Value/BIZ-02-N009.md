# Question

At the start of a customer-portal project, the sponsor describes the goal as "a better experience for our customers" and is keen for the team to start building while momentum is high. The organisation already runs a quarterly customer-satisfaction survey with two years of history. The project manager wants the project to be able to demonstrate value at the end. What should the project manager do first during planning?

## Options

- **A**: Work with the sponsor to translate the goal into specific measurable benefits with metrics, baselines and realisation owners, before committing the team to build.
- **B**: Adopt the existing quarterly satisfaction survey as the benefit measure and record its current score as the baseline, since a tracked metric with history is already in place.
- **C**: Define target benefits and metrics provisionally from the goal and let the first increments of real user feedback refine the baselines and targets as they emerge.
- **D**: Capture the sponsor's intent as a benefits hypothesis to be validated through delivery, starting build now and firming the measures up as the product takes shape.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A vague aspiration has to be converted into specific, measurable benefits with metrics, baselines and owners agreed with the sponsor before the team commits to building, because that agreement is what later lets the project evidence value and prevents scope drift against an undefined "better" (A). Adopting the existing survey as the measure (B) is appealing — a metric with two years of history is genuinely useful and likely belongs in the plan — but reaching for a convenient instrument before deciding what benefit the portal is actually meant to produce risks measuring the wrong thing or the wrong slice of it; the metric should follow the benefit definition, not pre-empt it. Defining everything provisionally and refining baselines from early increments (C) suits emergent product work but defers the agreed baseline that value will be judged against, and a baseline established after the portal starts changing behaviour is no longer a clean pre-state. Treating the goal as a hypothesis validated through delivery while building now (D) is a legitimate lean stance, but starting to build before any measurable benefit is agreed leaves the project with nothing concrete to demonstrate value against if the sponsor's intent and the team's output later diverge. The fine line is A versus B/C/D: all engage benefits thinking, but only A fixes the measures and the baseline with the sponsor before build, which is what makes value demonstrable.
