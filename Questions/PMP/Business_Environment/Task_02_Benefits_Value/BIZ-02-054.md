# Question

A retail bank's agile team is two-thirds through a programme to rebuild its mortgage origination workflow. A regulatory deadline forces an unexpected, mandatory feature into scope that will consume the next three sprints. The product owner must now drop something to stay within the release window. The candidate items to drop are all "in progress" or "ready," and each has a roughly similar development cost. On what basis should the product owner decide what to drop?

## Options

- **A**: Drop the item that is least complete, to waste the least amount of work already invested.
- **B**: Drop the most recently added item, since it has had the least stakeholder commitment behind it.
- **C**: Drop the item the development team finds least interesting to build, to protect morale on the mandatory work.
- **D**: Drop the item with the lowest expected contribution to the product's value, regardless of how much work it has had.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

When something must be cut, the decision should protect value: drop the item whose expected contribution to outcomes is lowest, because that sacrifices the least benefit (D). A optimises for sunk work rather than value — the effort already spent on a high-value item is irrelevant to whether keeping it is worthwhile, and abandoning it would forfeit the larger benefit. B uses recency as a proxy for importance, but how lately an item was added says nothing about how much value it delivers; a newly identified high-value item could easily outrank older ones. C substitutes team preference for value, which has no bearing on what the product is for.
