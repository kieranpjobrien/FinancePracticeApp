# Question

A hybrid project to launch an online permit-application service for a planning authority faces a hard statutory deadline in five weeks. The team will not finish all planned functionality in time. Analysis shows that a reduced release covering the three highest-volume permit types would handle about 80% of applications and deliver most of the intended public-service benefit, with the remaining permit types following in a later release. The sponsor is reluctant to release anything less than the full service. What should the PM recommend to best protect value?

## Options

- **A**: Release the reduced scope covering the three highest-volume permit types by the deadline, capturing the bulk of the benefit on time, and schedule the remainder for a follow-on release.
- **B**: Request an extension to the statutory deadline so the full service can launch complete, preserving the original scope.
- **C**: Hold the launch until the full service is ready and accept missing the statutory deadline, since a partial service risks confusing applicants.
- **D**: Compress the remaining work by adding contractors so the full scope is delivered within the five weeks despite the late stage.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

When a fixed deadline cannot accommodate full scope, protecting value means releasing the slice that delivers the most benefit on time — here the three highest-volume permit types cover 80% of applications, so a reduced launch captures most of the public-service benefit by the statutory date while the remainder follows (A). B assumes a statutory deadline is negotiable; such deadlines are typically immovable, and betting the launch on an extension that may not be granted risks delivering nothing on time. C forfeits the entire on-time benefit to avoid a manageable communications issue, missing a legal deadline for the sake of completeness. D bolts on contractors at week five to force full scope, which late-stage adds onboarding drag and quality risk for the lower-value permit types and is unlikely to recover the timeline without jeopardising the high-value core. The fine margin is A versus D: both aim to hit the deadline, but A guarantees the high-value 80% on time and defers the rest cleanly, whereas D gambles the whole release on absorbing late resources to chase the last 20%.
