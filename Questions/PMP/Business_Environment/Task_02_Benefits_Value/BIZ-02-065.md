# Question

An agile delivery group has one team and a backlog spanning two products. Product P generates steady, predictable value of about £40,000 of benefit per sprint of effort. Product Q is unproven but, if a key hypothesis holds, could generate roughly £150,000 of benefit per sprint — though there is a real chance it returns almost nothing. The product council must decide how to point the team for the coming quarter. Which consideration should most shape the decision?

## Options

- **A**: Direct all capacity to Product P, because its value is certain and certainty should be preferred over a gamble.
- **B**: Weigh the opportunity cost of each option, including running small experiments on Q to cheaply test the hypothesis before committing major capacity.
- **C**: Direct all capacity to Product Q, because its potential upside per sprint is far larger than P's.
- **D**: Split the team permanently in half across both products so neither value stream is starved.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

With a single team and competing claims on its capacity, every choice carries an opportunity cost, and the disciplined response to a high-upside but uncertain bet is to buy information cheaply first — small experiments to test Q's hypothesis before committing major capacity (B). A defaults to certainty and forgoes the chance to validate a potentially far larger benefit, which may leave significant value on the table. C chases raw upside while ignoring the real probability that Q returns almost nothing, betting the quarter on an untested hypothesis. D permanently halves a single team, which fragments focus and flow, reduces each stream's throughput, and is a structural commitment made before the value question has even been tested.
