# Question

A hybrid project to build a direct-to-consumer sales channel for a food producer is halfway through. Early released features show that customers love browsing and saving products but almost never complete a purchase online — they phone the call centre instead. The remaining backlog is heavily weighted toward checkout and payment features. The data suggests the real value lies in lead generation feeding the call centre, not online conversion. The project manager convenes the sponsor and product owner. What is the most appropriate course of action?

## Options

- **A**: Complete the remaining checkout and payment backlog as planned, since the business case approved an online sales channel and scope should be protected.
- **B**: Present the adoption evidence and propose re-validating the business case, potentially re-prioritising the backlog toward the value the data reveals.
- **C**: Pause the project and commission a separate market-research study before changing anything, to confirm the behaviour is not temporary.
- **D**: Quietly re-order the backlog toward lead-generation features and report progress against the original plan to avoid alarming governance.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Empirical adoption evidence that the value lies somewhere other than the original plan assumed is precisely the trigger to bring the data to the decision-makers and re-validate the business case, adapting the backlog to chase real value while runway remains (B). A protects scope for its own sake and presses on building checkout features the evidence says customers will not use, delivering output but little benefit. C defers action behind a fresh study when the project already has live behavioural data; commissioning research to confirm what the released increments are showing wastes time and runway. D makes arguably the right product move but bypasses governance and misrepresents the plan, which destroys the transparency that benefit and scope decisions depend on and exposes the project if the pivot is questioned.
