# Question

On a hybrid government-services programme, the operations director wants reduced processing cost, the policy lead wants improved citizen accessibility, and the digital lead wants legacy-system risk reduced. The next increment has capacity for only one major theme, and each stakeholder insists their outcome is the priority. The programme has a published set of value objectives and a benefits plan, but the three outcomes map to different objectives and the plan does not rank them against each other. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate a session that scores the three themes against the programme's value objectives and benefits plan, surfacing where the plan is silent and seeking agreement on the ranking from that evidence.
- **B**: Take the three themes and a recommended ranking to the programme's governance board, since deciding between objectives the benefits plan does not rank is a sponsor-level value judgement.
- **C**: Sequence the increment by cost of delay, prioritising the theme whose value erodes fastest if deferred so the most time-sensitive outcome is protected first.
- **D**: Prioritise the legacy-system risk reduction, since unaddressed technical risk could undermine the cost and accessibility outcomes the other two stakeholders want.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Competing stakeholder priorities are resolved by evaluating them against the programme's own value objectives rather than by who argues hardest, and because the benefits plan does not yet rank these three, the first move is to facilitate that scoring with the stakeholders, expose the gap in the plan, and build an evidence-based ranking they can accept (A). Escalating to the governance board with a recommendation (B) is legitimate — an unranked clash between objectives may ultimately need a sponsor decision — but escalating before the project manager has run the prioritisation analysis hands up a problem that facilitation could resolve and arrives without the evidence the board would need anyway. Sequencing by cost of delay (C) is a sound economic technique and may well be part of the scoring, but adopting it as the sole criterion pre-selects time-sensitivity as the deciding factor over the programme's broader value objectives, which include outcomes whose worth is not primarily about decay rate. Prioritising risk reduction on a dependency argument (D) is a plausible substantive case and might survive the analysis, but asserting it first substitutes the project manager's own value judgement for the structured comparison the situation calls for, and the dependency claim is itself something the scoring should test rather than assume. The fine line is A versus B: both treat the conflict as a value question, but A applies the prioritisation tools the project manager owns before deciding escalation is needed.
