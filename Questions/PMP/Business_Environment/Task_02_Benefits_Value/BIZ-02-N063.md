# Question

Within a programme to modernise a bank's lending operations, the PM is running a project that delivers a new credit-decisioning engine. The engine's forecast benefit — faster loan approvals — depends on a separate project, run by another team, retraining loan officers to use it; without that training the officers will keep using manual workarounds. The engine project is on track, but the training project has slipped badly and may not finish until months after the engine goes live. How should the PM treat this in managing the project's value?

## Options

- **A**: Surface the dependency to programme governance, making clear that the engine's benefit will not be realised until the dependent training completes, so the realisation timeline and any mitigation are managed across both projects.
- **B**: Proceed to deliver the engine on schedule and record the approval-speed benefit as realised at go-live, since the engine itself meets its commitments.
- **C**: Absorb the training into the engine project's own scope so the PM can control it directly and protect the benefit timeline.
- **D**: Hold the engine's go-live back to align exactly with the training project's completion, so both land together.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The engine is an enabler whose benefit is contingent on a dependency outside the project's control, so the right move is to make that benefit dependency visible to programme governance, which owns the cross-project view and can sequence or mitigate accordingly (A). B claims the benefit as realised at go-live when the workarounds mean approvals will not actually speed up, recording value that has not occurred. C grabs another team's scope to force control, which oversteps the project boundary, may duplicate or conflict with the other team's work, and is a programme-level coordination decision rather than the PM's to seize unilaterally. D unilaterally delays a project that is meeting its commitments to match a slipping one, potentially wasting a ready capability and a decision that again belongs at programme level once the dependency is surfaced. The fine margin is A versus D: both recognise the benefit is gated by the training, but only A routes the timing decision to the governance level that can weigh both projects, rather than the engine PM imposing a delay alone.
