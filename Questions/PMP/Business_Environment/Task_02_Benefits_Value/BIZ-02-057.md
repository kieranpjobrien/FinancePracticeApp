# Question

A predictive project to consolidate three regional data centres into one promises annual savings of £2.1m once consolidation completes. The benefits will accrue over the three years after go-live. The sponsor asks the project manager to set up tracking so the organisation can tell whether the savings are actually materialising and act if they are not. Which actions should the project manager build into the benefits tracking approach? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Define the baseline cost of the three existing data centres so realised savings can be measured against it.
- **B**: Schedule periodic benefit reviews after go-live with the accountable benefits owner, comparing actual savings to the plan.
- **C**: Establish thresholds that, if breached, trigger investigation and corrective action rather than passive reporting.
- **D**: Hold the project team intact for the full three years to operate the consolidated centre and guarantee the savings.
- **E**: Treat the £2.1m as realised at go-live, since that is when the consolidated centre becomes operational.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Effective benefits tracking needs a baseline to measure against (A), periodic reviews with the accountable owner to compare actual against planned realisation (B), and thresholds that turn the tracking into action when reality diverges (C) — together these let the organisation steer, not just observe. D keeps a delivery team running operations for years, conflating delivery with realisation and tying up resources a closed project should release; operating the centre is an operational responsibility, not the project's. E claims realisation at the moment of go-live, but the savings accrue over the following three years through actual operating-cost reductions, so booking them up front would record a benefit that has not yet occurred.
