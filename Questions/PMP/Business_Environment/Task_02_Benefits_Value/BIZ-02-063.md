# Question

A project manager is assigned to a newly approved initiative to roll out electronic prescribing across a hospital network. Reading the charter, the PM finds it lists deliverables and a budget but says nothing about the expected benefits or how success will be judged. Before planning begins, what is the most important thing for the PM to clarify with the sponsor?

## Options

- **A**: The expected benefits the initiative is meant to deliver and the measures by which they will be assessed.
- **B**: The detailed schedule and resource assignments for the first phase.
- **C**: The procurement route for the electronic prescribing software.
- **D**: The change-control procedure to be used once execution starts.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A project exists to deliver benefits, and without an explicit statement of the expected benefits and how they will be measured, the PM cannot make sound trade-offs or judge success — clarifying this with the sponsor is the priority before planning (A). B, C, and D are all legitimate planning concerns, but each presupposes a clear understanding of what value the project is meant to create: scheduling, procurement, and change control are means of delivering benefits efficiently, not substitutes for knowing what those benefits are. Settling the why before the how keeps the subsequent planning aligned to value.
