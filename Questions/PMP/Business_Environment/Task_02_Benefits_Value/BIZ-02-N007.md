# Question

A predictive product-development project has spent 70% of its budget. A revised market analysis shows the product will, at best, break even: its projected lifetime benefits now barely exceed the remaining cost to complete and fall well short of the original business case. A smaller competing initiative is queued and would return more on the same remaining funds. The sponsor asks for the project manager's recommendation at the go/no-go gate. What should the project manager base it on?

## Options

- **A**: Whether the remaining funds would return more if redirected to the queued initiative than if spent finishing this product, comparing both forward of the money already committed.
- **B**: Whether the benefits still achievable on this product exceed the cost still to be spent on it, since a positive forward return justifies completing what was started.
- **C**: Whether the revised analysis is robust enough to overturn an approved business case, since acting on a single new forecast could prematurely kill a viable product.
- **D**: Whether finishing protects benefits already partly delivered and relationships built, so the 70% invested converts to a usable outcome rather than a write-off.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A go/no-go decision allocates the remaining funds to their best forward use, and with a queued initiative that would return more on the same money, the recommendation turns on comparing the two forward returns — not merely on whether this product clears its own remaining cost — because money spent finishing a barely-break-even product is money not earning the higher return elsewhere (A). Testing whether this product's remaining benefits beat its remaining cost (B) is the right sunk-cost logic and a strong, tempting answer — it correctly ignores the 70% spent — but a marginal positive forward return is not sufficient when a better-returning alternative competes for the same funds; clearing its own bar is necessary, not decisive. Questioning whether one revised analysis should overturn an approved case (C) is legitimate due diligence and worth a sanity check, but the decision still has to be made on the best information available, and treating the approved figures as protected because they were signed off edges back toward honouring a now-overtaken baseline. Framing the choice around converting the 70% into a usable outcome and protecting relationships (D) is the sunk-cost fallacy in its most plausible dress — partly delivered benefit and built relationships are real, but spend already made cannot justify further spend; only future return can. The fine line is A versus B: both reason forward and ignore sunk cost, but A weighs the remaining funds against their best alternative use while B asks only whether this product pays its own way.
