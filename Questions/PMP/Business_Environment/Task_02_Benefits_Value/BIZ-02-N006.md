# Question

A bank released a new mobile payments feature two sprints ago, targeting increased transaction volume. Volume has not moved. Daily active users of the feature and completed first-time setups are both climbing steadily, but the share of users who finish a payment after starting one is flat and low. The sponsor asks whether the benefit is failing and whether to keep funding the next sprints. How should the project manager frame the response?

## Options

- **A**: Read the climbing adoption metrics as leading signals that normally precede volume, flag the flat payment-completion rate as the one indicator not yet tracking, and propose a checkpoint before changing funding.
- **B**: Report that rising active users and setups are leading indicators of the volume benefit and recommend staying the course, since adoption typically converts to volume once the base is large enough.
- **C**: Treat the flat completion rate as the signal that matters and recommend the next sprint investigate and fix the drop-off, since growing users who do not complete payments will not move volume.
- **D**: Advise that two sprints is too little signal to judge either way and recommend continuing unchanged until a fuller data window lets the leading indicators be interpreted reliably.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Leading indicators are useful only when read together and against the outcome they are meant to predict, and here the honest read is mixed: active users and setups are rising as expected, but completion — the conversion step that actually turns adoption into transaction volume — is flat, so the right framing presents both and proposes a checkpoint rather than a verdict or a funding change (A). Reporting only the favourable indicators and recommending stay-the-course (B) is the seductive half-truth: it correctly names active users and setups as leading signals but ignores the flat completion rate that breaks the chain to volume, telling the sponsor a cleaner story than the data supports. Pivoting straight to fixing the completion drop-off (C) identifies the real weak signal and may well be the eventual action, but jumping to a fix in the next sprint pre-empts diagnosis and treats one flat metric as decisive before the checkpoint has weighed the full picture. Calling two sprints too little to judge and continuing unchanged (D) is defensible caution, but it sets aside data that is already informative — the divergence between rising setups and flat completion is itself a finding — and defaults to no action where a checkpoint would extract more. The fine line is A versus B: both recognise the adoption signals, but A also surfaces the completion gap that B's optimistic framing leaves out.
