# Question

A project delivered a new automated invoice-matching system to a logistics firm's finance team, and at closure the targeted benefit — a 60% reduction in manual matching effort — was being achieved. Eight months later, the benefits owner reports the effort has crept back up almost to its original level. Investigation shows staff have reverted to manual checks because no one maintained the matching rules as supplier formats changed. What does this situation most clearly indicate was missing?

## Options

- **A**: The project's deliverable was defective and should have been built to handle any future supplier format automatically.
- **B**: Adequate user training at go-live, which would have prevented staff from reverting.
- **C**: A longer project warranty period during which the project team would continue to support the system.
- **D**: A sustainment plan defining who would maintain the solution and the conditions for the benefit to persist after handover.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

A benefit that is achieved at closure but decays because no one maintained the solution points to a missing sustainment plan — an explicit definition of who keeps the solution current and what conditions must hold for the benefit to persist after handover (D). A blames the deliverable for not being future-proof, but no system anticipates every future supplier format indefinitely; ongoing maintenance, not omniscient design, is what sustains it. B identifies a plausible contributor, yet the evidence is that staff reverted because the rules went stale, not because they never knew how to use the system. C would merely have the project team patch the gap temporarily; warranty support substitutes the project for a proper operational owner and ends when the warranty does, leaving the same sustainment hole.
