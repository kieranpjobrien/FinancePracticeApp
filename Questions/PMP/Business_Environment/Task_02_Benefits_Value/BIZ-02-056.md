# Question

At the end of each sprint, an agile team building an e-learning platform for a university holds a review. Recently the reviews have become a status briefing where the team narrates what it built, and busy faculty stakeholders have started skipping them. The project manager wants to restore the review's value. What change will most directly help?

## Options

- **A**: Refocus the review on demonstrating working increments and eliciting stakeholder feedback on whether they deliver the intended value.
- **B**: Make attendance mandatory for all faculty stakeholders so feedback is guaranteed.
- **C**: Replace the live review with a written summary circulated after each sprint to respect stakeholders' time.
- **D**: Lengthen the review so the team can explain its technical decisions in more depth.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A sprint review exists to inspect a working increment with stakeholders and adapt based on whether it delivers value, not to report status — refocusing it on demonstration and feedback restores exactly that purpose and gives faculty a reason to attend (A). B compels attendance without fixing why the meeting stopped being worth attending, which tends to produce resentful, disengaged presence rather than useful feedback. C removes the live inspect-and-adapt loop entirely; a one-way written summary cannot surface the dialogue that steers value. D adds more of what drove stakeholders away — internal technical narration — when the problem is the absence of a value conversation, not insufficient explanation.
