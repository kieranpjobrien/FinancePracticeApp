# Question

A PMO is consolidating projected benefits across the portfolio for the board next week. The project manager notices their workflow-automation project and a separate org-redesign project both claim the same headcount saving, in the same department, in the same year. Each business case was approved independently and each still relies on that saving to clear its own justification threshold. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Raise the overlap with the PMO so the portfolio consolidation attributes the saving once and the board total is not overstated, leaving how it is apportioned to the cross-project view the PMO holds.
- **B**: Approach the other project manager directly to agree between them how the shared saving should be attributed, then submit a single reconciled figure to the PMO.
- **C**: Flag the overlap but argue the saving should stay with the workflow-automation project, since its automation is what physically removes the roles the headcount reduction depends on.
- **D**: Report the duplication to the project sponsor and the steering committee as a benefits risk, since a double-count touching two approved business cases is a governance issue above the PMO's remit.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A benefit counted twice inflates the portfolio total and would mislead the board, so it must be surfaced to the PMO — the body holding the cross-project view — to attribute the saving once, with the apportionment decided at that portfolio level rather than by either project (A). Settling it directly with the other project manager (B) is collaborative and not wrong in spirit, but two project managers each depend on the saving to justify their own case, so a deal struck between them is conflicted and bypasses the PMO that actually owns consolidation; the reconciled figure should come from the portfolio view, not a side agreement. Flagging it but arguing the saving belongs to one's own project (C) correctly raises the issue yet advocates for the outcome that protects one's own business case, which is exactly the bias the PMO-level attribution exists to neutralise — surfacing honestly means not steering the answer. Escalating to the sponsor and steering committee as a governance risk (D) may eventually be warranted if the attribution cannot be resolved or a business case collapses, but reaching past the PMO first over-escalates a duplication that the consolidation process is designed to catch and correct routinely. The fine line is A versus B: both seek single attribution, but A routes it through the impartial portfolio owner while B negotiates it between two conflicted parties.
