# Question

A project to launch a loyalty mobile app for a coffee-shop chain is being assessed for value six weeks after launch. The marketing team is celebrating 500,000 downloads. The project's business case, however, justified the investment on the basis of increased repeat-visit frequency and higher average spend per member. The sponsor asks the project manager whether the project is delivering value. How should the PM respond?

## Options

- **A**: Yes, because download volume far exceeds the launch target and demonstrates strong market reception.
- **B**: No, because a loyalty app that only drives downloads without immediate revenue has failed to deliver value.
- **C**: Value cannot yet be confirmed from downloads alone; it must be judged against repeat-visit frequency and average spend per member, the outcomes the business case relied on.
- **D**: Yes, provided the download trend continues, since a large member base will eventually produce the spending benefits.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Value must be measured against the outcomes the business case used to justify the investment — here repeat-visit frequency and spend per member — and downloads are an activity count that does not by itself evidence those outcomes (C). A treats a vanity metric as proof of value; downloads measure acquisition, not the behavioural change the project was funded to produce. B overcorrects to a negative verdict prematurely, declaring failure before the relevant outcome metrics have had time to be measured. D assumes a large base will automatically convert to the target behaviours, but that causal step is exactly what the business-case metrics exist to test rather than presume.
