# Question

A council is delivering an automated parking-enforcement system forecast to raise £600,000 a year in additional penalty revenue and cut enforcement-officer costs. During a pre-launch review, the PM's analysis indicates the system will also generate roughly 1,400 extra appeals a year, requiring more case-handling staff, and local-business representatives are warning of reputational harm that could reduce town-centre footfall. The sponsor regards these as implementation details to manage later and wants the benefit recorded at the full £600,000. How should the PM handle the benefit case before launch?

## Options

- **A**: Record the £600,000 as the project benefit and log the extra appeals and footfall concern separately on the risk register to be managed during operation.
- **B**: Quantify the additional appeals-handling cost and the expected footfall disbenefit, and present the net value alongside the gross £600,000 so governance decides on the true position.
- **C**: Reduce the recorded benefit to the cost saving from fewer enforcement officers only, excluding the penalty revenue, since the revenue is offset by the disbenefits.
- **D**: Recommend deferring launch until the appeals process and business concerns are fully resolved, so the project delivers only clean benefit.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A true value case nets the project's disbenefits and consequential costs against its gross benefit, so the foreseeable appeals-handling cost and footfall disbenefit must be quantified and set against the £600,000 for governance to see the real position (B). A books the gross figure and shunts the offsetting effects to a risk log, which overstates the benefit and hides costs that are reasonably foreseeable rather than uncertain risks. C swings too far the other way, discarding the penalty revenue entirely instead of netting the disbenefits against it — that understates value just as A overstates it. D treats any disbenefit as a reason to delay indefinitely, but value cases routinely carry net-positive trade-offs; stopping until every concern is "resolved" can forfeit a benefit that remains worthwhile after netting. The fine margin is B versus C: both recognise the disbenefits matter, but only B keeps the revenue in and subtracts the costs to show net value, whereas C deletes a real benefit to compensate.
