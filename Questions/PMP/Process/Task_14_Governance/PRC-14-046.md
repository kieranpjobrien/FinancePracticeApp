# Question

A project manager is establishing the governance structure for a new programme to build a national electric-vehicle charging network spanning three states, multiple contractors, and a government funding body. The programme will run for four years using a hybrid approach. Which elements should the project manager ensure the governance framework itself explicitly defines? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: The decision rights and authority thresholds for each governing role and body, including what the project manager can decide alone.
- **B**: The escalation paths and the conditions (such as tolerance breaches) that trigger escalation to each level.
- **C**: The agreed acceptance criteria for the charging-station hardware deliverables across the programme.
- **D**: The cadence, membership, and remit of the governing bodies, such as a steering committee and any sub-boards.
- **E**: The detailed resource-loaded schedule and cost baseline the programme will be measured against.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

A governance framework defines how authority and oversight are exercised: who can decide what (A — decision rights and thresholds), when and how matters move up to a higher body (B — escalation paths and their triggers), and the bodies that exercise oversight along with their cadence, membership, and remit (D). Together these answer "who decides, within what limits, and how disputes rise." (C) and (E) are both legitimate, important programme artefacts — and governance will say *who approves them and within what tolerances* — but the acceptance criteria for hardware and the resource-loaded baseline are products of scope, quality, schedule, and cost planning, not elements of the governance framework itself. The distinction the question tests is between defining the authority-and-oversight structure (governance) and defining the work and the deliverables that structure governs.
