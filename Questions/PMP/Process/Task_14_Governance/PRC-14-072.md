# Question

An agile team delivering a mobile banking feature is missing sprint goals, and a retrospective surfaces that governance overhead is part of the cause. Which of the following are genuine signs that the governance arrangements are too heavy for this project and should be tailored down? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Routine decisions that sit well within the team's competence still require sign-off from a committee that meets only fortnightly, stalling work for days.
- **B**: The team must record decisions in a decision log that the sponsor reviews monthly.
- **C**: Every minor backlog re-ordering triggers a formal change request and an approval cycle before the team may act on it.
- **D**: The steering committee reviews the project against tolerance thresholds at each release.
- **E**: The project manager escalates only those issues that breach an agreed threshold, leaving the rest to the team.

## Answer

A, C

## Explanation

Governance should be proportionate; it is too heavy when it inserts approval delay into decisions the team is competent to make itself. Requiring committee sign-off for routine within-competence decisions (A) and forcing a formal change cycle for trivial backlog re-ordering (C) both impose oversight where delegated team authority would be faster and equally safe — classic over-governance. The other options describe healthy, proportionate governance: a periodically reviewed decision log (B) is lightweight transparency; tolerance-based steering reviews at releases (D) check the project at sensible intervals without micromanaging; and escalating only threshold breaches (E) is exactly the targeted escalation discipline good governance prescribes. Tailoring down means removing A and C, not the legitimate oversight in B, D and E.
