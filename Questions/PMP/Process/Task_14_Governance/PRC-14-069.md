# Question

A hybrid project to deploy a hospital's new electronic records system reports into two governance bodies: a clinical safety board that must approve anything affecting patient care, and an IT change advisory board that must approve anything touching the production network. A planned cutover affects both. The two boards have issued conflicting instructions — clinical safety wants the cutover during a weekday for full staffing, IT change wants it during a weekend maintenance window. Each board insists its remit takes precedence. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Follow the IT change advisory board's instruction, because the cutover is fundamentally a technical network event.
- **B**: Follow the clinical safety board's instruction, because patient safety always outranks technical scheduling preferences.
- **C**: Escalate the conflict to the sponsor or steering committee that sits above both boards, since no governance mechanism currently arbitrates between two bodies with overlapping authority.
- **D**: Convene a joint meeting of both boards and facilitate until they reach consensus, taking no decision to a higher authority.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The situation exposes a structural gap: two bodies hold legitimate but overlapping authority and the governance design provides no defined arbiter between them. The correct move is to escalate to the body that sits above both and can adjudicate the competing mandates (C). (A) and (B) each unilaterally privilege one board's remit, which the project manager has no authority to do and which would override a legitimate governance decision by the other body. (D) is a reasonable first attempt at facilitation, but the scenario states each board insists its remit takes precedence, so consensus is unlikely; persisting without escalation leaves a safety-and-network-critical decision deadlocked, when a higher authority exists precisely to break such ties.
