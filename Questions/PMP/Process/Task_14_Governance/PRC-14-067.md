# Question

On a predictive construction project to build a sports stadium, a project manager needs a decision that exceeds their delegated authority: a major design change that will increase cost beyond the approved budget. The governance structure includes an individual sponsor who champions the project and owns the business case, and a steering committee that includes the sponsor plus senior representatives from finance, operations, and the end-user organisation. To whom should the project manager take a budget-increasing change of this size?

## Options

- **A**: The finance representative on the committee, because the change is fundamentally a money decision.
- **B**: The sponsor alone, because the sponsor owns the business case and can therefore approve any change that affects it.
- **C**: The steering committee, because a change that exceeds the approved budget affects multiple stakeholder interests and requires the collective authority the committee holds.
- **D**: The end-user organisation's representative, because they will ultimately operate the stadium and bear the consequences of the design.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

A budget-increasing change of this magnitude touches the interests of finance, operations and end users simultaneously, which is exactly why governance vests such decisions in the steering committee as a collective body rather than any single member. (C) routes the decision to where the authority actually sits. (B) overstates the sponsor's individual remit; the sponsor owns and champions the business case but a material budget increase is normally a committee-level decision, not a solo one. (A) and (D) each elevate one functional perspective above the others, but no single representative holds authority to commit the organisation to a budget increase — that authority is shared and exercised through the committee.
