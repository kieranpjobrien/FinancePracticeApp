# Question

On a predictive satellite ground-station build, the executive sponsor issues the project manager a written directive to accelerate a milestone by reallocating contingency. A week later the steering committee — which under the governance charter must approve any use of contingency above a set threshold — formally resolves that contingency be preserved. The amount at stake is above the threshold, so the two instructions now conflict. The acceleration the sponsor wants would help recover a slipping schedule. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Treat the steering committee's resolution as governing because the charter assigns above-threshold contingency decisions to it, hold the contingency, and surface the conflict to the sponsor for reconciliation.
- **B**: Carry out the sponsor's directive, since the sponsor's written instruction is the more recent expression of intent from the project's most senior individual and the schedule is slipping.
- **C**: Take the sponsor's directive back to the steering committee, ask it to reconsider in light of the schedule slippage, and act only on whatever the committee then resolves.
- **D**: Hold all action and escalate the contradiction to the sponsor and committee jointly, asking them to agree a single instruction before the project manager moves either way.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Conflicting instructions are resolved by who holds the relevant decision right, not by seniority or recency. The charter reserves above-threshold contingency decisions to the steering committee, so its resolution governs now; the project manager preserves the contingency and raises the conflict with the sponsor so it can be reconciled at the right level. (B) is the classic trap — a recent written directive from the most senior person feels authoritative — but it elevates individual seniority over a decision right the charter has expressly assigned to the committee. (C) respects the committee's authority and is a reasonable downstream step, yet as the immediate action it leaves the project in limbo and, more importantly, re-opens a decision the committee has just formally made rather than first honouring the standing resolution while the sponsor's concern is taken up. (D) sounds balanced but stalls a matter the charter has already settled — the committee's resolution is the current valid decision, so there is no need to freeze everything pending a joint instruction; the project manager simply follows the resolution and routes the conflict for reconciliation.
