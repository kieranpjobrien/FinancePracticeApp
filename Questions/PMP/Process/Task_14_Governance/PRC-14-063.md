# Question

A project's governance framework grants the project manager delegated authority to approve non-scope expenditures up to $50,000, with anything above routed to the project steering committee. The project manager identifies a $48,000 tooling purchase that would retire a current delivery risk. However, the same tooling would commit the organisation to a vendor and licensing model that other projects in the portfolio would be expected to follow. What is the best action for the project manager to take?

## Options

- **A**: Approve the purchase under the delegated financial authority and record the rationale, the retired risk, and the portfolio implication in the decision log for later review by the steering committee.
- **B**: Bring the decision to the steering committee as a governance matter, because the commitment establishes a portfolio-wide standard that sits outside the intent of a per-project spending threshold.
- **C**: Approve the purchase under delegated authority and notify the sponsor of the decision, the reasoning, and the precedent it sets for other projects.
- **D**: Defer the purchase until the next scheduled steering committee meeting and accept the interim risk, since a commitment with portfolio-wide effect should not be made unilaterally.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A delegated spending threshold governs the *financial size* of a decision, not its *organisational reach*. The $48,000 sits within the financial limit, but the decision commits the wider portfolio to a vendor and licensing standard — a decision right that the governance framework vests in the steering committee, not the project manager. (B) routes the decision to the body that actually owns portfolio-level commitments, which is the correct read of what the threshold is and is not authorised to cover.

(A) and (C) both treat the matter as purely financial and absorb it under delegated authority; logging or notifying after the fact does not transfer a decision right the project manager was never delegated, and (C)'s "notify the sponsor" still bypasses the committee the framework names for above-authority matters. (D) correctly recognises the decision is not the project manager's to make alone, but waiting for the next scheduled meeting needlessly carries a known risk when the project manager could raise the matter to the committee out of cycle — making (B) the better governance move than passively deferring.
