# Question

A hybrid project has delivered a new customer self-service portal for an insurer on time and within budget. The business case promised a 20 percent reduction in call-centre volume within six months of launch, but realising that benefit depends on operational changes in the call centre that will continue long after the project closes. As the project moves toward closure, who should the governance structure hold accountable for actually realising the promised benefit?

## Options

- **A**: The project team, because they built the portal and best understand how it should be used to reduce call volume.
- **B**: The project manager, because the project manager committed to the benefit in the project charter and must see it through.
- **C**: The sponsor or business owner, because benefits realisation extends beyond delivery and depends on operational change the project itself does not control.
- **D**: The steering committee, which should remain convened indefinitely until the 20 percent reduction is measured and confirmed.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

A project typically delivers the capability that enables a benefit, but the benefit itself is realised through sustained operational use and change that outlive the project. Governance therefore assigns ongoing benefits accountability to the sponsor or business owner, who owns the business case and the operational area (C). (B) misplaces accountability on the project manager, whose remit ends at delivering the enabling output and who does not control the call centre's operations. (A) confuses building the tool with running the operation that produces value. (D) would keep a project-level body alive far beyond its purpose; benefits tracking after closure is an operational or portfolio responsibility, not a reason to leave a steering committee standing indefinitely.
