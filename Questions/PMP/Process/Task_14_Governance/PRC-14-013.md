# Question

An agile team has matured to the point where it can deploy any accepted increment to production on demand, and the product owner accepts increments continuously. The organisation's existing governance still requires a monthly release-approval board to authorise every production deployment, which now sits idle most of the month and then rubber-stamps a queue of increments. The board chair values the control and resists change. The project manager believes the governance no longer fits the delivery capability. What is the best action?

## Options

- **A**: Keep deploying through the monthly board to preserve the established control, since the board chair is accountable and prefers it retained.
- **B**: Bypass the board and deploy on demand, informing the board afterwards of what was released so it retains visibility.
- **C**: Reduce the board's cadence from monthly to weekly so approvals keep closer pace with the team's deployment capability.
- **D**: Propose to the governance owner that the board's per-release approval be replaced by agreed release criteria the team applies continuously, with the board moving to periodic assurance that those criteria are being met.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Governance should match delivery capability: when a team can release safely on demand, a per-release approval board becomes a queue-and-rubber-stamp ritual that adds latency without adding real control. The disciplined move is to take this to the governance owner and propose replacing per-release approval with codified release criteria the team applies every time, repositioning the board to periodic assurance that the criteria hold (D) — this preserves genuine control while removing the bottleneck. Retaining the board unchanged because the chair prefers it (A) lets personal preference override a governance design that no longer fits. Bypassing the board unilaterally (B) is an unauthorised change to the governance structure, however well-intentioned, and undermines the project manager's standing. Tightening the cadence to weekly (C) shrinks the delay but keeps the flawed per-release gate, so it palliates rather than resolves the mismatch.
