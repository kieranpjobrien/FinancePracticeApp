# Question

On a hybrid construction-technology programme, four governance situations arise in the same week. For each situation, match it to the role or body that should primarily handle it under a standard governance structure.

## Items

- **1**: The benefits the programme was funded to deliver are now in doubt, and someone must decide whether the investment case still holds and answer to the organisation for it.
- **2**: A dispute has arisen that sits beyond the project manager's delegated authority and needs collective senior judgement spanning several functions to resolve.
- **3**: A proposed modification would move the agreed scope, schedule, and cost baselines, and requires formal authorisation before it can be actioned.
- **4**: A run of routine cost and schedule variances, all inside the agreed tolerances, needs handling without troubling any oversight body.

## Matches

- **A**: Project manager
- **B**: Executive sponsor
- **C**: Change control board
- **D**: Steering committee

## Answer

1-B, 2-D, 3-C, 4-A

## Explanation

Each situation points to the role whose accountability it engages. A threat to the funded benefits and the investment case is the executive sponsor's to own (1-B) — the sponsor carries the business case and answers for value. A cross-functional dispute beyond the project manager's authority is what the steering committee exists to adjudicate collectively (2-D). A change that shifts the scope, schedule, or cost baselines must be formally authorised by the change control board (3-C). Routine variances within tolerance are precisely the band of delegated authority the project manager manages alone (4-A). The trap pairings are tempting: a steering committee can feel like the natural home for the benefits question (1), but ownership of the business case is the sponsor's singular accountability, not a committee vote; and the sponsor can feel like the right approver for a baseline change (3), but the structure constitutes the change control board for exactly that authorisation.
