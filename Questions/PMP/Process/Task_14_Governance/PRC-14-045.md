# Question

A hybrid project to roll out electronic health records across a hospital network has documented its decision-making responsibilities during planning. A data migration decision has now surfaced that will affect clinical safety, and three stakeholders each press to make the call: the clinical lead on safety grounds, the IT director on technical grounds, and the project sponsor on seniority grounds. The project manager wants to settle who decides without appearing to take sides. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the three stakeholders together and facilitate a joint decision they can all own.
- **B**: Check the project's documented decision responsibilities to identify the single party accountable for this decision and route it to that role.
- **C**: Refer the disagreement to the steering committee, on the basis that three senior stakeholders are at an impasse.
- **D**: Have the sponsor make the call, since the sponsor is the most senior of the three and the matter affects clinical safety.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Governance assigns accountability during planning precisely so that disputes about "who decides" are settled by reference to the agreed structure rather than re-litigated each time by consensus, escalation, or rank. The project's documented responsibilities already name one accountable party for a decision of this kind; checking them and routing the decision there is the neutral, structure-based resolution the project manager is after — and it is the first move because it may make the others unnecessary. (A) is a genuine collaborative instinct, but it converts a defined decision right into a shared one; the other two may be consulted, yet accountability rests with a single role the structure has named. (C) escalates before consulting the structure that already answers the question — escalation is for when the documentation is silent or the accountable party genuinely cannot decide. (D) equates seniority with accountability, but the accountable party is fixed by the documented assignment for this decision, not by who outranks whom, and the sponsor may well be a consulted party here rather than the decision-maker.
