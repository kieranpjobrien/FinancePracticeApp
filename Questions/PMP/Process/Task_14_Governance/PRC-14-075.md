# Question

A predictive project to upgrade an airline's crew-scheduling system has been running with its own steering committee. Partway through, the organisation establishes a portfolio governance board that now allocates shared specialist resources across all major projects. The project's steering committee approves a plan that depends on two scarce data engineers full time, but those engineers are also needed by a higher-priority safety project. The committee insists its approval stands. What is the best action for the project manager?

## Options

- **A**: Proceed on the project steering committee's approval, because it is the project's own governing body and has formally authorised the plan.
- **B**: Raise the resource conflict to the portfolio governance board, since allocation of shared specialist resources across projects is now that board's decision right rather than the project steering committee's.
- **C**: Negotiate directly with the safety project's manager to split the two engineers' time between the projects.
- **D**: Ask the sponsor to instruct the safety project to release the engineers, given that the steering committee has already approved the plan.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

When the portfolio board was established, the decision right over shared specialist resources moved up to it; the project steering committee's authority no longer extends to commandeering people that the portfolio must allocate across competing projects. The correct move is to surface the conflict to the body that now owns the trade-off (B). (A) treats the committee's approval as decisive over a resource it can no longer unilaterally claim, ignoring the new governance layer. (C) is a sensible tactic but cannot bind a portfolio-level allocation; two managers privately splitting scarce resources bypasses the board that exists to make exactly this prioritisation call. (D) asks the sponsor to override a higher-priority safety project, which inverts the portfolio's stated priorities and is not the sponsor's call to make against the board's remit.
