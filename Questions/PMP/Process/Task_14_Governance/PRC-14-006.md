# Question

An organisation is launching a programme delivered by five agile teams building one connected product. Leadership wants governance that protects each team's autonomy over how it works while still keeping the five teams aligned to one set of business priorities and a coherent architecture. The project manager is designing the governance structure. Which THREE elements best serve these intentions?

## Options

- **A**: A single prioritised programme backlog, with team backlogs derived from it, so all teams pull from one ordered set of business priorities.
- **B**: A central change board that must pre-approve every team's iteration plan before the team may commit to it.
- **C**: A regular cross-team coordination forum where teams surface and resolve dependencies and integration risks themselves.
- **D**: Architectural guardrails and shared standards that constrain key technical decisions while leaving teams free to design within them.
- **E**: A programme rule that all five teams adopt identical iteration lengths, ceremonies, estimation units, and tooling.

## Answer

A, C, D

## Explanation

The brief is to align five teams to shared priorities and a coherent product without dictating how each team works. A single prioritised programme backlog feeding team backlogs gives one source of business priority while leaving execution to the teams (A); a cross-team coordination forum lets teams manage their own dependencies rather than having them managed for them (C); and architectural guardrails constrain the few decisions that must be consistent while preserving design freedom inside the boundaries (D) — the classic "loose-tight" balance. A central board pre-approving every iteration plan (B) destroys the team autonomy leadership explicitly wants to protect and reintroduces the gatekeeping agile governance is meant to avoid. Mandating identical ceremonies, estimation units, and tooling (E) confuses alignment of outcomes with uniformity of method; it removes each team's freedom to tailor its way of working without improving business or architectural alignment.
