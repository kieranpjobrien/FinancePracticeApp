# Question

Midway through a predictive pharmaceutical-facility project, the executive sponsor leaves the organisation and the role is left vacant pending an internal appointment that is expected to take six weeks. Two decisions that exceed the project manager's delegated authority are due within the fortnight. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Approach the governance structure to confirm who holds the sponsor's decision authority during the vacancy, and route the two decisions through that interim authority.
- **B**: Make both decisions using best judgement and document them for ratification once a new sponsor is appointed.
- **C**: Defer both decisions until a permanent sponsor is in place, accepting the resulting six-week delay.
- **D**: Ask the steering committee to lower the delegation threshold so the two decisions fall within the project manager's own authority.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A vacant sponsor seat does not erase the need for sponsor-level decisions; governance frameworks anticipate this by providing for delegation or an interim authority to hold the role's decision rights. The project manager should confirm where that authority now sits — often the steering committee chair or a nominated deputy — and route the time-bound decisions there (A). Making the decisions unilaterally and seeking later ratification (B) usurps an authority the project manager does not hold and risks decisions a future sponsor cannot unwind. Deferring everything for six weeks (C) needlessly stalls the project when a legitimate interim path exists. Asking to lower the threshold so the decisions become the project manager's (D) solves a temporary gap by permanently transferring authority that ought to remain at sponsor level.
