# Question

On an agile initiative to build a customer-facing insurance quote engine, the governance framework gives the product owner authority over backlog prioritisation. The executive sponsor, alarmed by a competitor's launch, has begun instructing the development team directly each morning to reorder their work, bypassing the product owner. The team is now unsure whose direction to follow, the product owner feels undermined, and the sponsor's underlying concern — falling behind on a market-critical feature — is real and pressing. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Convene the product owner and sponsor together to re-confirm, in front of the team, that prioritisation runs through the product owner, settling the conflicting-direction problem in one session.
- **B**: Speak privately with the sponsor first, acknowledge the competitive urgency, surface how the direct instructions cut across the product owner's authority and disrupt flow, and agree a legitimate channel for the sponsor's priorities.
- **C**: Have the product owner reprioritise the backlog now to put the competitor-driven feature at the top, removing the sponsor's reason to keep intervening.
- **D**: Ask the team, in the interim, to keep following the product owner's ordering and route any instruction from the sponsor back through the product owner.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Governance defines decision rights, and the live problem is that the sponsor's well-intentioned interventions are overriding the product owner's defined authority and giving the team conflicting direction. The first move is to deal with the source privately: a candid conversation with the sponsor that takes the competitive concern seriously, explains the impact of bypassing the product owner, and agrees a legitimate channel (the sponsor raising priorities to the product owner) restores the structure without a confrontation. (A) tackles the right principle but stages it as a group correction in front of the team, which risks embarrassing the sponsor and hardening positions; a senior stakeholder is better engaged one-to-one before any joint reset. (C) treats the symptom — it may quiet the sponsor today by giving them what they want, but it concedes that going around the product owner gets results and leaves the bypass behaviour unaddressed. (D) is a sensible holding step for the team and may form part of the agreed channel, but issued on its own it manages the team's confusion while leaving the actual conflict with the sponsor untouched, so it is not the first thing to do.
