# Question

A hybrid project runs its discovery and design work in iterations but delivers a regulated manufacturing changeover through a fixed, stage-gated execution phase. The newly formed steering committee asks the project manager how it should receive information so it can govern both halves effectively. What is the most appropriate response?

## Options

- **A**: Standardise on the iteration review format for all reporting so the committee sees one consistent cadence across the whole project.
- **B**: Tailor the governance information to each part: empirical iteration metrics for the agile work and gate-readiness reviews for the stage-gated execution.
- **C**: Report only against the stage gates, since the regulated changeover is the part that carries the committee's accountability.
- **D**: Defer all committee reporting until the iterative discovery work concludes, then report the whole project on the predictive gate schedule.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Governance information should fit the delivery approach it is meant to oversee; a hybrid project legitimately carries two approaches, so the committee should receive the form of evidence each one produces. Empirical metrics (velocity, increment outcomes, emerging scope) govern the iterative work, while gate-readiness reviews govern the predictive changeover (B). Forcing everything into the iteration format (A) strips the stage-gated work of the readiness evidence regulators and the committee need. Reporting only against gates (C) leaves the discovery work ungoverned even though its decisions shape what is later built. Deferring all reporting until discovery ends (D) blinds the committee to early, often irreversible design choices and abandons governance precisely when it is most useful.
