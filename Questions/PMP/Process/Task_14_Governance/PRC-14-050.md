# Question

On a predictive rail signalling upgrade, the governance framework routes any change to the scope, schedule, or budget baselines through a change control board (CCB) that convenes weekly. A safety regulator has just issued a directive that forces an addition to scope, and the sponsor has told the project manager by email to "get it moving straight away." Acting now would shorten an already tight compliance deadline; the CCB's next scheduled sitting is five days off, though it can be convened earlier for urgent matters. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Raise a change request and put it to the CCB, calling an early sitting given the regulatory deadline rather than waiting for the routine meeting.
- **B**: Begin implementing the scope addition now on the sponsor's instruction and the regulator's directive, and submit it to the CCB at its next sitting for the record.
- **C**: Ask the sponsor to confirm the instruction in a documented directive, then proceed on that authority since the change is regulator-mandated and not discretionary.
- **D**: Hold the change for the regularly scheduled CCB meeting in five days, since the framework routes baseline changes through that board and the deadline can still be met.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A mandatory regulatory change still alters a baseline, so it runs through the CCB like any other — but urgency is a real factor the project manager must serve, and the right lever is to convene the board early rather than to bypass it or to wait passively. (B) is the strongest distractor: when a regulator compels the change and the sponsor is pushing, "just start" feels defensible, but the CCB owns baseline changes and starting before its decision substitutes the project manager's judgement of necessity for the board's authorisation and its assessment of schedule and cost impact. (C) dresses the bypass up with a documented sponsor directive, but a sponsor instruction is not a CCB approval; the framework names the board, not the sponsor, for baseline changes. (D) honours the board but treats the routine cadence as the only option, letting an early sitting go unused and eating into a tight compliance deadline when the framework expressly allows urgent convening.
