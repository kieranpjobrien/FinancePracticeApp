# Question

A predictive pharmaceutical project uses stage-gate governance: each gate needs a formal go/no-go from a gate review board before the next phase is funded. The design phase is complete, but a few of the gate's exit criteria are still open and being worked. The construction contractor is on standby charging a daily holding fee, and the sponsor is pressing to keep things moving. The board's next sitting can be brought forward on request. What is the most appropriate action?

## Options

- **A**: Request an early board sitting and present the actual status against every criterion, open items included, leaving the board to pass, pass with conditions, or hold.
- **B**: Begin limited, reversible construction mobilisation at risk to protect the schedule while the team closes the remaining criteria, then take the completed gate to the board.
- **C**: Wait until the team has closed every criterion, then bring a fully met gate to the board, absorbing the holding fees until then.
- **D**: Confirm with the sponsor that the residual criteria are acceptable to carry into the next phase, then proceed on the sponsor's authorisation and inform the board at its next sitting.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The gate is a decision point owned by the board; the project manager's job is to bring the true status to that body rather than decide on its behalf whether the open items are good enough to proceed. An early sitting with the real position lets the board choose — and a conditional pass against agreed conditions is itself a legitimate governance outcome only the board can grant. (B) is a genuine schedule-protection tactic and "reversible" limits the downside, but mobilising before any go decision spends at risk on the project manager's authority alone and pre-empts the gate; reversibility caps the damage, it does not supply the missing authorisation. (C) honours the gate but assumes waiting is the only compliant path and lets avoidable holding fees mount, when an early board might well grant a conditional pass — the project manager has pre-judged the answer as "hold." (D) is the trap: the sponsor is senior and is pushing to proceed, but the stage-gate governance vests the go/no-go in the board, so a sponsor sign-off cannot stand in for the gate decision or its record.
