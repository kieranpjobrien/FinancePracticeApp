# Question

A steering committee is uneasy about funding an agile product initiative because the team cannot commit up front to a fixed scope for a fixed price, which is how the organisation has always governed investment. The committee fears writing a blank cheque. The project manager wants to design a funding-governance model that gives the committee genuine control over the money while preserving the team's ability to adapt scope to what it learns. Which THREE arrangements achieve this balance?

## Options

- **A**: Fund the initiative incrementally in stages, with each stage's continuation contingent on demonstrated value and a go/no-go decision by the committee.
- **B**: Set a fixed budget and timebox, and let scope flex within it so the committee controls cost while the team prioritises what fits.
- **C**: Require the team to baseline the full scope up front and govern it through formal change control for every backlog adjustment.
- **D**: Tie continued funding to outcome and value metrics reviewed at defined checkpoints, so money follows demonstrated benefit rather than activity.
- **E**: Release the entire budget at initiation against a signed fixed-scope statement, then review only at final delivery.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

The committee wants real fiscal control without freezing scope, which is exactly what value-based, incremental funding provides. Funding in stages with go/no-go decisions lets the committee stop or redirect investment as it learns (A); a fixed budget and timebox with flexible scope caps the spend while the team adapts what it delivers within the envelope (B); and tying continued funding to outcome and value metrics at checkpoints ensures money follows demonstrated benefit (D). Baselining full scope up front and change-controlling every backlog tweak (C) reimposes the fixed-scope model the situation is trying to move beyond and buries the team in change paperwork. Releasing the whole budget against a fixed-scope statement and reviewing only at the end (E) is the literal blank cheque the committee fears — it removes interim control entirely and is the opposite of the adaptive, value-gated governance required.
