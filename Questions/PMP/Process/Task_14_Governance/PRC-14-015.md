# Question

A project manager is drafting the governance section of the charter for a hybrid programme that modernises an insurer's claims platform: discovery and customer-facing features run in iterations, while a regulated core-ledger replacement follows a stage-gated path. Leadership wants the charter to make governance unambiguous from day one. Which THREE items should the governance section establish to meet that intent?

## Options

- **A**: The decision rights and delegated authority limits, naming which roles and bodies decide which categories of decision and up to what thresholds.
- **B**: The escalation paths and tolerances that trigger when an issue must be raised beyond the project manager.
- **C**: A complete, baselined work breakdown structure with durations for every task across both the iterative and stage-gated work.
- **D**: The governance bodies and their cadence, including the steering committee, and how oversight differs for the iterative versus the stage-gated streams.
- **E**: The detailed sprint backlog for the first three iterations of the customer-facing work.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

A charter's governance section defines how the programme will be directed and controlled, so it should fix decision rights and authority limits (A), the escalation paths and tolerances that route issues to the right level (B), and the governing bodies, their cadence, and how oversight is tailored to each delivery stream (D) — together these make accountability unambiguous from the outset, exactly what leadership asked for. A fully baselined work breakdown structure with task durations (C) is detailed planning output, not governance, and pre-baselining the iterative stream contradicts its adaptive nature. A detailed sprint backlog for the first three iterations (E) is team-level execution detail owned by the product owner and team; placing it in the charter's governance section confuses what work will be done with who is authorised to direct and oversee it.
