# Question

An agile team builds a logistics tracking app that writes to a customer record shared with several other product teams across the company. The product owner has full authority over backlog priority and release content. Mid-sprint, a stakeholder requests a feature that needs a new field added to that shared customer record. The product owner is enthusiastic and wants it in the next release. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Support the product owner in adding the feature to the backlog and scheduling it, since release content is squarely the product owner's decision right.
- **B**: Establish whether changing the shared customer record falls under a cross-product authority outside the product owner's remit before the feature is committed to a release.
- **C**: Have the team estimate the work and let the product owner rank it in the backlog on that basis, raising any shared-data concern only if it later proves hard to build.
- **D**: Ask the product owner to confirm the other teams consuming the shared record have no objection, then proceed once they are content.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The product owner genuinely owns backlog and release content, but a change to an asset shared across product teams typically sits behind a cross-product gate (an architecture or data authority) that the product owner's remit does not override. The first move is to confirm where the decision right for the shared record lives before the change advances toward a release it may not be cleared to enter. (A) treats the product owner's release authority as unbounded and lets a cross-product change through on single-role authority. (C) is the tempting distractor — estimating and ranking is normal flow, and it sounds prudent — but it advances the change through the team's process while deferring the one question that determines whether the product owner can authorise it at all, so the shared-data dimension is treated as a build risk rather than a decision-rights question. (D) substitutes informal peer consent for the cross-product authority that actually owns the record; agreement from consuming teams is not the same as the gate's approval.
