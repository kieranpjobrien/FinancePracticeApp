# Question

An empowered agile team building a streaming-media recommendation engine is granted authority to make all decisions needed to meet its sprint goals. During a sprint the team realises that meeting its goal the way it intends would change a customer-facing pricing display, which the product owner has flagged as commercially sensitive and outside what the team may alter alone. The team argues that changing the display is necessary to meet the sprint goal it was empowered to deliver. How should the project manager handle the tension?

## Options

- **A**: Reinforce that empowerment to meet sprint goals does not extend to decisions the governance structure has explicitly reserved, and route the pricing-display change to the product owner for a decision.
- **B**: Back the team, since it was empowered to do whatever is needed to meet the sprint goal, and a reserved decision should not block committed work.
- **C**: Instruct the team to drop the sprint goal entirely so the reserved pricing display is never touched.
- **D**: Make the call on the pricing display yourself to keep the sprint on track, then inform the product owner of your decision.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Team empowerment is bounded by the decisions governance has deliberately reserved; "authority to meet the sprint goal" never silently overrides an explicit carve-out for a commercially sensitive change. The project manager should hold that boundary and send the pricing decision to the product owner who owns it, after which the team can adapt its approach (A). (B) lets a general empowerment statement erase a specific reservation, which is exactly backwards. (C) over-corrects by sacrificing the whole sprint goal when only the reserved element needs a proper decision-maker. (D) usurps the product owner's reserved authority; keeping the sprint on track does not entitle the project manager to make a decision the structure assigned to someone else, and informing afterwards does not cure the breach.
