# Question

A predictive research-and-development project to develop a new battery chemistry is structured around stage gates, each of which is a go/no-go decision point. At the gate closing the feasibility stage, the test data is mixed: one performance target was met, another was missed by a wide margin, and the market opportunity has shrunk since the business case was written. The project team has worked hard and morale is high. What is the primary purpose the project manager should ensure the gate review serves?

## Options

- **A**: Deciding, on current evidence, whether continued investment in the next stage is still justified — including the option to stop the project.
- **B**: Confirming that the team completed all planned feasibility activities so the stage can be formally signed off as done.
- **C**: Recognising the team's effort and securing their commitment to the next stage so momentum is not lost.
- **D**: Documenting the missed performance target as a lesson learned to improve estimation on future projects.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A stage gate is fundamentally an investment-control point: its purpose is to decide whether the organisation should commit further funding given everything now known, and that explicitly includes a no-go that stops the project (A). The mixed data and shrunken market are precisely the conditions a gate exists to weigh. (B) treats the gate as a completion checklist, but finishing the planned activities is not the same as the project still being worth continuing — a project can complete a stage and still rightly be stopped. (C) and (D) are secondary at best; morale and lessons learned matter, but allowing effort already spent or team enthusiasm to drive the continuation decision is the sunk-cost trap the gate is designed to prevent.
