# Question

A project manager supports a self-organising agile team building an internal analytics tool. The team's working agreement gives it authority to choose its own technical design and to manage the sprint backlog. A team member proposes adopting a new open-source library that carries a licence the organisation's legal policy prohibits for commercial products. The team is keen and argues that technical decisions are theirs to make. How should the project manager respond?

## Options

- **A**: Clarify that team autonomy over technical design operates within organisational policy boundaries, and that a prohibited licence is outside the team's decision rights regardless of technical merit.
- **B**: Defer to the team's decision, because the governance arrangement explicitly grants it authority over technical design.
- **C**: Overrule the team and select an approved library yourself, since the proposed one violates policy.
- **D**: Allow the team to adopt the library for now and seek a retrospective exception from legal afterwards.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Delegated authority always operates inside organisational constraints; granting a team design autonomy does not grant it the right to breach a binding legal policy. The project manager's role is to make that boundary explicit, so the team understands the decision is outside its remit, not to second-guess its engineering judgement (A). (B) reads the autonomy too literally and lets a policy breach through. (C) reaches the right outcome on the licence but does so by stripping the team of a design decision it legitimately owns within policy, undermining the self-organising principle; the project manager should mark the boundary and let the team choose a compliant alternative. (D) inverts governance by acting first and seeking permission later, exposing the organisation to the very risk the policy exists to prevent.
