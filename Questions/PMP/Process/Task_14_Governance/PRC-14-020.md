# Question

A hybrid programme inherited a governance model from a previous waterfall portfolio: every decision above a low threshold, including routine backlog and design choices, must go to a monthly steering committee. The agile teams now wait weeks for answers they are equipped to make themselves, and the committee's agenda is clogged with operational detail it is not positioned to judge well. The committee, however, is reluctant to "lose control." How should the project manager reframe and resolve this with the committee?

## Options

- **A**: Accept the inherited model unchanged to keep the committee comfortable, and coach the teams to plan further ahead so they wait less often.
- **B**: Ask the committee to meet weekly instead of monthly so the queue of operational decisions clears faster.
- **C**: Move all decisions currently going to the committee down to the project manager, who can then decide everything quickly.
- **D**: Propose tailoring the governance so operational decisions are delegated to the teams and product owner within clear tolerances, reserving the committee for strategic, cross-cutting, and tolerance-breaching matters.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The committee is conflating control with deciding everything, when effective control comes from holding the right decisions and delegating the rest within agreed tolerances. The project manager should reframe it that way and propose tailoring governance to delegate operational decisions to the teams and product owner inside clear tolerances, while the committee retains strategic, cross-cutting, and tolerance-breach decisions — preserving genuine oversight while restoring decision velocity (D). Leaving the model unchanged and coaching teams to wait less (A) accepts a structural mismatch and cannot eliminate delays the committee's monopoly causes. Meeting weekly (B) makes the committee process operational detail four times as often, entrenching the wrong involvement rather than removing it. Routing every decision to the project manager (C) merely relocates the bottleneck and over-centralises choices that self-organising teams and the product owner should own.
