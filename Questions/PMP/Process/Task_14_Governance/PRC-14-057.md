# Question

An agile team building an online university enrolment portal sets its own definition of done. Separately, a release management authority signs off every production release against regulatory and accessibility requirements before it goes live. A high-value feature has just met the team's definition of done, and the product owner wants it live the same day to catch peak enrolment traffic. The release authority's next scheduled review is in two days, though it can be approached for an out-of-cycle review. What should the project manager facilitate?

## Options

- **A**: Taking the feature to the release management authority for sign-off before production, and asking for an out-of-cycle review given how time-sensitive the traffic window is.
- **B**: Releasing the feature now on the strength of the team's definition of done, then booking it into the release authority's next review so the compliance record is completed.
- **C**: Having the team self-assess the feature against the regulatory and accessibility requirements and release if it passes, since those are the standards the authority would apply anyway.
- **D**: Asking the release authority to pre-authorise same-day release of any feature that has met the team's definition of done, removing the bottleneck for this and future high-value features.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Two distinct decision rights are in play: the definition of done governs whether the work is complete, while the release authority owns production-release approval for regulatory and accessibility compliance. "Done" does not equal "cleared to release," so the feature goes through the authority — and because timing is tight, the project manager pairs that with a request for an out-of-cycle review rather than just waiting. (B) collapses the two rights, treating completion as release authorisation and shipping before the compliance gate — the after-the-fact record does not cure the exposure the gate exists to prevent. (C) is the subtle trap: letting the team apply the authority's own criteria sounds equivalent, but it transfers the release-approval decision to a body that does not hold it; independence and accountability for that sign-off are the point of the separate authority. (D) seeks a blanket standing waiver that would gut the gate for exactly the high-value releases that most warrant compliance scrutiny — a far larger governance change than the situation calls for, and not the project manager's to engineer around a single deadline.
