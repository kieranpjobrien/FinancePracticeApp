# Question

A predictive construction project operates under a phase-gate governance model. The project charter sets a cost tolerance of plus or minus 8% within which the project manager may proceed without committee approval. Mid-phase, the forecast cost overrun reaches 6% and is trending upward, with two high-impact risks still unmitigated. The next gate review is eight weeks away. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Raise the trajectory to the steering committee now as an early warning, since the trend and open risks make breaching the tolerance a realistic prospect before the gate.
- **B**: Continue within the 8% tolerance and present the full cost picture at the scheduled gate review, as the project remains inside the agreed threshold.
- **C**: Reduce scope unilaterally to bring the forecast back toward baseline, keeping the variance well clear of the tolerance limit.
- **D**: Wait until the forecast reaches 8% before notifying the committee, because acting earlier would escalate a variance the project manager is still authorised to manage.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A tolerance defines the band within which the project manager may act without seeking approval, but it does not suspend the duty to give the governing body timely visibility of a credible threat to that band. With the overrun at 6%, trending up, and two high-impact risks open, a breach before the eight-week gate is foreseeable, so the right move is an early-warning escalation now (A). Staying silent until the gate (B) or until the 8% line is actually crossed (D) deprives the committee of the chance to intervene while options are still cheap, which is the purpose of escalation. Cutting scope unilaterally (C) may exceed the project manager's authority over deliverables and trades a cost problem for an unapproved scope change, creating a governance breach rather than resolving one.
