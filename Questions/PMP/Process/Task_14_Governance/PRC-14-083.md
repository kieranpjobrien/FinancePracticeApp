# Question

On a hybrid programme to consolidate three banks' core systems after a merger, an auditor questions a configuration choice made eight months earlier that materially affected interest calculations. No one can recall who authorised it or on what basis, and the change predates the current team. The sponsor wants the governance structure strengthened so this cannot recur. Which governance practice most directly prevents this problem in future?

## Options

- **A**: Maintaining a decision log that records each significant decision, who made it under what authority, the options considered, and the rationale.
- **B**: Increasing the frequency of steering committee meetings so decisions are discussed more often.
- **C**: Requiring all configuration work to pass through a single senior architect before implementation.
- **D**: Adding more detail to the weekly status report so decisions are visible to stakeholders as they happen.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The failure is one of traceability: a material decision exists but its author, authority and reasoning were never durably recorded, so it cannot be reconstructed later. A decision log captures exactly that — who decided, under what authority, the options weighed, and why — giving the lasting accountability trail the auditor needed (A). (B) creates more discussion but no permanent record of who decided what and on what basis, so the same gap recurs. (C) adds a control gate but does not by itself preserve the rationale and authority for each decision over time. (D) surfaces decisions transiently in status reports, but reports are point-in-time communications, not a maintained, queryable record that survives team turnover eight months on.
