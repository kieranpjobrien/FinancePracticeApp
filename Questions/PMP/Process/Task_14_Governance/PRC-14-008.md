# Question

An agile team delivering a payments feature has been releasing increments that the product owner accepts in the review, but two of the last three releases triggered production defects that the operations team had to fix out of hours. Investigation shows the team treats "the product owner is happy" as the only release criterion, and skips security and performance checks when under time pressure. How should the project manager strengthen governance here?

## Options

- **A**: Add a release-approval gate where the operations manager signs off each deployment before it reaches production.
- **B**: Establish a team-owned Definition of Done that includes the mandatory security and performance checks, applied to every increment before it can be called complete.
- **C**: Require the product owner to personally verify security and performance before accepting any increment in the review.
- **D**: Introduce a penalty in the team's metrics for each out-of-hours defect the operations team has to resolve.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The gap is that "complete" has been allowed to mean "the product owner likes it," with no agreed objective quality bar, so non-functional checks get dropped under pressure. A team-owned Definition of Done that explicitly includes the security and performance checks makes those checks a precondition of completion for every increment, embedding the quality standard in the team's own cadence (B). An operations sign-off gate (A) bolts on an external approver and slows the release flow without building the quality discipline into the team. Asking the product owner to verify security and performance (C) miscasts the role — the product owner judges value and fitness for business purpose, not technical quality gates. A defect penalty (D) punishes the outcome after the fact and encourages hiding defects rather than preventing them through an agreed standard of done.
