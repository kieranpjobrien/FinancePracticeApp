# Question

On an agile programme delivering a retail analytics platform, two senior business directors both believe they have the right to add items to the product backlog and reprioritise it. Their conflicting instructions reach the team in the same iteration, and the team has begun quietly choosing whichever direction seems easier. The project manager notices delivery is drifting from any stated business outcome. What is the best course of action?

## Options

- **A**: Ask the team to implement both directors' requests in priority order so neither director feels overridden.
- **B**: Hold a workshop for the two directors to negotiate a combined backlog they both endorse for the remainder of the programme.
- **C**: Work with the sponsor to confirm a single accountable product owner whose backlog decisions are authoritative, and route both directors' inputs through that role.
- **D**: Let the team continue selecting work, but require them to record which director's request each item satisfies for transparency.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The root problem is a contested decision right: two people each act as if they own backlog prioritisation, so there is no authoritative ordering and the team self-authorises by default. Governance is restored by establishing a single accountable product owner whose decisions are binding, with the directors feeding input into that role rather than instructing the team directly (C). Implementing both in "priority order" (A) is incoherent because there is no single priority order — that is the very thing in dispute. A one-off negotiated backlog (B) treats the symptom for one period and leaves the unresolved authority to resurface the moment priorities change. Recording which director each item serves (D) documents the drift without correcting who is entitled to direct the work.
