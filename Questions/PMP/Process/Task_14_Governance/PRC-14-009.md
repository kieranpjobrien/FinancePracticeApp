# Question

A predictive engineering project sits within an organisation that has a project management office (PMO) providing independent assurance. The PMO conducts a health check and reports to the steering committee that the project's earned-value indices look healthy, but its risk register has not been updated in two months and several closed risks show no evidence of the events that closed them. The sponsor asks the project manager to respond. What is the most appropriate action?

## Options

- **A**: Challenge the PMO finding on the basis that the earned-value indices demonstrate the project is performing well.
- **B**: Ask the steering committee to reduce the frequency of PMO health checks so the team can focus on delivery rather than documentation.
- **C**: Treat the finding as valid governance assurance, bring the risk register current, and put a routine in place to keep risk reviews and their evidence up to date.
- **D**: Reassign register upkeep to the PMO, since they are best placed to judge how it should be maintained.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The PMO is performing its independent assurance function on behalf of governance, and a stale, poorly evidenced risk register is a genuine control weakness even when cost and schedule indices look good — performance to date says nothing about emerging threats that have gone untracked. The right response is to accept the assurance, remediate the register, and institute a routine that keeps risk reviews and their evidence current (C). Dismissing the finding because earned value is healthy (A) misreads what the finding is about and undermines the assurance relationship. Asking to reduce health-check frequency (B) attacks the oversight rather than the deficiency it surfaced. Handing register upkeep to the PMO (D) compromises the PMO's independence by making the assurer responsible for the very artefact it is meant to assure.
