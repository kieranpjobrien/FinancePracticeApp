# Question

A predictive project to replace a hospital's nurse-call system has an executive sponsor and a newly formed steering committee comprising the directors of nursing, facilities, and IT. A vendor's price increase has put the approved budget at risk, and recovering it would mean dropping a feature the nursing director considers clinically important. The directors disagree about the trade-off, and several ask the project manager who carries this particular call so it can be made and the project can move on. What should the project manager tell them?

## Options

- **A**: The decision belongs to the steering committee as a body, because dropping a clinically significant feature is a cross-functional trade-off the committee was constituted to weigh and direct.
- **B**: The decision belongs to the executive sponsor, who owns the business case the budget pressure threatens and is accountable for whether the project still delivers its intended value.
- **C**: The decision belongs to the project manager, who is closest to the detail and is accountable for delivering within the approved budget.
- **D**: The decision belongs to the nursing director, since the feature at stake serves clinical safety and that is her area of accountability.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The trade-off pits the funded budget against the value case for the feature, and that is a business-case judgement. Governance assigns ownership of the business case and accountability for the project's intended value to a single role — the executive sponsor — so a change that puts both at risk is the sponsor's call to make (typically informed by, but not surrendered to, the committee). (A) is the strongest distractor: steering committees do adjudicate cross-functional matters, but a benefits/value trade-off of this kind is the sponsor's singular accountability, not a collective vote; routing it to the body as the decider dilutes the very accountability the role exists to hold. (C) keeps the call with the project manager, who manages within the budget but does not own the business case the trade-off turns on — this exceeds delegated delivery authority. (D) gives the decision to the stakeholder most affected by the outcome, which lets a single functional interest decide a whole-project value question rather than the role accountable for that value.
