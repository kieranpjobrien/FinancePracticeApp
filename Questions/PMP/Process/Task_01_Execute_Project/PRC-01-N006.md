# Question

A start-up on a tight runway is building an expense-tracking app, with an investor demo in six weeks that needs to show real users getting value. The team has scoped a thin end-to-end slice: capture an expense, categorise it, and see a running total. Three further candidates compete for the remaining capacity before the demo: receipt-photo capture (users repeatedly ask for it), an accountant-export format (one large pilot customer says it is a precondition to paying), and bank-feed auto-import (technically the hardest, but the feature most likely to differentiate the product long term). The product owner asks the project manager how to sequence the work after the thin slice. What should the project manager advise?

## Options

- **A**: Build the accountant-export next, because it converts the pilot customer from a trial into paying revenue the investors can point to.
- **B**: Build receipt-photo capture next, because the volume of user requests is the clearest signal of where value lies.
- **C**: Start the bank-feed auto-import now, because it is the hardest item and the long-term differentiator, so it needs the most runway.
- **D**: Have the product owner score the three candidates against the demo outcome and cost of delay, then sequence them, rather than committing to one on a single factor.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Each candidate is championed by a different, legitimate signal — revenue conversion, user demand, and strategic differentiation — and picking on any one in isolation can starve a more valuable item. (D) weighs all three against the specific near-term outcome (a demo proving paid value) and the cost of delaying each, which is how a value-driven backlog is ordered; it may well surface the export as first, but only after the trade-off is made deliberately. (A) is strong but assumes the single pilot's precondition outranks everything without testing that. (B) treats request volume as a proxy for value, which often misleads. (C) front-loads the hardest, latest-paying item against a six-week revenue-proof deadline, risking the demo for a long-horizon bet.
