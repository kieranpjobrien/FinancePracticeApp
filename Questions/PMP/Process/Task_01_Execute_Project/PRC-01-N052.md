# Question

Three sprints into an eight-sprint release, an agile team's average velocity is 32 points per sprint, but the committed release scope still has 210 points remaining across the five sprints left. A senior stakeholder insists the full scope must ship on the release date. The backlog contains a clear mix of high- and low-value items. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Share the velocity-versus-remaining-scope forecast with the stakeholder, show that roughly 50 points will not fit, and facilitate a decision on which lower-value items to defer to a later release.
- **B**: Work with the product owner to re-slice the remaining backlog into thinner increments so more of the high-value scope can be delivered within the five sprints before raising any gap with the stakeholder.
- **C**: Add a sixth sprint to the release timeline so the observed velocity can clear the full 210 points without changing scope.
- **D**: Bring in two extra developers for the remaining sprints to lift the team's throughput enough to close the 50-point gap.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The empirical forecast (five sprints at 32 points yields about 160 against 210 remaining) shows the scope will not fit, and with a fixed date the value-preserving response is to make the gap visible and facilitate prioritised descoping so the most valuable work ships (A). (B) is a useful technique and may recover some capacity, but thinner slicing of the same scope will not conjure 50 missing points, and acting on it without surfacing the shortfall leaves the stakeholder unaware of a gap they are accountable for. (C) moves the fixed release date, which the scenario rules out as the lever the stakeholder is defending. (D) adds people late in a release, where ramp-up and added coordination typically lower near-term velocity rather than raising it.
