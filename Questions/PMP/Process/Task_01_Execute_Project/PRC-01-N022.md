# Question

A predictive hospital-systems upgrade is four weeks behind on the critical path with a fixed go-live tied to a clinical accreditation audit that cannot move. The sponsor authorises whatever it takes to recover. The scheduler has run the numbers: overlapping two design-and-build activities would claw back the full four weeks but leaves the build exposed to rework if the design shifts; adding staff recovers two weeks at material cost with no rework exposure; doing both recovers the four weeks and caps the rework risk but costs the most. What should the project manager recommend?

## Options

- **A**: Overlap the two design-and-build activities only, since that single technique recovers the full four weeks at no extra labour cost.
- **B**: Add staff to recover two weeks now, then decide on overlapping later once the design has stabilised, keeping rework exposure to a minimum.
- **C**: Combine the staffing increase with the overlap, accepting the higher cost to hit the date while holding the rework exposure to the narrowest window.
- **D**: Recommend the team hit the date with the overlap and absorb any rework as a contingency activity after go-live.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The accreditation date is immovable, so recovering the full four weeks is non-negotiable, and the decision turns on which compression mix hits the date at a rework risk the project can survive in a clinical setting. (C) buys the full recovery while the added staff lets the overlapped work proceed with the tightest rework window, which is the defensible trade of cost for both date and risk. (A) hits the date on paper but rests entirely on the overlap, leaving full rework exposure on a clinical system with no buffer. (B) protects against rework but recovers only two weeks, missing a date that cannot move. (D) treats post-go-live rework on an accreditation-critical system as routine contingency, accepting quality risk precisely where it is least acceptable.
