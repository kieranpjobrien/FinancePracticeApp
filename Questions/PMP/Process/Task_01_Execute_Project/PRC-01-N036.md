# Question

Two days into a sprint, the team building a maritime port-scheduling tool discovers that a third-party API they depend on has changed without notice, breaking the integration central to this sprint's goal. The vendor's standard support queue quotes a ten-day response, but the sprint ends in eight days and the increment is committed to a customer demo. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Accept the ten-day vendor timeline and re-plan the increment for a later sprint, informing the customer the demo will slip.
- **B**: Escalate to the vendor through the contract's priority-support or account-management channel to compress the response, while the team investigates a temporary workaround in parallel.
- **C**: Have the team build a permanent replacement for the third-party API so the dependency is removed for good.
- **D**: Ask the team to work extended hours to absorb the delay once the vendor eventually responds.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Acting with urgency means attacking the impediment on multiple fronts immediately, not passively accepting a standard queue. (B) uses the contractual escalation path to shorten the vendor response and pursues a parallel workaround so the demo can still happen. (A) surrenders to the default timeline and slips a committed increment without trying to remove the blocker. (C) over-engineers a permanent replacement under time pressure, far exceeding what the sprint needs. (D) relies on overtime to absorb a delay that escalation and a workaround could prevent altogether.
