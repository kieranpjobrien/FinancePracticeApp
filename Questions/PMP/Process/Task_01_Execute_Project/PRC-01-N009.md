# Question

A flagship-store fit-out must open before a major sporting event fills the district; the date is fixed and commercially critical. The critical path runs two weeks long. It is dominated by joinery fabrication followed by on-site installation, then final finishing. The fit-out has a healthy cost contingency, the finishing trades are skilled and in short supply, and the client has stressed that finish quality is the brand's shopfront. Installation cannot meaningfully begin until fabrication is largely complete. How should the project manager compress the schedule?

## Options

- **A**: Fast-track by starting on-site installation of completed sections while the remaining joinery is still being fabricated.
- **B**: Crash the final finishing stage by bringing in additional finishing trades to shorten the last activity on the path.
- **C**: Crash fabrication by adding a second shift at the workshop, drawing on the cost contingency to pull the critical path in before installation starts.
- **D**: Fast-track by overlapping finishing with installation so the two final stages run partly in parallel.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The compression must respect the hard sequence dependency, the cost head-room, the scarce finishing skills, and the client's emphasis on finish quality. (C) crashes the *upstream* activity (fabrication) where adding a shift converts available contingency into time before the dependent installation begins, without touching the quality-critical finish — the lowest-risk way to recover the date here. (A) tries to overlap fabrication and installation, but the scenario states installation cannot meaningfully start until fabrication is largely done, so the overlap yields little real schedule and invites rework from installing against incomplete components. (B) crashes finishing, yet the finishing trades are scarce, so extra resource is hard to secure and crowding skilled finishers onto a brand-critical shopfront raises the very quality risk the client flagged. (D) overlaps the two final quality-sensitive stages, the option most likely to produce defects and rework on the showpiece finish — trading the brand's shopfront for schedule when an upstream crash is available.
