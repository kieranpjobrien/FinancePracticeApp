# Question

A predictive data-centre migration is 15% behind schedule at the halfway point, with a hard cut-over date driven by a lease expiry on the old facility. The project manager is preparing recovery options to present to the steering committee. Which actions are legitimate, value-preserving recovery levers to include? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Identify critical-path activities that could be crashed by adding qualified resources within the available budget reserve.
- **B**: Fast-track selected activities that can safely overlap, after assessing the added rework risk.
- **C**: Descope or defer lower-priority migration items that are not essential for the cut-over to a later phase.
- **D**: Reduce testing on migrated workloads to claw back time, accepting a higher chance of post-cut-over failures.
- **E**: Re-label in-progress activities as complete in the schedule so the reported variance improves before the committee meeting.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Crashing within reserve, risk-assessed fast-tracking, and deferring non-essential scope are all recognised recovery levers that preserve the value of the cut-over (A, B and C). (D) buys time by cutting testing, which trades schedule for a materially higher risk of failed workloads after an irreversible cut-over, destroying value. (E) falsifies status, which is dishonest and hides the real problem from the very people accountable for steering it.
