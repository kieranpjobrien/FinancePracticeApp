# Question

A predictive project is installing irrigation automation across a commercial vineyard before the growing season opens in five weeks; installing after bud-break would damage the vines and forfeit the season's yield. A flow-control valve on the critical-path installation has slipped and now threatens a ten-day delay. The original supplier's account manager says she will "try to pull it forward" but will not commit to a date. A locally stocked valve from a different supplier meets the same specification, though switching means re-issuing a purchase order and a one-day re-test of the control logic that the team has not yet scheduled. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Switch to the locally stocked equivalent valve and book the one-day re-test, since this is the option that can be guaranteed to land before bud-break.
- **B**: Press the original supplier's account manager for a firm expedited date before changing anything, since the valve is already specified and on order.
- **C**: Authorise the switch and direct the installation crew to begin the surrounding wiring now, so re-test and install can compress into the recovered window.
- **D**: Re-baseline to absorb the ten-day slip and warn the sponsor that part of the season's yield is at risk.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Against an immovable bud-break deadline, the first move is the one that makes recovery certain. The locally stocked, spec-compliant valve does that: re-issue the PO and book the re-test, and the threat is removed within a controllable window (A). (B) is the tempting first step — chasing a firm date costs nothing and the valve is already on order — but it stakes a hard deadline on a supplier who has already slipped and will not commit, burning days you may not get back when a sure option is in hand. (C) reaches the right decision to switch but jumps ahead to surrounding wiring before the valve and re-test are secured, risking rework if anything about the substitute changes position or logic; sequencing the recovery comes first. (D) accepts lost yield without first using an available, reliable recovery, abandoning the duty to deliver value with urgency.
