# Question

A retailer is building click-and-collect for its holiday peak. The full 16-story backlog needs eleven weeks but only eight remain before peak. A slice of order placement plus a store-pickup notification would let customers complete a real purchase, and the team can release to production at any time. Two facts shape the window: marketing's paid campaigns begin driving real traffic partway through the build, and the store-fulfilment staff need a short period of live but low-volume orders to settle their pickup process before volumes climb. The product owner is engaged daily. How should the project manager have the work sequenced?

## Options

- **A**: Sequence the transactable slice to go live a little before paid traffic ramps, so it is earning when demand arrives and store staff get a quiet window to bed in fulfilment before peak.
- **B**: Release the transactable slice the moment it passes the definition of done, even if that is week one, to begin learning from real orders as early as possible.
- **C**: Plan a single hardened release of the transactable slice close to peak, carrying the most stories possible behind it so the launch is feature-rich and operationally settled in one move.
- **D**: Release each of the 16 stories to production in priority order as it finishes, so value flows continuously and the transactable capability emerges once its component stories are individually done.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four ship incrementally; the margin is *when* the transactable slice lands relative to two things the scenario ties together — the paid-traffic ramp and the staff's need to settle fulfilment at low volume first. (A) lands the slice just ahead of the demand ramp so revenue coincides with traffic, and uses the quiet pre-ramp period for staff to stabilise pickups before volumes rise — capturing value and de-risking operations against the real deadline. (B) optimises for earliest learning, but a release before any traffic or fulfilment readiness spends a hardening window on feedback the ramped launch would supply anyway, and risks orders hitting an unprepared store process. (C) defers the slice to near peak, surrendering the earlier paid-traffic revenue and giving staff no low-volume window to settle before the busiest period — the riskiest moment to debut a new fulfilment flow. (D) lets the transactable capability "emerge" from individually-done stories, so its timing is uncontrolled; an enabling or low-value story could complete ahead of the minimum that lets a customer actually buy, delaying first revenue and giving no deliberate run-in for staff.
