# Question

A predictive project to commission a new blood-analysis instrument must complete validation before a pathology lab's accreditation audit in eight weeks; missing it forces the lab to outsource testing for a full quarter at significant cost. Validation is on the critical path and forecast to finish two weeks late. The validation manager says she could split the test runs across two more contract analysts, but they are unfamiliar with this instrument and her senior staff would lose roughly a day each bringing them up to speed and checking their runs. The contingency reserve can fund the contractors. Commissioning steps that follow validation are well understood and largely independent of the final validation results. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Add the two contract analysts to validation now, accepting the senior-staff supervision cost as the price of recovering the two weeks on the critical path.
- **B**: Begin the independent downstream commissioning steps in parallel with the remaining validation, recovering schedule without diluting the validation team or spending reserve.
- **C**: Hold validation staffing as is and ask the validation manager to prioritise the test runs most material to accreditation first, so a defensible result exists by the audit even if some runs trail.
- **D**: Brief the sponsor that validation will slip two weeks and recommend lining up the outsourcing arrangement now to limit the quarter's disruption.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The real contest is crash (A) versus fast-track (B): both can recover schedule, and the question is which fits a regulated deliverable. Crashing costs reserve and some senior-staff supervision, but it buys back time on the activity that actually gates the audit while keeping the full validation intact (A). (B) is the strongest distractor — the downstream steps are described as independent, so overlapping looks free — but fast-tracking commissioning ahead of complete validation on a regulated instrument means committing work that a late validation finding could force you to redo, importing rework and compliance exposure precisely where it is least acceptable. (C) sequencing the "most material" runs first quietly risks reaching the audit with mandated runs incomplete, trading the integrity of a pass/fail regulatory gate for the appearance of progress. (D) concedes a costly slip before using a funded, legitimate recovery, failing the duty to deliver value with urgency.
