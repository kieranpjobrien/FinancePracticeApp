# Question

A telehealth team has capacity this sprint for one of two items, both wanted for an upcoming release. Online appointment booking is the headline patient feature and is well understood. A clinician identity-verification step is less visible to patients but relies on a government identity API the team has never integrated, and a compliance adviser has flagged that the booking flow's go-live conditions are still being clarified with the regulator. The product owner leans toward booking to show patients tangible value first. The release date is firm. What is the project manager's strongest contribution to the sequencing decision?

## Options

- **A**: Surface that the verification work carries the only real unknown — an unproven third-party integration and unsettled go-live conditions — so doing it first converts the release's biggest uncertainty into knowledge while there is still time to react.
- **B**: Surface that booking is the higher-demand feature and should lead, with verification scheduled immediately after so the most visible patient value is demonstrable at the earliest review.
- **C**: Surface that both items can be partly advanced this sprint, keeping booking and verification moving together so neither becomes a late bottleneck before the release.
- **D**: Surface that the team should run a dedicated spike to size the identity integration and confirm the regulatory conditions before committing either item to a sprint.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Both items are genuinely wanted and either could be built first, so the sequencing turns on risk, not visibility. (A) tackles the part of the release carrying the live unknowns — an unfamiliar integration plus go-live conditions not yet pinned down — early enough that a nasty surprise can still be absorbed before the firm date; retiring the dominant uncertainty first is how a value-driven team protects a deadline. (B) sequences by standalone demand and earliest demo, which feels right but front-loads the well-understood work and pushes the only risky, condition-dependent piece toward the end of the runway, where a late integration or regulatory snag could jeopardise the whole release. (C) splitting capacity across both risks finishing neither and dilutes focus on the item that most needs a clear run to surface its unknowns. (D) a separate spike can characterise the risk but burns a sprint producing no shippable value, when the team could start the integration for real and learn by building against a pressing date.
