# Question

A team maintaining a logistics tracking portal faces a backlog of twenty-three items competing for the next two-week sprint, far more than capacity allows. The backlog was last ordered three weeks ago and several urgent client requests have come in since. The product owner started two weeks of leave today and named a business analyst as her delegate; she can also be reached by message for short questions. Sprint planning is tomorrow and delivery starts Monday. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Have the delegate re-order the backlog by current business value ahead of planning, escalating only the genuinely unclear calls to the product owner by message.
- **B**: Run planning against the existing order and commit the items that fit, since a recently maintained backlog is a reasonable proxy for value and waiting risks a late start.
- **C**: Message the product owner directly to re-rank the top of the backlog herself, keeping the accountable owner in control of value decisions despite her leave.
- **D**: Ask the team to pull the highest-value items by their own read of the client requests, since they are closest to the work and planning is tomorrow.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The backlog is stale relative to new client demand, so the sprint must be ordered on current value, and the named delegate exists precisely to make that call while the owner is away. (A) gets a fresh prioritisation from the empowered delegate and reserves the owner's limited availability for the few ambiguous trade-offs, hitting value and the Monday start. (B) leans on a three-week-old order that predates the urgent requests, risking a sprint spent on work that is no longer the most valuable. (C) routes a full re-rank to someone on leave, which both undercuts the delegate she appointed and is slower and less reliable than using that delegate. (D) puts genuine value trade-offs on a team that lacks the business context the delegate holds, substituting proximity to the code for proximity to value.
