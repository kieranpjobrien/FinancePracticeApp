# Question

A telecom billing migration must cut over before a regulatory rate change takes effect in nine weeks; the date is fixed by law. The team can hit it only by deferring some automated test coverage. The sponsor and product owner have already agreed, in the open, to accept managed technical debt to meet the date, and want the project manager to decide where the test coverage is trimmed. The tech lead can show which modules carry the regulated rate calculations and which are peripheral. How should the project manager direct the deferral?

## Options

- **A**: Defer test coverage on the peripheral, non-rate modules first and keep full automated coverage on the regulated rate-calculation path, logging the deferred tests as debt to repay next release.
- **B**: Defer an even slice of coverage across all modules so no single area is left disproportionately thin and the schedule saving is spread evenly.
- **C**: Defer coverage on whichever modules are largest and most time-consuming to test, since that frees the most schedule for the least number of tests dropped.
- **D**: Keep writing tests in strict backlog-priority order and simply stop when the cutover date arrives, letting the deadline draw the line on coverage.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The escalation has already happened and the debt is owned in the open, so the live decision is *where* to take the risk — and the value at stake is a correct, compliant rate calculation. (A) protects automated coverage exactly where a defect would breach the regulation or misbill customers, and concentrates the deferral on peripheral areas, matching the risk to the consequence and recording the debt for repayment. (B) spreads the cut evenly, which sounds fair but knowingly thins coverage on the regulated path to protect peripheral modules — optimising symmetry over consequence. (C) chooses by testing effort rather than risk, so the rate-calculation modules could lose coverage simply because they are large, exposing the highest-stakes code. (D) lets an arbitrary clock decide what goes untested; priority order need not align with regulatory risk, so the cutover could arrive with a critical rate path only partially covered.
