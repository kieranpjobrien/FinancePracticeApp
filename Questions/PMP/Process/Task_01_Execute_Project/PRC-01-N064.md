# Question

A fintech team must ship a payment-reconciliation capability quickly because a major client's renewal depends on it, and several more increments of related work will follow. The scrum master wants delivery to stay fast across all of those increments, not just this one. Which THREE actions best sustain the fast delivery of value over the increments ahead? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Order the backlog so the reconciliation slice the client needs for renewal is built and released ahead of lower-value items.
- **B**: Hold the team's definition of done intact, including the automated checks, so each release stays shippable rather than accumulating rework.
- **C**: Slice the capability into the smallest releasable pieces and ship each as it is completed, rather than batching the whole capability into one release.
- **D**: Pull the next increment's high-value stories forward into this increment too, so more value is committed while the team has momentum.
- **E**: Bring in two contractors for this increment to add throughput, integrating them into the team's existing practices as they ramp up.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Sustaining fast value over many increments means working on the right things, keeping each increment genuinely done, and shipping in small slices. (A) sequences the renewal-critical slice first so the most valuable outcome arrives soonest. (B) protects the definition of done — the safeguard that stops speed in this increment from breeding rework that drags the next ones. (C) small, releasable slices get value out earliest and surface feedback that keeps later increments aimed correctly. (D) is the strongest distractor: pulling more high-value work forward sounds like maximising value, but overcommitting a single increment inflates work in progress, lengthens cycle time, and stalls the very slice the renewal depends on — it sacrifices durable pace for the appearance of ambition. (E) adding contractors mid-push is a plausible capacity move, yet new people impose ramp-up and coordination load that typically slows this increment and only pays off later if at all, so it does not serve the goal of sustaining fast delivery from now on; the disciplined levers are sequencing, done-ness, and small slices.
