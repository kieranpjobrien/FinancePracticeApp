# Question

A Kanban team delivering a customer-onboarding service has seen cycle time roughly triple over two months while throughput has fallen, and stakeholders are pressing for completed work to resume flowing. Three things changed over the same period: the team raised its work-in-progress limit above its own head-count to "keep everyone busy", the business began sending occasional mid-item priority changes, and a downstream sign-off that one nominated approver performs has grown slower as that person's other duties expanded. Cumulative-flow and cycle-time data have been on the board throughout but no one has worked through them. The project manager has time to take one action first. Which is the most effective first move?

## Options

- **A**: Read the cumulative-flow and cycle-time data already on the board to locate where elapsed time is actually accumulating, and direct the first intervention at whatever that identifies.
- **B**: Lower the work-in-progress limit back to head-count so the team must finish in-flight items before pulling new ones, tightening flow across the board.
- **C**: Secure a second approver or delegated sign-off authority for the downstream review step, so finished work stops waiting on a single person.
- **D**: Agree a policy that in-progress items are not displaced except for a defined urgent class, removing the re-prioritisation churn from the system.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Three plausible causes are live at once — an over-set WIP limit, re-prioritisation churn, and a slowing single-approver step — and each maps to a real, sensible flow remedy (B, C, D respectively). The trap is to pattern-match to a familiar fix and act before confirming which cause actually dominates. The board already holds cumulative-flow and cycle-time data that nobody has analysed, so (A) is the disciplined first move: under the theory of constraints, an improvement only lifts system throughput if it is applied at the genuine bottleneck, and the data is what reveals it — the queue may be sitting at the approval step, but it could equally be churn-driven rework or work piling up because WIP exceeds capacity. (B), (C) and (D) are each defensible *after* the constraint is identified, but committing to any one first gambles scarce recovery time on a guess; improving a non-bottleneck yields little while the real constraint still gates flow. The margin is act-on-the-visible-evidence-first versus jump-to-a-plausible-remedy — not which remedy is "the agile answer".
