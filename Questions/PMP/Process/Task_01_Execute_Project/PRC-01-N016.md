# Question

A subscription media start-up is six weeks from a board funding checkpoint. The team has a thin slice ready that already lets a new customer sign up, pay, and stream content end to end — small in scope but a real paying journey. The founder wants the team to instead spend the six weeks building a polished, broad feature set so the product "demos impressively" at the checkpoint, arguing a richer product will read as more progress to investors. The board's stated checkpoint criterion is evidence that customers will pay. The product owner asks the project manager what to advise. What is the best advice?

## Options

- **A**: Release the thin paying slice to real customers now and spend the remaining weeks widening it by value, so the checkpoint shows actual paid sign-ups rather than an unproven demo.
- **B**: Build the broad polished feature set the founder wants for the checkpoint, since a richer demonstration carries more weight with investors and the thin slice can be released straight after funding.
- **C**: Release the thin slice now but freeze it there and devote the rest of the time to a guided showcase environment, so the board sees a controlled, impressive walkthrough backed by the live slice.
- **D**: Spend two weeks hardening the thin slice into a flawless single-journey demo and hold it back from real customers until the checkpoint, to avoid any live defect embarrassing the team in front of the board.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The checkpoint is decided by one criterion — evidence customers will pay — so value here is real paid conversion, and every option ships *something*, making the margin which one produces that evidence. (A) puts the paying journey in front of real customers now and grows it by value, so by the checkpoint the board sees genuine sign-ups and revenue against their stated test — the strongest possible evidence. (B) chases demo richness, building breadth that investors are not actually measuring while leaving the one thing they asked for — proof of payment — untested until after the decision. (C) keeps the slice live but diverts the remaining weeks into stagecraft; a controlled walkthrough is still a demo, not accumulated paid usage, so it under-uses the weeks that could have been compounding real conversions. (D) withholds a working paying product from customers to perfect a single scripted journey, trading the very paid-conversion evidence the board wants for a polished but unproven show, and delaying all real value to avoid a manageable risk.
