# Question

A grocery delivery start-up is building a same-day order-tracking feature in monthly increments. This increment delivers a live driver-location map; estimated arrival times, delivery photos, and a tipping flow remain on the backlog. The product owner believes a map without an arrival estimate may read as half-built and prompt support calls asking "when will it arrive?", and points out that the next increment could add arrival times within three weeks. The release pipeline can ship either now or with the next increment at no extra cost. The map increment meets the team's definition of done. What should the project manager advise?

## Options

- **A**: Release the live-map increment now, treating the support-call concern as feedback to validate rather than a reason to withhold a done, usable slice.
- **B**: Hold this increment and release it together with estimated arrival times in three weeks, so the first thing customers see answers the question the product owner expects them to ask.
- **C**: Release now but cap exposure to a small percentage of customers, expanding only once support-call volume confirms the map alone does not generate confusion.
- **D**: Keep the map in the increment but add a lightweight "arrival times coming soon" placeholder before releasing, so the slice signals completeness without waiting for the next increment.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

This turns on whether a finished, usable slice should wait for a subjective "coherence" concern that is itself an untested assumption. The map is done and independently useful, so releasing it now delivers value the team has already created and converts the product owner's worry into real data instead of a reason to defer (A). (B) is the most defensible-looking alternative — coupling to arrival times is genuinely valuable — but it gates done value behind three weeks of further work to pre-empt a problem no one has observed, which is the batching trap dressed as customer care. (C) a canary release is sound risk practice, but here it throttles value flow to hedge against a low-stakes, easily reversible concern (the map can be iterated in days), adding control overhead the situation does not warrant. (D) a "coming soon" placeholder ships the same value while spending effort managing perception rather than delivering the next real capability, and risks setting an expectation the team has not committed to.
