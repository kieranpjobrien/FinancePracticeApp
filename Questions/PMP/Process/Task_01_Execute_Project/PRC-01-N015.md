# Question

A defence-systems integration project reports SPI 0.82 and CPI 0.95 at the one-third mark. The customer has made clear the in-service date is the overriding priority and modest cost overruns are acceptable; rigorous integration testing is contractually fixed and cannot be cut. Analysis shows the schedule slippage is concentrated in one critical-path subsystem whose specialist work cannot simply absorb more bodies, while several non-critical work packages have float. What recovery approach best fits this context?

## Options

- **A**: Crash the critical-path subsystem by adding the few additional qualified specialists who can meaningfully contribute, and fast-track only the downstream activities where overlap does not compromise the fixed integration testing.
- **B**: Crash broadly by adding resources across all behind-plan work packages to lift the overall SPI back toward 1.0 as fast as possible.
- **C**: Fast-track aggressively by overlapping the critical subsystem's build with the start of integration testing to claw back the most calendar time.
- **D**: Reallocate the specialists from the floated non-critical packages onto the critical subsystem, accepting that those non-critical packages now slip.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Schedule is the dominant value driver and cost is tolerable, but two constraints bind: the critical subsystem cannot usefully absorb unlimited specialists, and integration testing is fixed. (A) respects both — it crashes the constraint only as far as qualified specialists can genuinely help, and fast-tracks solely where overlap is safe, recovering time without touching protected testing. (B) throws resources at every behind-plan package, but slippage sits on one critical subsystem; spending on non-critical packages and over-staffing a specialist task wastes the tolerable cost without recovering the date (and risks the mythical-man-month effect). (C) buys calendar time by overlapping into integration testing, which is contractually fixed and quality-critical in a defence system — the one overlap that must not be made. (D) starves floated packages to feed the critical one, but those packages have float to spare *now* and forcing more specialists onto a task that cannot absorb them yields little, while creating fresh slippage elsewhere on the path later.
