# Question

A manufacturer's two-year automation project is 60% complete and on budget. Over lunch, a colleague in strategy mentions that the product line the automation serves is likely to be discontinued in about 18 months — well before the automation would pay back its remaining spend. Nothing has been announced and the colleague is not on the steering committee. The project manager judges the source credible but unconfirmed. What is the most appropriate first action?

## Options

- **A**: Escalate the potential change in the business case to the sponsor and steering committee now, presenting options — pivot, descope, or terminate — for their decision.
- **B**: Confirm the discontinuation with the product-line owner or strategy lead so the escalation rests on a verified fact rather than corridor talk, then take it to the steering committee.
- **C**: Add "business case may no longer hold" as a high-priority risk to the register and table it at the steering committee meeting scheduled for next week.
- **D**: Ask the sponsor privately whether they are aware of any plan to discontinue the line, and let their answer determine whether a formal escalation is warranted.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The benefit, not on-plan cost and schedule, governs whether the work should continue — but the trigger here is a single unconfirmed source, so the fine margin is how to act responsibly *and* fast. (B) verifies the one fact the whole decision turns on with the person who would actually know, then escalates, so the steering committee receives a sound basis and can act without being sent chasing a rumour. (A) escalates with options, which is correct in principle, but launching a pivot-or-terminate decision to the full committee on unverified corridor talk risks alarm and wasted cycles if the line is in fact safe; a quick confirmation precedes it. (C) logs the risk but parks a potentially project-ending issue for a week behind a routine agenda, under-reacting to information that may warrant immediate action. (D) tests the sponsor's awareness but makes the sponsor's personal knowledge the gate; the sponsor may also be out of the loop, and a negative answer would wrongly close off the concern — confirming at the source is more reliable.
