# Question

During a sprint focused on a checkout-flow improvement, an influential stakeholder approaches the development team directly mid-sprint and asks them to add a small unrelated promotional banner "while they are in the code", saying it is genuinely urgent for a campaign launching next week. The team is on track to meet the sprint goal and judges the banner to be a few hours of work. The product owner is offsite for two days. What should the scrum master do?

## Options

- **A**: Capture the request and bring it to the product owner to weigh against the current sprint goal and the rest of the backlog, flagging the campaign timing as a priority input.
- **B**: Convene the team and product owner remotely now to decide together whether to pull the banner into the sprint, given the campaign deadline.
- **C**: Let the team fit the banner in this sprint since it is a few hours and the campaign genuinely cannot wait until the product owner returns.
- **D**: Add the banner to the sprint and have the team drop a checkout-flow story of similar size so the committed capacity stays unchanged.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Priority decisions belong to the product owner, so the scrum master routes the request to them with the campaign timing attached; the PO can then decide whether it justifies disrupting the sprint, and the legitimate urgency is still served (A). (B) is well intentioned but escalates a routine prioritisation into an immediate remote meeting that interrupts the offsite PO for a few hours of work, when capturing it for their normal attention suffices given the campaign is a week out. (C) lets the team self-authorise a scope change without the person who owns priority, the classic side-channel that threatens the sprint goal. (D) swaps committed scope on the scrum master's own judgement, a trade only the product owner is entitled to make.
