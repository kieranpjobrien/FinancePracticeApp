# Question

A hybrid software project delivers increments every two weeks. To hit the next two release dates with full scope, the team has been deferring automated test coverage, and production defect rates have risen in each of the last three releases. The business owner is tracking the release dates closely and wants the same cadence and scope maintained. The project manager has the next planning session tomorrow. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the defect trend and its link to the deferred testing to the business owner with the data, and work with them to re-balance the next releases so debt is paid down while value still ships each cycle.
- **B**: Protect the next two committed release dates and scope as the business owner asked, and ring-fence a dedicated debt-and-testing sprint immediately after them.
- **C**: Hold the cadence but quietly trim each upcoming release's scope by enough to restore the missing test coverage, keeping the dates intact.
- **D**: Allocate a fixed share of each sprint's capacity to testing and debt from now on, accepting that some lower-value scope will slip out of the next releases.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The defects are already destroying delivered value and the business owner is accountable for that value, so the project manager must make the trend and its cause visible with evidence and renegotiate the upcoming scope together (A). (B) defends the dates but lets defects keep climbing for two more releases and bets that a future sprint will somehow be protected from the same pressure. (C) reaches a similar capacity split but does it unilaterally and hides the trade-off from the person accountable, eroding trust the moment the dropped scope is noticed. (D) is a sound engineering policy but imposes the scope reduction without surfacing the defect data or securing the business owner's agreement, so the owner learns of the change only when scope goes missing.
