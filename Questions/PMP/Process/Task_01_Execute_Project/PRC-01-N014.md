# Question

An agile team building an internal procurement tool has released a working increment every two weeks; the early increments have already changed two planned features based on real user feedback. The sponsor asks the project manager to switch to a single release at the end of the eight-month build. Their reasons are concrete: staff report being unsettled by frequent changes, each release consumes deployment and comms effort, and the sponsor believes a polished one-time rollout will land better with leadership. The product owner still values the feedback the increments produce. How should the project manager respond?

## Options

- **A**: Show the sponsor that the incremental releases are what surfaced the feedback correcting two features — quantifying the rework avoided — and propose keeping the cadence while tackling the staff disruption directly through release communication and adoption support.
- **B**: Propose trialling a longer release interval for a couple of cycles and measuring whether feedback quality and staff disruption actually change, then decide the cadence on that evidence.
- **C**: Keep deploying every two weeks but batch user-facing communication and training into a monthly rhythm, so staff perceive fewer changes while the team keeps releasing.
- **D**: Accept the single end-of-build release the sponsor wants, but add regular internal demos through the build so the team still gathers feedback before the one live launch.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The sponsor's three concerns are real, but only one of them — staff disruption — is a genuine problem with the cadence itself; the other two (comms effort, leadership optics) are the price of, or orthogonal to, the feedback loop that is already de-risking the build. (A) meets the sponsor with evidence (two corrected features, rework avoided), keeps the value-generating mechanism, and fixes the actual pain through communication and adoption support rather than removing the loop — the disciplined response. (B) sounds evidence-based but proposes experimenting on a system that is already working: lengthening the interval degrades the proven feedback loop on a hunch, and two cycles is slow, weak evidence for an eight-month build. (C) addresses disruption but decouples announcements from delivered value, so staff meet changes that shipped weeks earlier without notice — trading one form of confusion for another. (D) gives the sponsor the rollout they want but swaps real-user feedback for internal demos, a markedly weaker signal that defers all genuine value and learning to a single end launch — the very risk the increments were reducing.
