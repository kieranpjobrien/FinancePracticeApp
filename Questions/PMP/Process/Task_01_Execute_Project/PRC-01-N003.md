# Question

Three sprints into a retailer's loyalty app, the head of marketing tells the team a gamified rewards engine must ship for a campaign launching in two weeks, and offers strong sales projections for it. The work was never in the backlog. The product owner is on leave for a week but is reachable for urgent decisions; a senior developer believes the engine could just fit the upcoming sprint if it displaces the committed sprint goal. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Refine the request with marketing into a backlog item with its benefit and the campaign deadline attached, and hold it for the product owner to prioritise at the next planning event a week away.
- **B**: Treat the reachable product owner as the decision-maker: brief them on the request, the projections, and the trade-off against the current sprint goal, and let them decide whether to interrupt the sprint.
- **C**: Protect the in-flight sprint goal as agreed, and have marketing and the product owner resolve the priority at the next sprint boundary, before the campaign date.
- **D**: Convene marketing, the development lead, and the product owner by call to weigh the engine against the sprint goal together, then proceed on the consensus reached.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A genuine value signal has arrived with a deadline that falls *before* the normal planning cadence, and the accountable decision-maker is reachable, so the question is who decides and how fast. (B) routes an urgent, time-boxed trade-off to the single accountable role with the information needed to choose, respecting both backlog ownership and the campaign clock. (A) is the right shape of action but mis-times it: holding a deadline-bound item for a planning event a week away can blow the two-week window before anyone with authority even weighs it. (C) defends the current sprint goal on principle, but a competing item with credible value and a hard date deserves an explicit owned decision, not automatic deferral to the next boundary. (D) seeks group consensus on a call, which diffuses a prioritisation decision the product owner alone is accountable for and can stall behind competing voices; the PM's job is to put the trade-off in front of the owner, not to manufacture a vote.
