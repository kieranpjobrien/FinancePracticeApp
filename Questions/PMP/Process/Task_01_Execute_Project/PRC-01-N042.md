# Question

A predictive infrastructure project to upgrade a regional water-treatment plant is four weeks behind on the critical path because a permit arrived late. The sponsor needs commissioning to begin on the original date to meet a regulatory deadline, and there is unspent contingency in the budget. Design review and procurement for the next package are scheduled to run one after the other. The project manager is weighing how to claw back the four weeks. Which action best positions the project to recover the time?

## Options

- **A**: Overlap the design review and procurement so they run partly in parallel, after checking that the design is stable enough that ordering early is unlikely to force rework.
- **B**: Bring an additional design engineer and procurement officer onto the two activities, funded from contingency, to shorten each one's duration.
- **C**: Issue the long-lead procurement orders now while the design review continues, so the items are en route as early as possible.
- **D**: Re-sequence the downstream non-critical work to run earlier, freeing float that can absorb part of the four-week slip.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The slip sits on the critical path, so recovery has to come from the critical activities themselves. Overlapping design review and procurement (fast-tracking) recovers time at no extra cost, and the discipline that makes it defensible is confirming design stability first so the early ordering does not trigger rework (A). (B) crashes the work, which does shorten durations but spends contingency and may add coordination overhead for less time saved than overlapping; it is a reasonable second choice, not the best one. (C) overlaps the same two activities but commits to ordering before testing whether the design is stable, exposing the project to expensive rework. (D) reorders non-critical work, which cannot recover a delay that lies on the critical path.
