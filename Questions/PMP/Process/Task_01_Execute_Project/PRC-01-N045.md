# Question

On a predictive project building a customer-facing reporting module, a key deliverable is due to the client tomorrow under a contractual milestone tied to a payment. During final review the project manager confirms the deliverable meets every documented acceptance criterion, but the team has spotted a cosmetic labelling inconsistency the client never specified. Fixing it would push delivery two days past the milestone. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Deliver on time against the agreed acceptance criteria and log the cosmetic issue as a planned correction in the next scheduled update.
- **B**: Hold delivery for two days to fix the labelling first, so the client's first sight of the deliverable is fully polished.
- **C**: Deliver on time, and at handover flag the labelling issue to the client along with the proposed date for its correction.
- **D**: Deliver on time, then raise a change request to add the corrected labelling as formally tracked additional scope.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The deliverable already satisfies every agreed criterion, so meeting the payment-linked milestone preserves value and honours the contract; transparency then requires telling the client about the known cosmetic issue and when it will be fixed, rather than letting them discover it (C). (A) also delivers on time but stays silent about a defect the team is aware of, which is weaker professional practice even though the issue is minor and outside the criteria. (B) sacrifices a payment milestone for a cosmetic item the client never asked for, trading real value for marginal polish. (D) treats correcting an inconsistency the team itself introduced as new billable scope, which inflates process around a trivial fix and may read as opportunistic.
