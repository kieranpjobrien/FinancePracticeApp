# Question

A bank's hybrid project will deliver a mortgage-application platform in a single launch in eight months. A self-service pre-qualification step could be released in ten weeks, cutting broker workload and capturing leads early. The sponsor is cautious for a concrete reason: pre-qualification gives customers an indicative outcome, and the sponsor fears that releasing it before the full application journey exists could create regulatory exposure (customers acting on an indicative result) and reputational risk from an incomplete experience. The early benefit is real, and so is the sponsor's concern. What should the project manager recommend?

## Options

- **A**: Work with compliance and the sponsor to define the conditions under which an early pre-qualification release is safe — clear "indicative only" controls and a defined customer pathway — and bring that conditioned proposal for the sponsor's decision.
- **B**: Propose the early release on the strength of the broker-workload and lead-capture benefits, noting the sponsor's concern but leaving the regulatory and reputational questions to be worked out after approval in principle.
- **C**: Accept the sponsor's caution as decisive and keep to the single eight-month launch, since the regulatory and reputational risks of an early release outweigh ten weeks of earlier benefit.
- **D**: Recommend a limited early release to an internal broker-only audience that captures the workload benefit while avoiding customer-facing regulatory exposure entirely.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The opportunity and the sponsor's concern are both legitimate, so the PM's job is to make the value *achievable* by resolving the specific risk, then let the sponsor decide on a sound basis. (A) engages compliance to turn the sponsor's worry into defined conditions, producing a proposal where the early benefit and the controls are visible together — value pursued without hand-waving the risk. (B) leads with benefits but defers the very regulatory and reputational questions the sponsor raised, asking for approval before the blocking concern is addressed, which is unlikely to earn it and is poor risk practice. (C) treats the concern as automatically decisive and abandons real near-term value without testing whether the risk can be controlled — the easy retreat rather than the disciplined option. (D) is a thoughtful idea, but a broker-only release forgoes the customer lead-capture benefit and may not even need pre-qualification's customer-facing logic; it narrows the value to sidestep a risk that (A) shows can likely be managed while keeping the full benefit in play.
