# Question

A hybrid programme is rolling out self-service kiosks across a regional bus network. Kiosks for the three busiest interchanges have passed acceptance testing; kiosks for the remaining quieter stops are still in development. The operations lead notes that passengers at stops without kiosks may be frustrated to see them at other stops first, and the marketing team had planned a single network-wide launch announcement. Each kiosk works as a standalone unit. What should the project manager recommend to deliver value with urgency?

## Options

- **A**: Deploy the three completed interchange kiosks now, accepting that uneven coverage is a manageable communications matter rather than a reason to withhold a working benefit.
- **B**: Hold the completed kiosks until enough stops are ready that coverage looks even, so passengers at unserved stops are not left feeling overlooked.
- **C**: Deploy the three kiosks now but hold their public announcement until the network-wide launch, so marketing's single-message plan is preserved.
- **D**: Pilot one of the three completed kiosks at a single interchange and pause the other two, gathering usage data before committing to wider deployment.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The contest is whether a working, independently useful benefit should wait on coverage optics or a marketing preference. Deploying the three busiest interchanges now starts cutting queues for the most passengers immediately, which is the essence of incremental delivery (A); uneven coverage is real but is a communications problem to manage, not a reason to sit on finished value. (B) withholds high-impact, ready value to chase uniformity — the classic batching mistake — and the "overlooked passengers" concern is better handled by signalling the rollout plan than by delaying everyone's benefit. (C) deploying but suppressing the announcement is a defensible nod to marketing, yet it muffles the value just delivered and lets a messaging plan, not customer benefit, set the pace. (D) piloting only one kiosk is sound when a concept is unproven, but these units already passed acceptance testing, so holding two finished kiosks for more data needlessly defers value that is ready to flow.
