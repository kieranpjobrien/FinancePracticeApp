# Question

A logistics company's hybrid project must deliver a warehouse-routing optimisation before the peak shipping season in seven weeks, or the benefit slips a full year. The current forecast misses by roughly ten days, and the routing rules for a handful of high-volume lanes carry most of the value. The project manager wants to protect that value without taking on hidden risk. Which THREE actions are appropriate? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Carve out a minimum increment that delivers the high-volume-lane routing at peak, releasing the low-volume-lane rules after the season once they are fully ready.
- **B**: Make the cost and risk of each compression option explicit to the sponsor, so the value-versus-cost trade-off is owned at the level accountable for it.
- **C**: Add experienced contractors familiar with this routing engine for the final two sprints, funding the cost from the approved contingency to close the ten-day gap.
- **D**: Hold the full scope and present the team's best progress at the season deadline, letting the date itself draw the line on what ships rather than pre-committing to a reduced increment.
- **E**: Move the optimisation behind a feature flag and cut over the high-volume lanes at peak, while keeping the engine running the existing routing for every lane until each new rule set is verified live.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

All five are things a PM under a perishable-value deadline might say aloud; the discrimination is which three protect the core benefit without buying the date through hidden exposure. (A) defines a minimum increment that captures the high-value lanes at peak and releases the rest only when *ready*, so no value is shipped half-tested. (B) puts the compression cost-benefit decision with the accountable sponsor rather than the PM absorbing it silently. (C) adds resource that already knows this engine — onboarding drag is low, so the ten-day recovery is credible and the cost is pre-approved, making it a clean schedule buy rather than a mythical-man-month gamble. (D) is the trap: "let the deadline draw the line" sounds disciplined but commits to no plan, so the team most likely arrives at peak with an untested, half-finished superset and ships nothing reliable — it surrenders the value rather than protecting it. (E) reads as a safe phased cut-over, but routing the high-volume lanes through *new* rules at peak is exactly the high-stakes path; gating low-volume lanes while flipping the high-value ones live at the busiest moment concentrates the untested risk where the value and the load are greatest — the opposite of protecting the core benefit.
