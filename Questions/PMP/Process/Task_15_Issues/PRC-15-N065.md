# Question

On an agile e-commerce team, a checkout-timeout defect has been logged, fixed, and closed in three consecutive sprints. Each time it reappears it surfaces in a different part of the journey — once at payment, once at address validation, once at order confirmation — but customers describe the same symptom and conversions are dropping. The product owner wants it gone for good and is also pushing to stop the customer impact this sprint. During sprint planning the team weighs how to handle the latest recurrence. What is the most appropriate response?

## Options

- **A**: Treat the three recurrences as one problem and dedicate effort this sprint to finding and removing their common root cause.
- **B**: Apply a quick guard around the latest failing point now to stop the customer impact, and log the recurrence for the team to fix next sprint.
- **C**: Add monitoring and alerting across the checkout journey so the next recurrence is caught early wherever it surfaces.
- **D**: Reproduce and fix this specific occurrence at the order-confirmation step as a fresh high-priority defect, as the team did for the previous two.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The pattern — one symptom resurfacing in different modules despite three closures — points to a single shared cause the point fixes never reached, so the response that actually ends the cycle is to treat the recurrences as one problem and invest in finding and removing that common cause (A). A quick guard relieves the customer impact but, like the three prior fixes, treats the surface and leaves the underlying cause live to resurface somewhere else next sprint (B). Monitoring shortens detection but improves how fast the team reacts to a recurrence rather than preventing it, so the defect keeps returning (C). Reproducing and fixing this occurrence as a fresh defect is precisely the approach that has already failed three times, repackaged with higher priority (D).
