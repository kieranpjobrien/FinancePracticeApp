# Question

During a daily scrum on a media-streaming team, a developer reports she is blocked because a design decision she needs is owned by the UX designer, who is in the meeting. The two have not discussed it and resolving it will take a detailed conversation about several screens. The daily scrum is half-way through its fifteen-minute timebox and three people have yet to speak. What should the scrum master do?

## Options

- **A**: Note the blocker, agree the developer and designer will meet straight after the standup, and continue the daily scrum.
- **B**: Let the developer and designer work through the design decision now while the others wait.
- **C**: Ask the developer to defer the blocker to tomorrow's daily scrum once she has thought it through.
- **D**: Cancel the rest of the daily scrum so the design conversation can happen immediately.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The daily scrum is a short synchronisation event, not a problem-solving session, so the scrum master records the blocker and arranges a focused follow-up immediately after, allowing the standup to finish on time for everyone (A). Letting the pair solve a multi-screen design problem during the standup blows the timebox and wastes the rest of the team's time on a two-person discussion (B). Deferring the blocker to tomorrow needlessly delays unblocking the developer when the right people are available today (C). Cancelling the remainder of the standup denies the three who have not spoken their synchronisation and is an over-reaction to a blocker that only needs a side conversation (D).
