# Question

A project manager on a renewable-energy construction project keeps a well-maintained issue log reviewed each week. At today's review, several open issues have target resolution dates that passed more than a week ago, yet their status still reads "in progress" and no owner has flagged a blocker. The same owners are active and responsive on other items. What is the project manager's most useful first action?

## Options

- **A**: Contact each owner to establish the true current status, then re-baseline or escalate each overdue issue on its merits.
- **B**: Add a standing agenda item that walks every overdue issue at the weekly review so the team addresses ageing together.
- **C**: Escalate the cluster of overdue issues to the sponsor as a sign that issue resolution is slipping across the project.
- **D**: Tighten the process by requiring owners to update status and re-justify any date before an issue can become overdue.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

An overdue date sitting against an unchanged "in progress" status, with no flagged blocker and from owners who are otherwise responsive, tells the PM only that the *log no longer matches reality* — not whether the issues are actually stuck. The first action is therefore to get the ground truth from each owner and then act per item: re-baseline the date with a reason where work is genuinely progressing, or escalate where it is not (A). Adding a standing review item (B) is a reasonable ongoing improvement but jumps to a recurring process fix before the PM even knows the current facts, and it batches potentially urgent items into a weekly cadence. Escalating the whole cluster to the sponsor (C) presumes a project-wide resolution problem that the data does not yet support — the drift may simply be unupdated records, and escalating ahead of verification wastes credibility. Strengthening the update process (D) addresses the *recurrence* of stale records but does nothing about the issues already overdue right now. The discriminator is establishing the real status before choosing a response, rather than reaching for a process change or an escalation on the strength of a log that is itself unreliable.
