# Question

On a predictive bridge-strengthening project, the site engineer reports during a status meeting that a delivery of structural bolts has arrived with mismatched certification paperwork, halting the affected work front. The bolts cannot be installed until the certification is reconciled, and resolving it will involve the procurement lead, the supplier, and the quality inspector over the coming days. Several team members at the meeting start discussing what to do. How should the project manager respond first?

## Options

- **A**: Capture the problem in the issue log with a description, a single named owner, and a target resolution date, then task the owner to drive it.
- **B**: Ask the procurement lead to phone the supplier from the meeting to demand corrected certificates before the work front loses more time.
- **C**: Redeploy the idle crew to another available work front so the labour is not wasted while the paperwork is sorted out.
- **D**: Walk the meeting through the likely cost and schedule impact of the stoppage so everyone understands what is at stake.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The certification mismatch is a present, materialised problem stopping work — an issue — and the controlling first move is to capture it with a clear description, a single accountable owner, and a target date, then let that owner drive the resolution; without this the matter has no owner and can drift (A). Phoning the supplier is a plausible resolution action, but it is one step the assigned owner takes after the issue is captured and owned, and committing the whole meeting to it now skips establishing accountability (B). Redeploying the crew is sensible mitigation of the idle-labour symptom and may well follow, but it neither captures nor progresses the underlying issue, which would remain untracked (C). Briefing the impact is useful for awareness but produces no owner and no action, leaving the issue exactly where it started (D).
