# Question

A scrum master on a logistics-optimisation team is reviewing five impediments the team has raised and deciding how to act on each. For which TWO is raising it beyond the team the appropriate next step, rather than something the team should handle itself first? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: A firewall rule, controlled solely by central IT, is blocking the team's calls to a required external API; IT has not responded to the team's ticket for four days.
- **B**: The team's continuous-integration server is slow because the team has accumulated many redundant test jobs it could prune itself.
- **C**: A regulatory sign-off that only the compliance office can grant is overdue, and the team cannot release without it despite chasing it twice.
- **D**: Two developers disagree about a design approach and have not yet sat down to talk it through.
- **E**: A third-party library the team chose has a bug, but a maintained fork the team can adopt is freely available.

## Answer

A, C

## Explanation

Raising a blocker beyond the team fits only where it sits outside the team's authority and the team has already done what it reasonably can. (A) qualifies because the firewall is controlled solely by central IT, which has ignored the team's ticket for days, so the team can neither proceed nor fix it alone. (C) qualifies because only the compliance office can issue the sign-off and the team has already chased it twice without success. (B) is within the team's gift — it can prune the redundant jobs itself — so going outside would abdicate an internal fix. (D) is an internal disagreement the developers have not even attempted to discuss, so the team has not yet exhausted its own options. (E) has a ready self-service remedy in the maintained fork, so no outside help is needed.
