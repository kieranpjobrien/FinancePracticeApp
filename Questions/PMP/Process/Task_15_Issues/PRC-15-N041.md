# Question

During a daily scrum on a software delivery team, a developer mentions that a shared test environment has been intermittently failing for two days, which is slowing several stories but has not yet blocked any of them outright. The environment is run by a separate platform team, not the delivery team. The scrum master notes it. What is the most appropriate next action?

## Options

- **A**: Log it as a tracked impediment with an owner and target, and take it to the platform team to get the environment stabilised.
- **B**: Ask the developers to route around the instability with local or mocked test setups for now, and keep the slowdown on the team's radar without formally tracking it yet.
- **C**: Raise it to the product owner so a story to harden the test environment can be prioritised into a future sprint backlog.
- **D**: Hold it as a watch item and raise it formally only if it escalates into an outright blocker, to keep the impediment list focused on things stopping work today.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A current, known problem already costing the team time is an impediment the scrum master is accountable for clearing; because the environment belongs to the platform team, tracking it with an owner and target and taking it to that team is what actually drives resolution before the slowdown becomes a stoppage. (B) offers a reasonable interim workaround but leaving the underlying impediment untracked means no one is accountable for the real fix, and the cause persists. (C) misclassifies an operational impediment as product scope and defers it to a future sprint — environment stability the team needs now is not a backlog increment for the product owner to prioritise. (D) waits for the problem to worsen before acting, which forfeits the lead time that makes impediment removal effective. The discrimination is between clearing it through the accountable party now versus working around, deferring, or waiting.
