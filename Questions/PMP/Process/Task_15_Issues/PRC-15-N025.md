# Question

During sprint execution on a retail e-commerce team, a developer discovers that the payment-gateway sandbox the team was told to integrate against has been decommissioned by the vendor, so the in-progress checkout story cannot be completed or tested this sprint. The developer raises it at the daily scrum. How should the team handle ownership and the immediate next step?

## Options

- **A**: The product owner takes it, decides whether checkout still justifies the effort given the disruption, and reprioritises the sprint backlog around the blockage.
- **B**: The developer takes it and spends the day building a temporary mock of the gateway so the checkout logic can at least be unit-tested while the environment is sorted out.
- **C**: The scrum master takes it as an impediment and works with the vendor and product owner to secure a replacement sandbox or environment.
- **D**: The team logs it and raises the lost environment with the vendor through the formal supplier-escalation channel, leaving the checkout story blocked until the vendor responds.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

An external environment being pulled out from under the team is a classic impediment, and owning the effort to clear it — coordinating with the vendor and the product owner — is the scrum master's responsibility, so the block is actively worked rather than simply absorbed. (C) puts ownership where it belongs and drives toward restoring the environment. (A) is a real product-owner power and may well follow, but reprioritising the sprint around the block accepts the loss of value before anyone has tried to remove the impediment — it is a reasonable second move, not the first. (B) is a sensible technical mitigation a developer might do in parallel, yet a mock does not restore an integration or system test, and one developer improvising does not get the environment back; it complements clearing the impediment rather than replacing it. (D) correctly identifies that the vendor must be engaged, but routing it straight to a formal escalation channel and then waiting is slower and more passive than the scrum master owning it and pursuing the vendor and a replacement actively. The discriminator is who drives the impediment to closure and whether the team removes the block before descoping or waiting.
