# Question

An issue has been open for three weeks on a project that depends on a regulatory change. The change sits at the boundary between two departments, and each department head maintains it is the other's cost to bear. The project manager has facilitated several meetings between them; both remain firm, and the dependent work cannot start until the funding question is settled. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Keep facilitating between the two department heads, reframing the cost-sharing options until one of them agrees to fund the change.
- **B**: Escalate the issue to the steering committee or sponsor with a clear summary, options, and the impact of continued delay, requesting a funding decision.
- **C**: Fund the regulatory change from the project's management reserve to keep the dependent work moving, and record which department should reimburse it later.
- **D**: Document the dependency as formally blocked, set the dependent work aside, and revisit it once either department changes its position.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A decision two peer department heads will not make, after three weeks of facilitation, has to move to a level that can actually adjudicate it; escalating to the steering committee or sponsor with options and the cost of delay is how a stalled cross-boundary funding dispute gets resolved. (A) is tempting because facilitation is the PM's instinct, but the same approach has already failed repeatedly and the PM cannot compel either budget, so more meetings will not break a genuine standoff. (C) reaches for management reserve, which exists for in-scope unknowns within the project, not to settle whose department pays — and spending it pre-empts a decision that is not the PM's to make. (D) accepts an open-ended block on regulatory-dependent work and waits passively, instead of using the governance route built for exactly this deadlock. The discrimination is between escalating, persisting with a failed tactic, overstepping with project funds, or doing nothing.
