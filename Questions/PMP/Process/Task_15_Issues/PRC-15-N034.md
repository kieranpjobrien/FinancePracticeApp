# Question

On a large rail-signalling upgrade, two open issues now conflict. Resolving Issue A (a safety-certification gap) the way the engineering team proposes would consume the contingency that Issue B's owner is relying on to resolve a supplier-penalty exposure. Both owners have escalated to the project manager, each insisting their issue takes precedence. What is the project manager's best course of action?

## Options

- **A**: Direct the contingency to Issue A, because anything involving safety certification automatically outranks a commercial penalty.
- **B**: Assess both issues together against project priorities, constraints and risk, and make or escalate a single informed decision on how the shared contingency is used.
- **C**: Split the contingency evenly between the two issues so neither owner is left without resources to act.
- **D**: Tell both owners to resolve their issues without drawing on the contingency, since it should be preserved for genuinely unforeseen events.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The two issues are competing for one constrained resource, so the PM must evaluate them jointly against priorities, constraints and risk and then make or escalate one coherent decision rather than letting each owner pull in opposite directions. (A) applies a slogan ("safety always wins") without analysing whether the certification gap actually needs that contingency now or whether the commercial exposure is the larger threat. (C) splits the resource arbitrarily and may leave both issues underfunded and unresolved. (D) refuses to use contingency for two real, materialised issues, which is precisely a legitimate use, and abdicates the prioritisation decision.
