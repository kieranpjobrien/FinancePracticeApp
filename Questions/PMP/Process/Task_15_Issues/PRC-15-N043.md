# Question

A project manager on a hybrid insurance-platform programme returns from leave to find the issue log holds 34 open issues, several months old, with no severity ratings and overlapping owners. The sponsor is asking why issues "never seem to close". What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the team together to work the 34 issues down in a focused push, starting from the oldest, so the backlog visibly shrinks and the sponsor sees movement.
- **B**: Give the sponsor a candid summary of the log's state and a date by which it will be triaged and back under control, before changing anything in the log itself.
- **C**: Triage the log, rating each issue by impact and urgency and confirming a single accountable owner and next action, before deciding what to escalate.
- **D**: Reassign every issue to a single co-ordinator who will chase each owner daily, so accountability for closing the backlog sits in one place.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

An unprioritised, unowned log cannot be managed, so the first move is to triage it into severity with clear single-owner accountability and a next action — which then tells you what genuinely needs escalating, resourcing or closing. (A) burns the team on whichever issues happen to be oldest, with no view of which actually matter, and "movement" on low-impact items is not progress. (B) is good stakeholder management and should follow, but reassuring the sponsor before you understand the log gives a commitment you cannot yet substantiate; the triage has to come first. (D) centralises chasing without first establishing which issues are real or urgent, so one co-ordinator is left pursuing 34 unrated items — accountability for the wrong things. The choice is which first move makes the log manageable, and only triage does.
