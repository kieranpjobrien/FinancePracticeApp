# Question

A project manager on a predictive pharmaceutical-packaging upgrade notices that several issue-log entries have sat at "in progress" for weeks with no updates, while the assigned owners report in meetings that they are "on top of things." Deadlines on two of those issues have already passed. The project manager wants to restore control over the log. What is the most effective action?

## Options

- **A**: Institute a regular issue-review cadence where each owner accounts for status, blockers, and a revised resolution date for every open entry.
- **B**: Reassign the overdue issues to the project manager to drive them personally to closure.
- **C**: Add a programme-wide metric for ageing and overdue issues and report it to the sponsor each period.
- **D**: Require every issue to carry an impact assessment and a documented resolution plan before it is accepted onto the log.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Stale, unmoving entries are a monitoring-and-control failure, and a regular review cadence where each owner must account for status, blockers, and a revised date re-establishes accountability and surfaces what is genuinely stuck, while keeping ownership with the team — the direct remedy for owners who claim progress that the log does not show (A). Reassigning the overdue issues to the project manager strips the owners of the accountability that drives closure and turns the project manager into a bottleneck (B). An ageing metric reported to the sponsor improves visibility of the symptom but, on its own, neither explains why entries are stuck nor moves them, so the staleness persists (C). Requiring impact assessments and plans up front raises the quality of new entries but does nothing for the entries already languishing, which is the actual problem (D).
