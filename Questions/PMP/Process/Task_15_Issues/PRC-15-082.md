# Question

During a daily stand-up on an agile payments platform, a developer says a shared test environment has been throwing intermittent errors for two days, costing the whole team rework, and that everyone assumed someone else was looking into it. The Scrum Master confirms it is not on the impediment board. What is the most effective next step?

## Options

- **A**: Add it to the next retrospective agenda so the team can agree how to prevent shared-environment problems in future.
- **B**: Ask each developer to log the errors they personally hit so the team can size the total impact before deciding what to do.
- **C**: Capture it as a tracked impediment with a single named owner accountable for driving it to resolution, then follow up daily until it clears.
- **D**: Raise it directly with the platform vendor's support desk, since an external party owns the environment infrastructure.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The core failure is that a live impediment is hurting the team yet nobody owns it; the fix is to make it visible and assign a single accountable owner who drives it to closure with daily follow-up. (B) delays action to quantify impact that is already obviously material, leaving the team to keep bleeding time. (A) defers a current blocker to a future retrospective, which addresses prevention but not the problem hurting this sprint. (D) may be a step the owner later takes, but jumping straight to the vendor skips ownership and tracking, and the root cause may not even be the vendor's.
