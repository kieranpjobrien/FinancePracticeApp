# Question

At sprint review, stakeholders praised a demo that went smoothly and are now planning downstream work on the assumption the feature is done. Afterwards, a junior developer privately tells the scrum master that the demo only worked because the team hard-coded a result to mask an unresolved integration defect they were embarrassed to surface. The defect remains open and undocumented. What should the scrum master do first?

## Options

- **A**: Get the defect logged as an open issue and ensure the product owner and stakeholders learn the feature is not actually complete, before they build on it.
- **B**: Work with the team to log the defect and pull the real fix into the next sprint, then have it demonstrated genuinely before correcting the stakeholders' understanding.
- **C**: Raise it at the next retrospective so the team can examine why they felt unable to surface the defect and agree how to make it safe next time.
- **D**: Privately coach the junior developer who came forward, reinforcing that raising the concealment was the right call and they will be supported for it.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The immediate hazard is that stakeholders are about to commit downstream work on a feature that does not work; transparency means the scrum master's first move is to get the defect on the issue log and correct the false "done" signal before others build on it. (B) eventually tells the truth but only after the next sprint, leaving stakeholders acting on a known-false status in the meantime — the concealment continues. (C) and (D) both matter for the underlying fear that caused the hiding and should follow, but addressing team safety or thanking the messenger while the inaccurate status stands leaves the live deception uncorrected. The discrimination is sequencing: restore an accurate, transparent status now; address the conditions that produced the concealment next.
