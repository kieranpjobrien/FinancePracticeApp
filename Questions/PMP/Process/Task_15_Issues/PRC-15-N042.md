# Question

On a predictive bridge-rehabilitation project, the project manager learns that the steel supplier has just gone into administration. No deliveries have been missed yet, but the next critical-path delivery is due in three weeks and no alternative supplier is qualified. How should the project manager classify and handle this in the project records?

## Options

- **A**: Record it as a new risk in the risk register, since no delivery has actually been missed and the impact is still in the future.
- **B**: Record it as an issue in the issue log, assign an owner, and trigger response planning because the triggering event has already occurred.
- **C**: Add it to the assumptions log, since the team had assumed the supplier would remain solvent throughout delivery.
- **D**: Update the risk register entry that already covered "supplier delivery failure" by raising its probability to near-certain, and manage it through the existing risk response.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The uncertain event — the supplier failing — has already happened, so it is no longer a risk but a present issue that needs an owner and an active response now. (A) misapplies the risk register to a condition that has already materialised. (C) is tempting because the solvency assumption has indeed been falsified, and recording that is fair, but logging a broken assumption is not the same as managing the live, critical-path problem it created. (D) is the subtlest trap: a related risk may well have existed, but once a risk's trigger occurs it converts into an issue and is managed as one — leaving it on the risk register and merely raising its probability denies that it has already materialised and keeps it out of active issue management. The discrimination is recognising that a triggered risk becomes a present issue, not a higher-probability risk or a documented assumption.
