# Question

A scrum master is triaging several impediments the team has raised. Escalation beyond the team is warranted only where the blocker genuinely sits outside the team's authority to resolve and the team has already done what it reasonably can. For which THREE is active escalation the appropriate next step? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: A corporate firewall rule, controlled by central IT, is blocking access to a needed external service; the team has raised a service ticket that has sat unactioned for a week.
- **B**: A required software licence is unpurchased; the team has prepared the justification, but the spend sits with a department head outside the team who has not yet approved it.
- **C**: A dependency on another team's deliverable is slipping; the team has agreed a revised date with that team's lead, who is now meeting it.
- **D**: An external vendor keeps missing an interface specification; the team has clarified the spec twice, but the vendor's account manager will not commit a fix date without his director's sign-off.
- **E**: The team disagrees internally on a coding approach and two members have asked the scrum master to "get a ruling" from the engineering manager.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

The give-away the question masks is that escalation hinges on two things together — the blocker is outside the team's authority *and* the team has exhausted what it can do itself. (A), (B) and (D) meet both: a stalled central-IT ticket, a spend decision owned by another department, and a vendor whose fix is gated above the account manager all require someone with authority the team lacks, and in each case the team has already taken the reasonable first steps. (C) is the trap — it looks like a classic cross-team escalation, but the team has already resolved it itself by agreeing a new date that is being met, so there is nothing to escalate. (E) sits inside the team's own authority: an internal technical disagreement should be resolved through self-organisation and light facilitation, not by outsourcing the decision to a manager.
