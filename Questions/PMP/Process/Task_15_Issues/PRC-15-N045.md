# Question

Midway through a two-week sprint, a third-party API the team depends on starts returning errors, putting the sprint goal at risk. The vendor's support queue quotes a five-day response time, and the organisation holds a paid support contract with a named vendor-management contact. The scrum master has logged the impediment. What is the best next step for the scrum master?

## Options

- **A**: Escalate through the organisation's vendor-management contact to shorten the vendor's response, and work with the team on an interim way to keep progressing toward the sprint goal.
- **B**: Bring the team and product owner together to re-plan the sprint backlog around the outage, so a revised goal can still be met without the API.
- **C**: Have the developers build a temporary stub that mimics the API's responses, so the team can keep coding against it until the vendor restores service.
- **D**: Raise it with the product owner to decide whether the sprint should be cancelled, since a dependency failure may have made the sprint goal unattainable.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Removing impediments is the scrum master's core accountability, and the strongest move both attacks the blocker at its source — escalating via the vendor-management relationship rather than waiting in the standard queue — and keeps the team moving with an interim approach, preserving the sprint goal. (B) jumps to re-scoping before any attempt to clear the impediment; re-planning is the lever once removal genuinely fails, not the first response. (C) is a sensible part of "an interim workaround" but on its own it leaves the actual outage unescalated, so the team codes against a stub indefinitely while the root problem festers. (D) reaches for cancellation, which is reserved for when the sprint goal is obsolete or truly unattainable — not yet established here, since the impediment has not even been worked. The discrimination is between clearing the impediment now versus prematurely re-scoping, partially patching, or abandoning the sprint.
