# Question

A Kanban-based infrastructure team is working under a strict work-in-progress limit. A card sits in the "In Progress" lane flagged as blocked because the network team has not opened a required firewall port. The blocked card is consuming a WIP slot, and pulling new work would breach the limit. The team lead asks how to handle the blocked card. What should the project manager advise?

## Options

- **A**: Temporarily raise the WIP limit so the team can pull another card and stay busy while the block persists.
- **B**: Keep the blocked card visibly flagged and prioritise clearing the firewall dependency with the network team before pulling anything new.
- **C**: Move the blocked card back to the backlog so the lane frees up and the team can pull new work immediately.
- **D**: Split the blocked card into smaller cards so at least some of the work can progress within the WIP limit.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

In Kanban a blocker is a signal to swarm on the impediment, not to route around it; keeping the card visible and clearing the firewall dependency is the disciplined response that respects the WIP limit. (A) inflates the limit to mask a block, defeating the purpose of WIP limits and hiding the real constraint. (C) hides the impediment by shoving it back to the backlog, losing context and visibility of a live problem. (D) is a useful technique for genuinely separable work but here the whole card is blocked by one external dependency, so splitting does not unblock it.
