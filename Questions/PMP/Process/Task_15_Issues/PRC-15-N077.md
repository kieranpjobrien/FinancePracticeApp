# Question

A project manager on a hospital-laboratory relocation project has just logged a newly raised issue: a calibration gas needed to commission analysers has failed an incoming purity check, blocking commissioning. The project manager wants to manage this issue well through to closure. Which THREE actions are appropriate parts of properly managing this issue? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Assign a single owner and set a target resolution date proportionate to its impact.
- **B**: Analyse the impact on commissioning scope, schedule, and cost so the response is informed.
- **C**: Immediately raise a change request to extend the schedule before any resolution is attempted.
- **D**: Track the issue's status through resolution and formally close it once verified resolved.
- **E**: Delete the issue from the log as soon as a workaround is identified, before it is confirmed effective.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

Sound issue management assigns clear ownership with a proportionate target date (A), assesses the impact so the chosen response is informed rather than guessed (B), and tracks the issue to a verified resolution before closing it (D) — together a complete, controlled lifecycle. Raising a change request to extend the schedule before any resolution is even attempted presumes an impact prematurely and may be wholly unnecessary if the issue is quickly resolved (C). Deleting the issue the moment a workaround is identified, before confirming it actually works, removes visibility and risks the problem resurfacing unrecorded, which is the opposite of formal closure after verification (E).
