# Question

At a daily scrum for a SaaS analytics team, two developers begin a detailed back-and-forth about how to resolve a data-migration defect one of them raised; it threatens a story central to the sprint goal. The exchange is technical and involves only the two of them, and the rest of the team is standing idle as the event passes the fifteen-minute mark. What should the scrum master do?

## Options

- **A**: Let the discussion run to its conclusion here, since the defect threatens the sprint goal and resolving it now is more valuable than holding to the timebox.
- **B**: Note the defect, confirm one of the two developers owns it, and ask them to continue the resolution in a session straight after the stand-up.
- **C**: Close the stand-up on time as usual, and arrange a separate problem-solving session later in the day once the wider team's input can be gathered.
- **D**: Bring the whole team into the discussion now, since a sprint-goal threat is a shared concern the team should resolve together before dispersing.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The daily scrum is the developers' event for inspecting progress toward the sprint goal and adapting the plan, not a forum to solve a defect that only two people need; the scrum master keeps it on track by confirming ownership and moving the deep-dive to immediately after, so the defect is addressed without delay and the rest of the team is freed. (A) protects the goal but lets two people consume the whole team's event and erodes the timebox the team relies on. (C) is tidy but pushes resolution of a sprint-goal threat to "later in the day" and pulls in wider input that is not needed, losing urgency. (D) treats a two-person technical problem as whole-team work, keeping everyone idle on something most cannot contribute to. The choice turns on acting on the threat promptly while protecting the event — not on whether the timebox or the goal "wins".
