# Question

A predictive water-treatment plant project is winding down its main construction contract. The client has signed off the deliverables and the contractor has submitted its final invoice. The project manager is working through the procurement closeout and wants to take the next step correctly. Which action should the project manager take?

## Options

- **A**: Verify that every contractual obligation, including outstanding defects-liability items, has been discharged before authorising final payment and issuing the closure notice.
- **B**: Authorise the final payment against the submitted invoice and issue the formal completion notice, then reconcile any remaining obligations afterwards.
- **C**: Issue the formal completion notice now to release the contractor, and process the final payment once finance completes its routine checks.
- **D**: Hold the closeout open and retain the final payment until the full defects-liability period has elapsed, regardless of the current defect status.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Procurement closure follows a sequence: confirm all contractual obligations are satisfied, then settle the final payment and issue formal notice that the agreement is closed. (A) is correct because verification of obligations, including any open defects-liability items, must precede payment and the completion notice. (B) reverses that order, paying and certifying completion before confirming nothing is outstanding, which can certify a contract that is not actually complete. (C) issues a completion notice while payment and verification are still pending, so the notice is premature and the closeout is not evidenced. (D) withholds payment for the maximum liability period as a blanket rule even where obligations may already be met, tying up funds and the contractor's release without a basis tied to actual status.
