# Question

A predictive municipal water-treatment upgrade has been accepted and the project manager is completing administrative closure. The organisation has a records-management policy specifying retention periods, classification, and a central repository. The as-built drawings and acceptance record will be needed by the operations team, and the contracts may be needed for a future audit. The operations manager has asked for her own copy of everything so her team can find it easily. How should the project manager handle the final document set?

## Options

- **A**: Archive the full set in the organisation's central repository under the records-management policy's classification, retention, and access rules, and point operations to it.
- **B**: Place the set in the operations team's own shared drive as the primary copy, since they are the main day-to-day users and need the fastest access.
- **C**: Archive only the contracts and acceptance record centrally for the audit trail, and let the operations team hold the as-built drawings, treating drawings as a working operational document rather than a closure record.
- **D**: Issue controlled copies of the full set to operations and each key stakeholder so every party retains its own complete record alongside the central archive.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Project records are organisational assets, so the closure aim is a single authoritative archive in the central repository governed by the records-management policy for retention, classification, and access; operations can then be granted access rather than holding the master (A). (B) makes a team drive the system of record, which suits one team's convenience but loses organisation-wide governance, retention enforcement, and discoverability for audit. (C) fragments the record by splitting custody on a false distinction — the as-built drawings are a closure deliverable and an operational reference, and excluding them from the controlled archive risks the authoritative version drifting. (D) multiplies "complete" copies across parties, which undermines version integrity and access control even with an archive in place; access to the single archive, not proliferating masters, is what good records governance provides.
