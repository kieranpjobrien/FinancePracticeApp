# Question

A predictive bridge-strengthening project has finished construction and passed its load testing. The client's site engineer has walked the structure and verbally told the project manager she is happy with it. The contract ties final payment to sign-off and a defects-liability period begins at handover. The project manager is ready to start administrative closure and the sponsor is keen to release the team to the next job. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Obtain the authorised client representative's formal written acceptance of the deliverables against the acceptance criteria.
- **B**: Issue the final invoice now, since the engineer has confirmed satisfaction and payment can run in parallel with the remaining paperwork.
- **C**: Walk the deliverables through the project's own quality checklist again to be certain no defect surfaces during the liability period before involving the client.
- **D**: Release the specialist subcontractors first so the sponsor's deadline is met, then complete the acceptance formalities with the client.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The verbal comment from the site engineer is encouraging but is not acceptance; formal closure is triggered by documented acceptance from the authorised representative against the agreed criteria, and here it also unlocks payment and starts the liability clock cleanly. (A) is therefore the correct first step. (B) acts on an informal nod and invoices before the entitlement is established, which is premature and risks dispute. (C) is a defensible quality instinct, but re-running an internal check before securing client acceptance inverts the sequence and delays the event that actually permits closure; the client's sign-off is the gate, not another internal pass. (D) releases resources before responsibility has transferred, so if acceptance surfaces snags the people needed to fix them are gone — the deadline pressure does not justify reversing the order.
