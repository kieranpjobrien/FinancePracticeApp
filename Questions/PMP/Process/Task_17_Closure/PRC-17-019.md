# Question

A predictive enterprise-payroll implementation has passed every test in the agreed acceptance plan, and the results are signed by the client's own testers. Despite this, the client's sponsor is withholding final sign-off, saying the system "doesn't feel complete," but cannot name any unmet acceptance criterion. The contract's terms favour the project, and the project manager needs to move towards closure. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Present the signed test results and the acceptance criteria to the sponsor as evidence the criteria are met, and ask the sponsor to confirm sign-off on that basis.
- **B**: Note the formal entitlement to acceptance, but defer pressing for sign-off and let the sponsor sit with the system for a short settling-in period before revisiting.
- **C**: Meet the sponsor to draw out the specific concern behind "doesn't feel complete," compare it against the agreed acceptance criteria, and route anything genuinely new through change control.
- **D**: Escalate to the project's steering committee to rule on whether the criteria have been satisfied and direct the sponsor to sign.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Acceptance is being withheld without a stated criterion, so the first move is to understand the real concern before invoking evidence, entitlement, or authority. (C) engages the sponsor to surface the issue, tests it against the agreed criteria, and uses change control for anything genuinely new — resolving the block while protecting scope and the relationship. (A) is reasonable but leads with proof against a stakeholder who has not yet articulated the problem; pressing the paperwork before hearing the concern often hardens resistance rather than removing it. (B) is conflict-avoidant: a settling-in period may help, but passively waiting does not progress closure and leaves the concern unexamined. (D) escalates to governance before any attempt to understand the sponsor — disproportionate as a first step and damaging even where the contract favours the project. Understanding precedes evidence and escalation.
