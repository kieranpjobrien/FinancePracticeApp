# Question

Halfway through a regional retail-expansion project, the steering committee decides to cancel it because a competitor has acquired the target sites. There are part-built fit-out designs, an open contract with a shopfitting vendor, and a team drawn from several functions. The project manager is asked to wind the project down in an orderly way and the committee wants people freed quickly. Where should the project manager focus the closure effort?

## Options

- **A**: Move straight to settling the vendor contract and releasing the team, since with no further deliverables the usual closure documentation adds little for a project that was stopped.
- **B**: Document the completion level reached and the cancellation rationale, capture and store the reusable designs and lessons, then settle the contract and release the team through formal closure.
- **C**: Preserve every part-built design and open work item in full detail in case the expansion is revived later, before turning to contract settlement and resource release.
- **D**: Prioritise the vendor closeout and final accounts above all else, treating documentation of completion and reusable outputs as optional given the cancellation.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A terminated project is still closed formally, and the closure must do three things together: record how far the work got and why it was stopped, preserve reusable outputs and lessons for the organisation, and discharge obligations such as the vendor contract before releasing people. (B) keeps all of these in balance. (A) and (D) both subordinate the learning and reuse value to a fast financial/administrative closeout — settling the contract is necessary, but skipping the completion record and reusable designs discards information the organisation paid for and may need. (C) over-invests in archiving every open item against a speculative revival; closure should capture what is genuinely reusable proportionate to its value, not freeze the entire workspace, and it still has to settle the contract and release the team. The discriminator is balance, not picking the single most urgent administrative act.
