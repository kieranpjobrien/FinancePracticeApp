# Question

An agile programme has delivered its final release and the product is in production. The team holds frequent iteration retrospectives, and the scrum master argues that because retrospectives have happened throughout, a separate end-of-project review adds no value and the team should move straight to their next assignments. The PM disagrees. What is the strongest justification for holding an end-of-project review before the team disperses?

## Options

- **A**: The end-of-project review is a governance deliverable the PMO requires before the project can be formally closed, so it has to be completed.
- **B**: Iteration retrospectives feed improvements straight back into the next sprint, so their lessons stay within the team and are not consolidated for the wider organisation to reuse.
- **C**: The team should hold a formal event to mark the end of the project and recognise their contribution before they are reassigned.
- **D**: An end-of-project review looks across the whole initiative for systemic and cross-team lessons that sprint-bounded retrospectives do not surface, and records them as organisational assets.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Iteration retrospectives optimise the next sprint and are bounded to recent, team-level events, whereas an end-of-project review examines the whole initiative for systemic, cross-team patterns and preserves them for reuse beyond the team. (D) is the strongest justification because it names the distinct analytical value the final review adds and ties it to capturing durable organisational lessons. (B) correctly notes that retrospective improvements stay local, which is a real gap, but it describes the shortfall rather than the consolidating purpose and durable output that make the final review worthwhile. (A) leans on a procedural mandate and misses the substantive reason the review matters. (C) names a genuine but secondary benefit, closure and recognition, that does not by itself justify the review's analytical purpose.
