# Question

A predictive office-relocation project has delivered all scope and the sponsor has accepted the outcome. The finance business partner is waiting to close the project's cost centre. The project manager's records show several purchase orders still open with vendors that have delivered everything ordered, and a small unspent contingency balance. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Confirm with finance that the cost centre can be closed and let any residual purchase-order activity and the contingency be swept up under the organisation's standard year-end reconciliation.
- **B**: Reconcile and close the outstanding purchase orders, confirm no further charges are due, and release the unspent contingency before the cost centre is closed.
- **C**: Keep the purchase orders open and the cost centre active until the vendors' warranty periods lapse, so any follow-on charges can still be billed to the project.
- **D**: Close the purchase orders and the cost centre now, and ask finance to hold the unspent contingency against the project in case a late charge appears.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Financial closure requires settling every open commitment, confirming no further costs will be incurred, and releasing unused funds so the accounts show a true final position before the cost centre closes. (B) is correct because reconciling and closing the purchase orders and releasing the contingency lets the project's finances close cleanly and completely. (A) hands the project's own reconciliation duty to a generic year-end process, leaving the closing accounts unverified and possibly carrying open liabilities. (C) keeps commitments and the cost centre open for warranty matters that belong to operations after handover, distorting the financial record and tying up funds. (D) closes the cost centre yet asks finance to ring-fence funds against it, which is contradictory and leaves a notionally closed project still holding money.
