# Question

An agile team has shipped the final increment of a customer-facing payments platform, which is live and processing transactions. The product owner has accepted the functionality. A standing site-reliability team, which was not involved in delivery, will own the service long-term. The project manager has a fixed window to close out before the squad is reassigned and is deciding what to prioritise in that window. What should the project manager focus on?

## Options

- **A**: Securing the product owner's formal acceptance certificate for the release, as that sign-off is the gate that authorises closure.
- **B**: Running working sessions that transfer runbooks, on-call procedures, and the platform's failure modes to the site-reliability team.
- **C**: Arranging a ninety-day hypercare period during which the original squad stays on call to handle incidents while the site-reliability team observes.
- **D**: Publishing the source code, architecture diagrams, and design decisions to the shared repository for the site-reliability team to study.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

When ownership passes to a team that did not build the product, the transition is complete only once that team can actually operate and support it, which depends on transferring operational and tacit knowledge, not just recording acceptance or leaving artefacts. (B) is correct because direct knowledge transfer of runbooks, on-call procedures and failure modes is what makes the permanent owner operationally ready within the closeout window. (A) is a genuine closure gate, but acceptance is already given and a certificate alone leaves the new owners unable to run the service. (C) bridges the gap by keeping the wrong group accountable beyond the project and defers the real transfer rather than achieving it before the squad disperses. (D) supplies reference material but omits the hands-on transfer needed for the receiving team to respond under live conditions.
