# Question

Reading "minimal documentation" as "none", a Scrum team stopped recording architecture decisions. Three sprints on, two squads have built incompatible integration approaches because neither knew the other's choices. The sponsor wants this fixed without heavyweight upfront specs. The two squads share a regular scrum-of-scrums and a common code repository. What should the project manager facilitate?

## Options

- **A**: Have each squad keep lightweight decision records for significant architectural choices, made part of their definition of done, and reviewed where the squads already meet.
- **B**: Stand up a shared architecture decision log that both squads write to whenever a cross-cutting choice is made, and review it at the scrum-of-scrums.
- **C**: Add a brief architecture-alignment segment to the existing scrum-of-scrums so squads surface and agree integration decisions as they arise.
- **D**: Have each squad capture its own architectural decisions in its sprint documentation so the choices are recorded close to the work.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The specific failure was cross-squad: each squad may well have known its *own* decisions — what was missing was visibility of the *other's* integration choices. A shared decision log both squads write to, reviewed where they already coordinate, directly closes that gap with just-enough documentation, so (B) is best. (A) is good practice and nearly right, but per-squad records made part of each squad's done can still leave each blind to the other unless they are deliberately shared — it improves discipline without guaranteeing cross-squad visibility. (C) adds useful conversation but, like the verbal coordination that already failed once squads scaled, leaves nothing durable for future members or later disputes. (D) keeps decisions inside each squad's own documents — exactly the siloing that caused the incompatibility.
