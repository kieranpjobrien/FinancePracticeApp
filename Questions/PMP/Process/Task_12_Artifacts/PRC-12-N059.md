# Question

Three sprints into a mobile-banking project a stakeholder challenges why a two-factor authentication feature was built the way it was, saying it does not match what was agreed. The product owner recalls a refinement discussion that settled the approach but cannot point to where it was recorded; the backlog item only said "implement 2FA". The project manager wants to stop this ambiguity recurring on future items. What is the most useful artifact practice to introduce?

## Options

- **A**: Capture acceptance criteria and the rationale for significant design choices on each backlog item, so both the agreed behaviour and why it was chosen are recorded with the work.
- **B**: Keep a separate decision log in which the product owner records the key choices made during each refinement session.
- **C**: Require each backlog item to carry detailed acceptance criteria defining the expected behaviour before it enters a sprint.
- **D**: Record refinement discussions in the sprint review notes so the reasoning behind each item is preserved alongside the demo.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The stakeholder's challenge had two parts — *what* was agreed and *why* it was built that way — and capturing both the acceptance criteria and the decision rationale on the item itself keeps them together and traceable to anyone who later questions it; (A). (C) acceptance criteria fix the agreed behaviour but, without the rationale, still would not answer *why* this approach was chosen over alternatives, which is what was actually disputed. (B) a separate decision log can hold the rationale but splits it from the item it concerns, so future readers must cross-reference two artifacts and the link is easily lost. (D) sprint review notes capture what was demonstrated, not the refinement reasoning, and burying decisions in review minutes makes them hard to trace back to a specific item.
