# Question

A project manager coaching a new agile team at an insurance firm hears four different recurring complaints, each pointing to a missing or misused artifact. Match each complaint to the artifact that most directly addresses it. Each artifact is used once.

## Items

- **1**: "A choice we settled two months ago came back this week, and nobody could say what we landed on or what trade-off we accepted, so we argued it out again from scratch."
- **2**: "A test failed on a feature and we spent half a day working out whether the feature was even meant to behave that way, and which business need it traces back to."
- **3**: "A sponsor stopped by and asked how the iteration was tracking, and three of us gave three different answers because there was nowhere to just point and show them."
- **4**: "In planning the team argued over whether a particular item was actually accepted work for this release or still somebody's wish-list idea."

## Matches

- **A**: A decision log (or architecture decision records)
- **B**: A requirements traceability matrix
- **C**: An information radiator (e.g. a task board or burndown on display)
- **D**: A prioritised, refined product backlog

## Answer

1-A, 2-B, 3-C, 4-D

## Explanation

Each complaint is a symptom; the match is the artifact whose specific content closes that gap. A settled choice resurfacing with no record of what was decided or why is what a decision log prevents, so item 1 pairs with (A). Not knowing whether a feature is behaving as intended, and which need it serves, is a broken trace from requirement to deliverable — the requirements traceability matrix restores it, giving item 2 to (B). Three people giving three answers about iteration progress is the absence of a visible, shared status display, which an information radiator supplies, mapping item 3 to (C). A dispute over whether an item is committed work or still just an idea is resolved by a prioritised, refined backlog that draws the line between accepted scope and unrefined wishes, pairing item 4 with (D). The discriminator is what each artifact actually records — rationale for decisions, requirement-to-deliverable links, visible current status, or the committed-versus-proposed boundary.
