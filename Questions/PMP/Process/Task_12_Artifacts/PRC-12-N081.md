# Question

A newly formed agile team at a logistics start-up tracks its work in a spreadsheet that the scrum master maintains and opens when asked. During the iteration, developers repeatedly message the scrum master to ask which items are still open and who holds what, and twice two people unknowingly started the same task. The scrum master has a half-day to invest in one change before the next iteration. What should the scrum master do first?

## Options

- **A**: Put the iteration's work on a shared board the whole team can read and update directly throughout the day.
- **B**: Have each developer announce at the daily stand-up what they will pick up next, so overlaps are caught before work starts.
- **C**: Set a per-person work-in-progress limit so fewer items are open at once and collisions become less likely.
- **D**: Give every developer edit access to the spreadsheet and require an "owner" to be recorded the moment an item is started.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Every option is a legitimate agile practice and each touches the symptoms, so the choice turns on which one removes the condition producing them rather than easing them. The condition is that the team's work status lives somewhere only the scrum master operates, so it is invisible until someone asks and is never current between asks. Putting the work on a shared board the team reads and updates directly (A) makes status continuously self-service — it ends the constant queries and lets a developer see an item is already taken before duplicating it, in the same move. (B) coordinates intentions, but only once a day; for the rest of the day the picture is as opaque as before, and items picked up after stand-up still collide. (C) is sound flow hygiene that lowers the odds of a clash, yet it manages how much is in flight without making what is in flight visible, so the queries and the blind starts persist. (D) is the closest rival — shared edit access and a recorded owner could in principle expose status — but a spreadsheet someone must deliberately open is not an at-a-glance radiator, so the information still has to be sought rather than simply seen. The fine margin is between making status continuously visible and merely improving when or how it is recorded.
