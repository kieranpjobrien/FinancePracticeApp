# Question

On a hybrid project the governance body requires a signed design specification at a defined level of detail before build starts; the delivery team works in two-week iterations and says a full document of that depth is outdated before the ink dries. The PM has confirmed the governance gate is a genuine assurance obligation, not a preference, and that the team's iteration approach is delivering well. A design checkpoint is two weeks away. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Produce the full signed specification the gate names, then let the team treat it as a fixed reference and capture subsequent design evolution in their iteration artifacts.
- **B**: Work with governance and the team to agree which design decisions genuinely need locking before build and the lightest artifact form that still gives the gate its assurance, documenting only that at the checkpoint.
- **C**: Have the team produce just-enough design notes at the depth they judge useful, and present those to governance at the checkpoint as the project's design record.
- **D**: Sign off only the architecturally significant decisions now and defer the rest of the specification to a second gate once the iterations have settled the remaining design.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The obligation is real but its *form* is negotiable, so the move is to agree with both sides which decisions truly need locking before build and the lightest artifact that still satisfies the gate's assurance purpose — tailoring the document to need rather than to a fixed template. That is (B). (A) honours the gate but pours effort into a full-depth document whose detail the iterations will outdate, the exact waste in question, and "fixed reference" understates how much will change. (C) optimises for the team but lets the team set the assurance bar unilaterally; the gate is a confirmed obligation and may reject notes pitched purely at the team's convenience. (D) is a credible tailoring tactic — baseline the significant decisions, defer the rest — but it presumes a two-gate structure governance has not agreed to and commits to splitting the sign-off before testing whether a single lighter artifact would satisfy them; the discrimination against (B) is that (B) renegotiates the artifact with the gate owner first rather than imposing a phasing on them.
