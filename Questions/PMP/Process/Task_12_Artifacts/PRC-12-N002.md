# Question

A scrum team has moved from a co-located office to fully remote work. The team has rebuilt its task board and burndown inside its delivery tool and keeps them current each day, but the stakeholders who used to glance at the wall now email the Scrum Master several times a day asking for status, and a couple have said they do not know where progress lives any more. What should the Scrum Master do first?

## Options

- **A**: Send the stakeholders a concise written status summary each day drawn from the digital board and burndown.
- **B**: Give the stakeholders read access to the digital board and burndown and walk them through how to read progress for themselves.
- **C**: Add a short stakeholder review to the end of each sprint where the team walks the board and burndown and answers questions.
- **D**: Ask the product owner to take the stakeholder status questions so the development team is not interrupted mid-sprint.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The artifacts already exist and are current; what has broken is that the stakeholders lost their line of sight to them, so the most direct fix is to restore self-serve visibility by granting access and teaching them to read it, which answers the recurring questions at source without anyone having to relay status (B). A daily written summary (A) does inform them, but it duplicates information that is already live in the tool, lags reality between sends, and keeps the Scrum Master as the conduit rather than removing the dependency. A sprint-end stakeholder review (C) is good practice and worth having, but its cadence does not satisfy people who want a status read several times a day, so the emails continue between reviews. Routing the questions to the product owner (D) shields the developers but simply moves the same manual relaying onto the PO instead of restoring the transparency that makes relaying unnecessary. The fine margin is that A, C, and D each keep a person in the loop pushing status on some cadence, whereas only B re-establishes the radiator so the information serves itself.
