# Question

At the start of a mid-sized hybrid project the project manager is defining how artifacts will be managed. The team is small and co-located, the client is risk-averse and audit-conscious, and a few subcontractors will need controlled access to specific documents. The organisation's PMO offers a full artifact-control procedure originally written for a defence programme, plus a lighter template for small internal projects. How should the project manager proceed?

## Options

- **A**: Tailor the artifact-management approach to this project's scale, audit sensitivity, and controlled-access needs, recording which controls apply and why.
- **B**: Start from the PMO's defence procedure and strip out the controls that are clearly disproportionate for a small co-located team.
- **C**: Start from the PMO's lighter small-project template and add the access controls and audit-trail features the client and subcontractors require.
- **D**: Ask the audit-conscious client which of the two PMO procedures it would prefer the project to adopt, and apply that one.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The right approach is to fit the controls to *this* project's specific drivers — its size, the client's audit sensitivity, and controlled subcontractor access — and to document the tailoring decisions; that is (A), and it is what tailoring means regardless of which template is the starting point. (B) and (C) are both reasonable *mechanics* of tailoring, but each fixes a single starting template and risks anchoring on it — trimming the defence procedure may leave disproportionate overhead, while padding the light template may miss audit needs — and neither captures the rationale that makes the result defensible. (D) outsources a project-management judgement to the client and frames it as an either/or between two off-the-shelf procedures, when the project's needs may not match either as written.
