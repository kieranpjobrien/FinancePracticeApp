# Question

An agile team delivering an internal HR portal maintains a 40-page solution design document, updated each iteration to match the code, which the team estimates costs about a day per iteration. In a retrospective the developers say they never consult it and that the working software and backlog already tell them the design. The document appears on the organisation's governance checklist of required artifacts, though no one on the team knows why it is there. The project manager wants to stop spending effort that adds no value. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Drop the document from the iteration now and redirect the day to delivery, since the people who use the design no longer rely on it.
- **B**: Keep the document on the checklist but cut updates to once per release, so the artifact survives at a fraction of the cost.
- **C**: Continue updating it as before; it is a mandated governance artifact and the team is not the body that can decide it is unnecessary.
- **D**: Ask the governance owner what need the document serves and who relies on it, then retire or trim it only if that need is genuinely already met elsewhere.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The team has clear evidence the document adds no value to them, but the artifact was placed on the governance checklist by someone outside the team for a reason the team admits it does not know — possibly audit, regulatory, or a downstream consumer invisible from where they sit. The lean response is to eliminate waste, yet eliminating it responsibly means first establishing with the governance owner what the artifact is actually for, then retiring or paring it back once that need is confirmed met (D). (A) reclaims the day but acts unilaterally on a requirement the team has just acknowledged it cannot see the basis for, risking a real compliance gap. (B) reduces the cost but keeps producing an artifact whose purpose is still unverified, treating frequency as the question when the real question is whether it is needed at all. (C) defers entirely to the checklist and never tests whether the requirement still holds, preserving waste rather than challenging it. The fine margin is between cutting waste now and cutting it only after confirming with the one party who knows why the artifact exists.
