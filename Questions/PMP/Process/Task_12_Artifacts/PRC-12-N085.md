# Question

A defence-sector systems-integration project is closing under time pressure. The receiving operations team needs a working subset of the artifacts to run the system from day one and is pressing for the handover. The contract also imposes statutory retention on certain technical and contractual records, and the organisation's information-security policy requires controlled disposal of anything not retained. The project manager is deciding how to sequence the closure of the artifact set. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Classify the full artifact set against the retention, handover, and disposal requirements before moving, transferring, or deleting anything.
- **B**: Transfer the operational subset to the operations team straight away so they are unblocked, then sort retention and disposal for the remainder.
- **C**: Archive the statutory-retention records first, since failing that obligation carries the gravest legal exposure, then handle handover and disposal.
- **D**: Securely dispose of everything outside the contractual retention list first to close the security exposure, then complete the handover.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Each option advances a legitimate closure obligation, so the question is what must come first — and the trap is that handover, retention, and disposal all draw from the same undivided pile. Until every artifact is classified by which obligation governs it, any of the other moves risks acting on the wrong items: an operational copy handed over may also be a record that must be retained, and a file that looks disposable may be statutorily protected. Classifying first (A) is the step that makes the subsequent transfer, archive, and disposal safe and is therefore the correct starting point. (B) unblocks operations but may release or duplicate records before their retention status is settled, and a working subset is not necessarily a disposable one. (C) prioritises the highest-exposure obligation, which is tempting, but you cannot reliably pull the retention set out of an unclassified pile without first distinguishing it. (D) acts on disposal before classification and is the most dangerous order, since it can destroy material operations needs or records that should have been kept. The discrimination is sequencing under shared inputs, not picking the one action that does everything.
