# Question

A Scrum team's product owner maintains the backlog in the team's tooling. A senior business sponsor keeps a separate "priorities" deck that he updates after each steering meeting and expects the team to follow. The two now order the next several items differently, and the developers do not know which to build next. The sponsor's deck reflects genuine recent business decisions the product owner has not yet incorporated. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate a working agreement that the backlog is the team's single ordered queue, and that the sponsor's steering decisions are fed to the product owner to reflect there before the team acts on them.
- **B**: Bring the product owner and sponsor together to reconcile the two orderings now, and have the product owner update the backlog to match the agreed result.
- **C**: Have the product owner pull the sponsor's latest deck into the backlog after each steering meeting so the backlog always reflects his priorities.
- **D**: Ask the sponsor to bring his priorities to backlog refinement and agree the ordering with the product owner directly in the tool.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

There are two problems — today's conflicting order *and* the structural fact that a second priority list exists with no agreed authority — and only (A) addresses both by establishing the backlog as the single queue and routing the sponsor's input through the product owner, who owns ordering. (B) resolves the immediate clash but settles nothing about which artifact governs, so the next steering meeting reproduces the conflict. (C) keeps the backlog current but treats the sponsor's deck as the real source the backlog merely mirrors, undercutting the product owner's accountability for ordering. (D) is a good mechanism for the sponsor's input and may well form part of the agreement, but on its own it does not resolve that two competing lists exist or whose ordering prevails when they differ.
