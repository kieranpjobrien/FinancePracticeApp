# Question

A pharmaceutical project is closing. Its artifacts are mixed: some records carry a multi-year regulatory retention requirement, some contain personal data that privacy law says must not be kept beyond its purpose, and the rest are routine working files. The organisation has a records retention and disposition policy. The project sponsor wants the closure done quickly and asks the PM to "just get it archived safely". How should the project manager handle the archive?

## Options

- **A**: Classify the artifacts against the retention and disposition policy and archive, retain, or dispose of each category accordingly.
- **B**: Archive the entire project record set under the longest applicable retention period, so nothing required is ever lost.
- **C**: Archive the regulated and working records together and route the personal-data records to the privacy or legal team to handle under privacy law.
- **D**: Apply the retention policy to the regulated records and the personal data, and archive the routine working files in bulk to save time.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Different record types carry conflicting obligations, and the organisation already has a policy that resolves them, so applying that policy across *all* categories is the correct, defensible approach — (A). (B) blanket long retention seems safe but actively breaches privacy law for the personal data, which must not be kept beyond its purpose, so "keep everything" is not the safe option here. (C) handles the personal data correctly but lumps regulated and working records together and applies no disposition rule to the working files, leaving part of the archive ungoverned. (D) applies the policy to the sensitive categories yet exempts the working files from it; some "routine" files may still be project records that the policy governs, so bulk-archiving them without classification is premature.
