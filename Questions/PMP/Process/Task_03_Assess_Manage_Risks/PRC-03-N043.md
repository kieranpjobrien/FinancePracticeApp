# Question

To address the threat of a key specialist subcontractor being unavailable during a critical pharmaceutical validation phase, the project manager has just signed a standby contract with a second firm. While briefing the team, the quality lead points out that the standby firm uses a different validation toolset the quality team has never reviewed, which could itself jeopardise the validation. The standby firm will only ever be engaged if the primary subcontractor falls through. What should the project manager do about the toolset concern?

## Options

- **A**: Analyse and respond to it as a fresh threat introduced by the standby contract, prioritising it alongside the project's other risks rather than only if the standby firm is engaged.
- **B**: Add it to the contingency plan for the original unavailability risk, so the toolset review is only triggered if and when the standby firm is actually brought in.
- **C**: Accept and document it as the unavoidable leftover exposure of the subcontractor-availability risk now that a response has been put in place.
- **D**: Hold contingency reserve to cover a failed validation caused by the unfamiliar toolset, since the exposure is real but the standby firm may never be used.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Signing the standby contract created a new threat that did not exist before (an unreviewed toolset)—a secondary risk. The disciplined move is to bring it into the risk process in its own right: identify, assess, and prioritise it with the others, then choose a response (A). Crucially, the toolset can be reviewed proactively, before any engagement, so its exposure can be reduced cheaply now. (B) defers all action to the contingency trigger, which forfeits the chance to review the toolset early and leaves the secondary risk unassessed in the meantime. (C) misframes it as residual risk—the leftover portion of the original threat—when it is a distinct, newly introduced one, and acceptance skips analysis of a threat that could be mitigated. (D) jumps to a single response (reserve) before the secondary risk has been assessed, and funding a failure does nothing to reduce the chance of one when a proactive review is available.
