# Question

A significant approved change adds a new third-party integration to a banking project's scope. The project manager has already updated the schedule, budget, and resource plans. The integration vendor is well known and has flagged a couple of obvious threats, and the change board expects the project manager to keep overall exposure within the project's risk thresholds. Turning to risk, what should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Run a focused risk identification and analysis on the changed scope, covering how the integration creates new risks and reshapes existing ones, then update the register and responses accordingly.
- **B**: Add the specific threats the vendor flagged to the risk register with responses, since those are the concrete new risks the change has introduced.
- **C**: Reassess the project's overall risk exposure against its thresholds to confirm the added scope has not pushed the aggregate beyond tolerance.
- **D**: Increase the contingency reserve in proportion to the size and cost of the change, to cover the additional uncertainty the new integration brings.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A scope change is a trigger for a proper reassessment of the changed area: systematically identifying and analysing what the new integration introduces and how it alters existing risks, then updating the register and responses. (A) is the foundational first step that the others depend on. (B) captures only the threats the vendor happened to surface—a partial list that skips the team's own identification and the effect on existing risks, so it is part of (A), not a substitute. (C) is a valid and important check, but you cannot meaningfully judge overall exposure against thresholds until the new individual risks from the change have actually been identified and assessed; aggregate analysis comes after, not before. (D) sizes a response before the new risks are known, putting funding ahead of assessment. Identifying and analysing the change's risks first is what makes the threshold check and any reserve adjustment defensible.
