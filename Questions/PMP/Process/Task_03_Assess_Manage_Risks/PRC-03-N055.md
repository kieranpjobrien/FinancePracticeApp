# Question

On an offshore wind project, a novel foundation design carries a real but uncertain chance of failing structural validation, which would force an expensive redesign late in the schedule. The design is central to the project's cost advantage, so abandoning it would undermine the business case. Validation is months away. Which response best fits this threat at this point?

## Options

- **A**: Add an early-stage prototype and accelerated test of the foundation design now, to reduce the probability of a late validation failure before the design is committed.
- **B**: Switch to a conventional, proven foundation design so the validation risk is removed from the project entirely.
- **C**: Place the foundation works with a fixed-price specialist subcontractor so the supplier bears the cost if the design has to be reworked.
- **D**: Record the risk and hold a contingency reserve to fund a redesign if validation fails, taking no action until then.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Every option is a legitimate strategy, so fit decides. The threat is central to the value case, uncertain, and still months from validation—conditions that favour mitigating the probability now, while change is cheap, by prototyping and testing early (A). (B) avoids the risk but discards the design the business case depends on, so it removes the threat by sacrificing the benefit. (C) transfers cost contractually, but suppliers price uncertain design risk heavily and may exclude it; transfer also does nothing to reduce the chance of failure, which is what hurts the schedule. (D) is passive acceptance that leaves a known, reducible risk to surface at the worst time—late and expensive—when an early, affordable mitigation exists. The presence of a cheap early action to lower probability makes mitigation the strongest fit.
