# Question

On a hybrid pharmaceutical packaging-validation project, the team adopts a response to a supply threat: it dual-sources a critical sterile component from a second approved vendor. During a response-planning review, an analyst points out that the second vendor's lead times are less predictable, which could itself introduce schedule variability, and that even with two sources a regional shortage could still partially affect supply. The project manager wants the risk records updated correctly. How should these two effects be recorded?

## Options

- **A**: The lead-time variability from the new vendor is a secondary risk arising from the response; the remaining shortage exposure is residual risk that persists after the response.
- **B**: Both effects are residual risks, since both remain after the dual-sourcing response has been put in place.
- **C**: The lead-time variability is residual risk left over from the original threat; the remaining shortage exposure is a secondary risk created by adding a vendor.
- **D**: Both effects are secondary risks, because both became visible only once the dual-sourcing response was designed.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A secondary risk is a new risk introduced by implementing a response, whereas residual risk is the exposure that remains after the response is applied. Dual-sourcing introduces the second vendor's lead-time unpredictability—a risk that did not exist before the response, hence secondary (A). The shortage that could still occur despite two sources is what is left of the original threat after responding, hence residual. (B) miscategorises the new lead-time risk, which the response created rather than left behind. (C) inverts both definitions. (D) is wrong because the lingering shortage is a remnant of the original threat, not something the response generated; visibility during planning does not make a risk secondary. The discriminator is whether each effect was created by the response (secondary) or merely survives it (residual).
