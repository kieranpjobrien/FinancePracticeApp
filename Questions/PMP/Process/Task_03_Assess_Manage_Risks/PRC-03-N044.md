# Question

Midway through an agile programme to modernise a retail point-of-sale system, a cloud vendor announces a new managed service that could replace three months of custom integration work the team had planned. The vendor's launch promotion, which materially improves the commercial case, closes in five weeks. The development team is keen, the architecture group will need to weigh in on a platform change, and the migration effort is not yet understood. The project manager is deciding how to take this forward. What is the best first action?

## Options

- **A**: Add a time-boxed spike to the next sprint to test the managed service's fit and migration effort, and bring the findings to the product owner to weigh against the five-week promotion window.
- **B**: Have the product owner reprioritise the backlog now to slot the migration ahead of lower-value work, so the team can still capture the promotion before it closes.
- **C**: Take the opportunity to the architecture group first for an in-principle decision on the platform change, since their endorsement is needed before the team commits effort either way.
- **D**: Ask two engineers to begin a parallel proof-of-concept migration straight away, treating the build itself as the fastest way to learn whether the service works.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The opportunity is real but the effort is unknown and a deadline is pressing, so the first move is to reduce the unknowns cheaply and put the value trade-off where it belongs—a bounded spike whose findings the product owner weighs against the closing window (A). The other options are all plausible ways to pursue the opportunity but each commits before the team has learned enough. (B) reorders the backlog and effectively commits to the migration before fit or effort is known, betting scarce capacity on an unvalidated saving to beat the clock. (C) routes to architecture for a decision before anyone can tell them what the migration involves; their input matters, but seeking a verdict ahead of any assessment front-loads governance over learning. (D) starts an open-ended build rather than a time-boxed investigation—it can learn the same things but without a fixed budget or a decision point, risking sunk effort on a service that may not fit. The discriminator is investigating within a bound and returning the call to the value owner, versus committing capacity, governance, or build effort first.
