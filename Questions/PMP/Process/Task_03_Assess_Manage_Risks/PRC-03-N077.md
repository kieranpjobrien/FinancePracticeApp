# Question

A project manager preparing a quantitative risk analysis for a large rail-signalling programme is selecting the right analytical technique for each question the sponsor has asked. Match each sponsor question to the technique whose output most directly answers it.

## Items

- **1**: "Across all our uncertainties combined, what is the probability we finish the project within the £40M budget?"
- **2**: "Of all our individual risks, which few have the greatest influence on the overall cost outcome, so we know where to focus?"
- **3**: "For the make-or-buy decision on the signalling control unit, which option gives the better expected value once the chance of rework is factored in?"

## Matches

- **A**: Sensitivity analysis (e.g. a tornado diagram)
- **B**: Monte Carlo simulation
- **C**: Decision tree analysis

## Answer

1-B, 2-A, 3-C

## Explanation

Each question maps to the technique whose output fits it. A probability of meeting an overall budget across combined uncertainties comes from Monte Carlo simulation, which produces a distribution of total outcomes (1-B). Identifying the few risks with the greatest influence on the outcome is the purpose of sensitivity analysis, typically visualised as a tornado diagram (2-A). Choosing between defined options while weighting probabilistic outcomes such as rework is decision tree analysis using expected monetary value (3-C). The trap is that all three are quantitative techniques; what distinguishes them is whether the sponsor wants an aggregate probability (simulation), a ranking of drivers (sensitivity), or a choice between discrete options (decision tree).
