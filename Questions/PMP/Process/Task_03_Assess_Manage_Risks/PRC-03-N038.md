# Question

While planning risk management for a pharmaceutical-facility build, the project manager finds that the sponsor is highly risk-averse about patient-safety-related risks but willing to take schedule risk, whereas the operations director is the reverse. This divergence is causing disagreement over which risks deserve responses. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Apply the project manager's own balanced judgement of acceptable risk so a single consistent standard is used throughout.
- **B**: Default to the most risk-averse stakeholder's view in every category so no one's tolerance is exceeded.
- **C**: Escalate the disagreement to the steering committee for a ruling before any risk planning continues.
- **D**: Work with stakeholders to define agreed risk thresholds by category, reflecting the organisation's risk appetite, and record them to guide prioritisation.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Differing risk attitudes are reconciled by establishing explicit, agreed risk thresholds (often by category) that express the organisation's appetite and then drive consistent prioritisation; this is core to the risk management plan. (D) resolves the root cause. (A) substituting the project manager's personal tolerance ignores the legitimate, differing stakeholder and organisational appetites that should govern the project. (B) blanket worst-case aversion across all categories may over-spend on areas where the organisation is genuinely willing to take risk. (C) escalation is premature; the project manager can and should facilitate agreement on thresholds before involving the committee.
