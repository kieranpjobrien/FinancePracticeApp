# Question

On a hybrid programme, the predictive hardware track has a contingency reserve of 15 working days, drawn from quantitative schedule risk analysis. Three months in, the team has consumed 12 of those days on risks that have now passed, but several large schedule risks remain in later phases. The sponsor asks whether the reserve is "on track". What is the most appropriate response from the project manager?

## Options

- **A**: Reassess the remaining schedule risks and recalculate the reserve needed for the rest of the programme, reporting whether the residual reserve is adequate rather than just how much is left.
- **B**: Report that 3 of 15 days remain and request an immediate top-up to restore the reserve to its original 15 days.
- **C**: Report that the reserve is on track because consumption (12 days) is proportional to elapsed time (three months of roughly one year).
- **D**: Release the remaining 3 days back to the organisation since the early high-impact risks have now passed.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Reserve adequacy is judged against remaining exposure, not against the original figure or elapsed time. (A) re-evaluates the risks still ahead and asks the real question—is what's left enough? (B) reflexively tops up to the old number without analysing whether 15 was ever right for the remaining risks. (C) confuses calendar progress with risk burn-down; consumption proportional to time says nothing about whether the residual reserve covers later threats. (D) prematurely surrenders reserve while significant later-phase schedule risks are still open.
