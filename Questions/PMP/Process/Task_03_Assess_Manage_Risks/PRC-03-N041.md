# Question

During a daily scrum, two developers mention in passing that the third-party payment gateway the product depends on has begun returning intermittent timeouts in the test environment. No current sprint item touches payments, and the team is keen to keep the standup to its timebox. The Scrum Master wants the signal handled properly without derailing the event. What is the best way to handle it?

## Options

- **A**: After the standup, log it as a candidate risk, flag it to the product owner, and table it for analysis at the next backlog refinement.
- **B**: Capture it on the team's risk board immediately during the standup and ask the two developers to give a rough probability and impact before the event closes.
- **C**: After the standup, raise it directly with the product owner and let them decide whether it warrants a place in the team's risk handling at all.
- **D**: After the standup, add it to the impediment board so it is visible and tracked alongside the team's other blockers until someone picks it up.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

An emergent signal must be captured so it is not lost, routed to the person who owns value (the product owner), and assessed in the proper forum (refinement)—all without consuming the standup. (A) does all three. (B) respects capture but pulls analysis into the timeboxed standup, which is for coordination, not risk scoring, and forces premature estimates on two people. (C) routes to the product owner but skips logging the candidate risk and effectively delegates the keep-or-drop decision before any assessment, so a real threat could be dismissed unrecorded. (D) treats an unrealised future threat as a current impediment; the gateway is not blocking any active work yet, so the impediment board is the wrong artefact and it bypasses risk assessment entirely.
