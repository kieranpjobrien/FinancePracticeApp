# Question

A two-year construction project will run a riverside site office through one wet season. Historical data puts the chance of a damaging flood at roughly 8%, with an estimated impact of $3 million in lost equipment and rework. The site must stay where it is to remain close to the build. The project manager has a modest contingency reserve and is choosing a response. Which option best fits this threat?

## Options

- **A**: Buy flood cover for the site office and equipment, paying a premium so the financial impact falls on the insurer if the flood occurs.
- **B**: Move equipment to higher ground each evening and bund the office perimeter during the wet-season months to reduce the impact if a flood occurs.
- **C**: Fund the full $3 million within the project's own contingency reserve and proceed, monitoring river-level forecasts for early warning.
- **D**: Bring the most flood-exposed work forward so it completes before the wet season, shortening the window during which the office is at risk.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four are real responses, so the choice turns on fit. The impact is large ($3M), the probability low, the location fixed, and the reserve modest—conditions under which transferring the financial consequence to an insurer for a premium is the most efficient response (A). (B) is mitigation that lowers impact but cannot eliminate a $3M loss and imposes daily cost and effort across the whole wet season; it also leaves the office structure exposed. (C) is active acceptance, but self-funding $3M from a modest reserve concentrates a catastrophic loss the project may be unable to absorb. (D) is a partial avoidance/mitigation that only helps for work that can move; the office itself, and any work that cannot be resequenced, stays exposed. Transfer is the best match for a rare, severe, unavoidable financial exposure.
