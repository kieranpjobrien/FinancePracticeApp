# Question

A bridge-rehabilitation project faces a threat that an unproven welding technique specified in the design could fail inspection, causing costly rework. The project manager proposes replacing the unproven technique in the design with a well-established welding method that inspectors already accept, removing the possibility of that particular failure. Which threat response strategy is the project manager applying?

## Options

- **A**: Avoid, by changing the plan so the threat can no longer occur.
- **B**: Mitigate, by reducing the probability that the threat occurs to an acceptable level.
- **C**: Transfer, by shifting the consequences of the threat to the welding subcontractor.
- **D**: Accept, by acknowledging the threat and holding contingency in case it occurs.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Avoidance eliminates the threat by altering the plan so the risk cannot materialise; substituting an accepted welding method removes the unproven-technique failure mode entirely. (B) mitigation only lowers probability or impact while the threat still exists, but here the cause is removed outright. (C) transfer would keep the technique and merely shift financial consequences to a subcontractor, not eliminate the failure. (D) acceptance takes no action to change the situation, which contradicts the deliberate design change described. The total removal of the failure possibility is the signature of avoidance.
