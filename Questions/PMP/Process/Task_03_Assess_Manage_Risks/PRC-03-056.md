# Question

During a risk identification workshop, the project's most senior engineer speaks first and confidently names three risks. Subsequent contributors largely restate those three with minor variations. The PM recognises this as anchoring bias. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Continue the workshop but explicitly ask each subsequent person whether they have any genuinely different risks to raise.
- **B**: Pause the workshop, have each attendee silently write their top risks, then share each in round-robin without interruption.
- **C**: Split the workshop into two smaller groups with different facilitators to diversify the conversation and reduce seniority effects.
- **D**: Invite the senior engineer to step out of the workshop for the remaining time so others can contribute without anchoring.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Nominal group and silent-write-then-share techniques are the PMI-recommended counter to anchoring bias in facilitated risk work. (B) applies the technique directly. (A) polite prompting rarely overcomes the anchor without structure. (C) splitting groups can create two anchors rather than one. (D) excluding a senior contributor is disrespectful and damages participation culture.
