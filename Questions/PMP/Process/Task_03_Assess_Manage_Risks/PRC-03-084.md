# Question

A SaaS delivery team running two-week iterations has historically reviewed risks only at the start of each quarterly increment. Two threats materialised mid-increment last quarter with no prior reassessment, and the team wants its risk handling to keep pace with how quickly conditions change. Which approaches would help the team assess and manage risk more continuously within its cadence? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Make a short review of the top risks and their early-warning signs a standing part of each iteration's planning and review events.
- **B**: Include risk and impediment discussion in the retrospective so the team can adapt its responses based on what the last iteration revealed.
- **C**: Encourage team members to raise emerging risks during daily coordination as soon as warning signs appear, rather than holding them for the increment boundary.
- **D**: Make the Scrum Master the single owner of risk between increments, channelling all emerging risks through that person to keep the team focused on delivery.
- **E**: Keep the quarterly review as the only point where the risk register is formally updated, to avoid duplicating risk records across multiple events.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Continuous risk handling in agile means weaving reassessment into the regular events rather than batching it, so embedding a top-risk review in iteration planning and review (A), using the retrospective to adapt responses (B), and surfacing warning signs in daily coordination (C) all shorten the loop between change and response. (D) is plausible because someone often facilitates risk tracking, but funnelling all risk through one role undercuts the shared ownership that lets the whole team spot exposures early; it concentrates rather than distributes attention. (E) sounds tidy—one authoritative update point avoids duplication—but keeping the register frozen between quarters reinstates the very batching that let last quarter's two threats mature unnoticed; visibility, not record-keeping neatness, is the goal. The discriminator is increasing the frequency and shared ownership of reassessment versus concentrating it in one role or confining updates to a single periodic event.
