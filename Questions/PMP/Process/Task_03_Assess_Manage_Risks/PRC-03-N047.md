# Question

An agile team tracks a risk burndown alongside its task board. Across the last three sprints the team has closed a steady number of risks each sprint, yet the chart's total exposure line has barely moved. At the review, the product owner points to the closed-risk count as evidence the team is on top of risk and proposes carrying on unchanged. The project manager is preparing what to say. What is the most appropriate response?

## Options

- **A**: Note that flat exposure despite steady closures points to new exposure arriving as fast as it is retired, and ask the team to look into what is driving emergence and whether responses are reducing exposure as intended.
- **B**: Agree the closure rate is the meaningful signal, and propose tracking closed-risk throughput per sprint as the primary measure so the team can see the progress the exposure line is obscuring.
- **C**: Accept that exposure has levelled off and treat that as a stable plateau, refocusing the team's risk effort on the few highest-severity items rather than the overall trend.
- **D**: Suggest the recent additions are mostly low-value noise inflating the line, and tighten the bar for what gets added so the burndown reflects only material risks.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Flat total exposure while risks are being closed means new exposure is entering at roughly the rate old exposure leaves—the closure count is real but says nothing about whether the project is actually getting safer. (A) reads the signal correctly and drives the right investigation: why are risks emerging, and are responses biting? The other three are each a coherent-sounding move a PM might be tempted by, but each explains the signal away instead of investigating it. (B) elevates a throughput count that looks encouraging precisely because it hides the severity the exposure line captures—swapping the better measure for the flattering one. (C) treats a stalled trend as an acceptable plateau, which abandons monitoring of the aggregate just when it is telling you something; narrowing to top items ignores that exposure as a whole is not falling. (D) assumes, with no evidence, that the new risks are trivial, and raising the threshold would suppress the very emergence the data is flagging. The discriminator is investigating the cause of flat exposure versus re-measuring, re-framing, or filtering it away.
