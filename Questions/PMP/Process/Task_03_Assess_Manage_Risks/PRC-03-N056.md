# Question

Late in planning a national identity card rollout, quantitative analysis shows the combined effect of several individual risks pushes the overall project risk beyond the organisation's stated tolerance, even after every cost-effective individual response has been applied. The project manager has exhausted responses within the project's authority. What is the most appropriate next step?

## Options

- **A**: Escalate the overall project risk to the sponsor, presenting the analysis and options such as rescoping, rebaselining, or additional funding to bring exposure within tolerance.
- **B**: Add a larger management reserve at the project level to cover the excess exposure and continue as planned.
- **C**: Re-run the qualitative analysis with revised probability and impact scores until the overall exposure falls inside tolerance.
- **D**: Accept the overall risk, since each individual risk already has the best available response in place.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

When overall project risk exceeds tolerance and no further responses lie within the project's authority, the threat belongs above the project—escalation to the sponsor with options (rescope, rebaseline, fund) is the disciplined move. (A) is correct. (B) sets management reserve unilaterally, which is normally a sponsor/management decision and treats a strategic breach as a funding tweak. (C) manipulates the assessment to force a "within tolerance" result—an integrity failure. (D) accepts an exposure the organisation has explicitly said it will not tolerate, conflating sound individual responses with acceptable aggregate risk.
