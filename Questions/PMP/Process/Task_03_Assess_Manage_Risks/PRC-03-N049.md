# Question

A project's contingency plan for a supplier-delay risk specifies that if the supplier misses the second milestone, the team will switch to an in-house workaround. The supplier has just missed the second milestone, but the project manager believes the supplier can still recover within a week. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Treat the missed milestone as the agreed trigger, convene the relevant parties, and decide whether to execute the contingency plan now or accept a defined short delay with clear criteria.
- **B**: Wait the week the project manager expects the supplier to need, since executing the workaround prematurely would waste in-house effort.
- **C**: Execute the in-house workaround immediately and without discussion, because the trigger condition has been met.
- **D**: Update the risk register to remove the trigger, since the supplier is expected to recover and the trigger no longer applies.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A trigger fires a decision, not blind automation; the missed milestone obligates the project manager to act on the plan—convening owners to choose between executing contingency or consciously accepting a bounded delay. (A) honours the trigger while applying judgement transparently. (B) silently lets the trigger pass with no decision or agreed criteria, eroding the plan's discipline. (C) executes mechanically without weighing the recovery prospect, potentially wasting effort the plan intended to allow judgement on. (D) deletes the safeguard precisely when it is being tested, based on optimism.
