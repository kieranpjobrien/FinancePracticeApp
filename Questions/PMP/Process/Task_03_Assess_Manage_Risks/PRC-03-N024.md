# Question

During a risk review on a road-resurfacing project, a subsoil-instability risk is logged with an agreed response that fires if the embedded settlement gauges start drifting. Four people are involved: the geotechnical engineer who reads the gauge data daily, the works supervisor whose crew would execute the response, the project manager who signs off the schedule impact, and the civil subcontractor who carries the cost. The project manager must name one risk owner. Whom should it be?

## Options

- **A**: The geotechnical engineer, who is closest to the warning signs and can recognise the gauge drift earliest.
- **B**: The works supervisor, since the owner should be whoever directs the crew that actually carries out the response.
- **C**: The project manager, so that the schedule authority needed to act on the response sits with the owner.
- **D**: The civil subcontractor, because the party bearing the financial impact has the strongest incentive to manage the risk down.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A risk owner monitors a specific risk and triggers its response, and is normally the person closest to the early-warning indicator so the trigger is recognised as early as possible; here that is the engineer reading the settlement gauges (A). Ownership is about monitoring and initiating the response, not about who performs the work, so the supervisor (B) can be assigned the response actions without being the owner. (C) the project manager holds overall accountability and the schedule authority, but cannot personally watch every indicator and need not own each risk to authorise its response. (D) bearing the cost gives the subcontractor an interest, but it sits furthest from the daily gauge data and would detect drift last, making it the weakest monitor.
