# Question

While planning risk management for a hospital IT migration, the project manager finds that the chief medical officer is extremely averse to any risk affecting patient safety, while the finance director is comfortable accepting moderate risks if they reduce cost. These differing attitudes are creating disagreement about which risks deserve responses. What should the project manager do to give the team a consistent basis for prioritising risks?

## Options

- **A**: Work with the sponsor and key stakeholders to define agreed risk thresholds that reflect the organisation's overall risk appetite for different objectives.
- **B**: Default to the most risk-averse stakeholder's view for every risk, since patient safety should always dominate decision-making.
- **C**: Average the stakeholders' risk attitudes to produce a single moderate tolerance level applied uniformly to all risks.
- **D**: Let each risk owner apply their own judgement about acceptable exposure when planning responses for their risks.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Documented risk thresholds, agreed with the sponsor and stakeholders, translate varied individual attitudes into a consistent, objective-specific standard the whole team can apply; safety-related thresholds can be tighter than cost-related ones. (B) blanket deference to the most averse stakeholder may unnecessarily constrain non-safety objectives where appetite is higher. (C) averaging attitudes ignores that appetite legitimately differs by objective and could set unsafe tolerances for patient safety. (D) leaving it to individual owners reproduces the inconsistency that caused the disagreement. Thresholds tied to objectives resolve the conflict at its source.
