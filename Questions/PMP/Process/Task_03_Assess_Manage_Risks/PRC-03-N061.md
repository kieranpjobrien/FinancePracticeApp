# Question

During the second iteration of a hybrid water-treatment plant upgrade, a junior engineer mentions to the project manager in the corridor that a key valve supplier has just been acquired and may change its lead times. The information is credible but not yet confirmed. What should the project manager do first with this information?

## Options

- **A**: Record it as a new risk in the risk register with a preliminary description and owner so it is tracked and can be analysed.
- **B**: Wait until the supplier formally confirms the lead-time change before taking any action, to avoid acting on rumour.
- **C**: Immediately add a schedule buffer to the procurement activities to absorb any possible delay from the change.
- **D**: Escalate the matter to the steering committee so they are aware of a potential supply disruption.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A newly surfaced uncertainty belongs in the risk register so it is not lost, even before it is confirmed; capturing it with an owner enables later analysis and response planning. (B) waiting risks losing the information and the lead time to respond; risks are recorded while still uncertain by definition. (C) adding buffer is a response that should follow analysis, not precede it. (D) escalating before the risk is even assessed is premature and bypasses the normal risk process. Recording first preserves the option to do any of the others later.
