# Question

On a renewable-energy project, the team mitigated a power-spike threat to grid equipment by fitting surge protection. The protection cuts the chance of damage sharply but does not remove it entirely; a small chance of a costly failure remains, and the team has decided to live with it. The sponsor asks the project manager how this remaining exposure should be handled in the plan. What is the most appropriate response?

## Options

- **A**: Document the remaining exposure as a residual risk that is being accepted, and hold contingency reserve sized to that residual exposure in case the failure still occurs.
- **B**: Remove the risk from the register, since the surge protection has been installed and the agreed response is complete.
- **C**: Re-open the risk as a new secondary risk caused by fitting the surge protection, and plan a fresh response for it.
- **D**: Move the remaining exposure to the issue log so it is actively tracked through the rest of the project.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

After a mitigation is applied, the exposure that is left over is residual risk; choosing to live with it is acceptance, and prudent acceptance of a low-probability, high-cost residual means holding contingency reserve sized to it (A). (B) deletes a risk that still has a real chance of occurring—installing a control does not make leftover exposure disappear, and dropping it loses the contingency rationale. (C) misnames it: a secondary risk is a new threat introduced by the response (for example, if the surge protection itself created a different hazard), not the leftover portion of the same threat. (D) is premature—the failure has not occurred, so it is still a risk, not an issue; it only moves to the issue log if it actually happens.
