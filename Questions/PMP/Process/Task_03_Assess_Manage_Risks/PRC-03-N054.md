# Question

A logistics project identified the risk of a customs clearance delay and agreed a pre-approved contingency plan: if the delay exceeds five days, reroute the shipment through a named alternative port. The delay has now reached day six and is hitting the schedule. The plan has never been triggered before, and since it was written the team has heard that the alternative port is working through congestion of its own, though no one has confirmed how bad it is. The project manager must act. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Quickly confirm the alternative port can still take the shipment on the timeline the plan assumes, then execute the reroute and manage the now-realised delay as an issue.
- **B**: Execute the pre-approved reroute immediately exactly as written, since the trigger has fired and the plan was sanctioned to let the team act without further checks.
- **C**: Hold the reroute and reassess the customs delay's probability and impact now that it has materialised, to decide whether rerouting is still the right response.
- **D**: Split the shipment, sending the time-critical portion via the alternative port and holding the rest at the original port pending clearance, to hedge against both routes slipping.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The trigger has fired, so action is due now—but the plan rests on an assumption (the alternative port is available on time) that the team has fresh reason to doubt. The first step is a fast check that the plan's premise still holds, then execute and manage the realised event as an issue (A). This is a brief verification, not a re-approval. (B) executes mechanically as written; normally defensible for a pre-approved plan, but here it commits the shipment to a port that may be just as congested, the precise condition that could make the sanctioned response fail. (C) over-corrects in the other direction: re-scoring a risk whose probability is now 100% stalls action on a schedule that is already slipping, when a targeted check of the response—not a full reassessment of the threat—is what the new information calls for. (D) jumps to redesigning the response before confirming either route's status, adding split-shipment cost and complexity to hedge an uncertainty a single check could resolve. The discriminator is verifying the plan's key assumption before acting, versus executing blind, reassessing the whole risk, or improvising a costlier hedge.
