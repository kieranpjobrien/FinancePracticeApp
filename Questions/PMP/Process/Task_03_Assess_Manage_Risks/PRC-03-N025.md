# Question

A known threat on a satellite-ground-station project materialises and its agreed contingency response will cost slightly more than the contingency reserve allocated to it. The project manager has already drawn down most of the contingency reserve on earlier risks. The management reserve is intact. What is the most appropriate course of action?

## Options

- **A**: Fund the response from the management reserve at the project manager's discretion, since reserves exist precisely to absorb risk costs.
- **B**: Implement the response, fund it from remaining contingency, and request access to management reserve through the change/governance process for the shortfall.
- **C**: Scale back the response to fit within the remaining contingency reserve so no additional funding approval is needed.
- **D**: Raise a change request to increase the overall project budget before taking any action on the threat.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Contingency reserve covers identified ("known-unknown") risks and is under project-manager control; management reserve covers unforeseen work and normally requires management/governance approval to release. The right move is to act on the materialised risk using contingency, then seek management-reserve access for the gap through the proper channel. (B) does both. (A) treats management reserve as freely available to the project manager, which it is not. (C) compromises an agreed, analysed response purely to avoid a funding conversation, potentially leaving the threat inadequately handled. (D) a budget-increase change request is slower and broader than simply requesting reserve drawdown for a known risk that is occurring now.
