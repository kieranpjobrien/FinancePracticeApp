# Question

On a predictive light-rail signalling upgrade, the safety regulator rejects a trackside cabinet design, forcing a redesign and re-test. This rejection was one of the threats the team identified and analysed during planning, and the project's cost baseline carries both a contingency reserve and a management reserve. The redesign quote comes in near the cost the team had attached to this threat during analysis. A junior planner suggests raising a change request to the sponsor before any money is spent. How should the project manager respond?

## Options

- **A**: Fund the redesign from the contingency reserve; because the rejection was an identified and analysed threat, this sits within the project manager's existing authority and needs no change request.
- **B**: Raise a change request to the sponsor to release management reserve, since drawing down any reserve against a realised risk requires governance sign-off.
- **C**: Re-baseline the schedule and cost first to absorb the redesign through replanning, keeping the contingency reserve intact for risks still ahead.
- **D**: Reallocate funds from a later, lower-priority work package so the contingency reserve is preserved for threats that have not yet occurred.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Contingency reserve is the budget provisioned for identified, analysed ("known-unknown") risks, and spending it when such a risk occurs is within the project manager's authority, so the redesign is met from contingency without a change request (A). The distractors are each a defensible-sounding instinct, which is where the discrimination lies. (B) treats all reserve draw-downs as needing sponsor release; that is true of management reserve (for unidentified risks) but not of contingency for a risk already analysed and funded—escalating here is unnecessary governance. (C) reaches for replanning and a re-baseline when the project already holds money sized for precisely this event; re-baselining a realised, funded risk is heavier than the situation warrants. (D) protects the reserve by raiding committed future work, which manufactures fresh schedule and scope exposure downstream and wastes the contingency created for this scenario. The call turns on recognising that an identified, analysed risk is met from contingency under existing authority, not escalated, replanned, or funded by cannibalising other work.
