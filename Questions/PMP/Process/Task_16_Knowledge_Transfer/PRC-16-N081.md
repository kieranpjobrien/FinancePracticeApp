# Question

Two of the five developers on a Scrum team supporting a retail loyalty platform will rotate off at the end of the current sprint to staff a new initiative. The product backlog is healthy and the codebase is well covered by tests. The sprint board still carries a substantial integration story that the two departing developers picked up before the rotation was confirmed, and most of the remaining work on it falls in their area. The Scrum Master has one sprint to act and wants the team to be self-sufficient on these integrations afterwards. What should the Scrum Master encourage first?

## Options

- **A**: Reassign the open integration story to the remaining developers now so they own it end to end while the two departing developers stay available to answer questions.
- **B**: Keep the two departing developers on the integration story but pair a remaining developer with each of them to finish it jointly this sprint.
- **C**: Let the two departing developers finish the integration story quickly so they can spend the rest of the sprint writing up the integration approach for the team.
- **D**: Have the two departing developers record architecture walkthrough videos of the integration patterns and ask the remaining team to work through them next sprint.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The discriminator is who is doing the live integration work while the source developers are still present, and whether the people who will stay are actively engaged in it. (B) keeps the departing developers on the real task but embeds the remaining developers alongside them, so the design reasoning surfaces and transfers in context — the strongest route for tacit knowledge, and it still gets the story done. (A) hands over ownership but separates the learners from the source: the remaining developers work alone and reach for the departing pair only when stuck, which surfaces far less of the unspoken reasoning while it is still recoverable. (C) and (D) both convert the reasoning into artefacts — a write-up or videos — under a one-sprint deadline, where nuance is lost and the recipients are passive; (D) also defers all learning to a sprint when the source is gone. The fine margin is (A) versus (B): both keep the source available, but only (B) puts learners and source on the same live work.
