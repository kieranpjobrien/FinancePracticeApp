# Question

A project manager on a predictive avionics-software project finds that one contractor is the sole holder of knowledge of a safety-critical certification toolchain, with no backup and a long lead time to grow another person into the role. The contractor is committed for the remainder of the project, so no departure is expected. The sponsor asks why the PM is investing effort in knowledge transfer now when the contractor is staying. Which is the strongest justification for acting now?

## Options

- **A**: An unplanned, unavoidable loss of the sole holder — illness, emergency reassignment, accident — would, given the long lead time to replace the capability, leave the project unable to certify and effectively stall, so the exposure exists whether or not a departure is planned.
- **B**: Concentrating safety-critical knowledge in one undisclosed individual is the kind of single-point dependency that certification authorities and auditors expect to see actively managed, so the project should be able to demonstrate a mitigation.
- **C**: While the contractor is present and not under departure pressure is the lowest-cost, lowest-disruption time to build a second capable person, so acting now is more efficient than waiting until it becomes urgent.
- **D**: Relying on a single contractor for a critical capability weakens the project's negotiating position on rates and renewal terms, which transferring the knowledge would relieve.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The discriminator is which justification names the *primary* exposure that makes this worth acting on now, and three of the four are genuinely valid considerations rather than throwaways. (A) states the core continuity risk: a single point of failure on safety-critical knowledge with a long recovery lead time can halt the project if the holder is lost for any reason — and that risk is fully present even though no departure is planned, which is exactly the sponsor's objection answered. (B) is a real and sensible point about audit and certification expectations, but it is a compliance/assurance driver that rests on the underlying risk in (A); on its own it is a weaker reason than the operational exposure itself. (C) is a legitimate efficiency argument — now is the cheapest time to act — but timing efficiency explains *when*, not *why* the risk matters, so it is secondary to the exposure. (D) raises a plausible commercial benefit, but de-risking critical knowledge is not undertaken to improve rate negotiations, so it is the weakest rationale. The fine margin is risk (A) versus its derivatives (B, C): only (A) identifies the standing continuity threat that justifies mitigation regardless of departure.
