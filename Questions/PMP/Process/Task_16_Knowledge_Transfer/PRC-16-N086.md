# Question

A hybrid product-development project moves from its build phase to a separately staffed sustainment phase next month. The sustainment team has the design documents and a series of formal handover meetings is booked. A senior engineer tells the project manager that the build team keeps an unstable subsystem running through a set of operational workarounds that nobody has written down and that only show themselves when the subsystem misbehaves, which it does irregularly. The build team disbands at month end. The PM has roughly four weeks. What is the best way to get those workarounds across?

## Options

- **A**: Have the senior engineer write the workarounds up as a runbook and add it to the handover package so the sustainment team has a reference when the subsystem misbehaves.
- **B**: Embed the sustainment staff with the build team for the remaining weeks to operate the subsystem together and handle its misbehaviour as it occurs.
- **C**: Devote the booked handover meetings to the senior engineer talking the sustainment team through each workaround and the conditions that trigger it.
- **D**: Set up a defined on-call arrangement so the sustainment team can reach the senior engineer for a period after handover when the subsystem next misbehaves.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The discriminator is that the knowledge only manifests when the subsystem misbehaves, so the highest-value transfer is the one that puts the receivers in front of the real behaviour while the people who handle it are still here. (B) embeds the sustainment staff so they experience the misbehaviour and the response together during the only window the build team exists — the reliable way to move demonstrated-in-action know-how. (A) is a worthwhile supplement and the most concrete artefact on offer, but instinctive workarounds rarely survive being written down, and a runbook cannot rehearse the judgement of when to apply each one. (C) gets the engineer explaining the workarounds, which beats documents, but it is talk-through against an idle subsystem; the team hears about the behaviour without seeing it, so recognition in the moment is untested. (D) is a sensible safety net for residual gaps, yet it defers the learning to after the source is gone and depends on the subsystem happening to misbehave while the call-off is live. The fine margin is observing the live behaviour (B) versus capturing or narrating it (A, C, D): only (B) transfers the recognition-and-response while it can still be watched.
