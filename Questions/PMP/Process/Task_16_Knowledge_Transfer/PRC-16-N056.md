# Question

A managed-services contract for a business-critical claims system is ending, and a competing vendor takes over support in eight weeks. The project manager is accountable for continuity of service across the cutover and is now planning the transition. The incoming vendor wants the outgoing vendor's run-books, configuration details, and time with its engineers; the outgoing vendor is busy winding the account down. Which step will most reliably secure the knowledge the new vendor needs?

## Options

- **A**: Invoke the handover and transition obligations in the outgoing vendor's contract to require defined transfer deliverables and engineer availability within the cutover window.
- **B**: Convene joint transition sessions between the two vendors and rely on professional goodwill to get the outgoing team to share what the new team asks for.
- **C**: Have the incoming vendor reconstruct the system's configuration from the available documentation and live environment, treating the outgoing vendor as unavailable.
- **D**: Escalate to the outgoing vendor's account executive to request that its engineers be made available for the handover as a relationship gesture.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The outgoing vendor is exiting in favour of a rival and has no commercial reason to invest effort, so the question is which lever actually compels the transfer. (A) treats handover as a contractual deliverable the vendor is bound to meet, which holds even when willingness is absent — assuming such terms were secured up front, the reliable position. (B) is sound practice and should run in parallel, but goodwill is thin against a competitor cutover and cannot be depended on for a business-critical system. (C) forfeits the knowledge only the outgoing vendor holds and is slow and error-prone on a live claims platform — a fallback, not a plan. (D) depends on the same absent goodwill as (B), merely routed higher, and a favour can be declined whereas an obligation cannot. The enforceable contractual route beats the three that hinge on cooperation the vendor has no reason to give.
