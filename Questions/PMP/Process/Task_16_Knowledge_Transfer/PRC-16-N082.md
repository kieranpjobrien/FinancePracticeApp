# Question

Midway through a predictive railway-signalling upgrade, the project manager runs a lessons-learned session that produces several accurate, well-written findings relevant to two other signalling projects starting in the same portfolio next quarter. The PM files the lessons in the project's repository, lists them in the next monthly status report, and mentions them at the portfolio review the sponsor and the other two project managers attend. Three months later, one of the new projects repeats a mistake the lessons had clearly flagged. The PM wants to understand why the transfer failed so it can be fixed for next time. What is the most likely explanation?

## Options

- **A**: The lessons were filed only in this project's repository, so the receiving teams had no reason or prompt to look there and never encountered them.
- **B**: The lessons were shared as information at a point in time but never built into the receiving projects' planning inputs, such as their risk registers or checklists, so they did not shape the work.
- **C**: The lessons were communicated to the other project managers rather than reaching the team members who actually made the decision that went wrong.
- **D**: The lessons were raised at portfolio level but no one was made accountable for confirming the receiving projects had acted on the relevant ones.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The discriminator is the difference between *making people aware* of lessons and *embedding them where decisions are actually made*. The stem deliberately establishes that the lessons were not buried — they were filed, reported, and raised verbally with the receiving project managers at a portfolio review. (B) names the real gap: awareness was created, but the lessons were never converted into the receiving projects' own working inputs (risk register entries, planning checklists), so nothing forced them to surface at the decision point — which is the mechanism by which lessons actually get reused. (A) is tempting but the stem rules it out: the lessons were not confined to the repository, they were also reported and discussed. (C) is plausible — awareness can stop at the manager layer — but the lessons being known to the right managers still should have shaped planning if they had been integrated, so it is a weaker root cause than the missing integration in (B). (D) describes a governance gap that contributes, but accountability to "confirm action" only matters if there is a defined action to confirm; the prior failure is that the lessons were never operationalised, which (B) captures.
