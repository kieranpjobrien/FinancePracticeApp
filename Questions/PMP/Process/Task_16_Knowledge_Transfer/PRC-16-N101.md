# Question

A predictive water-treatment upgrade is halfway through its eighteen-month schedule. The design and procurement phases produced several non-obvious decisions — a valve-supplier substitution, a reworked commissioning sequence, a hard-won permitting compromise — that the later construction and commissioning phases will lean on. The roster turns over substantially between phases, and on past projects of this kind the specialists who made such calls have mostly moved on by the time the work that relies on them begins. The current team is fully committed to delivery and the project manager has limited slack to spend. The project manager wants the reasoning behind these decisions to reach whoever runs the later phases. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Have the departing specialists hold focused handover sessions with the incoming phase leads as they rotate off, walking through their decisions and the reasoning.
- **B**: Capture the key decisions and rationale into the project's records now, while the specialists are still on the project, structured so the later teams can find what bears on their phase.
- **C**: Run a single structured lessons-learned workshop at the end of the design and procurement phases and circulate the output to the construction and commissioning teams.
- **D**: Schedule the lessons-learned capture for project close, when the full picture is known, and brief the later teams from it as they start their phases.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

For *continuity within this project*, the test is whether the knowledge is reliably available to later phases regardless of who is on the project when those phases run — and the specialists leave before then. (B) records the decisions and rationale while their authors are still present and ties the capture to the project's own records, so it survives the rotation and is retrievable when construction and commissioning need it. (A) transfers well in the moment but depends on the right incoming leads being in place exactly when each specialist leaves; staggered departures and later-arriving staff fall through the gap, and nothing persists if a recipient also moves on. (C) captures at a sensible boundary but a one-off workshop fixes knowledge to a point in time and to whoever attends, and detail learned after it is lost. (D) defers capture to close, by which point the specialists are long gone and the construction and commissioning phases it was meant to serve are already finished — the classic mistake of treating lessons learned purely as a closeout artefact. The discriminator is durability across the staffing change, which favours capturing into the persistent record now over a personal handover (A) or a deferred event (C, D).
