# Question

A predictive rail-signalling installation is approaching its handover gate to the client's operations and maintenance organisation. The asset is documented to specification, as-built drawings are complete, and the maintenance crews have passed the delivered training courses. In a readiness review, the operations manager notes that in a recent drill his crews followed the written procedures correctly on routine tasks but were slow and uncertain when a fault arose that the procedures did not cover step by step. The handover date is fixed by a regulatory milestone. What should the project manager do before handover?

## Options

- **A**: Arrange a period of supervised joint operation in which the crews work real and injected faults alongside the installation team before taking sole responsibility.
- **B**: Add fault-diagnosis modules to the training programme, run the crews through realistic fault scenarios, and re-assess them before the gate.
- **C**: Expand the maintenance documentation with a structured troubleshooting guide that maps observed symptoms to diagnostic steps for the crews to follow.
- **D**: Proceed with handover on the regulatory date and put the installation team on call to support the crews through the first months of operation.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The crews can follow defined procedures but falter when a situation needs diagnosis rather than a script — a capability built by working faults under realistic conditions, which the fixed date makes more urgent, not excusable to skip. (A) provides that directly: supervised joint operation on real and injected faults lets the crews build diagnostic judgement while the installation team is still there to guide and catch errors, so they own the asset already competent. (B) is a real improvement — scenario-based training and re-assessment go beyond rote procedures — but exercises in a training setting still differ from live equipment, and passing a scenario assessment is weaker evidence of readiness than having actually operated under supervision. (C) gives a genuinely useful reference, yet a symptom-to-step guide helps most where faults resemble the catalogue and least in the novel cases that exposed the gap, and consulting a guide is not the same as being able to diagnose. (D) meets the date and provides a fallback, but it transfers sole responsibility before the capability exists and leaves the crews to develop diagnostic skill during real incidents on a live railway. The discriminator is which option builds judgement under supervision before the gate, where joint operation (A) beats stronger training (B), a better reference (C), or a post-handover safety line (D).
