# Question

Two senior engineers consistently make good calls on a recurring class of design trade-off, but when asked to explain how, they say it is mostly a feel they have built up and they struggle to put rules to it. The project manager wants the junior engineers to start making comparable calls before the seniors rotate off in two months. Which approach is most likely to transfer this capability?

## Options

- **A**: Have juniors work the trade-offs alongside the seniors on live decisions, talking through each call as it is made.
- **B**: Ask the seniors to write up the heuristics they apply as a decision guide the juniors can follow.
- **C**: Have the seniors record a series of talks walking through how they have approached past decisions.
- **D**: Give the juniors the seniors' archive of past decisions and the outcomes so they can study the patterns.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The seniors' capability is judgement they cannot fully articulate, so the test is which method moves know-how that resists being written down. (A) embeds juniors in the act of deciding, where reasoning surfaces in context and can be questioned and practised in real time — the route that actually transfers unspoken expertise. (B) is tempting and worth doing, but the seniors have already said they cannot reduce the feel to rules, so a written guide will capture only the part that codifies and miss the rest. (C) conveys more nuance than a document yet stays one-way, with no chance for the junior to attempt a call and be corrected. (D) gives raw material to infer from but leaves the junior to reconstruct the reasoning unaided; pattern-spotting from outcomes is slow and easily learns the wrong lesson. The interactive, in-context option beats the codified and one-way alternatives.
