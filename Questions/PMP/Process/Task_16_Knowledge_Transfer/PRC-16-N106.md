# Question

A hybrid insurance-claims platform is delivered by three sub-teams in different time zones: one owns the rules engine, one the integrations, one the front end. Each is highly effective on its own component and keeps thorough internal documentation, but recurring defects arise at the seams between their components, and when one sub-team is blocked overnight waiting on another, a sprint's progress can be lost. The sponsor wants the cross-team defects and handoff delays reduced, but will not merge the teams or give up the time-zone coverage. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Set up ongoing cross-team knowledge exchange — rotating engineers between teams, joint design sessions on the interfaces, and shared review of seam defects — so understanding of the boundaries is held in common.
- **B**: Define explicit interface contracts for each seam, owned jointly and versioned, and require any cross-component change to update and review the contract.
- **C**: Create a small integration team that owns all interfaces between the three components and coordinates changes that cross the seams.
- **D**: Adopt a single documentation standard and a shared repository so each sub-team can read the others' internal documentation directly.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The defects cluster at the boundaries because each team understands its own component deeply but not how its neighbours behave at the edges, and that shared boundary understanding is built by people working across the seams — the heart of this task. (A) transfers boundary knowledge directly through rotation, joint interface design, and shared defect review, so teams come to anticipate each other and both the seam defects and the overnight blocking shrink, all without merging teams or touching the time-zone coverage. (B) is a sound engineering practice and reduces ambiguity, but a contract codifies the agreed interface, not the working knowledge of behaviour and intent behind it, so misunderstandings at the edges persist and changes still stall waiting on the other zone. (C) can cut seam defects by concentrating interface ownership, yet it moves boundary knowledge *out* of the three teams and creates a new coordination bottleneck — the opposite of transferring understanding to where the work happens. (D) makes the existing documentation accessible, but access was never the gap: thorough internal docs do not convey how another team's component actually behaves at the boundary. The discriminator is which option builds common boundary knowledge across the teams, where active exchange (A) beats a contract (B), a central owner (C), or shared filing (D).
