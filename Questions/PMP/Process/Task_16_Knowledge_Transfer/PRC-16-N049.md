# Question

On a long-running data-platform project, one developer is the only person who understands the bespoke data-pipeline framework the whole product depends on. He is highly reliable and shows no sign of leaving, so the team has never seen a problem. During a risk review, a stakeholder raises this as a continuity concern. The developer is resistant, saying documentation would slow him down and the knowledge is too intricate to share usefully. How should the project manager respond?

## Options

- **A**: Treat the single point of knowledge as a continuity risk and introduce gradual measures — pairing, code walkthroughs, and rotating others through pipeline work — to spread understanding without halting delivery.
- **B**: Accept the developer's view for now, since he is reliable, and revisit the concern only if he signals an intention to leave.
- **C**: Direct the developer to produce full documentation of the framework as a priority, accepting the short-term hit to delivery velocity.
- **D**: Recruit a second specialist with similar expertise so the framework is no longer dependent on one person.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A sole holder of mission-critical knowledge is a continuity risk regardless of how reliable the person is, and the fix must spread tacit understanding without stopping delivery. (A) does this incrementally through pairing and rotation, which also overcomes the "too intricate to document" objection because knowledge moves through working together, not text. (B) leaves a known single point of failure unmitigated and waits for the worst possible moment to act. (C) forces heavy documentation the developer has flagged as low-value and slow, and documents alone rarely transfer intricate tacit knowledge anyway. (D) is slow and expensive, may not be justified, and a second hire still needs the knowledge transferred — which (A) is the mechanism for.
