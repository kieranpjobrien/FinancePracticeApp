# Question

An external partner has built and run a logistics-optimisation service for an agile programme under a contract ending in ten weeks, after which the client's own team takes over support and ongoing development. The vendor has met every sprint commitment and the backlog is healthy, but operational understanding of the service sits almost entirely with the vendor's engineers. The client team is capable but has never worked on this codebase. The vendor's account manager proposes a two-day handover workshop in the final week plus a complete documentation pack, and is open to other arrangements. The contract end date is firm. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Accept the workshop-and-documentation handover and negotiate a paid support retainer with the vendor for the first months after transition.
- **B**: Begin a structured transition now, embedding client engineers into the vendor's team to deliver real backlog items under vendor guidance through the remaining weeks.
- **C**: Have the vendor produce a comprehensive runbook and architecture-decision record now, and run two-way shadowing sessions in the final fortnight before go-live.
- **D**: Keep the proposed handover but bring it forward to a series of workshops spread across the final month, each followed by client-led exercises the vendor reviews.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The continuity risk is that operational knowledge lives with a party who is gone in ten weeks, and operational knowledge of a running service is built by operating it, so the strongest move spends the remaining weeks turning the client team into people who have actually done the work. (B) does exactly that: delivering live backlog items under guidance produces capability proven by use before the deadline, while the vendor is still there to correct mistakes. (A) secures a safety net but defers real transfer to a two-day event and then leans on the retainer; the team learns little until they operate the system alone, by which point the vendor is contracted help rather than embedded teacher — capability is rented, not built. (C) is better than a single workshop and the late shadowing adds hands-on exposure, but front-loading documents and confining practice to the final fortnight wastes weeks of available overlap and gives only a short window of supervised work. (D) spreads the handover sensibly and adds client-led exercises, yet exercises reviewed after the fact still fall short of doing the team's actual production work alongside the people who know it. The discriminator is how much genuine supervised operation each option lands before the firm date, where embedding now (B) beats a retainer (A) or transfer compressed into the final stretch (C, D).
