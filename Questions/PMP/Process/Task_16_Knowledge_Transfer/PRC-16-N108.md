# Question

A programme director is standardising how four project managers handle knowledge transfer across a hybrid construction-technology portfolio. The four situations below have arisen across the portfolio in the same quarter, and each project manager must pick one mechanism to apply. Each mechanism is the strongest fit for exactly one situation; assign all four.

## Items

- **1**: On a building-controls fit-out, the engineer who commissions the bespoke automation rig leaves the country in three months. Her commissioning notes are thorough, but the last two handovers to colleagues from notes alone still produced rigs that behaved unpredictably on site. A colleague is free to work the remaining commissioning runs with her.
- **2**: Across six site-delivery teams, the same problems — temporary-works sequencing, crane-lift coordination, inspection sign-off — get solved afresh each time, and a good fix on one site rarely reaches the next. The teams run on their own schedules and the portfolio will keep generating problems of this kind for the next two years.
- **3**: A regulator can audit any completed structure years after handover and expects the exact as-installed dimensions, materials, and certification on demand. The design teams that produced them will have long since rolled off to other programmes by the time most audits land.
- **4**: A complex enabling-works phase has just finished, having made several unobvious calls — a revised dewatering approach, a substituted ground-anchor, a negotiated access window — and the main-works phase that depends on them is about to start with a largely different team.

## Matches

- **A**: A community of practice spanning the teams.
- **B**: Pair working on live tasks with the named successor.
- **C**: A lessons-learned review captured and shared at the phase boundary.
- **D**: A maintained documentation repository.

## Answer

1-B, 2-A, 3-D, 4-C

## Explanation

Several mechanisms could partly serve more than one situation, so each is assigned where it is the strongest fit. (1) has already shown that notes alone do not carry this skill — two from-notes handovers failed — and a colleague is available to work alongside her, so pair working on live commissioning runs (B) is the only option that builds the operating fluency the system needs; a repository would just reproduce the notes that already failed. (2) is recurring problems spread across teams that keep their own schedules and will keep recurring, which is what a community of practice (A) is for — it aligns practice across teams over time without taking over their delivery, where a one-off review or a document store would not keep catching the next recurrence. (3) is stable, factual, as-installed information that must survive years and outlive its authors for on-demand audit — durable retrieval is exactly a maintained repository (D), and pairing or a community would be wasteful for static facts that simply need to be findable. (4) is decision rationale from a just-closed phase needed immediately by the next phase's new team, which a lessons-learned review captured at the boundary (C) delivers while it is still fresh and before the people disperse. The tension is that (1) and (4) both involve a departure and (3) and (4) both involve outliving authors; the mapping holds because (1) needs reproduced *skill* (B not D), (4) needs *rationale* passed at a boundary (C not D), and (3) needs *static facts* kept retrievable (D not C).
