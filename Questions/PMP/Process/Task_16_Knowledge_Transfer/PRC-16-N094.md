# Question

A co-located agile team building a travel-booking platform keeps colliding because a decision made in one part of the team is often unknown to the rest until rework results. The Scrum Master wants the team to stay aware of decisions and their context as they are made, without adding much overhead to a team that already meets daily. Which approach is most appropriate?

## Options

- **A**: Maintain a shared decision log on an information radiator in the team area that anyone can update and see at a glance throughout the day.
- **B**: Add a short "decisions made" round to the daily stand-up so each part of the team states what it decided the previous day.
- **C**: Capture each significant decision as a dated entry in the team's shared wiki and rely on members to check it when they need context.
- **D**: Have whoever makes a decision post it to the team's chat channel as it happens so everyone is notified in real time.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The discriminator is sustained, at-a-glance *awareness* in a co-located team with minimal added overhead, and each option is a reasonable visibility mechanism. (A) an always-visible radiator in the shared space gives passive, continuous awareness — people absorb it without doing anything, ideal when the team is physically together. (B) the stand-up round is lightweight and routine, but it conveys decisions once verbally and only the next day, so context is not retained or visible afterwards. (C) a wiki preserves decisions durably, but it is pull-based and easily goes unread; people collide precisely because they do not think to check. (D) chat notifies in real time, yet messages scroll away, context gets buried, and it nudges toward interruption rather than a persistent shared view. The fine margin is the persistent visible radiator (A) versus the transient channels (B, D) and the unread store (C): only (A) keeps current decisions continuously in view where a co-located team will see them.
