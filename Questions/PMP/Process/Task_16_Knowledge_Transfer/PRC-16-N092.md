# Question

A predictive enterprise-CRM implementation is closing and the system transfers to a permanent support organisation that was not involved during delivery. The delivery team disbands at closure. The project manager has a few weeks of overlap and wants the support organisation able to run and maintain the system after the team is gone. Which THREE actions best serve that goal? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Run hands-on transition sessions in which support staff operate the system on real scenarios with the delivery team alongside them.
- **B**: Compile the operational runbooks, known-issue list, and configuration decisions into an owned, accessible repository handed to the support organisation.
- **C**: Identify the critical know-how and key contacts the delivery team holds informally and deliberately walk the support team through them before the team disbands.
- **D**: Produce a formal acceptance package — final design baseline, test results, and outstanding-defect list — and obtain the support organisation's sign-off that the handover is complete.
- **E**: Archive the full project mailbox and grant the support organisation read access so they can search past discussions for context when needed.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

The discriminator is which actions transfer *operating capability* — across the explicit and tacit layers — versus which mainly close the project out or leave the receiver to dig. (A) builds working competence through supervised real operation while the source still exists, the strongest hands-on transfer. (B) gives the explicit operational knowledge a trusted, findable home the support team will actually use. (C) surfaces the informal know-how and contacts that otherwise evaporate at closure, the part most often lost. (D) is a legitimate and necessary closure step — and the most defensible distractor — but acceptance sign-off and a baseline package record that responsibility has passed; they do not by themselves make the receiver able to operate. (E) read access to an unstructured mailbox is a haystack, not curated transfer; most operational know-how is not reconstructable from old threads. The fine margin is choosing the three that build and curate usable capability (A, B, C) over the formal handover-completion step (D), which governs the transfer rather than effecting it.
