# Question

Three new developers are joining an established agile team that maintains a complex payments engine where much of the working knowledge is undocumented and held by a few experienced members. The team is under delivery pressure. The Scrum Master needs the newcomers contributing to the engine as soon as reasonably possible while limiting the drag the onboarding puts on the experienced members' own output. Which onboarding approach fits best?

## Options

- **A**: Pair each newcomer with an experienced member on real backlog items, using the wiki as a reference, so they learn the engine in the flow of actual work.
- **B**: Have the experienced members run a focused week of guided sessions and code walkthroughs of the engine for all three newcomers together, then move them onto backlog items.
- **C**: Start the newcomers on the team's smaller, lower-risk backlog items so they build familiarity with the codebase before taking on engine-critical work.
- **D**: Give the newcomers the wiki and have them work through it, raising questions in the team channel and to a nominated experienced member as they arise.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The discriminator is that the engine knowledge is largely tacit and the experienced members' time is the scarce resource, so the best approach transfers context-rich know-how on real work while keeping those experts productive — and each option is a credible onboarding pattern. (A) pairing on actual backlog items moves the undocumented knowledge in context, ramps the newcomers fastest on the engine, and keeps the experienced members delivering the very items they would be doing anyway. (B) batched walkthroughs are efficient use of expert time and give a shared grounding, but a front-loaded week is largely passive, much will not stick until applied, and it pulls the experts fully off delivery for that week. (C) easing in on low-risk items limits early disruption, but it delays exposure to the engine — the thing they most need to learn — and the lower-risk areas may not carry the tacit knowledge that matters. (D) self-serve from the wiki barely dents expert time but stalls newcomers on exactly the undocumented detail the wiki lacks, so it is slowest to real engine competence. The fine margin is pairing (A) versus batched walkthroughs (B): both use the experts to teach, but only (A) embeds the learning in delivered work rather than spending a week away from it.
