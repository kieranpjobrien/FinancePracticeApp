# Question

On a hybrid renewable-energy project, a junior planner follows the scheduling procedures correctly but keeps escalating minor schedule variances that turn out to be noise while occasionally missing ones that matter. A seasoned planner who has a year left on the project reads these calls well. The project manager wants the junior to be making sound escalation calls independently well before the seasoned planner rolls off. Which approach fits best?

## Options

- **A**: Have the seasoned planner mentor the junior over the coming months, working through real escalation calls together and discussing the reasoning behind each.
- **B**: Have the seasoned planner review the junior's escalation decisions before they go out and give feedback on each, for the rest of the project.
- **C**: Have the seasoned planner produce a guide with worked examples and thresholds for which variances warrant escalation, for the junior to apply.
- **D**: Enrol the junior in an external schedule-risk-management course and have the seasoned planner debrief the key points afterwards.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The discriminator is that the gap is *judgement* — contextual, slow-maturing — and the aim is the junior deciding *independently* before the source leaves, with a year to get there; every option tries to close it, so the choice turns on which actually grows judgement and survives the planner's departure. (A) mentoring on real calls builds that judgement through sustained, reasoned practice and transfers the underlying thinking, so it persists. (B) review-and-feedback keeps current decisions correct and is genuinely instructive, but routing every call through the expert fosters dependence and, by gating rather than handing over the decision, delays true independence. (C) a guide with examples and thresholds helps codify the obvious cases, yet fixed thresholds cannot capture the contextual feel that separates a real risk from noise — the very thing the junior lacks. (D) external training teaches general method, not this project's specifics, and a debrief is too thin to instil judgement. The fine margin is mentoring (A) versus structured review (B): both pair the two planners on real calls, but only (A) is built to transfer the judgement and let the junior own the decision.
