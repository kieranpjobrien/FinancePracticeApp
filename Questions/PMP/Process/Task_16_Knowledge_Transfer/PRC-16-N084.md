# Question

On a hybrid water-treatment construction project, a specialist subcontractor's lead process engineer completes his scope and demobilises in four weeks. The commissioning he is performing now will have to be run and maintained by the client's own operations team for years afterwards. The subcontract included no handover obligation, the engineer is fully occupied finishing active commissioning, and the operations team is already on site but assigned to other duties. The project manager has budget but limited time. What is the most effective action?

## Options

- **A**: Negotiate a variation requiring the subcontractor to produce a detailed commissioning manual and acceptance-tested as-built records before final payment is released.
- **B**: Arrange for the operations team to be released from other duties to work the live commissioning alongside the engineer for his remaining weeks.
- **C**: Book a series of structured workshops in the final week where the engineer takes the operations team through the commissioning procedures and answers their questions.
- **D**: Retain the engineer on a part-time advisory call-off for six months after demobilisation so the operations team can reach him once they begin operating.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The discriminator is that the knowledge is hands-on commissioning know-how, the source is performing it right now, and his window closes in four weeks — so the highest-value move is the one that puts the future operators on the live work while he is doing it. (B) does exactly that: side-by-side practice on real commissioning is the strongest transfer of operational tacit knowledge, and it uses the irreplaceable window. (A) is a defensible governance step that secures durable artefacts and uses real contractual leverage, but a manual and records capture the explicit layer, not the feel of commissioning, and the work happens after the deadline pressure rather than transferring the doing. (C) gets the operators face time with the source, but workshops are largely one-directional and crammed into the final week, building far less working competence than performing the task together over the full four weeks. (D) is a sensible safety net for residual questions, yet by the time the operators are operating, the commissioning the engineer can best teach is already finished — it backstops, it does not transfer. The fine margin is releasing the operators to the live work (B) versus securing it as artefacts or a future hotline (A, D): only (B) moves the tacit ability while it is observable.
