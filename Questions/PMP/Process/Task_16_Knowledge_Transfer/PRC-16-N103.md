# Question

On a hybrid pharmaceutical-validation project with eight months remaining, one analyst built and runs a bespoke stability-data pipeline. As each batch of incoming data arrives she adjusts the pipeline to fit it, and she is currently the only person who does this. She is committed to the project for its duration and is fully loaded with delivery work; her colleagues are competent analysts who have not worked on this pipeline. The project manager is concerned about how the project would cope if she were unavailable for a stretch, and has a few weeks of slack to invest now before the next heavy validation push. Which approach should the project manager take first?

## Options

- **A**: Have a second analyst take on selected live pipeline work alongside her over the coming weeks, making the data-fit adjustments under her guidance.
- **B**: Have her write up how she adjusts the pipeline — each judgement call and edge case — into an operating guide, reviewed by a second analyst.
- **C**: Run a series of demonstrations to the analytics team in which she works through how she adjusts the pipeline and answers questions.
- **D**: Engage an experienced external contractor who can step in on the pipeline if she becomes unavailable during the remaining months.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The risk is concentrated in perishable, hands-on judgement — adjusting the pipeline to each batch of data — held by one person, so the gap is closed only when someone else can actually make those calls, not merely read about them. (A) builds that person directly: a named successor doing real adjustments under guidance develops genuine operating capability, the thing a single point of failure actually lacks. (B) is a reasonable contender because nothing is written down yet, but converting nuanced, data-dependent judgement into prose reliably loses the part that matters, and a guide still does not make anyone able to exercise the judgement under pressure. (C) spreads exposure across the team and surfaces some reasoning, yet demonstrations build recognition rather than the fluency that comes from making the calls oneself, and shared thinly no one becomes a credible backup. (D) imports capability but a contractor does not yet know *this* bespoke pipeline, so the transfer problem reappears as onboarding the contractor — and it spends money without first using the analyst's own availability. The fine margin is between (A) and (B): both engage the analyst now, but only working the live pipeline alongside her produces someone who can do it, whereas the write-up produces a document about it.
