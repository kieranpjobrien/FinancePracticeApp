# Question

An agile team building a clinical-trials portal has one developer who holds most of the undocumented knowledge of a complex consent-management module. She is reducing to part-time in two months but remains available until then, and delivery on the module must continue throughout. The project manager wants her understanding spread across the wider team rather than locked to one person. Which two actions would most effectively transfer her knowledge to the team? (Choose two.)

## Options

- **A**: Pair her with another developer on live consent-module stories over the coming sprints, with the partner gradually leading the work.
- **B**: Have her run regular working sessions where the team walks through the module's design reasoning with her and tackles its open questions together.
- **C**: Ask her to write a thorough specification and decision record for the module and have a teammate review it with her before she reduces hours.
- **D**: Have her record narrated walkthroughs of the module that the team can watch on demand and refer back to later.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

Most of the value here is undocumented reasoning and operating skill, which transfers best when others engage with it actively while she is still available, and the goal is capability spread across people rather than a stored artefact. (A) is the strongest single mechanism: a partner doing real consent-module work and gradually taking the lead builds genuine capability and is verified by delivery. (B) complements it by surfacing the design reasoning in a forum where the team interrogates the *why* behind the module, distributing understanding beyond the one partner. (C) is worth doing and the joint review adds some transfer, but a written spec converts deep tacit knowledge into prose that loses nuance and may go unread; it captures more than it transfers. (D) preserves a useful reference, yet narrated walkthroughs are one-way and convey steps without the dialogue that makes reasoning stick — watching does not build fluency. The two correct actions are the interactive ones (A, B); the discriminator against (C) and (D) is that capture-and-review and recordings move artefacts, while pairing and joint reasoning move the knowledge into people.
