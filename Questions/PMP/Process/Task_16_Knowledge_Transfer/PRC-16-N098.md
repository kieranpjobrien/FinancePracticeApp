# Question

A key agile team member maintaining a high-traffic telecoms billing service is leaving in two weeks and cannot transfer everything she knows in that time. With the project manager she lists three areas she could cover: the routine batch-processing procedures the service runs on; the way she has tuned the service's performance over the years and why she made those non-obvious choices; and her familiarity with the standard industry tools the team uses. The team runs the batch procedures daily and they sit in the team wiki; the wider team also uses the same industry tools. How should the PM direct the two weeks of transfer effort?

## Options

- **A**: Focus the time on her performance-tuning choices and the reasoning behind them.
- **B**: Focus the time on the batch-processing procedures, since they are what the service depends on day to day.
- **C**: Split the two weeks across all three areas so each gets some coverage before she leaves.
- **D**: Focus the time on her familiarity with the industry tools, since fluent tool use underpins everything else the team does.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The discriminator is to spend the scarce window on what is simultaneously *most at risk of being lost* and *hardest to recover elsewhere* — and the stem gives the facts to work that out rather than labelling the answer. The tuning reasoning is undocumented and lives only with her, so (A) targets the one area that genuinely disappears when she leaves. (B) is tempting because the batch procedures are what the service runs on daily — but precisely because they are routine and already in the wiki, the team can keep running and recover them without her; criticality-to-operations is not the same as risk-of-loss. (C) splitting evenly feels safe but dilutes the window onto knowledge that is documented or easily reacquired, leaving the irreplaceable reasoning only half-transferred. (D) the industry tools are used across the wider team and widely known, so that knowledge is the least at risk and the lowest-value claim on her time. The fine margin is (A) versus (B): both name important knowledge, but only (A) is the knowledge that vanishes with her.
