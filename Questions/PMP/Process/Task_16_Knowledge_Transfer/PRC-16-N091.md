# Question

A newly formed agile team supporting a logistics-tracking product inherited almost no documentation from the predecessor team, which has been disbanded. The team is delivering, but every few days it stalls on an undocumented domain rule the old team simply knew, loses time rediscovering it, and sometimes rediscovers the same rule twice. A senior member proposes pausing feature work for a full sprint to reconstruct the domain rules as a body of documentation; the product owner is reluctant to give up a sprint of delivery. How should the project manager handle the gap?

## Options

- **A**: Capture each domain rule the moment work hits it, starting with the rules currently blocking the team, and fold the write-up into ongoing sprints.
- **B**: Run the proposed documentation sprint to reconstruct the domain rules systematically in one concentrated effort before they are lost from collective memory.
- **C**: Keep delivering and resolve each undocumented rule as it arises, but add a recurring backlog item to revisit and document the rules once the immediate pressure eases.
- **D**: Stand up a lightweight decision/rules register and require the team to record any domain rule they uncover, while continuing feature work at normal pace.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The discriminator is how to stop the *repeated* loss — the same rule being rediscovered — without sinking a whole sprint into reconstruction, and every option here is a defensible attempt. (A) ties capture to the moment of need and prioritises what is actively blocking work, so effort lands only on rules that matter and each is captured once as the team learns it — the value-driven path. (B) reconstructs everything up front; it is decisive and tempting because memory is fading, but the disbanded team is unavailable to fill the deepest gaps, and much of what is documented may never be needed — a large bet of delivery time. (C) defers documentation to "once pressure eases", which in practice rarely arrives, so the team keeps re-incurring the same losses in the meantime. (D) creates the right home for the knowledge, but a passive "record what you uncover" rule without prioritising the blocking rules tends to capture incidentally and unevenly. The fine margin is (A) versus (D): both capture-as-you-go, but (A) drives the effort at the rules causing the stalls now, whereas (D) leaves capture to whatever happens to surface.
