# Question

Three developers join a stable Scrum team mid-release to add capacity. Two sprints in, velocity has dropped: experienced members are fielding newcomer questions much of the day, the newcomers feel they are a drag, and the product owner is pressing the project manager about when the extra headcount will show up in output. A senior developer has quietly suggested the newcomers be parked on side work until they are up to speed so the core team can hit its commitments. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Keep the newcomers paired with experienced developers and reset the next few sprint commitments downward, explaining to the product owner why throughput dips before it climbs.
- **B**: Move the newcomers onto a separate low-risk workstream until they are productive, protecting the core team's release commitments in the meantime.
- **C**: Have the newcomers ramp up from the codebase and documentation on their own, reserving the experienced developers' time for the release.
- **D**: Hold current sprint commitments and ask the experienced developers to absorb the onboarding load on top of their delivery work.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Every option protects something real — the release date, the seniors' focus, or the team — so the choice turns on which sustains both knowledge transfer and credible commitments. (A) keeps the fastest transfer channel open (pairing) and brings the plan into line with reality by lowering near-term commitments and levelling with the product owner; the dip is paid down once, deliberately. (B), the senior's suggestion, defends this release but splits the team and defers integration — the side work still needs context to be useful, and the newcomers arrive at the core no faster. (C) frees the seniors short-term but starves the newcomers of the interactive support that makes onboarding stick, stretching the slow period and inviting avoidable errors. (D) preserves the commitment on paper while overloading the seniors, so velocity keeps sliding and the plan stays a fiction. Sustaining transfer while resetting expectations beats sacrificing either one.
