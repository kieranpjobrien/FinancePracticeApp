# Question

A retailer is scaling a digital-loyalty programme across five Scrum teams. The teams release independently and each has built its own approach to feature-flag rollout, payment-gateway retries, and consent handling; the implementations diverge and a fair amount of effort goes into rebuilding things a neighbouring team has already worked out. Leadership has asked the project manager to cut the duplicated effort and lift the weaker implementations toward the stronger ones, and the delivery roadmap will keep generating shared technical problems of this kind. The teams release on their own cadences and the project manager wants to keep it that way. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Have the team with the strongest implementations package them as a shared internal library and offer it to the others to adopt in place of their own.
- **B**: Establish a community of practice across the five teams so practitioners regularly compare approaches and converge on shared patterns and assets by agreement.
- **C**: Stand up a cross-team architecture guild that reviews each team's solutions and publishes recommended standards the teams are expected to follow.
- **D**: Have every team record its solutions against a common template in a shared repository so others can find and reuse them before building anything new.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Two things have to hold at once: cut today's duplication, and keep good approaches spreading as the roadmap throws up more shared problems — all while the teams keep releasing on their own cadences. (A) can clear the present duplication quickly and respectfully, since the library is offered rather than imposed, but a single team's snapshot ages and nothing in it handles the *next* batch of duplicated work — it is a one-time fix to a recurring condition. (C) does target recurrence through published standards, yet a review-and-mandate guild moves decisions over the teams' own code outside the teams, trading independent delivery for a compliance gate. (D) makes solutions findable, but a passive repository offers no forum to reconcile genuinely different approaches, so teams keep diverging and seldom consult it unprompted. (B) is the one that both converges the current solutions and becomes the standing channel that catches each later recurrence, with the alignment reached by the practitioners themselves rather than handed down — so the teams keep deciding for their own components while still pooling what works. The fine margin is between (A)'s clean but static fix and (B)'s slower mechanism that keeps paying out as the duplication recurs.
