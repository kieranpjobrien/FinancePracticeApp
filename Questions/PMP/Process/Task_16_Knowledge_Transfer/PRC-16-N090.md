# Question

A programme manager is choosing how to handle four knowledge needs that surfaced this quarter. Each need could plausibly be addressed several ways; match each to the single most appropriate mechanism. Each mechanism is used once.

## Items

- **1**: A team repeatedly answers the same handful of "how do I set up X" questions for newcomers, the steps are stable, and the answers given vary by who is asked.
- **2**: An expert is comfortable being recorded but cannot free up time for repeated one-to-one sessions; the team needs her explanation of why a fragile component is built the way it is, available on demand.
- **3**: Two products in the same division keep hitting the same class of compliance problem, and neither team learns from how the other solved it.
- **4**: The delivery team senses recurring process friction but has no regular point at which the whole team stops to examine and adjust how it works.

## Matches

- **A**: A maintained, owned knowledge repository with named content owners
- **B**: A recorded walkthrough by the expert explaining the component's design reasoning
- **C**: A cross-team community of practice in the compliance area
- **D**: A regular team retrospective

## Answer

1-A, 2-B, 3-C, 4-D

## Explanation

Each need is matched on what the knowledge is and the realistic constraint attached, and the items are written so more than one mechanism looks possible. (1) is stable, explicit procedure whose problem is no single authoritative source — an owned repository with content owners (A) makes the canonical answer findable; a recording or community would be overkill for fixed setup steps. (2) is person-held design reasoning where the binding constraint is the expert's limited time and willingness to be recorded — a recorded walkthrough (B) captures the *why* once for on-demand reuse; pairing was ruled out by the time constraint, and a repository alone would not capture her reasoning. (3) is the same lesson failing to cross peer teams in a shared domain — the defining purpose of a community of practice (C), not a repository, which stores artefacts but does not create the cross-team exchange. (4) is recurring process friction with no regular inspect-and-adapt point — the retrospective (D). The traps are routing the design reasoning (2) to a repository, or the cross-team learning (3) to a shared folder: both store content without moving the specific knowledge each need requires.
