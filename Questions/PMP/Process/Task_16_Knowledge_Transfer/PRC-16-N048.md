# Question

A newly formed Scrum team has completed three sprints. The team holds a retrospective each sprint and identifies useful improvements, but the project manager notices the agreed actions are discussed and then forgotten by the next sprint, so little actually changes. What should the PM encourage the team to do?

## Options

- **A**: Capture each retrospective's agreed actions visibly, assign an owner to each, and review their progress at the start of the next retrospective.
- **B**: Limit each retrospective to one or two improvement actions so the team is not overloaded and can focus on the most important ones.
- **C**: Have the scrum master add the agreed actions to the next sprint's backlog as low-priority items for the product owner to schedule when capacity allows.
- **D**: Bring an experienced agile coach into each retrospective to suggest higher-quality improvements for the team to adopt.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The give-away the question hides is that the failure is *follow-through*, not the quality, quantity, or sourcing of the ideas — the team already produces useful improvements but never closes the loop on them. (A) fixes exactly that by making actions visible, owned, and revisited, so they are tracked instead of lost. (B) sharpens focus, which is sound practice, but a smaller set of actions with no ownership or review will still be forgotten. (C) parks improvements as low-priority backlog items the PO may never schedule, which buries them rather than driving them — and team-process improvements are usually owned by the team, not gated on PO prioritisation. (D) raises idea quality but does nothing about implementing the ideas the team already has.
