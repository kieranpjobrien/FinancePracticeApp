# Question

An agile programme runs eight feature teams building microservices on a shared platform. The same integration and observability problems are being solved independently by different teams, each unaware of the others' solutions, and a good fix invented in one team rarely reaches the rest. The programme manager has tried encouraging people to "share more" with little effect and now wants durable changes that keep solutions flowing between the teams as the programme continues. Which TWO actions best address this? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Stand up a platform-engineering community of practice that interested members from every team join, with a recurring forum to demonstrate solutions and agree emerging practices.
- **B**: Create a curated shared space where teams publish reusable solutions and patterns, with named owners who keep it current and prune what is stale.
- **C**: Appoint a central architecture review board that signs off every team's integration design before build, so consistency is enforced from one point.
- **D**: Rotate one engineer between teams each sprint so individuals physically carry techniques from team to team.
- **E**: Have each team add a "what we learned and reused" section to its sprint review and circulate the recording to the other teams.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

The discriminator is which actions create a *durable, low-friction, peer-to-peer* channel that keeps working after launch, rather than a one-off push or a central choke point. (A) is the canonical mechanism for horizontal sharing: a standing community of practice spreads solutions across peer teams through participation, and it persists. (B) gives those solutions a curated, findable home with accountable owners, so reuse happens by design instead of by chance — the structural counterpart to the community. (C) is a real practice but it centralises control at an approval gate; it can drive consistency yet does little to spread invented solutions sideways and risks becoming a bottleneck. (D) genuinely moves knowledge and is tempting, but one rotating engineer carries only what that individual happens to know, depends on continual reassignment, and does not scale to eight teams or create a shared record. (E) surfaces learning, but a recording pushed at busy teams is passive and easily ignored, and it relies on people watching rather than on a place they pull from — weaker and less durable than a living community plus an owned space. The fine margin is (B) versus (E): both target reuse, but only the curated, owned space gives solutions a maintained home rather than a stream of recordings.
