# Question

On a hybrid automotive-software programme, the project manager set up pairing sessions, a curated repository, and a community of practice, yet six months on knowledge sharing is still poor: the sessions are sparsely attended, the repository is rarely added to, and engineers continue to work as lone experts on their subsystems. The mechanisms are available and reasonably well run, but people are not using them. The PM wants to understand why before changing anything. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Talk with the engineers to understand what is discouraging them from sharing, before deciding which intervention will actually change the behaviour.
- **B**: Make knowledge sharing a recognised and rewarded part of how engineers are evaluated, so contributing is valued rather than treated as optional.
- **C**: Set an expectation that each engineer contributes to the repository and attends the community of practice, and track participation.
- **D**: Improve the mechanisms themselves — simpler publishing, better-run sessions — so that sharing is easier and more worth people's time.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The discriminator is that the PM has good mechanisms but does not yet know *why* they are unused, and the right first move is to diagnose before prescribing — every option is otherwise a reasonable response to a sharing problem. (A) investigates the cause directly; the same symptom (low uptake) could stem from time pressure, fear of looking exposed, job-security worries, or poorly fitted tools, and the correct fix differs for each, so understanding must precede action. (B) changing incentives is the strongest *remedy if the cause is motivational* — and a strong distractor — but committing to it before confirming the cause risks solving the wrong problem if, say, the real barrier is workload. (C) mandating participation can lift attendance numbers while producing hollow compliance, and it treats the symptom without knowing what suppresses genuine sharing. (D) improving the mechanisms assumes the barrier is friction, which the stem already undercuts by noting they are reasonably well run. The fine margin is diagnose-first (A) versus act-on-the-most-likely-cause (B): because the PM explicitly wants to understand the cause before changing anything, the investigative step precedes even the well-chosen remedy.
