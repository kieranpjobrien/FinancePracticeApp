# Question

Midway through a two-week Scrum sprint to build a payments module, a regulator publishes a rule that changes the required transaction-reporting fields. The product owner says the module must ship compliant and wants the affected fields handled this sprint. The team has examined it: the change is small, well understood, and they are confident the current sprint goal still holds with it included. What should the Scrum Master encourage the team to do?

## Options

- **A**: Have the product owner add the reporting change to the in-scope sprint work after a quick joint check that it does not displace anything needed for the sprint goal.
- **B**: Add the reporting change to the product backlog for the product owner to rank into the next sprint, keeping the current sprint's committed scope fixed.
- **C**: Let the developers fold the amendment into the in-progress stories straight away, since they have judged it small and the goal still holds.
- **D**: Pause the affected stories and raise a change request so the reporting change is assessed and authorised before the team resumes.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Sprint scope is not frozen: the development team and product owner can renegotiate it as they learn more, provided the sprint goal is protected, and a small, well-understood compliance change the product owner wants now is a reasonable in-sprint adjustment made together. (A) keeps the product owner accountable for what enters the sprint and adds a deliberate check that the goal is not quietly eroded, which is the discriminator against (C). (C) reaches the same outcome but lets the developers expand committed scope on their own size judgement without the product owner formally folding it in or anyone confirming nothing critical is displaced; the right answer differs from it by who decides and the explicit goal check, the classic fine margin. (B) defers reporting work the product owner has said must ship now and a regulator may not allow to slip, sacrificing value to protect an artificial scope freeze. (D) imposes predictive change-request governance on a self-managing Scrum team, where the product owner, not a change authority, owns what the team builds. The Scrum Master facilitates a product-owner-led scope adjustment with the goal kept in view.
