# Question

On a predictive programme, the project manager has authority to approve changes with no cost or schedule impact, the change control board approves anything within the project budget, and the sponsor must approve anything requiring additional funding. A proposed change has no net cost because its expense is offset by descoping a low-value feature, but it moves a milestone that an external customer relies upon contractually. Who should approve this change?

## Options

- **A**: The change control board, which can approve the cost-neutral element and refer the contractual milestone impact up to the authority that owns the customer commitment.
- **B**: The project manager, since the change is cost-neutral and so has no budget impact to escalate.
- **C**: The change control board, because the descope offset keeps the change within the project budget.
- **D**: The party with authority over the contractual milestone impact, escalating beyond the cost-neutral and within-budget thresholds because the governing consequence is contractual, not financial.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Approval authority must match the nature of the impact, not just its budget classification; a milestone the customer relies on contractually carries a consequence that none of the cost-based thresholds were designed to govern, so it escalates to the level that owns the customer commitment, typically the sponsor. (D) reasons from the real impact. (B) reads only the cost dimension and ignores the contractual milestone entirely. (C) treats budget neutrality as sufficient when a contractual obligation is the binding constraint. (A) is the closest miss: the board would be a sensible coordinator, but the change is defined by a contractual impact that sits above the board's authority, so the board is not the approver and routing it there first misplaces the decision rather than taking it straight to the authority that owns the commitment. Matching the decision to the authority that owns the governing impact is central to controlled change.
