# Question

On a predictive construction project, the change control board approves a request to have the steelwork subcontractor fabricate to a revised design. The subcontractor works under a fixed-price contract administered by the organisation's procurement function. The project manager updates the project baselines, logs the change, and emails the subcontractor's site supervisor the new drawings, who has the crew begin fabricating to them. Weeks later the subcontractor's commercial team disputes that any change to price or schedule was ever agreed. What should the project manager have done to prevent this dispute?

## Options

- **A**: Obtained the subcontractor's written acceptance of the revised drawings from the site supervisor before fabrication began.
- **B**: Had the subcontractor submit their own change request into the project's change control board so both sides logged the same change.
- **C**: Routed the approved change to procurement to be issued and agreed as a formal contract change order signed by both parties' authorised representatives.
- **D**: Recorded the cost and schedule impact in the project's change log and risk register so the figures were documented before work started.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

A change that alters a seller's contracted work, price or schedule is only binding when it is executed as a formal contract change order through the procurement agreement and signed by each party's authorised representative; the buyer's internal approvals and baseline updates do not modify the contract. (C) names exactly the missing instrument and the authority that issues it, which is why the commercial team can dispute the change. (A) obtains acceptance, but from a site supervisor who typically lacks authority to vary a fixed-price contract, so it would not bind the seller's commercial position. (B) routes a seller change back through the buyer's internal board, which still produces no contractual instrument and confuses who governs the agreement. (D) documents the impact internally, which is good hygiene but again never reaches the contract. Both internal change control and a signed contractual change order are required when a change touches a seller, and only the latter binds them.
