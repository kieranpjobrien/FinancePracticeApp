# Question

Two days into a two-week sprint, a production incident on the previous release is traced to a story this team delivered. The fix is understood and would take roughly a day. The product owner is in a customer workshop and unreachable until tomorrow. The team has capacity to take the fix now, and the sprint goal does not depend on the affected area. What should the Scrum Master encourage the team to do?

## Options

- **A**: Pull the fix into the current sprint now as a self-organising decision, since the team has capacity and the sprint goal is unaffected.
- **B**: Hold the fix until the product owner returns tomorrow so they can decide whether it enters the sprint and what it displaces.
- **C**: Route the incident to the organisation's defined production-incident process and let that channel decide whether the team is tasked with the fix.
- **D**: Have the team agree a same-day fix as an interim measure and confirm with the product owner at the next opportunity how it is reflected in the sprint and backlog.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

A live production incident the team caused is a genuine urgency that the team can act on, but the product owner still owns what the sprint contains, so the strongest move neither freezes nor bypasses that ownership: act to protect the customer now and reconcile the sprint and backlog with the product owner at the earliest opportunity. (D) does both, treating the fix as an interim response while keeping the product owner's authority over scope intact. (A) is genuinely defensible self-organisation, but a unilateral decision to absorb a full day of unplanned work silently changes what the sprint delivers without the one person accountable for that ever weighing in, which is exactly the discipline a Scrum Master protects. (B) over-corrects the other way: making a customer-affecting production incident wait a day purely to preserve process is the kind of rigidity agile is meant to avoid when the team can safely act. (C) is plausible if such a process exists, but the scenario gives no such process and outsourcing a fix the team itself can do immediately needlessly delays restoring service. Acting on the urgency while reconciling scope with the product owner balances responsiveness against role accountability.
