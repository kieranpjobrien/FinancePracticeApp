# Question

On a predictive highway-signage project, inspection finds a batch of installed signs is reflecting below the specified luminance and fails the documented acceptance criteria. Rework to bring the signs back to specification will consume crew time and budget that were not in the current plan. The schedule has little float. How should the project manager proceed with the rework?

## Options

- **A**: Raise the rework as a defect repair through integrated change control so the unplanned effort is assessed and authorised before crews are committed.
- **B**: Direct the crews to re-do the batch straight away, since restoring agreed specification is part of original scope and needs no separate authorisation.
- **C**: Submit a scope change request, because the additional rework effort and cost expand the work beyond what was planned.
- **D**: Log a preventive action and tighten the installation procedure so the luminance shortfall does not recur, then proceed with the rework.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Reworking nonconforming output to meet the existing specification is defect repair, and defect repair is an approved change action processed through integrated change control rather than performed at the team's discretion, particularly when it consumes unplanned time and cost. (A) handles it correctly. (B) treats unplanned rework as routine execution; the work and its cost still need authorisation, especially with thin float. (C) mislabels it as a scope change, but the baseline is being restored, not enlarged, so it is defect repair, not new scope. (D) describes preventing recurrence on future installs, which may be worthwhile but does not address authorising the correction of the defective batch already in the ground. The correct path is to process the defect repair through change control.
