# Question

An agile team is committed to a fixed external launch date for a mobile banking release; the date cannot move and velocity is stable. In the third of five sprints, user testing surfaces two high-value features. Fitting them in would require dropping or deferring some originally committed items, which external stakeholders are expecting at launch. The product owner asks the project manager how to proceed. What is the best course of action?

## Options

- **A**: Have the product owner reprioritise the backlog to include the two new features and bring the resulting trade-off, which committed items would be dropped or deferred, to the stakeholders for a transparent decision before the team reorders its work.
- **B**: Have the product owner reprioritise the backlog to pull the two new features ahead of lower-value committed items, exercising the product owner's authority over backlog ordering, and report the revised release contents to stakeholders at the next review.
- **C**: Add the two new features to the backlog as candidates and let the team pull them only if their stable velocity creates slack before launch, so no currently committed item is displaced.
- **D**: Run a change request through the organisation's governance so the change to the committed release scope is formally assessed and approved before the backlog is reordered.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The product owner owns backlog priority, but the originally committed feature set was promised to external stakeholders, so dropping committed items to make room is a scope trade-off those stakeholders must see and agree before the team reorders, not a decision the product owner makes alone and reports afterward. (A) keeps the product owner's prioritisation authority while surfacing the trade-off to the people relying on the promise. (B) is the closest trap: it correctly invokes the product owner's authority over ordering but treats dropping externally committed scope as ordinary reprioritisation, informing stakeholders after the fact rather than before, which is exactly the expectation gap that erodes trust at launch. (C) sounds safe by displacing nothing, but "pull only if slack appears" on a stable, fully-committed velocity is wishful capacity that quietly drops the new value while pretending to keep everything, and never confronts the real trade-off. (D) imposes formal change-request governance on an agile team's own backlog, which is heavier than this situation needs; the decision belongs with the product owner and the affected stakeholders, not a change board. The margin is between (A) and (B): both respect the product owner's role, but only (A) treats a change to externally promised scope as something stakeholders decide on beforehand.
