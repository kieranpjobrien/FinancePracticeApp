# Question

On a fixed-price predictive contract to deliver a water-treatment control system, the client's engineer verbally directs the project manager to add redundant sensors "to be safe", insisting this falls within the original intent. The project manager judges it to be genuinely extra scope and cost. The client engineer is adamant and the delivery team is ready to start. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Begin the work now to keep the client engineer's goodwill and preserve schedule, recording the direction in the project record so the cost can be claimed once the disagreement over scope is resolved.
- **B**: Document the directed work in a change notice setting out the added scope, cost and schedule impact, and obtain the authorisation the contract requires before the team proceeds.
- **C**: Treat the disagreement as a scope dispute, hold the work, and escalate to the client's contract authority for a ruling on whether the sensors fall inside the original scope.
- **D**: Have the team install the sensors and add the additional cost as a separately identified line on the next progress invoice, with the supporting direction attached.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A disputed scope direction on a fixed-price contract is dispositioned by capturing it as a change and securing the contract-required authorisation of the added scope, cost and time before the work is performed, so the contractor is not absorbing unpriced scope and the basis for payment is agreed up front. (B) does exactly that. (A) is the closest trap: keeping a record and reserving the right to claim later sounds prudent, but starting the work before authorisation on a fixed-price deal puts the contractor at risk of performing scope it is never paid for, and a contemporaneous record is no substitute for prior agreement on price and time. (C) is defensible if the parties were truly deadlocked, but jumping to a contract-authority ruling skips the ordinary first step of pricing the direction through the change mechanism, where most such disagreements are resolved without escalation. (D) acts and then bills for unauthorised work after the fact, which invites a payment dispute precisely because no agreement preceded the work. The margin is that (A), (B) and (D) all proceed with or bill for the work, but only (B) secures authorisation before the team builds, while (A) and (D) perform first and seek payment afterward.
