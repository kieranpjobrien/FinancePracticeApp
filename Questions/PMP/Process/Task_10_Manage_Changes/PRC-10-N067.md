# Question

On an infrastructure programme, a delivery team builds the station's software in two-week iterations while a contractor delivers the civil works against a fixed cost-and-schedule commitment agreed with the client. A stakeholder requests one change that adds a feature to the software and also relocates a cable duct in the civil works. The project manager must decide how to handle the request. What is the best approach?

## Options

- **A**: Submit the whole request as a single change request through integrated change control so its combined cost and schedule impact is assessed and approved together.
- **B**: Have the product owner take the software part into the backlog for ranking, and submit the civil-works part as a change request through integrated change control.
- **C**: Have the product owner take the whole request into the backlog, then plan the cable-duct work as a dependency the team sequences alongside the feature.
- **D**: Ask the stakeholder to split the request into two and resubmit, then route each part once they arrive separately.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The two parts sit under different governance: the software is delivered adaptively, where a new requirement is ranked in the backlog and pulled when capacity allows, while the civil works is delivered against a fixed client commitment, so moving its scope requires a formal change request assessed and approved through integrated change control (B). Bundling everything into one change request (A) is tempting for impact visibility but forces a predictive approval gate onto the adaptive software work, slowing value the backlog would handle without a board. Putting the whole request on the backlog and treating the duct as a sequenced dependency (C) leaves a committed, baselined deliverable being altered without the formal assessment and approval the client agreement demands. Telling the stakeholder to split and resubmit (D) offloads the PM's own routing job and delays both parts for an administrative formality. The discriminator is matching each component to its own workstream's change mechanism, not forcing one process across the whole request or pushing the triage back to the requester.
