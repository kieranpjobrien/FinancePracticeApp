# Question

The change control board has just approved a change request to add a data-retention feature on a predictive project. The project manager is ready to put the approved change into effect. Several follow-up actions are valid. Which should the project manager treat as the priority to get right?

## Options

- **A**: Updating the affected baselines and the components of the project management plan and documents that the change touches.
- **B**: Communicating the approved change to the team and the stakeholders who will act on it.
- **C**: Recording the board's approval decision and its rationale against the request in the change log.
- **D**: Directing the team to begin building the feature so the approved change starts delivering value.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four are things that happen around an approved change, but the action that everything else depends on is propagating the change into the baselines, plan and documents, because those updated artefacts are what the team builds to, what communication refers to, and what keeps the project's single source of truth accurate. (A) is the integration step at the heart of implementing a change. (B) is essential but only meaningful once the artefacts people are pointed to actually reflect the change; communicating against stale baselines spreads the old version. (C) records that the decision was made and should be done, yet logging the disposition does not itself put the change into effect. (D) starts execution before the baselines and documents are updated, which is how teams end up building to superseded requirements. Updating the affected artefacts is the priority that makes the remaining actions correct.
