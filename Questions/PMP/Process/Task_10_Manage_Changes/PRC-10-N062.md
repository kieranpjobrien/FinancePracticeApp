# Question

During a hospital pharmacy system go-live, a defect is found that miscalculates a paediatric dosing field, and patients are already being treated using the system. The change control board next convenes in six days. The developer is confident the corrected code is ready, and the clinical safety officer wants the dosing risk removed today. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Have clinicians switch the affected dosing to a manual calculation now, then take the code fix to the board on its normal schedule.
- **B**: Authorise the fix now under the change management plan's emergency change provision, then document it and seek the board's ratification at its next meeting.
- **C**: Apply the corrected code to production immediately on the clinical safety officer's authority, logging it as a defect repair rather than a change.
- **D**: Obtain written sign-off from the board chair and sponsor today, deploy the fix, and record the approval retrospectively in the change log.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A live patient-safety defect is what an emergency change provision exists for: it lets the PM authorise and apply the fix promptly through a pre-defined expedited path and then obtain board ratification afterwards, preserving both speed and traceability (B). A manual-calculation workaround (A) removes the system from the dosing path but substitutes human-error risk and leaves the real defect in production for six days when a vetted fix is ready. Deploying on the safety officer's authority and reclassifying it as a defect repair outside change control (C) skips the authorisation the plan requires and assumes a production clinical change needs no controlled record. Chasing ad hoc sign-offs from the chair and sponsor (D) invents an approval route the plan does not define and risks an unauthorised production change; the legitimate mechanism is the emergency provision, not an improvised one. The discriminator is using the plan's own expedited path rather than working around the system, bypassing control, or fabricating an approval.
