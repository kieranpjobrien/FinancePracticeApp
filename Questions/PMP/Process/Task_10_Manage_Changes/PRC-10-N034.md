# Question

The change control board rejects a change a department head championed, judging the current cost case unjustified. Afterwards the department head tells the project manager privately that the capability still matters to their area and they intend to "find another way to get it done." The project manager believes the underlying need is legitimate even though the board was right on the numbers as presented. What is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: Confirm the rejection stands and explain to the department head exactly why the board found the cost unjustified, so they understand the decision and what a viable case would need to overcome.
- **B**: Offer to work with the department head to build a stronger business case, quantifying the benefit to their area, and re-submit the change to the board with that new evidence.
- **C**: Record the board's decision and rationale in the change log and let the department head decide for themselves whether to pursue a re-submission through the proper channel.
- **D**: Raise the department head's stated intention to circumvent the process with the sponsor so governance is protected before any workaround can take hold.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The board rejected the case as presented, not the need itself, which the project manager judges legitimate, so the most valuable move both protects governance and serves the project by helping convert that need into a decision-grade case the board can properly weigh. (B) keeps the change inside the process while addressing the actual gap, the weak benefit evidence, and gives the department head a constructive, legitimate route that makes a workaround unnecessary. (A) is correct and necessary as far as it goes, explaining the rationale, but it stops at defending the past decision and leaves the legitimate need stranded, doing nothing to move it forward. (C) records the decision properly yet is passive: it discharges the documentation duty but abdicates the project manager's role in steering a real need back through the right channel, which invites the workaround the department head signalled. (D) treats a privately voiced frustration as a governance breach and escalates to the sponsor before anything improper has actually happened, damaging the relationship over an intention rather than an act. Helping strengthen and re-submit the case is the response that upholds the process and resolves the underlying problem.
