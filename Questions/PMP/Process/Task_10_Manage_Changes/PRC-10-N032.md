# Question

On a predictive project, testing reveals that building an agreed, baselined requirement exactly as written would produce a confusing user experience. A developer proposes an alternative that satisfies the requirement's underlying intent but departs from its literal wording. The business analyst agrees the alternative is better, the effort is the same, and the affected stakeholders are likely to prefer it. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Have the business analyst confirm the alternative still meets the stakeholders' intent and record their acceptance, then let the team build the alternative.
- **B**: Raise a change request to amend the baselined requirement to the alternative, so the documented requirement, the solution, and the acceptance criteria stay aligned.
- **C**: Build to the alternative now to hold the schedule, and capture the wording change in the next requirements-document revision.
- **D**: Take the alternative to the change control board as an information item, noting it as a clarification of intent rather than a change to scope.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The delivered solution will diverge from a baselined, agreed requirement, and any deviation from a baseline runs through change control so that the requirement, what gets built, and the criteria it is accepted against all remain consistent and traceable. (B) is the controlled move. (A) is the strongest trap because securing the analyst's and stakeholders' agreement feels like genuine validation, but informal acceptance does not amend the baseline; the documented requirement and its acceptance criteria still say one thing while the team builds another, and the next person to read the requirement or audit traceability inherits the gap. (C) gets the sequence backwards, committing the build before the baseline is updated and trusting that documentation will catch up, which is how the as-built drifts from the as-specified. (D) mislabels the situation: changing what the requirement says, even to better serve its intent, is a change to a baselined item, and routing it as a mere information item denies the board the chance to authorise the amendment and have the baseline formally updated. Only a change request keeps requirement, solution, and tests in step.
