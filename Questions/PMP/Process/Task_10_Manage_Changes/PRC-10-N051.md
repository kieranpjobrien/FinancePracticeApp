# Question

A change request to add multilingual support to a predictive e-commerce platform is logged and the project manager has assessed its schedule, cost and risk impacts. The change control board meets in nine days. The development lead points out that two activities on the critical path of the current approved plan are starting next week and will be slower to unpick later if the multilingual change is approved, and asks how to handle next week's work. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Proceed with next week's approved critical-path activities as planned, and make no commitment of effort toward the multilingual change until the board has decided.
- **B**: Pause next week's approved critical-path activities so the team is not building work that the multilingual change might rip out, and hold them until the board decides.
- **C**: Ask the board to convene early to decide the multilingual change before next week, so the team can sequence around a known outcome rather than guess.
- **D**: Have the team begin the design groundwork for the multilingual change next week in parallel, so no time is lost if the board approves it.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The approved plan is the authority until the board changes it, so the team keeps executing the baselined critical-path work and commits nothing to the unapproved change; the possibility of later rework is not a reason to stop approved work or to pre-start unapproved work. (A) does exactly that. (B) is the subtle trap: stopping approved, baselined critical-path activities to hedge against a change that may be rejected is itself an uncontrolled deviation that delays the committed plan on speculation. (C) sounds proactive but pressures the governance body to compress its process around the team's sequencing convenience, and an early decision on a still-fresh assessment risks a worse disposition; the board's cadence is not the PM's to bend for this. (D) commits real effort to an unapproved change, the definition of pre-empting the board. The fine margin is that (A), (B) and (C) all look like responsible ways to manage the looming dependency, but only (A) neither stops approved work nor disturbs the board's authority over both the change and its own schedule.
