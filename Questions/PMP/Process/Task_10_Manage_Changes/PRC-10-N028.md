# Question

On a construction project, the client's site representative asks the project manager to upgrade the lobby finishes and says the client's project director "is fine with it" and wants the crew to start tomorrow. The project manager's assessment shows added cost, two extra days, and no critical-path impact, and contingency exists to cover it. The contract requires signed change orders for scope changes, and the site representative is not named in the contract as authorised to direct changes. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Begin the upgrade on schedule and prepare the signed change order in parallel, since the impact is minor, funded, and the client clearly wants it.
- **B**: Decline to start until a change order is signed by the client's contractually authorised representative, then schedule and execute the work.
- **C**: Ask the site representative to confirm the project director's approval by email, treat that as authority to proceed, and formalise the change order afterward.
- **D**: Issue the change order for signature and, to hold the client's preferred timeline, let the crew begin the upgrade once the site representative verbally re-confirms.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The decisive issue is not whether the change is worth doing, which the assessment and available contingency settle, but whether anyone with contractual authority has actually approved it: a verbal "fine with it" relayed by someone the contract does not empower to direct changes is not authorisation. (B) is correct because the project manager must secure a signed change order from the contractually authorised party before committing the crew, which both complies with the contract and protects the client and contractor from a later dispute over an unapproved scope change. (A) is the strongest trap: starting work in parallel feels efficient and the change is genuinely minor and funded, but it commits resources to a scope change that is not yet contractually authorised, which is precisely the exposure the change-order clause exists to prevent. (C) treats an emailed relay of approval from an unauthorised representative as sufficient authority, which still falls short of the signed instrument from the right signatory the contract demands. (D) dresses the same premature start up with a verbal re-confirmation, but proceeding before signature is the same contractual breach regardless of how the timeline pressure is rationalised. Authorisation from the right party, evidenced as the contract requires, gates the work.
