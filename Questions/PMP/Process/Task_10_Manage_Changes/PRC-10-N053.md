# Question

On a predictive infrastructure project the change control board approved a request three weeks ago to add a firewall tier, conditional on the security team signing off the design before build. During an audit walkthrough today, the auditor asks the project manager to show evidence that the sign-off condition was met and that the cost and schedule baselines were updated accordingly. The project manager believes the security review did happen but is not certain it was captured against the change record. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Check the change log entry and its linked records to establish what is actually evidenced for the approval, the sign-off condition and the baseline updates before responding to the auditor.
- **B**: Tell the auditor the condition was met to the best of the project manager's knowledge and offer to follow up with the documented evidence immediately after the walkthrough.
- **C**: Contact the security lead now to confirm the design review took place and have them point to the dated record of it, so the project manager can answer the auditor accurately.
- **D**: Acknowledge to the auditor that the evidence cannot be located in the moment, and log a corrective action to reconstruct the change record and close the traceability gap.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The change log is the system of record meant to tie the approval to its conditions and the resulting baseline updates, so the first move is to interrogate that record to see what is actually evidenced, before asserting anything or chasing people. (A) does that and grounds whatever the PM says next in the record itself. (B) commits to a factual assertion of compliance from memory, exactly the unverified claim the auditor is testing, before checking the record that should prove it. (C) jumps to a single source (the security lead's recollection and any record they hold) without first consulting the project's own change log, which may already contain or link the sign-off and is the authoritative place to look. (D) concedes a gap and launches a corrective action before establishing whether the evidence exists at all, potentially manufacturing a finding where the record may be intact. The margin is that (B), (C) and (D) are all plausible, good-faith responses, but each acts (asserts, chases, or concedes) before reading the record that the change control system maintains for precisely this question.
