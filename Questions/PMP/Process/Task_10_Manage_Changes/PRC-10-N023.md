# Question

On a predictive project, the change control board has approved a change that adds a reporting module. The project manager begins implementing it: the cost and schedule baselines are revised and the team is briefed. During the work the project manager realises the approved change, as scoped, will also require altering a data-retention requirement that two other in-flight deliverables are built against, an interaction that was not surfaced when the board reviewed the request. No work on those other deliverables has started against the new assumption yet. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Continue implementing the approved change and, in parallel, raise a separate change request covering the data-retention requirement and its effect on the two dependent deliverables.
- **B**: Pause implementation of the affected portion and return to the change control board with the newly identified interaction, so the board can reassess the change with its full impact known.
- **C**: Implement the change as approved, since the board has already authorised it, and update the data-retention requirement and dependent deliverables to match.
- **D**: Update the data-retention requirement and the two dependent deliverables now to keep them aligned, then note the interaction in the change log for the board's information.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The board approved the change on an impact picture that was materially incomplete; an interaction it never saw now changes what approval actually entails, so the disciplined move is to take the new information back to the authority that owns the decision rather than absorbing the difference unilaterally. (B) restores integrated change control by letting the board reassess the change it thought it was approving. (A) is the sharpest trap: splitting the newly found interaction into a parallel change request looks procedurally correct, but it lets the original change proceed as though its scope were settled when the board's approval no longer matches reality, and it fragments one coupled change into two decisions that should be weighed together. (C) treats the original approval as covering consequences the board never evaluated, quietly expanding the change's footprint on the project manager's own reading of what was authorised. (D) goes further still, changing the dependent deliverables before any reassessment and reducing the board to being informed after the fact of a scope shift it should have decided. Returning the enlarged impact to the board before building on it is what keeps the approved baseline honest.
