# Question

On a predictive bridge-refurbishment project, an approved change request added a new waterproofing layer. The project manager updated the cost and schedule baselines and the WBS, logged the change with its disposition, notified the site and procurement teams, and asked the contractor to proceed. Two weeks later the quality manager reports that inspectors are still testing against the old acceptance criteria, because the inspection checklist derived from the quality management plan was never revised. Of the change-implementation steps, which omission best explains this outcome?

## Options

- **A**: The updated scope was not communicated to the inspection team, who therefore kept working to what they last knew.
- **B**: The quality management plan and its derived inspection checklist were not updated to reflect the approved change.
- **C**: The requirements traceability matrix was not updated to link the new waterproofing requirement to its verification method.
- **D**: A quality audit was not scheduled after the change to confirm the new work would be inspected correctly.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Implementing an approved change means propagating it into every affected component of the project management plan and project documents, and here the governing artefact the inspectors actually work from, the quality management plan and its derived checklist, was never updated, so even a fully informed inspector would test against stale criteria. (B) names the omission that directly produces the symptom. (C) is a real and related gap, an unrevised traceability matrix would leave the new requirement unlinked to its verification, but a current matrix still would not change what the checklist instructs inspectors to do; it is a downstream documentation lapse, not the cause of testing to the wrong criteria. (A) is plausible and the project manager's communication was indeed incomplete, yet the scenario shows the inspection team is following the checklist as written, so the controlling failure is the unrevised document itself rather than a missed conversation; updating the checklist is what fixes it, briefing alone would not. (D) would likely have detected the problem after the fact, but a missing audit is a control that catches the error, not the omission that caused inspectors to use superseded criteria. The decisive failure is that the affected quality document was not carried through with the change.
