# Question

A senior stakeholder catches the project manager in the corridor and asks for a "small tweak" to a deliverable that was formally accepted last week. It genuinely looks minor to the project manager, the stakeholder is waiting on an answer, and a small amount of slack exists in the schedule to do it. What is the most appropriate first action?

## Options

- **A**: Log the request and run it through the change control process so its impact is assessed and the decision is recorded.
- **B**: Carry out a quick impact analysis on the spot and, if it confirms the tweak is minor, action it and note it in the next change log update.
- **C**: Ask the stakeholder to submit the request in writing so there is a documented trigger before the project manager does anything with it.
- **D**: Take the tweak to the product owner of the deliverable to decide, since they own its content.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A change to an already-accepted deliverable enters the defined change control process regardless of how small it appears, so the first action is to log it and let the process assess impact and record the decision. (A) is the disciplined, methodology-correct move. (B) is the strongest trap: it sounds rigorous because it includes an impact analysis, but it lets the project manager pre-judge "minor", authorise the work themselves, and document after the fact, which bypasses the authority that the process vests in the appropriate decision-maker. (C) over-weights the form of the request; whether it arrives verbally or in writing, the project manager can and should log it and start the process now rather than gating on paperwork. (D) misapplies a role from adaptive delivery to a formally accepted deliverable under change control, where a product owner is not the change authority. Logging and assessing through the process, not the project manager's own size judgement, governs the change.
