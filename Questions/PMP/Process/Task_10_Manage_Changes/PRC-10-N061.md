# Question

Halfway through a release on an agile e-commerce project, the marketing sponsor tells the project manager that a loyalty-points feature she has just thought of is now critical to the launch and asks how quickly it can be delivered. The team is six days into a two-week sprint with a committed sprint goal. How should the project manager respond?

## Options

- **A**: Take the request to the next change control board so the feature is assessed and formally approved before any work begins.
- **B**: Ask the product owner to size the feature with the team and bring it into the current sprint, swapping out lower-value committed items to keep the sprint goal intact.
- **C**: Have the product owner add the feature to the product backlog and rank it against existing items for the team to pull into a future sprint.
- **D**: Convene the team immediately to re-plan the current sprint around the new feature so the sponsor sees it prioritised.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

In adaptive delivery the product owner owns priority, and a newly raised requirement enters the ranked backlog to be pulled when capacity allows, without disturbing a sprint already underway (C). Routing it to a change control board (A) imposes a predictive gate that agile deliberately replaces with continuous backlog reprioritisation. Swapping committed items mid-sprint (B) is a real technique, but altering the sprint backlog after commitment is the team's and product owner's call at the sprint boundary, not a mid-sprint reshuffle the PM negotiates with the sponsor, and it sacrifices in-flight work for an unsized ask. Re-planning the live sprint around the feature (D) breaks the sprint goal and the team's protected focus to signal responsiveness. The discriminator is honouring both the product owner's authority over ranking and the sprint's protection from mid-flight change: the backlog is where a new requirement belongs, not a board, an in-sprint swap, or a re-plan.
