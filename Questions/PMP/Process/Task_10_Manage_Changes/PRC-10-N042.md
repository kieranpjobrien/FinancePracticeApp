# Question

On a predictive offshore wind installation project, a vessel crew discovers a seabed obstruction that the survey missed, making the planned cable route unsafe. Proceeding would endanger the crew and could damage equipment; the vessel is on hire at a high day-rate, and the next change control board meeting is four days away. A safe alternative route is available. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Use the project's emergency change provision to authorise the safe reroute now, applying only the deviation needed to make the work safe, then document it and submit it for the board to ratify.
- **B**: Authorise the full alternative route now under the emergency provision and update the schedule baseline to the new route so the team is working to a current plan.
- **C**: Hold the vessel on standby and bring the board together early on a virtual call to approve the reroute before any cable work resumes.
- **D**: Authorise the immediate reroute to remove the hazard and notify the sponsor, leaving the change documentation and board submission until the installation campaign is complete.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A genuine safety-and-cost emergency is exactly what an emergency change provision exists for, but acting under that delegated authority means doing the minimum needed to make the situation safe and then taking the change through ratification, not treating the delegation as licence to settle the wider plan. (A) removes the hazard now and preserves governance by limiting the deviation and submitting it for board ratification. (B) acts promptly but overreaches: changing the schedule baseline is a board outcome, and baselining the new route on the project manager's emergency authority pre-empts the ratification that should confirm it. (C) respects the board but misuses an emergency: standing the vessel down to assemble even a virtual board, while a hazard is live and day-rate accrues, defeats the purpose of having a pre-authorised emergency path. (D) acts on the hazard correctly but defers documentation and ratification to the end of the campaign, leaving an unratified baseline-affecting change outstanding for weeks rather than submitting it promptly. The margin is that (A), (B) and (D) all rightly act at once under emergency authority, but only (A) keeps the action proportionate and the change promptly back under board control.
