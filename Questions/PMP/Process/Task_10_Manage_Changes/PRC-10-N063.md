# Question

On a predictive office-relocation project, a department head asks the project manager directly for an extra meeting room on the new floor plan. Judging it minor, the project manager instructs the fit-out contractor to proceed and plans to mention it at the next steering meeting. During the subsequent variance review the steering committee asks the project manager to explain what went wrong. Which response identifies the primary error?

## Options

- **A**: The project manager committed the fit-out contractor to extra work before confirming its feasibility and a price.
- **B**: The project manager altered a baselined deliverable before its impact was assessed and the change approved through integrated change control.
- **C**: The project manager accepted scope from a department head who had no authority to approve changes to the project.
- **D**: The project manager deferred informing the steering committee instead of seeking their decision before acting.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The primary error is implementing a change to a baselined scope before its impact was assessed and approval obtained through integrated change control; the informal go-ahead bypassed the very process that would have surfaced the cost and schedule effects and produced an authorised decision (B). Not confirming feasibility and price first (A) is a real lapse, but it is one input the impact assessment would have gathered had the process been run, not the root failure. The department head's lack of authority (C) matters, yet authority to approve is exactly what integrated change control assigns; the issue is not who asked but that no approving body assessed and decided. Failing to brief the steering committee in advance (D) describes the same omission from the governance angle, but the committee is one possible approval route within change control, not a separate error. The discriminator is that the other lapses are all symptoms of one root cause: acting on the change outside the change-control process.
