# Question

A sponsor funding an agile project tells the project manager they are uneasy: requirements keep changing between iterations and the sponsor cannot tell whether the budget is buying the outcomes that matter most. The project manager wants to address the concern in a way that fits the adaptive approach. What is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: Show the sponsor how the prioritised backlog is ordered by value and walk them through how reprioritisation keeps the highest-value work flowing first.
- **B**: Reassure the sponsor that each change is being absorbed by the team without affecting the budget or the delivery timeline.
- **C**: Offer to baseline the current requirements and route any further change through a change control board so the scope stops moving.
- **D**: Invite the sponsor to review and help reprioritise the backlog at the start of each iteration so they can steer where the budget goes.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The sponsor's real worry is not that change happens but that they cannot see the budget being directed to the outcomes that matter, so the strongest response gives them an ongoing lever over priorities rather than a one-off explanation. (D) brings the sponsor into backlog reprioritisation each iteration, which both fits the adaptive approach and directly answers "is my money buying the right things". (A) is genuinely good and explains the mechanism, but it leaves the sponsor as an observer of a process rather than a participant steering it, so it informs the concern without resolving the sponsor's sense of control. (B) offers false comfort: reprioritisation has trade-offs, and new work displaces other work, so claiming no budget or schedule effect misrepresents how agile delivers. (C) abandons the chosen approach by freezing scope under predictive governance, trading away the responsiveness the project was set up to provide. Engaging the sponsor in steering the backlog treats the underlying concern honestly.
