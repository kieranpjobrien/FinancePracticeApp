# Question

The change control board rejects a marketing director's request to add a loyalty-points feature to a predictive app release, citing schedule risk to a fixed launch date. Afterwards the marketing director tells the project manager privately that the board "didn't understand the commercial upside" and that the business case was much stronger than the board appreciated. The project manager records the rejection in the change log. What should the project manager do next?

## Options

- **A**: Offer to help the marketing director strengthen the business case, particularly the schedule-risk concern the board raised, and resubmit the request for the board to reconsider on the new information.
- **B**: Explain the board's specific rationale to the marketing director and discuss realistic alternatives, such as scheduling the loyalty feature into the next release where it does not threaten the fixed launch date.
- **C**: Escalate the marketing director's commercial argument to the project sponsor and ask the sponsor to overturn the board's decision in light of the business value at stake.
- **D**: Add the loyalty feature to a candidate list for a future release and let the marketing director know the door is not closed, without revisiting the current decision.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A rejected change is communicated back with the board's reasoning and a constructive forward path, keeping both governance and the relationship intact; the board's decision stands unless genuinely new information justifies reopening it, which a stakeholder's after-the-fact insistence is not. (B) closes the loop on the actual rationale (schedule risk to a fixed date) and offers the realistic alternative. (A) is the closest trap: helping rebuild the case is reasonable in principle, but immediately re-routing the same request back to the board on the strength of the director's displeasure, with no changed facts, churns the governance the board just exercised. (C) goes over the board's head to the sponsor to reverse a decision that was the board's to make, undermining the control structure. (D) parks the request as a vague future candidate without ever conveying why it was rejected or offering a concrete path, leaving the stakeholder informed of nothing. The margin is that (A), (B) and (D) are all polite and non-confrontational, but only (B) both respects the standing decision and gives the stakeholder something real to act on.
