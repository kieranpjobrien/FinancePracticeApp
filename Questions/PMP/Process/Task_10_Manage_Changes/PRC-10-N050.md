# Question

Three days into a two-week sprint, a senior executive corners the development team and insists they immediately start an unplanned analytics feature for a board demo on Friday. The team, intimidated, begins shifting their attention. The product owner is unaware and the sprint goal is now at risk. What is the best action for the Scrum Master?

## Options

- **A**: Speak to the executive directly, explain that the sprint is committed and the team cannot take on the feature mid-sprint, and ask them to hold the request for the next sprint planning.
- **B**: Bring the executive and product owner together promptly so the request is evaluated against the sprint goal, and protect the team from changing course until the product owner decides.
- **C**: Add the analytics feature to the current sprint immediately, since the request came from a senior executive and the demo is imminent.
- **D**: Help the team find a way to deliver the analytics feature by Friday alongside the sprint work, since a board demo is a legitimate business need the team should support.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The Scrum Master shields the team from mid-sprint disruption and routes the request to the product owner, who alone decides what the team works on; the request is then weighed against the sprint goal rather than obeyed or refused on the Scrum Master's say-so. (B) restores proper authority and protects the sprint without dismissing a possibly valid need. (A) is the closest trap: protecting the sprint is right, but the Scrum Master unilaterally rejects and reschedules a scope decision that belongs to the product owner, who might choose to renegotiate the sprint. (C) lets seniority override the product owner and the sprint goal. (D) quietly accepts the scope addition and pushes the team to absorb it, the very mid-sprint disruption the Scrum Master should be channelling to the product owner first.
