# Question

A hybrid product-development project delivers features in two-week sprints, but its total budget and a regulator-mandated feature set are baselined and governed by a change control board. Late in a sprint a senior stakeholder emails the product owner and the project manager together, asking for a substantial new capability that is not in the regulated set and would push the programme over its approved budget. The stakeholder wants it "in the next sprint or two". What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Ask the product owner to add the capability to the backlog and schedule it across the next sprints, treating it as a normal evolving requirement the team can absorb.
- **B**: Raise the request with the change control board only, and let the board decide both whether to fund it and which sprint the team should build it in.
- **C**: Decline the request until the next budget cycle, since it exceeds the approved funding and the team has no authority to commit money.
- **D**: Have the request assessed for its budget impact and taken through the change control board, since it breaches the funding baseline, while the product owner ranks it in the backlog for sequencing once any funding is approved.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The request has two distinct dimensions that belong to two different owners: it crosses the funding baseline, which is formal change control's domain, and it is a feature to be sequenced, which is the product owner's domain. (D) splits them correctly, board for the money, product owner for backlog sequencing once funding is settled. (A) treats a baseline-breaching, over-budget request as ordinary backlog churn, ignoring the governance the budget baseline requires. (B) routes everything to the board including the sprint-level sequencing decision, which the board does not own; deciding which sprint builds a story is the product owner and team's call, not the board's. (C) reaches a plausible outcome but pre-empts the assessment and the board's decision, foreclosing options such as a reprioritisation or partial funding before the impact is even evaluated. Matching each dimension of the change to the body that owns it is the disciplined response.
