# Question

Reviewing progress on a predictive office-fit-out project, the project manager notices the team has delivered several small enhancements the client mentioned casually over the past month. None went through change control, the client is delighted, but the project is now trending over budget and the schedule is tightening. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Issue retrospective change requests for each enhancement so the work already done is brought into the change log and the baselines are reconciled to it.
- **B**: Brief the team that any further client request, however small, must be logged and assessed before it is actioned, closing off the leak before it widens.
- **C**: Quantify the cumulative effect of the undocumented enhancements on scope, cost and schedule before deciding how to restore control.
- **D**: Raise the cost and schedule trend with the client now, since they asked for the enhancements, and open the conversation about funding or rescoping the remaining work.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Every option here is a real part of recovering from uncontrolled change, so the question is purely one of sequence: which must come first to make the others land correctly. The project manager does not yet know the size of the problem, and the aggregate impact on the constraints is the fact base every subsequent decision depends on. (C) establishes it. (A) is the natural next step and will be needed to regularise the baselines, but raising retrospective change requests before the combined impact is known means formalising the changes one by one without seeing whether, together, they have breached a tolerance that itself needs escalating; the paperwork follows the analysis, not the reverse. (B) is a sound preventive move and stops the situation worsening, but it addresses only future requests and does nothing about the overrun already incurred, so it cannot be the first priority when the project is already trending over. (D) takes the trend to the client prematurely: until the cumulative figure is in hand the project manager cannot say what is being asked for, and opening a funding or rescoping conversation without it invites a commitment made on an unquantified position. Measuring the cumulative impact is what makes the retrospective change requests, the client conversation and the preventive tightening each correctly scoped.
