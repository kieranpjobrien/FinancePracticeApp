# Question

During a sprint to build a hospital scheduling system, testers find that a story already accepted two sprints ago does not handle daylight-saving transitions, causing duplicate appointment slots. The product owner agrees it must be fixed. The team debates whether this is a change request or just defect work. What should the Scrum Master guide them to do?

## Options

- **A**: Raise a formal change request because the behaviour of an already-accepted story is being altered, so the alteration is governed and traceable.
- **B**: Treat it as a defect, add it to the product backlog so the product owner can order it for an upcoming sprint, and fix it like any other backlog item.
- **C**: Have the team pull the fix into the current sprint now, since defects in their own delivered work are the team's responsibility to remediate without waiting on prioritisation.
- **D**: Capture the defect in the backlog but, because the daylight-saving fault is causing live duplicate slots, have the product owner expedite it into the current sprint and renegotiate the sprint scope to accommodate it.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A defect in already-delivered functionality is corrective work that enters the product backlog and is ordered by the product owner like any other item, not a scope change requiring change-request governance, and the product owner, not the team or the severity alone, decides when it is scheduled. (B) handles the defect through normal Scrum flow with the product owner setting priority. (A) imposes change-control overhead on defect remediation, which is not a scope change to the committed baseline. (C) lets the team unilaterally pull work into the committed sprint without the product owner weighing the defect against the sprint goal, bypassing the priority decision that belongs to the product owner. (D) is the strong trap: expediting and renegotiating the sprint is a legitimate option the product owner may choose, but the question asks what the Scrum Master guides the team to do as the handling of this work, and prescribing the expedite-and-renegotiate outcome pre-empts the product owner's call; (B) routes the defect to the product owner who then decides whether it is urgent enough to disrupt the current sprint or be ordered into an upcoming one. The margin is that (B) and (D) both correctly treat it as a backlog defect, but only (B) leaves the timing decision with the product owner instead of mandating it.
