# Question

For three sprints running, an agile team has carried unfinished stories into the next sprint, and the project manager can no longer give stakeholders a reliable delivery forecast. Reviewing the sprints, the project manager sees that stories were being expanded after the team had committed to them, and that most of those additions were small requests the product owner had agreed to on the spot. What is the most effective action?

## Options

- **A**: Stop forecasting on velocity and instead report a wider delivery range to stakeholders until the team's throughput settles on its own.
- **B**: Work with the product owner so that requests arriving mid-sprint are taken into refinement and weighed at the next planning session rather than agreed into committed stories.
- **C**: Have the team size every story more conservatively in planning so there is slack to absorb the additions the product owner agrees to.
- **D**: Introduce a rule that no story may be altered once the sprint has started, and route any proposed addition through a written approval before work continues.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The forecast is unreliable because committed work keeps growing after planning, and the additions are entering through on-the-spot agreement during the sprint; the durable fix is to redirect those mid-sprint requests into refinement and the next planning session, restoring a stable sprint boundary the product owner still controls. (B) does this. (A) changes how the instability is reported rather than removing its cause, and waiting for throughput to "settle on its own" leaves the injection mechanism in place. (C) builds permanent slack to accommodate the very behaviour that is destabilising the sprint, masking the problem inside padded estimates and lowering effective capacity. (D) is the strong trap: a hard freeze with written approval does stop mid-sprint growth, but it imposes change-control machinery on a self-managing team for what is really a planning-discipline issue, and a blanket ban also blocks the legitimate trade-offs a product owner may want to make. The margin is that (B) and (D) both target the mid-sprint additions, but (B) re-channels them through the team's own cadence while (D) bolts on heavyweight governance the situation does not call for.
