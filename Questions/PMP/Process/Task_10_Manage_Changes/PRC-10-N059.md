# Question

Late in a predictive telecom billing migration, the sponsor asks to remove a rarely used legacy report from scope to save cost and pull in the go-live date. The report is in the approved scope baseline, and one regional team still relies on it monthly. The development team confirms removing it is technically easy. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Remove the report immediately since the sponsor requested it, it is technically easy to drop, and it directly delivers the sponsor's cost and schedule goal.
- **B**: Raise a change request, assess the impact on the regional team that depends on the report, and bring it to the change control board before changing the baseline.
- **C**: Confirm with the sponsor that the regional team's monthly dependency can be met another way, then proceed to drop the report on the sponsor's authority as the change requestor.
- **D**: Keep the report out of the go-live build to capture the cost and date benefit now, and raise a change request afterward to formalise the descope retrospectively.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Removing scope is still a change to the approved baseline and must go through change control, with the impact on the dependent regional team assessed before the board decides, not after the report is already gone. (B) treats a descope with the same rigour as an addition and surfaces the affected stakeholder for a proper disposition. (A) drops baselined scope on a verbal request without any assessment of who relies on it, the sponsor's goal notwithstanding. (C) is the subtle trap: checking the regional team's needs is the right instinct, but it then lets the sponsor's status as requestor stand in for the board's authority and skips the formal assessment and disposition, so a single stakeholder effectively decides a baseline change. (D) captures the benefit first and back-fills the change request, inverting the process so the board ratifies a descope that has already happened and the dependent team's impact is assessed too late to matter. The margin is that (A), (C) and (D) all act on the descope before a controlled decision exists, while only (B) assesses and disposes before the baseline moves.
