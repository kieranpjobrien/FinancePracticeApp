# Question

At 02:00 a production outage on a live banking platform is traced to a defect in the project's most recent release. A one-line configuration fix would restore service immediately. The change control board does not convene until 09:00, and the project's change procedure includes a documented emergency-change provision naming an on-call approver for exactly this kind of situation. Customers cannot transact. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Contact the designated emergency approver to authorise the fix now under the emergency-change provision, apply it, and submit it for the board's retrospective review.
- **B**: Apply the fix immediately on the project manager's own authority given the severity, and raise the emergency change for approval as soon as the approver is reachable.
- **C**: Call the sponsor and ask them to personally authorise the fix, since their seniority outranks the board and removes any doubt about approval.
- **D**: Have the on-call engineer apply the fix now and open a standard change request at 09:00 so the board records it through the normal route.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The project has a defined emergency-change provision with a named approver precisely so urgent fixes are authorised and applied without waiting for the full board, then reviewed afterwards. (A) uses that provision exactly as designed. (B) applies the fix with no authorisation at all and only seeks approval later; urgency does not remove the need for the emergency approver who exists for this moment. (C) escalates to the sponsor instead of using the route the procedure already defines, treating it as needing an ad hoc bypass. (D) applies the fix now but routes it through the standard request afterwards rather than the emergency path, so the change is made without the emergency authorisation the procedure requires. Following the defined emergency provision is the correct, governed action.
