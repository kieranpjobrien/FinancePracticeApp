# Question

A change control board reviews a change request on a logistics project and rejects it because the cost outweighs the benefit. The requesting stakeholder is disappointed. The project manager wants to close this out properly so the decision holds up if the request resurfaces. What is the most important thing for the project manager to do?

## Options

- **A**: Record the change request, the rejection and its rationale in the change log so the disposition is traceable, and inform the requester of the outcome.
- **B**: Notify the requester of the rejection and reasoning directly, so they understand the decision and feel heard.
- **C**: File the board's meeting minutes documenting the rejection, since the minutes already evidence that the decision was taken.
- **D**: Archive the rejected request with the other closed requests so it no longer appears on the active change list.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The change log is the controlled artifact that captures every change request and its disposition, including rejections and the reasons, so there is a single defensible record that survives staff turnover and resurfaced requests. (A) does this and closes the loop with the requester. (B) is good stakeholder practice but a verbal or email notification is not the controlled record of disposition. (C) relies on meeting minutes, which evidence the meeting but are not the change log where dispositions are tracked and looked up. (D) removes it from the active list without ensuring the disposition and rationale are logged, so the "why" can be lost. Logging the disposition in the change log is the step that makes the decision traceable.
