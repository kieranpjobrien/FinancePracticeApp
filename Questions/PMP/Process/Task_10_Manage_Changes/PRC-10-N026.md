# Question

In an agile product team, a stakeholder requests a high-value capability that the product owner agrees should rank near the top of the backlog. In refinement, the developers explain it conflicts with an architectural decision the team made and documented two sprints ago; honouring the request would mean reworking that architecture, which carries its own cost and risk. The product owner and developers are discussing it together. What is the most appropriate way for them to resolve this?

## Options

- **A**: Treat the documented architectural decision as the constraint and have the product owner reshape the request into something that fits within it, keeping the agreed architecture intact.
- **B**: Have the product owner rank the capability at the top and let the developers redesign the architecture during the sprint in which they pick it up, deciding the technical approach as they go.
- **C**: Spike the architectural change first to quantify its cost and risk, then have the product owner weigh that against the capability's value to decide whether and when to take it on.
- **D**: Keep the capability and the architectural rework as separate backlog items so the product owner can prioritise each on its own merits independently.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

All four options involve the product owner and developers working together, so the discriminator is which collaboration actually equips the value-versus-cost trade-off to be made well. The architectural rework's cost and risk are currently unknown, and that unknown is what blocks a sound decision, so the strongest move is to reduce it first with a spike and then let the product owner prioritise on real information. (C) sequences the work correctly: learn the cost, then decide. (A) prematurely lets the old architectural decision override current business value without ever testing whether the rework is actually worth it, freezing a past choice the team is entitled to revisit. (B) commits to the capability before the architectural impact is understood and defers the redesign into the delivery sprint, which is how a team ends up mid-sprint discovering the rework is far larger than assumed. (D) is superficially tidy but splitting the two into independently prioritised items is misleading, because the capability cannot be delivered without the rework, so prioritising them separately hides a hard dependency. Quantifying the architectural cost before prioritising is what lets the value judgement be made honestly.
