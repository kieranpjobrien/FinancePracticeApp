# Question

Three days into a two-week sprint, the product owner of a retail-analytics team wants to swap a story currently in progress for a newly urgent one of similar size. The new story still serves the sprint goal. Developers have already written code for the in-progress story. The team holds daily scrums and tracks work on a board. What is the most appropriate way to handle this?

## Options

- **A**: Have the product owner and developers weigh the trade-off together; if they agree the goal is still met, return the in-progress story to the product backlog and pull in the new one, accepting the parked work.
- **B**: Let the product owner make the swap, since the product owner owns the backlog and the new story is the higher priority.
- **C**: Have the team finish the in-progress story first so its code is not wasted, then pull the urgent story into the remaining sprint capacity.
- **D**: Hold the swap until the next sprint planning so the current sprint's committed scope stays stable, and rank the urgent story at the top of the backlog.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Sprint scope can be renegotiated between the product owner and the developers as understanding changes, provided the sprint goal is not endangered; a like-for-like swap by mutual agreement is legitimate and is a team decision, not the product owner's alone. (A) reflects that shared negotiation and the real cost of parked work. (B) treats backlog ownership as authority to reorder work already in progress unilaterally, sidelining the developers who own how the sprint work is done. (C) preserves the written code but forces both stories into the sprint, threatening the capacity and the commitment. (D) protects stability but needlessly defers a same-size swap the team could absorb now by agreement, which is more rigid than agile intends when the goal still holds. Collaborative renegotiation that protects the sprint goal is the mechanism.
