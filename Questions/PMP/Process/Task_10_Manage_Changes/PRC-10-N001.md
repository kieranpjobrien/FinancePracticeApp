# Question

During execution of a predictive water-treatment upgrade, an engineer tells the project manager she has already roughed out an automated chemical-dosing feature the customer never requested. She is confident the operations team will value it, it fits inside the remaining budget, and it would not move the delivery date. The customer's acceptance criteria do not mention dosing automation. The engineer wants to finish and ship it in the current release. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Capture the idea as a proposed change so the customer can weigh it against their priorities, and leave the current release scoped to the agreed baseline.
- **B**: Let her complete it for this release since it is within budget and on schedule, and note the added capability in the as-built documentation.
- **C**: Add the feature to the product roadmap for a future phase and ask the customer's representative to confirm whether it belongs there.
- **D**: Ask her to pause and raise the suggestion with the customer's operations lead informally before deciding whether to build it.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Building capability the customer never asked for is gold plating; the customer, not the team, decides whether unrequested value is wanted, and that decision belongs in change control where its impact and desirability are assessed against the agreed baseline. (A) routes it correctly and protects the current release. (B) lets the team enlarge scope unilaterally and ship it, the defining error, however benign the cost looks. (C) and (D) both keep the idea alive and involve the customer, which is reasonable, but each pre-judges where the feature lands (a future phase; an informal chat) instead of submitting it as a change for proper evaluation and decision. Surfacing it as a controlled proposed change is the disciplined move.
