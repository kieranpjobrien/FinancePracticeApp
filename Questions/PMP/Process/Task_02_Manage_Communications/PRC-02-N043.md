# Question

A severity-1 outage on a banking platform is in active response under a named incident commander, who owns all decisions during the incident. The PM is on the bridge managing project-side coordination. The head of corporate communications joins and says a holding statement must go to customers within the hour; she asks the PM to confirm the customer impact and the restoration time so she can draft it. Engineering has isolated the fault and confirmed which services are degraded, but has not yet validated a restoration estimate, and the incident commander is mid-decision on the recovery path. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Give corporate communications the confirmed impact now and tell her the restoration estimate will follow once engineering validates one, then have the incident commander authorise the statement before it goes out.
- **B**: Give corporate communications the confirmed impact and a clearly labelled engineering best-guess restoration window so she can draft a complete holding statement to the one-hour deadline.
- **C**: Ask corporate communications to hold the statement until engineering validates a restoration estimate, so the customer message does not have to be corrected later.
- **D**: Confirm the impact with corporate communications and ask her to route the restoration time and final sign-off through the incident commander, since recovery timing and external release sit with that role.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The PM's job is to supply accurate project information promptly while respecting that, in a declared incident, the incident commander owns the decision to release. The first move is to hand over what is confirmed (impact and degraded services), be explicit that the restoration estimate is not yet validated, and route the statement through the commander for authorisation. (A) communicates now, avoids an unvalidated public commitment, and keeps the decision with its owner. (D) is the closest rival and correctly recognises the commander's authority, but it makes the PM's first act the deferral of confirmed, releasable information rather than supplying it, slowing a message that the one-hour clock makes urgent. (B) pushes an unvalidated restoration time into an external channel, risking a public promise the team cannot keep. (C) withholds impact information that is already confirmed and useful, leaving a vacuum that speculation will fill before the hour is out.
