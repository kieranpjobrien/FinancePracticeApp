# Question

A new agile team's daily stand-up has become a round of detailed status updates delivered to the Scrum Master, with members addressing only the Scrum Master and rarely each other. Dependencies and coordination gaps between members keep surfacing later in the sprint. The Scrum Master wants the stand-up to start producing the cross-member coordination it is meant to. Which change is most likely to achieve that?

## Options

- **A**: Reframe the stand-up around the team coordinating toward the sprint goal, asking members to raise dependencies to one another rather than report upward.
- **B**: Step out of receiving the updates and let the team run the stand-up itself, so members naturally turn to each other.
- **C**: Switch to walking the board right to left, focusing on in-progress items and what each needs to finish, so the conversation centres on the work and the hand-offs between members.
- **D**: Keep the stand-up brief and move surfaced dependencies into a short follow-on session where the affected members coordinate.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

A brand-new team has not yet learned how to coordinate, so an instruction to "talk to each other" gives them the goal without a mechanism, whereas changing what the stand-up is organised around gives them a concrete structure that produces the behaviour. (C) reorients the meeting onto in-progress work and what it needs to finish, which naturally forces members to surface hand-offs and dependencies to whoever owns the adjacent work, teaching coordination through the format itself. (A) names the right outcome but, for an inexperienced team, "raise dependencies to one another" is the very skill they lack, so the reframe can leave them still unsure how; it is necessary framing but a weaker lever alone. (B) removes the Scrum Master as audience, which helps, but stepping back without a new structure can leave a vacuum for a team that has not learned to self-organise. (D) defers coordination to a second meeting rather than getting the stand-up to do its job, adding overhead. The fine margin is between giving the team a concrete work-centred structure that generates coordination (C) and asking for the coordinating behaviour directly before they have a way to do it (A/B).
