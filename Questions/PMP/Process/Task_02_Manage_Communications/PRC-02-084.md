# Question

A hybrid infrastructure programme reports monthly to an executive sponsor. This period the schedule and budget are tracking to plan, but a long-lead equipment supplier has signalled a possible eight-week slip that could push a regulatory milestone the sponsor personally committed to the board. The PM has not yet worked up mitigation options and the slip is not yet confirmed. The monthly report to the sponsor is due now. How should the PM handle this period's communication to the sponsor?

## Options

- **A**: Send the standard monthly report with schedule, budget, scope and risks all covered, the supplier slip noted in the risk section alongside the other risks.
- **B**: Lead with a short, focused flag of the potential supplier slip and its link to the board-committed milestone, stating the decision the PM will bring her once options are worked up.
- **C**: Hold the supplier slip out of this report until it is confirmed and mitigation options are ready, so the PM brings her a problem and a recommendation together rather than an unresolved worry.
- **D**: Send the routine report now and, separately, request a short call to brief her on the supplier slip so she hears the nuance directly rather than reading it cold.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The sponsor's distinguishing feature here is that she personally owns the threatened commitment, so the period's communication must make the one decision-relevant item unmissable and tell her what will be asked of her, even though the slip is unconfirmed and options are not yet ready. (B) surfaces the threat, ties it to her board milestone, and sets up the decision to come, which is the actionable upward flag a time-sensitive executive issue demands. (A) includes the slip but levels it with routine risks, so the item most likely to need her attention competes with everything else for notice. (C) is a defensible instinct, bring a problem with its solution, but here the slip threatens a commitment only she can act on at board level, and waiting for confirmation can close her window; early credible signals on owned commitments should be raised, not held. (D) reaches her with nuance but defers the flag to a call that may not happen before her next board contact, and splits the signal from the report rather than making it prominent now. The discriminator is whether the emerging threat to her own commitment is raised promptly and prominently or deferred, buried, or held.
