# Question

A PM leads a hybrid programme modernising a bank's mortgage origination platform. One monthly report goes to both the executive steering committee and the three delivery teams. The teams use it heavily. The executives have said it is hard to act on: twelve pages of feature-level detail, from which they cannot quickly tell whether the programme is on track to deliver the business outcomes they funded. The PM has limited time to spend on reporting each month. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Add a one-page front summary to the existing report, organised around progress towards the funded outcomes and decisions the executives need to make.
- **B**: Produce a separate one-page executive view built around the funded outcomes and pending decisions, and keep the detailed report for the teams.
- **C**: Restructure the single report so it leads with outcome-level status and relegates the feature detail to clearly labelled appendices the teams can still use.
- **D**: Keep one report but reorder it so the highest-priority items for executives appear first, ahead of the routine team detail.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The executives and the teams are making different decisions from the same data: executives need outcome-level signal and the asks directed at them, the teams need feature-level operational detail, and the executives' difficulty is the absence of an outcome-oriented view, not merely where the detail sits. (B) gives each audience a product shaped for its decisions while preserving the teams' detail intact, the cleanest fit to both needs and sustainable to produce once the executive view is templated. (A) prepends a summary but leaves the executives navigating a document built for a different audience, and a précis of feature detail is not the same as a view framed around business outcomes. (C) improves the ordering and protects the team detail, yet still asks one document to serve two decision sets and frames executive status as the top of a delivery report rather than on its own terms. (D) only re-sequences the same content, so the executives still receive a feature-level report and must extract the outcome picture themselves. The discriminator is whether one reshaped document can serve both decision sets or whether the audiences genuinely need distinct products.
