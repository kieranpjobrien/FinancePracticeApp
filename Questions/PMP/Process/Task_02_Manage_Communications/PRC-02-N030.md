# Question

A critical supplier has just told the PM a key component will arrive two weeks late, affecting a milestone due next week. The PM has a register showing exactly which stakeholders depend on that milestone; some must replan their own work within a day, while others are only loosely affected. The PM has 30 minutes before a previously scheduled all-stakeholder status call. What is the most appropriate way to communicate the delay?

## Options

- **A**: Contact the stakeholders who must act within a day directly and now, then cover the delay for the wider group on the scheduled call.
- **B**: Announce the delay and its impact to everyone on the all-stakeholder call shortly, so all parties hear it at the same time from one source.
- **C**: Send the delay and its impact in a single written notice to the full stakeholder distribution immediately so nothing is missed.
- **D**: Notify the sponsor and project board first to agree a response, then communicate the delay to affected stakeholders.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Urgency and impact differ across the audience, so the PM should push hardest to the few who must replan within a day while using the existing forum for the rest. (A) gets the time-critical message to exactly those who need to act, immediately, and avoids making them wait 30 minutes for a call that also carries irrelevant content for many attendees. (B) is timely but lets the most affected stakeholders learn of an hours-critical impact via a general meeting, slower and less certain for them. (C) reaches everyone fast but treats a one-day-deadline stakeholder and a loosely affected one identically, burying the urgent action in a mass notice. (D) is prudent for deciding a recovery plan, but holding the warning from stakeholders who must replan now, pending a board discussion, costs them time they do not have. The fine margin is between targeting the urgent few first (A) and broadcasting uniformly to all (B/C).
