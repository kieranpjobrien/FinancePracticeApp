# Question

A globally distributed team already rotates its weekly synchronous meeting so the awkward hours fall on a different region each month, and substantive decisions are supposed to be made asynchronously in a shared document. Yet the PM notices the same two regions still drive most decisions: they post in the async thread first and at length, replies from the other two regions arrive after the document has effectively converged, and the rotating call mostly ratifies what the thread already settled. Delivery is on track. The PM wants every region to shape decisions on fair terms, not just receive a fair share of bad meeting times. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Set a defined async decision window for each item that stays open across all four regions' working hours before any decision is taken as settled, so later time zones contribute before convergence, not after.
- **B**: Ask the two dominant regions to hold their async posts until the other two have commented first, so the quieter regions are not anchored by an early position.
- **C**: Assign each decision a rotating regional facilitator who gathers all four regions' input and summarises it before the decision is confirmed, spreading ownership of the process.
- **D**: Keep the rotation and async model but add a pre-read circulated a day ahead of each decision thread, so every region arrives with the context to engage promptly.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The structure is already fair on meeting times and nominally async, so the real defect is timing: decisions converge inside the dominant regions' hours before the others are awake to weigh in. Guaranteeing a decision window that spans all four regions' working time before anything is settled directly removes that asymmetry. (A) attacks the convergence-timing cause. (B) is the closest rival and targets anchoring, but policing the order of posts is hard to enforce and still lets a fast thread close before the held-back regions are online; it treats sequence when the problem is the clock. (C) spreads process ownership and helps, yet a facilitator summarising input does not by itself stop a decision converging before two regions have engaged unless a spanning window also holds it open. (D) improves readiness so regions can engage faster, but a better pre-read does not help a region that is asleep when the thread reaches consensus. The gap between (A) and the rest is fine: each improves participation, but only (A) makes elapsed time across all zones a precondition for settling the decision.
