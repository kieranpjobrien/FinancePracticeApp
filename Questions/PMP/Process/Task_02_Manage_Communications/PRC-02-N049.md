# Question

A Scrum Master notices the daily stand-up now runs 30 to 35 minutes. Two patterns are driving it: developers regularly open up live debugging of blockers during the event, and the product owner plus two delivery managers attend as observers, with the managers occasionally asking developers to justify slow items. The team is meeting its sprint commitments, but several developers have privately said the stand-up now feels like a status interrogation and they have started withholding detail about where they are stuck. What should the Scrum Master do?

## Options

- **A**: Re-establish the stand-up's purpose with the team as a short coordination checkpoint toward the sprint goal, agree that detailed problem-solving moves to a directly-following session for those affected, and coach the observing managers on the questioning that is undermining safety.
- **B**: Reaffirm a strict 15-minute timebox and a three-question round-robin so each developer reports in turn, keeping the event short and predictable for everyone present.
- **C**: Ask the two delivery managers to stop attending the stand-up, since their questioning is the source of the interrogation feeling and the event is for the developers.
- **D**: Hold a focused after-stand-up slot for blockers and managers' questions, leaving the stand-up itself for quick coordination, so the deep discussion still happens but outside the timeboxed event.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Two distinct problems are in play: the event has lost its purpose (coordination has been displaced by debugging) and psychological safety is eroding (managers' questioning makes developers withhold detail). A durable fix has to address both, and the safety problem is rooted in behaviour, not mere attendance. (A) restores the event's intent, relocates problem-solving, and tackles the questioning at its source by coaching the managers, who are legitimately allowed to observe if they do not disrupt. (D) is the closest rival and correctly moves problem-solving out, but it leaves the managers' questioning behaviour unaddressed, so developers may still self-censor in the stand-up itself. (C) treats removal as a substitute for fixing behaviour; observers are permitted, and excluding them avoids the real issue while signalling the team cannot work transparently in front of leadership. (B) controls duration but a rigid report-round can deepen the report-out feeling, and timeboxing alone neither restores the coordination purpose nor protects safety.
