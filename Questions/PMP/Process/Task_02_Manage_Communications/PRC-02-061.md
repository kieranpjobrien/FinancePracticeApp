# Question

A predictive civil-engineering project began with six people who all spoke directly to one another. The sponsor has just confirmed four more team members will join next week, and the PM notices coordination is already getting noticeably harder to keep on top of. As the PM updates how the team will communicate, which understanding should most shape the revised approach?

## Options

- **A**: Adding four people roughly doubles the team, so the coordination effort the PM should plan for will also roughly double.
- **B**: The number of possible communication paths grows far faster than headcount, so informal all-to-all contact will not scale and structure is needed.
- **C**: Larger teams communicate more efficiently because more people can share the load of passing information around.
- **D**: Coordination difficulty depends on personalities rather than team size, so the headcount change itself should not drive the approach.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The number of communication channels follows n(n-1)/2, so six people have 15 paths while ten have 45, a tripling of potential links for a less-than-doubling of headcount. (B) captures why unmanaged all-to-all contact breaks down and why the PM must introduce structured channels. (A) understates the growth by treating it as linear with headcount. (C) is the opposite of what happens, more people add coordination overhead, not efficiency, absent structure. (D) is partly true but ignores the structural driver the question is testing, the non-linear rise in channels with size.
