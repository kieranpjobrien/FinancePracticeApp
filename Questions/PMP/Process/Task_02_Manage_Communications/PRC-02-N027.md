# Question

A predictive programme's sponsor reads the monthly status report on a phone between meetings and acknowledges receipt each time. At a governance review the sponsor reacts with alarm to a schedule risk that the report had flagged, with a clear heading, two months earlier. The report is accurate, well structured, and was opened by the sponsor both months. What should the PM change?

## Options

- **A**: Introduce a brief monthly conversation with the sponsor to walk through the report's key messages and confirm what they have taken from it.
- **B**: Restructure the report so the most decision-critical items, such as live schedule risks, lead the document instead of sitting mid-way through.
- **C**: Add a short "what's changed and what needs your attention" callout box at the top of each report.
- **D**: Switch critical risk escalations to a separate, immediate alert to the sponsor rather than relying on the monthly report alone.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The report was accurate, well structured, opened, and acknowledged, yet the message did not register, so improving the artefact alone is unlikely to help; what is missing is confirmation that the content was actually understood. (A) closes that loop by adding a two-way exchange that surfaces a misread before it becomes a governance shock. (B) and (C) are good formatting practice, but the risk already had a clear heading and was still missed, so reordering or boxing it assumes an attention problem the evidence does not support. (D) would catch a brand-new risk, but this item was present for two months in a report the sponsor opened, so the failure is comprehension, not channel speed. The fine margin is between verifying understanding (A) and making an already-clear message yet more prominent (B/C/D).
