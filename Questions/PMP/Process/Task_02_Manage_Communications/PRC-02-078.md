# Question

On a virtual programme, the PM identifies three distinct communication barriers, each with a different underlying cause. Match each barrier (left) to the remedy that addresses its actual cause (right).

## Items

- **1**: Two team members in different time zones keep missing each other; by the time one reads a message and replies, the other is offline, and simple questions take two days to resolve.
- **2**: A stakeholder consistently reads the PM's reports through the lens of a past failed project and assumes the worst, regardless of what the report actually says.
- **3**: Technical updates written in heavy jargon are not understood by the business sponsors they are sent to, who quietly disengage.

## Matches

- **A**: Reframe and rebuild trust through direct conversation that addresses the prior experience colouring how messages are received.
- **B**: Establish a short overlapping synchronous window, or a clear async protocol with expected response times, to bridge the time gap.
- **C**: Adjust the language to the audience, replacing jargon with terms the recipients understand.

## Answer

1-B, 2-A, 3-C

## Explanation

Each barrier has a different cause, and an effective remedy must match the cause, not the symptom. The time-zone lag (1) is a structural availability problem, solved by creating overlap or an agreed async cadence (1-B). The stakeholder's pessimistic reading (2) is a perceptual filter rooted in past experience, which no amount of clearer writing will shift; it requires direct work on trust and reframing (2-A). The jargon problem (3) is a straightforward encoding mismatch, fixed by speaking the audience's language (3-C). The trap is that all three look like "they didn't get the message", but pairing, say, the perception barrier (2) with clearer language (C) would treat the wrong cause entirely. Correct diagnosis of the underlying barrier is what drives the right remedy.
