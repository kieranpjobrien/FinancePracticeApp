# Question

A serious data-handling incident has occurred. The PM must communicate with stakeholders while facts are still emerging, regulators are watching, and emotions are high. Which actions reflect sound communication practice here? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Acknowledge the incident promptly to affected stakeholders, stating clearly what is and is not yet known and when the next update will come.
- **B**: Tailor the depth and channel of each message to the audience, such as regulators versus the internal delivery team.
- **C**: Route all external messages through a single agreed spokesperson and approved wording so the programme speaks with one consistent voice.
- **D**: Hold each update until it has been fully verified, even if that means longer gaps, so the PM never communicates anything that later proves wrong.
- **E**: Lead the first stakeholder message with a clear account of which supplier or individual appears to have caused the incident.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

In an unfolding incident, communication should be timely, honest, audience-appropriate, and consistent. (A) prevents an information vacuum and sets expectations under uncertainty; (B) matches sensitive content to the right audience and channel; (C) coordinating one approved voice prevents conflicting public statements during a sensitive event. (D) sounds responsible but, taken to "never communicate anything that could prove wrong," it produces silence exactly when stakeholders need acknowledgement, the opposite of the prompt, transparent updating that maintains trust. (E) rushes to attribute blame before facts are established, which is premature, can be wrong, and inflames an already charged situation rather than informing it. The discrimination is between prompt, framed, coordinated honesty (A/B/C) and either over-withholding (D) or premature blame (E).
