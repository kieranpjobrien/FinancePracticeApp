# Question

A PM is planning communications for a predictive bank-systems migration that will reduce headcount in the operations department once it goes live. The stakeholder register spans the board, regulators, the operations staff whose roles are affected, an external systems vendor, and the customer base. The PM is deciding which factors should most shape how each group is communicated with. Which three considerations should most influence the tailored approach for these groups?

## Options

- **A**: The sensitivity and confidentiality of the information, since headcount and migration details cannot be released to every group at the same time or in the same form.
- **B**: Each group's level of interest in, and influence over, the project, which shapes how much detail they receive and how often they are engaged.
- **C**: The reporting cadence the PM already uses on other projects, applied uniformly here so the approach stays consistent and easy to run.
- **D**: The cultural, language and channel needs of each group, including the regulator's mandatory reporting format and timing.
- **E**: The volume of information per group set in proportion to headcount, so larger groups such as customers and operations staff receive correspondingly more detail.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

Tailoring communications flows from stakeholder analysis and the specific characteristics of each audience, so the PM must pick the three factors that genuinely differentiate the approach across groups this varied. (A) is decisive because headcount-sensitive and regulator-bound information must be timed and restricted by group rather than released uniformly, with real consequences if leaked early. (B) reflects the interest-and-influence assessment that sets engagement depth and cadence for audiences as different as the board and the general customer base. (D) captures the audience-specific factors, language, channel, culture, and the regulator's mandated format and deadlines, that determine how each message is framed and delivered. (C) anchors the plan to the PM's existing habit rather than to these audiences' needs, which is the opposite of tailoring even though consistency sounds reasonable. (E) sizes information by group headcount, a plausible-looking fairness rule, but volume should track each group's needs and the decisions they make, not their size, since a two-person regulator may warrant more, and more formal, communication than the entire customer base. The discriminator is whether a factor differentiates by audience need (A, B, D) or imposes the PM's convenience or a crude proportionality rule (C, E).
