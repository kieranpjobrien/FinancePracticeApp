# Question

A PM runs a fortnightly review for a distributed pharmaceutical project entirely over audio-only conference calls because some participants have limited bandwidth. The PM has noticed that decisions made on these calls are frequently reopened afterwards, and that quieter participants later say they had reservations they "could not get across" on the call. Spoken participation on the calls themselves seems normal. What adjustment is most likely to help?

## Options

- **A**: Switch the reviews to video so the PM and participants can read facial expressions and visible hesitation during the discussion.
- **B**: After each decision on the call, explicitly invite dissent and pause, and circulate the decision afterward for a short confirmation window.
- **C**: Shorten the calls and move detailed discussion to written threads where quieter participants can compose their points in their own time.
- **D**: Appoint a facilitator to call on each participant by name in turn so everyone is required to voice a position.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A large share of meaning in interactive communication is carried non-verbally, and audio-only strips the facial and postural cues that signal hesitation or disagreement, which is precisely why reservations are going unread and decisions unravel. (A) restores that channel and lets the PM see the reluctance that audio hides. (B) helps and is good practice, but verbally inviting dissent does not recover the visual cues that reveal unspoken reservation, and the quiet participants already struggle to "get it across" in words. (D) forces a spoken position but can pressure reluctant people into false agreement and still lacks visual feedback. (C) abandons real-time dialogue for contentious decisions that benefit from live back-and-forth, trading one weakness for another. The bandwidth constraint is real but is a problem to solve, not a reason to keep a channel that is causing rework.
