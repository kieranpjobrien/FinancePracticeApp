# Question

A PM on a predictive public-sector programme is preparing the communications approach for a large, diverse stakeholder body. The PM wants the plan to be genuinely useful rather than a document that is written once and ignored. Which considerations should shape what goes into the plan and how it is maintained? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: The specific information needs, preferred channels, and language of each stakeholder group, derived from analysing them rather than assumed.
- **B**: A defined way to gauge whether communications are working and a commitment to revise the approach when they are not.
- **C**: Who is responsible for producing and releasing each communication, so accountability for it is unambiguous.
- **D**: A complete, fixed schedule of every message for the whole programme, locked at the outset so stakeholders know exactly what to expect.
- **E**: A single uniform format and cadence applied to all stakeholders so the programme communicates consistently and fairly.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

A useful communications plan rests on understanding each audience, building in a feedback-and-revision mechanism, and assigning clear ownership. (A) tailors communication to analysed stakeholder needs; (B) makes the plan adaptive by measuring effectiveness and committing to change; (C) fixes accountability for producing and releasing each communication. (D) sounds disciplined but locking the entire schedule at the outset denies the adaptability (B) demands; stakeholder needs and circumstances evolve, and a frozen plan goes stale, which is the very outcome the PM wants to avoid. (E) confuses fairness with uniformity: forcing one format and cadence on a diverse body ignores (A) and serves some audiences poorly; equitable communication means meeting differing needs, not treating everyone identically. The margin separates audience-tailored, adaptive, accountable design (A/B/C) from rigid uniformity dressed up as consistency (D/E).
