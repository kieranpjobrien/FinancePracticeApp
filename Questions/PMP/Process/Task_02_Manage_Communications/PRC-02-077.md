# Question

Halfway through a hybrid programme, the PM is reviewing whether the communications approach is still working. Which observations are valid signals that the communications management plan needs revising, as opposed to signals that should be interpreted differently before acting? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: A newly onboarded regulator with distinct reporting needs has become a key stakeholder since the plan was written.
- **B**: Several stakeholders report receiving information too late to act on it, despite the plan's stated frequencies being followed.
- **C**: A scheduled monthly report drew no questions or comments this month from its usual readers.
- **D**: The chosen channel for one stakeholder group has changed because the organisation has retired the tool the plan specified.
- **E**: One stakeholder sent a single terse reply to an update during a week the team knew she was travelling.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

A communications plan should be revisited when the stakeholder landscape, the plan's effectiveness, or the available channels have genuinely changed. (A) is a new key stakeholder with new needs, a clear trigger; (B) shows the current cadence is failing its purpose even though it is being followed, so the plan, not compliance, is at fault; (D) is a channel named in the plan that no longer exists, forcing an update. (C) is ambiguous: no questions could mean the report was clear and sufficient, not that anything is wrong, so it should be interpreted, perhaps with a quick check, before assuming a defect. (E) is a single data point with an obvious situational explanation, travel, and reading too much into one terse reply would be over-reacting. The fine margin is between durable changes in stakeholders, effectiveness, or channels (A/B/D) and isolated signals that warrant interpretation rather than a plan revision (C/E).
