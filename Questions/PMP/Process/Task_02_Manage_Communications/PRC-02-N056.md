# Question

Business stakeholders rarely attend the Friday-afternoon sprint review, so their feedback arrives weeks later by email and often contradicts decisions already shipped. The product owner has spoken with them and learned the cause: the review clashes with a standing leadership meeting, and even when free, the stakeholders find a full increment walkthrough more detail than they need to give useful direction. The reviews run well for the technical attendees who do come. What should the team do?

## Options

- **A**: Keep the review as is for the technical attendees and run a separate fortnightly business feedback session timed around the leadership meeting.
- **B**: Move the sprint review to a slot the business stakeholders can attend, and add a short business-focused segment that asks them the specific direction-setting questions their feedback should answer.
- **C**: Reschedule the sprint review to the confirmed free slot, since removing the clash is what stops the feedback arriving late.
- **D**: Send the stakeholders a concise written summary of each increment with a 48-hour window to respond, so their input is captured close to the review without requiring attendance.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The cause is twofold, a timing clash and a format pitched at the wrong altitude for business input, so the fix must remove the clash and reshape the event to elicit the direction the stakeholders are best placed to give. (B) addresses both, keeping feedback inside the single delivery-cadence event. (C) fixes only the timing and leaves a walkthrough that still over-serves business stakeholders, so they may attend yet still give little useful steer. (A) solves both problems but splits one feedback loop into two events, doubling coordination and risking the business and technical pictures diverging. (D) brings feedback closer than the current weeks-late email but reverts to a one-way written loop, forgoing the real-time exchange the review is meant to provide.
