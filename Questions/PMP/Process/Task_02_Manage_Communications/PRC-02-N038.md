# Question

A steering committee tells the PM that the monthly reports, full of completed-task counts and hours logged, leave them unable to judge whether the project will finish on time and on budget or decide whether to intervene. The PM has reported these figures diligently and on time. What change to the report's content will best serve the committee?

## Options

- **A**: Add forecasts such as estimate at completion and projected finish date, with the assumptions and key risks behind them.
- **B**: Add earned value measures, the schedule and cost variances and performance indices, showing how far ahead or behind the project currently is.
- **C**: Add a trend of the completed-task and hours figures over recent months so the committee can see the direction of travel.
- **D**: Add a RAG status against each major milestone so the committee can see at a glance which milestones are at risk.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The committee needs to judge where the project will end up and whether to act, which is a forward-looking question that only projected outcomes answer. (A) supplies the forecast of final time and cost, plus the assumptions and risks that let the committee weigh whether to intervene, addressing their question head-on. (B) is strong and closely related, but variances and indices describe performance to date and require the committee to extrapolate the outcome themselves rather than handing them the projection. (C) trends the same backward-looking activity data, showing direction but still not a finish position. (D) flags risk per milestone usefully but conveys current standing, not a quantified end-state for time and budget. The fine margin is between giving the committee the projected outcome outright (A) and giving them present-performance measures from which they must infer it (B/C/D).
