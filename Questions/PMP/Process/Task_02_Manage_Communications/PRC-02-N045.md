# Question

A Scrum team has already moved its task board and burndown into a shared digital tool that updates in real time, after three developers went permanently remote. The tool is accurate and everyone can reach it. Yet the remote developers still ask the Scrum Master for verbal status, and the on-site product owner keeps using a whiteboard sketch during conversations that the remote members never see. Velocity is unchanged. The Scrum Master wants the digital radiator to actually carry the team's shared picture rather than sit unused beside informal channels. What should the Scrum Master do?

## Options

- **A**: Establish that the digital radiator is the team's single authoritative view, and fold the off-tool exchanges — the product owner's sketches and the verbal status asks — into it so progress is read there by all.
- **B**: Have the product owner photograph each whiteboard sketch and post it to the team channel, so the remote developers see the same artefacts the on-site members discuss.
- **C**: Add a standing item to the daily scrum where the Scrum Master verbally walks the remote developers through the board, since their requests show they need the spoken context.
- **D**: Schedule a short twice-weekly video call where the on-site and remote members review the digital board together, keeping everyone aligned on the same live data.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The radiator already exists and works; the failure is that information is leaking into side channels (whiteboard sketches, verbal asks) that the tool does not capture, so the fix is to make the digital view authoritative and route those exchanges through it rather than around it. (A) closes the leak at its source. (D) is the closest rival and uses the right artefact, but a twice-weekly synchronous review reintroduces a scheduled push for status that a live radiator is meant to make unnecessary, and between calls the sketches and verbal asks continue. (C) institutionalises the very verbal-relay habit that signals the radiator is being bypassed, turning the daily scrum into a status read-out. (B) propagates the sketches but as stale snapshots into a second channel, splitting the picture across tool and chat instead of consolidating it. The distinction between (A) and (D) is fine: both keep one data source, but only (A) removes the parallel informal channels rather than adding a meeting on top of them.
