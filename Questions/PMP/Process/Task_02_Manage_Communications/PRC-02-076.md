# Question

A predictive programme spans an English-speaking head office and a delivery centre where English is a second language. The PM has noticed that written instructions in idiomatic English are often misinterpreted at the delivery centre, while the same teams perform well when requirements are conveyed in structured, literal language with diagrams. A new, complex set of acceptance criteria must now be communicated to the delivery centre. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Convey the acceptance criteria in clear, literal language supported by diagrams, and confirm understanding through the delivery centre's lead.
- **B**: Have the acceptance criteria professionally translated into the delivery centre's first language to eliminate any ambiguity.
- **C**: Hold a live video session to walk through the acceptance criteria so questions can be answered in real time as they arise.
- **D**: Send the criteria as written, then ask the delivery centre to restate each one back so misreadings can be corrected.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The PM has direct evidence of what works for this audience, literal, structured language with visual support, so the right move is to communicate in the form already shown to land and then confirm understanding. (A) applies that evidence and closes the loop. (B) assumes the issue is language rather than style, but the teams succeed with literal English plus diagrams, so translation is unnecessary and may introduce its own errors on technical terms. (C) adds useful real-time dialogue but, on its own, still risks the idiomatic-speech misunderstandings the PM has observed unless the material is first cast in the literal, visual form; live talk does not fix the medium. (D) keeps the failing idiomatic written form and relies on catching every misread afterwards, fixing symptoms rather than preventing them. The discriminator is using observed audience evidence to choose the medium, not defaulting to translation or a meeting.
