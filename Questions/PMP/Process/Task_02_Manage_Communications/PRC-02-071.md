# Question

A PM joins an agile programme where stakeholder communication has quietly broken down: updates go out but produce no questions, no decisions, and occasional surprise when stakeholders later object to things they were "never told". The PM wants to rebuild communication that actually lands. Which actions directly improve whether communication is received and understood, rather than merely whether it is sent? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Establish feedback channels and check, through questions and responses, that messages were understood as intended.
- **B**: Match each communication's method and timing to how the audience prefers and is able to receive information.
- **C**: Increase the frequency of the existing status broadcasts so stakeholders are exposed to the information more often.
- **D**: Confirm intended outcomes after key communications, such as decisions taken or actions started, not just that the message was issued.
- **E**: Standardise all updates into one template and channel so stakeholders always know exactly where to look.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

Effective communication is judged by reception and understanding, not transmission, so the actions that close the loop are the discriminators. (A) builds the feedback that confirms understanding; (B) tailors delivery so the message can actually be received; (D) verifies that communication produced its intended effect rather than just leaving the outbox. (C) increases volume of the same one-way broadcasts that are already failing, more of an ineffective channel does not make it effective and risks fatigue. (E) improves consistency and findability, a reasonable hygiene step, but a single tidy template guarantees neither that the audience reads it nor that they understood it, which is the actual problem. The fine margin is between actions that confirm impact (A/B/D) and actions that only refine or repeat transmission (C/E).
