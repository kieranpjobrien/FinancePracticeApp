# Question

A PM emails a detailed written instruction to an offshore test team about a configuration change. The team acknowledges receipt, but when the work comes back two days later it has been done in a way the PM did not intend, and the offshore lead says the email "clearly meant" something different. The instruction itself was unambiguous to the PM and to two colleagues who read it. What change to how the PM communicates such instructions would most reduce a recurrence?

## Options

- **A**: Mark future instructions as high importance and require a same-day read receipt so the team treats them more carefully.
- **B**: Build in a step where the receiver restates their understanding of the instruction back before acting on it.
- **C**: Write instructions in shorter sentences and simpler English so less can be lost in interpretation.
- **D**: Follow every written instruction with a recorded video walkthrough the team can replay as needed.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The breakdown is in decoding, the receiver formed a different meaning than the sender encoded, and the only reliable guard against that is a feedback loop that surfaces the gap before work starts. (B) is that loop and directly attacks the failure mode. (C) reduces some ambiguity but cannot guarantee shared meaning, the original was already clear to fluent readers, so the problem is interpretation, not complexity. (D) adds richer one-way media but still has no confirmation of understanding, so a misread can persist. (A) addresses attention and timing, not comprehension, and the team already acknowledged receipt, so importance flags solve nothing here.
