# Question

A predictive infrastructure project reports to its steering committee with a green/amber/red dashboard each fortnight, governed by thresholds the committee approved: schedule turns amber only when slippage consumes agreed float. The schedule has slipped three days against a 14-month plan with ample float, so the indicator is correctly green. One committee member, reacting to a corridor rumour that "the project is behind," emails the PM privately and copies two other members, questioning whether the dashboard is masking trouble. The next committee meeting is in nine days. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Reply only to the member who raised it, walk them through the float position, and ask them to reassure the others, keeping the exchange off a wider thread.
- **B**: Bring a short agenda item to the next committee proposing a review of whether the float-based amber threshold still reflects the committee's risk appetite, and address the rumour there.
- **C**: Reply to the member and the two copied, confirming the slip, showing it sits within the approved float threshold, and offering the schedule analysis for anyone who wants it.
- **D**: Issue an interim note to the full committee confirming the green status holds, explaining the threshold logic, and flagging the three-day slip so no one relies on the rumour.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The rumour is already spreading among the copied members, so the PM should respond to exactly the people now engaged, correct the misunderstanding with the threshold evidence, and offer the detail without pushing it at everyone. (C) matches the response to the actual audience and need. (D) escalates a non-issue to the entire committee, lending the rumour more weight than it warrants and over-communicating to members who never raised it. (A) under-communicates by leaving two members who saw the concern without a direct answer and delegates reassurance to a peer who lacks the data. (B) is a reasonable governance action for later, but treating a fortnight-old rumour as a trigger to renegotiate agreed thresholds confuses a communication gap with a threshold defect and defers the correction nine days.
