# Question

Three months in, the PM finds that decisions are repeatedly reopened because stakeholders say they "never heard" about them. Distribution logs show the relevant emails were sent and opened. In a quick sounding, several stakeholders say the decisions were buried mid-thread in long weekly digests they skim, and that they had no easy way to flag a concern at the time, so objections only surfaced later. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Survey all stakeholders to confirm channel preferences and rebuild the communications management plan around the results before changing how decisions are communicated.
- **B**: Add a "decisions this week" section to the top of the existing digest so the items the stakeholders are missing become easy to find at a glance.
- **C**: Replace the buried-in-digest pattern with decision communications that are issued discretely and carry a short window for stakeholders to raise objections before the decision is treated as settled.
- **D**: Begin recording each decision in a shared decision log that stakeholders can consult, so there is an authoritative record when someone claims they were unaware.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The sounding points to two specific defects, decisions lost inside skimmed digests and no timely channel to object, so the fix is to communicate decisions distinctly and build in a feedback window that closes the loop before they are locked. (C) addresses both causes the evidence revealed. (B) improves findability within the digest but still relies on stakeholders catching items in a format they admit they skim, and adds no way to raise concerns in time. (D) creates a useful record but a log is pull communication that stakeholders consult after the fact; it settles "who knew" disputes without preventing the late objections. (A) restarts broad analysis the PM has effectively already done for this problem, deferring an evident fix to re-run discovery the targeted feedback from stakeholders has made unnecessary.
