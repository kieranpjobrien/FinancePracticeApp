# Question

A PM consistently sends accurate weekly risk summaries up to the steering committee through the programme director, who insists all upward communication route through him. Over two months the committee has acted on a noticeably softened picture: high risks are arriving described as moderate, and two issues the PM flagged never reached the committee at all. The PM has confirmed the original summaries were clear and complete. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Begin sending the risk summaries directly to the steering committee in parallel so they receive the unfiltered version.
- **B**: Raise with the programme director, with specific examples, that the summaries are reaching the committee altered, and agree how risk information will be conveyed faithfully.
- **C**: Add quantified severity scores and trend charts to the summaries so the information is harder to soften when passed on.
- **D**: Document the discrepancy in the risk register and the issue log so there is a record that the PM reported the risks accurately.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The information is being distorted as it passes through an intermediary, a filtering barrier, and the first step is to address it with the person introducing the distortion, using concrete evidence, and agree a faithful conveyance. (B) tackles the root cause directly and respectfully. (A) bypasses the director's stated channel without first attempting to fix it, undermining a relationship and overstepping; parallel reporting may follow if the conversation fails, but it is not the first move. (C) makes softening harder but a determined intermediary can still reframe or omit, and two items were dropped entirely, which formatting cannot prevent. (D) protects the PM but does nothing to get accurate risk information to the decision-makers, which is the actual objective.
