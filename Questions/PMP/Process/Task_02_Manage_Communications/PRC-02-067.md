# Question

A PM on a hybrid logistics-automation programme sends the same fortnightly update to two audiences: the delivery teams and the executive steering group. The teams find it useful. The executives, however, have begun skimming or skipping it, and the sponsor remarks that she "can never quite tell from it whether we are getting what we paid for". The update is accurate and detailed, listing completed stories, velocity, and open defects. What should the PM change?

## Options

- **A**: Produce a separate executive version framed around business outcomes, benefits realised, and decisions needed, distinct from the delivery detail.
- **B**: Add an executive summary at the top of the existing update so leaders can read the headline before the delivery detail.
- **C**: Invite the executives to the sprint review so they see progress in context rather than relying on a written update.
- **D**: Convert velocity and defect figures into a trend dashboard so executives can absorb programme health at a glance.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The same content cannot serve both audiences because executives need information matched to their decisions, business value, benefits, and choices required, not engineering throughput, and trying to make one artefact do both is why it fails for them. (A) tailors the communication to the audience's actual information needs, which is the core principle. (B) keeps a single delivery-centric artefact and bolts on a summary, but a summary of story counts and defects still does not tell the sponsor whether value is being delivered. (C) is useful engagement but does not replace a fit-for-purpose written update and burdens executives with team-level ceremony. (D) makes the existing metrics prettier without changing that they are the wrong metrics for an executive audience asking about value for money.
