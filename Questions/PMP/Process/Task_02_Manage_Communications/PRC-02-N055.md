# Question

Late on a Friday, the PM confirms a critical supplier will miss a delivery, pushing a regulated go-live date by at least two weeks. The communications plan names the sponsor as the approver of all external go-live messaging, and the sponsor is travelling and unreachable until Monday. An external customer announcement confirming the original go-live date is queued to send automatically on Saturday morning, and the PM has the access to stop it. Which action should the PM take first?

## Options

- **A**: Stop the automated Saturday announcement, then prepare a briefing so the sponsor has the facts, the slip, and the messaging options ready to decide on Monday.
- **B**: Stop the automated Saturday announcement, then issue a short corrected external note stating the date is under review, so customers are not left with stale information over the weekend.
- **C**: Reach the sponsor's delegate or the next-most-senior available leader tonight to get authority to amend the external message before Saturday, rather than acting on it alone.
- **D**: Let the Saturday announcement stand and brief the sponsor on Monday, since the date may still be recoverable in part and changing external messaging is the sponsor's call.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The single urgent, reversible harm is the automated broadcast of a date the project can no longer meet; stopping it is squarely within the PM's authority and is the necessary first act, after which the sponsor (the named approver) is equipped to decide the external message. (A) prevents the misinformation and respects who owns the messaging decision. (B) correctly stops the announcement but then issues a replacement external statement the plan reserves for the sponsor, pre-empting an approval the PM does not hold. (C) is reasonable as a next step for the external message, but seeking sign-off before halting the imminent send risks the false announcement going out while the PM hunts for a delegate, so it is not the first move. (D) leaves the wrong message to ship to customers, treating a confirmed two-week regulated slip as tolerable simply because it pre-empts a sponsor decision.
