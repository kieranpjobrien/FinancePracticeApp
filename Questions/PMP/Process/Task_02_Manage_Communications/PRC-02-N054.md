# Question

On an agile product, several engaged end users keep sending feature ideas straight to individual developers via private messages. A few developers occasionally act on them without telling anyone, which surfaces as unplanned scope and conflicting priorities at sprint review, where the product owner first learns the work happened. The user feedback itself is often valuable, and the team does not want to discourage it. What should the team do?

## Options

- **A**: Direct users to submit feature ideas to the product backlog intake, where the product owner triages and prioritises them transparently against everything else, and make clear that is the route for requests.
- **B**: Have the product owner hold a short fortnightly intake session with the user group to hear ideas directly and add the agreed ones to the backlog at the right priority.
- **C**: Keep users' direct line to developers but require any developer who receives a request to log it in the backlog before acting, so the product owner sees it and can reprioritise.
- **D**: Have the Scrum Master gather the direct requests each week and forward a consolidated list to the product owner to fold into backlog refinement.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The structural problem is that requests enter through a private back channel the product owner cannot see or order, so the durable fix is a single transparent intake that routes every request to the backlog the product owner controls, with users told where to go. (A) closes the back channel and gives one visible, prioritisable queue. (B) is a genuine improvement and keeps users engaged, but a periodic session both delays time-sensitive ideas and still leaves the private-message channel open between sessions, so off-channel work can continue. (C) preserves the very direct-to-developer line that caused the problem; relying on each developer to self-log before acting is fragile and still invites them to start work ahead of prioritisation. (D) adds a manual weekly relay that keeps the back channel alive and inserts a lag, rather than giving users a single place to submit and the product owner a live view.
