# Question

A PM leads a newly formed agile team split across offices in Tokyo, Munich and São Paulo. After two sprints, retrospectives surface a tangle of problems: decisions reached on calls are later acted on differently in each location, the same calls run at awkward hours for two of the three offices, a fast-talking subgroup dominates while several non-native English speakers stay silent, and the shared channels have filled with so many threads that people miss the messages that matter. The PM can take two actions before the next sprint. Which two would most improve how communication actually works across the team?

## Options

- **A**: After each call, circulate the decisions in writing and ask each location to confirm their understanding before they act on them.
- **B**: Rotate meeting times so the burden of unsociable hours is shared fairly, and record sessions for whoever cannot attend live.
- **C**: Adopt round-robin and written-input techniques in discussions so contribution does not depend on speaking fastest in English.
- **D**: Introduce a tagging and triage convention on the shared channels so important messages are easy to find among the noise.
- **E**: Pair each non-native speaker with a fluent buddy who relays their points into the live discussion on their behalf.

## Answer

A, C

## Explanation

Four problems are on the table; the PM must choose the two whose fixes most directly repair how communication works, weighing comprehension and equal participation against the scheduling and channel-hygiene issues. (A) targets the decisions-decoded-differently problem with a written record plus a confirmation-of-understanding loop, the single most consequential failure because it is already producing divergent action across locations. (C) targets the silencing of quieter and non-native members by removing the advantage of the fastest English speakers, widening whose input actually reaches the team. (B) is a genuine and fair improvement, but shared hours and recordings ease attendance and morale without fixing why decisions are misread or why people stay silent, so it ranks below A and C. (D) is worth doing and would cut the channel noise, yet finding messages is a lesser harm than systematically misunderstanding decisions or losing members' input. (E) tries to help the silenced but routes their voice through a proxy, which preserves rather than removes the dependence on a vocal few; (C) lets them contribute directly. The discriminator is impact on comprehension and direct participation versus convenience and findability.
