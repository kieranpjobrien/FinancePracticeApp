# Question

Three weeks into execution, a new operations director joins the client and will be a major user of the project's deliverables. The PM already holds a detailed communications plan covering the original stakeholders. The operations director has told the PM directly, "keep me in the loop," and the PM's existing reporting plausibly covers much of what an operations user would want. What is the PM's best next step?

## Options

- **A**: Add the operations director to the distribution lists whose reports most closely match an operations user's likely interests, then refine once their feedback comes in.
- **B**: Meet the operations director to establish which decisions they own and what information they need and when, and record the result in the communications plan.
- **C**: Walk the operations director through current project status and the reporting they can expect, capturing any preferences that surface during the session.
- **D**: Ask the sponsor and project board how they want this new senior stakeholder engaged, then reflect their direction in the plan.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A new, influential stakeholder is a trigger to perform stakeholder analysis and feed the result into the plan, and the fact that the existing reports plausibly fit is precisely the trap, because "plausibly" is an assumption about a person the PM has not yet spoken to. (B) gets the actual decisions and information needs first and records them, keeping the plan an accurate baseline. (A) is tempting because the reports look close, but enrolling them on assumed-relevant lists risks both overload and gaps before anyone has confirmed what this director actually decides. (C) partly honours their request, yet leading with a status walkthrough supplies information before the PM knows what is relevant and frames the meeting as a briefing rather than an analysis. (D) is a sound governance courtesy but premature, and a board's view does not substitute for the stakeholder's own stated needs. The fine margin is between confirming the need directly before acting (B) and acting on a reasonable-looking assumption about what they need (A/C).
