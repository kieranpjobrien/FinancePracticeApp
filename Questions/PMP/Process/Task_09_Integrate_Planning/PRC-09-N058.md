# Question

An executive sponsor new to agile insists the project manager produce a fully baselined, fixed scope-schedule-cost plan for an eighteen-month digital-banking product that the team intends to deliver in iterations with an evolving backlog. The PM knows a rigid baseline will not fit the adaptive delivery but must still give the sponsor a basis for governance and funding. What is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: Fix cost and time, manage scope through a prioritised backlog, and govern via incremental value delivery and forecast burn-up, explaining to the sponsor how this gives control without a fixed-scope baseline.
- **B**: Baseline fixed scope, schedule, and cost as requested, and manage the divergence as it emerges through the integrated change control process the sponsor already trusts.
- **C**: Baseline only the next quarter's scope in detail with fixed cost and time, and re-baseline scope quarter by quarter as the backlog firms up, presenting each quarter as a fixed plan.
- **D**: Set a fixed scope-schedule-cost baseline and attach a sized change-and-contingency budget so the expected scope evolution is funded and visible within the original baseline.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

For an adaptive product, the integrating approach fixes cost and time while flexing scope through a prioritised backlog and frames governance around demonstrated value and burn-up forecasts — giving the sponsor genuine control without a fixed-scope baseline that the delivery model will defeat, with the rationale explained (A). (B) honours the request but commits to a fixed-scope baseline designed to be wrong, converting normal backlog evolution into a flood of change requests. (C) is closer, but re-baselining scope every quarter still imposes a predictive ceremony on emergent work and presents false fixity the team will keep breaking. (D) dresses a fixed-scope baseline in a change budget, which funds churn but still anchors governance to a scope commitment that does not suit an evolving backlog.
