# Question

A project manager on a hospital information system rollout has finished separate planning workshops with the scope, schedule, cost, and procurement leads. Each plan is internally sound, but when the PM assembles them, the procurement lead-time for the imaging hardware lands two weeks after the date the schedule assumes installation begins. The sponsor is pressing to baseline so delivery can start. What should the PM do next?

## Options

- **A**: Baseline the project management plan now and raise the hardware date as a schedule risk to be managed during execution.
- **B**: Reconcile the procurement and schedule plans by reworking the affected logic with both leads before establishing the integrated baseline.
- **C**: Accept the procurement lead-time as fixed and compress the downstream schedule activities to preserve the original finish date.
- **D**: Ask the sponsor to choose between a later finish date and paying expedite fees to pull the hardware delivery forward.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Integrating planning activities means producing a project management plan whose subsidiary plans are mutually consistent before it becomes the baseline; a known internal contradiction must be resolved, not deferred. (B) reconciles the conflict at the source with the people who own those plans, which is the core integration act. (A) baselines a plan that is already known to be infeasible, converting a planning defect into an execution problem. (C) jumps to a single compression response without first confirming the conflict cannot be resolved more cheaply through resequencing. (D) escalates a trade-off decision prematurely — the PM should first develop integrated options for the sponsor, not present an unanalysed either/or.
