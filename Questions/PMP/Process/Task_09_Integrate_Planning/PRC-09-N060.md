# Question

During integrated planning for a brewery expansion, the procurement plan schedules a tank order with a 26-week lead time to begin after detailed design sign-off, but the master schedule needs the tanks on site in week 30 to hit the commissioning window. The design, procurement, and schedule plans were each prepared by different leads and look reasonable in isolation. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Compress the commissioning activities later in the schedule to absorb the late tank delivery without changing procurement timing.
- **B**: Accept the procurement lead's timing as fixed and move the commissioning window out by the shortfall, informing the sponsor afterwards.
- **C**: Surface the lead-time dependency across the design, procurement, and schedule plans and adjust the sequencing — for example, ordering long-lead items against frozen design parameters — so the integrated plan is achievable.
- **D**: Keep the three plans as they are and rely on expediting the tank supplier once the slip becomes visible during execution.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Integration means catching a cross-plan dependency like a long lead time colliding with a fixed milestone, then re-sequencing — often by ordering long-lead items early against frozen parameters — so the combined plan actually works (C). (A) tries to squeeze downstream work to mask an upstream timing flaw, risking quality and feasibility. (B) silently moves a key milestone and informs the sponsor only after deciding, removing their input on a material trade-off. (D) baselines a known infeasibility and banks on expediting, which is reactive and may not recover 26 weeks of lead time.
