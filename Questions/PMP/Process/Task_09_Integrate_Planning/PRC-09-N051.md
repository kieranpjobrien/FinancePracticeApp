# Question

Two weeks after the project management plan for a logistics-warehouse automation project is baselined, the scope lead and the schedule lead each independently update their own plan to accommodate a new conveyor specification, without coordinating. The project manager discovers the scope baseline now includes work the schedule does not, and the cost baseline reflects neither. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Accept the scope lead's update as correct and instruct the schedule and cost leads to align their plans to it.
- **B**: Roll back both leads' edits to restore the original baseline and forbid further changes until the conveyor decision is final.
- **C**: Treat the change through integrated change control, assess its effect across scope, schedule, and cost together, and update all affected baselines as one coordinated change once approved.
- **D**: Allow each lead to keep maintaining their own plan and reconcile the three at the next monthly integration review.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Once baselined, changes must flow through integrated change control (C), where impact is assessed across all knowledge areas and the baselines are updated together — this is the mechanism that keeps an integrated plan integrated. (A) anoints one uncoordinated edit as truth without assessing schedule and cost impact. (B) discards work and freezes the project rather than processing a legitimate change properly. (D) tolerates divergent, uncontrolled baselines for a month, which is exactly the failure integrated change control exists to prevent.
