# Question

A five-year urban rail programme is ready to start enabling works, but several later phases depend on land parcels still under negotiation that may not settle for two to three years. The sponsor needs an approved, integrated plan now to release initial funding, and the programme board expects a single plan that can be governed end to end. How should the project manager integrate the planning?

## Options

- **A**: Plan the imminent work to execution-level detail and the land-dependent phases to a coarser level within the same integrated plan, refining the later phases as parcels settle.
- **B**: Produce execution-level detail for all five years now using the negotiating team's most likely land outcomes, so the funding gate sees a complete plan and the board can govern the whole scope from day one.
- **C**: Approve and baseline only the enabling works and land-independent phases, and charter the land-dependent phases as a follow-on project once acquisitions complete.
- **D**: Plan every phase to the same intermediate level of detail now, so no phase is privileged and the integrated plan presents one uniform basis for funding and control.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The work is known near-term and genuinely uncertain later, so integrating detailed near-term planning with higher-level later-phase planning in one plan — and elaborating the later phases as land deals close — matches effort to available information while still giving the sponsor a single governable plan (A). (B) manufactures execution-level detail on outcomes that may shift for years, giving the gate false precision and guaranteeing extensive rework. (C) keeps each part internally clean but severs one programme into two, losing the integration of dependencies that cross the boundary and deferring governance of scope already foreseeable. (D) plans uncertain phases to the same depth as firm ones, wasting effort on detail that cannot yet be real and under-detailing the imminent work that needs it most.
