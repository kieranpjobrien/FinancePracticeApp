# Question

While integrating the subsidiary plans for a wind-farm construction project, the project manager notices that the risk management plan calls for a contingency reserve sized at 8% of project cost to cover identified weather and geotechnical risks, but the cost management plan's budget contains no contingency line — the estimate was rolled up bottom-up from activity costs only. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Incorporate the risk-based contingency reserve into the cost baseline so the budget reflects the agreed risk responses before baselining.
- **B**: Reduce the risk management plan's reserve to zero to match the cost plan, since the bottom-up estimate is the more rigorous figure.
- **C**: Hold the 8% as a management reserve outside the cost baseline and release it only with sponsor approval per event.
- **D**: Baseline both plans as drafted and reconcile the gap at the first cost performance review once actuals are available.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Integration requires the cost baseline to embody the outputs of risk planning; contingency reserves for identified risks belong inside the cost baseline, allocated to the work packages or activities they protect. (A) makes the two plans consistent and gives the project an executable budget. (B) discards analysed risk responses to force agreement — it makes the documents match by ignoring known exposure. (C) confuses contingency reserve (for identified risks, inside the baseline, PM-controlled) with management reserve (for unknown-unknowns, outside the baseline, sponsor-controlled); reclassifying identified-risk money as management reserve strands the responses. (D) knowingly baselines an inconsistent plan and defers the fix until the project is already spending, which is exactly what integration is meant to prevent.
