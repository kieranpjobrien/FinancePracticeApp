# Question

A project manager integrating the plans for a satellite assembly project finds that the integrated schedule is feasible only if the specialist thermal-vacuum chamber is used continuously across two parallel subsystems whose plans were developed separately. Resource levelling to remove the over-allocation pushes the integrated finish date four weeks beyond the launch window committed in the charter. The two subsystem leads each insist their sequence is fixed. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Analyse schedule-compression and resource options across both subsystems jointly — fast-tracking, an alternative chamber, or re-sequencing — and present an integrated recovery option with its trade-offs.
- **B**: Accept the four-week slip and update the integrated schedule baseline, then notify the sponsor that the launch window will be missed.
- **C**: Keep the chamber over-allocated in the integrated schedule and rely on the leads to work it out during execution.
- **D**: Choose one subsystem's sequence as primary and force the other to wait, since one of them must give way.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The over-allocation is a genuine integration conflict surfaced only when the separate plans are combined; the PM must work the problem across both subsystems before conceding the date. (A) examines compression and resource alternatives jointly and frames a recovery option with trade-offs, which is the integrating analysis required. (B) capitulates on a charter commitment without first exploring whether the constraint can be resolved. (C) baselines a schedule that is physically impossible because the chamber cannot be in two places at once, deferring a known infeasibility. (D) arbitrarily privileges one lead without analysing whether a less costly option (e.g. an alternative chamber or fast-tracking) preserves the window.
