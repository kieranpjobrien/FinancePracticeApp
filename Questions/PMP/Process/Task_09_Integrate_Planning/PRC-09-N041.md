# Question

A project manager on a regional water-treatment upgrade has received the completed scope, schedule, cost, quality, and resource management plans from the relevant leads. Each plan is internally sound, but the schedule assumes night-shift commissioning while the resource plan budgets only for day-shift labour, and the cost baseline does not include the shift premium. What should the project manager do before submitting the project management plan for approval?

## Options

- **A**: Reconcile the conflicting assumptions across the subsidiary plans and confirm the integrated baselines are internally consistent before seeking approval.
- **B**: Submit the plan as assembled and raise the night-shift premium as a change request once the discrepancy surfaces during execution.
- **C**: Adopt the schedule lead's night-shift assumption as authoritative because the critical path governs the other plans.
- **D**: Ask the sponsor to rule on whether commissioning should run day or night shift before the leads revise anything.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The core integration responsibility is to ensure subsidiary plans are mutually consistent so the combined baselines are realistic; resolving the shift conflict and its cost impact is precisely this work. (B) knowingly baselines a flawed plan and defers a problem already visible, which is poor stewardship. (C) privileges one plan without verifying the resource and cost plans can support night work, and the critical path does not automatically override resourcing reality. (D) escalates a planning-integration detail the PM should first analyse and frame, rather than handing the sponsor an unframed either/or.
