# Question

A project manager integrating the plans for a hybrid factory-automation upgrade discovers that the change management plan assumes a two-week production shutdown for installation, while the stakeholder engagement plan promises the operations director that production will continue uninterrupted. Both plans were approved by different reviewers. The integrated plan is due at the gate in three days. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Raise the contradiction with the operations director and the relevant leads, resolve which assumption holds, and align both plans before the gate.
- **B**: Keep the shutdown assumption because installation genuinely requires it, and quietly update the stakeholder plan to remove the continuity promise.
- **C**: Present the integrated plan at the gate with both statements intact and let the approval board decide which is correct.
- **D**: Defer the gate indefinitely until the operations director independently confirms the production schedule.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A direct contradiction between two subsidiary plans is precisely what integration must catch and resolve so the combined plan is coherent and the affected stakeholder is not blindsided. (A) engages the right people, settles the assumption, and aligns both plans. (B) silently overrides a commitment made to a key stakeholder without consultation, risking trust and a planning surprise. (C) hands an unresolved internal conflict to the board as if it were a decision, when the PM should have reconciled it first. (D) escalates to an open-ended delay instead of actively driving the short reconciliation the situation needs.
