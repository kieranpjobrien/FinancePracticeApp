# Question

The project management plan for an airport retail-fit-out has just been approved at the planning gate. The next morning the quality lead wants to revise the inspection-and-test approach in the quality management plan to reflect a lesson from a sister project, and asks the project manager how to proceed now that the plan is approved. What does approval of the integrated plan mean for this request?

## Options

- **A**: The quality lead may update the quality management plan directly, because each subsidiary plan remains owned and maintained by its knowledge-area lead after approval.
- **B**: The change must go through integrated change control, because approval established the subsidiary plans and baselines as a single controlled whole whose parts cannot be altered in isolation.
- **C**: The change is unconstrained, because only the scope, schedule, and cost baselines are controlled by approval and the quality management plan is not a baseline.
- **D**: The change must wait until the next planning gate, because an approved plan is frozen in full until the sponsor formally reopens planning.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Approval makes the project management plan an *integrated*, controlled artefact: the subsidiary plans and baselines now form one coherent whole, so a change to any part — even a sensible improvement to the quality approach — must run through integrated change control to assess cross-plan impact and keep the plan consistent (B). (A) treats the plans as still independently owned, which is the pre-integration state approval is meant to end. (C) wrongly assumes only the three baselines are under control; the integrated plan as a whole is, including the subsidiary management plans. (D) overstates control as a total freeze — approved plans are not reopened only at gates; they change continuously but through controlled change, not a periodic thaw.
