# Question

A predictive bridge-refurbishment project is four weeks behind on a 30-week schedule. The critical path runs through roughly a dozen activities across several trades; deck waterproofing then parapet installation is one sequential pair on it, scheduled that way because the parapet crew normally prefers a fully cured deck, and the project manager's eye is drawn to it as an obvious place to overlap. Several other critical activities have their own discretionary dependencies, differing crew costs, and varying overlap risk. The contingency reserve still holds two weeks of schedule buffer, and a small amount of the management reserve could be requested. The sponsor wants the date held. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Ask engineering whether the parapets can be safely set on a partially cured deck, and if so overlap the two activities to claw back time on the critical path.
- **B**: Release the two weeks of schedule contingency reserve against the slip and present a recovery plan for the remaining two weeks.
- **C**: Crash the parapet activity by requesting management reserve to fund a second crew working extended shifts.
- **D**: Analyse the critical path across all its activities to find where overlapping or crashing recovers the most time for the least cost and risk before committing to a compression technique.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

All four are legitimate recovery moves, but with a dozen critical activities of differing cost and overlap risk, compression should target wherever it yields the most schedule for the least cost and risk, and that is established by analysing the whole path first rather than fixing on the one pair that happens to catch the eye. (D) compares the candidates so the chosen technique is applied where it pays off most. (A) is a strong candidate and may well be the chosen technique, but the deck-parapet pair is salient, not proven optimal; another pair may overlap with less rework exposure or a cheaper crew, so committing here first risks recovering less for more risk. (B) contingency covers identified risks that occur; a vendor-independent execution slip is better recovered by compression, and burning the buffer first leaves nothing for later threats. (C) crashing with management reserve is the costliest option and should follow, not precede, the lower-cost fast-tracking analysis.
