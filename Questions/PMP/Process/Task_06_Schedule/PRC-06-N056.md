# Question

A delivery manager asks the project manager for an early forecast of when an agile team will finish a 200-point backlog. The team has completed four sprints with velocities of 24, 31, 19, and 26, and the backlog is still being refined. Which practices produce a credible, defensible forecast? (Choose all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Express the forecast as a range of sprints derived from the team's observed low and high velocities, rather than a single date.
- **B**: Forecast from the average velocity while stating that the 200-point figure will move as the backlog is refined and re-estimated.
- **C**: Adjust the projected future sprints for known upcoming capacity changes such as planned leave or a new joiner ramping up.
- **D**: Discard the 19-point sprint from the calculation because it would drag the average down and make the forecast look worse to the delivery manager.
- **E**: Forecast the date by extending the four-point trend line (24, 31, 19, 26), projecting that velocity will keep rising over the remaining sprints.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Credible empirical forecasting works from the team's actual spread and known future conditions. (A) a sprint range built from observed low and high velocities conveys honest uncertainty; (B) forecasting from the average while flagging that backlog size will change keeps the projection grounded in how agile scope evolves; (C) adjusting future sprints for known capacity shifts (leave, a ramping joiner) reflects real upcoming velocity. The two wrong options are plausible but unsound: (D) drops a data point not because its cause was investigated and ruled non-recurring, but to make the number more flattering — that is cherry-picking the forecast toward a desired answer, which destroys its credibility. (E) treats four noisy sprints as a rising trend and extrapolates continued acceleration; with only four data points and ordinary sprint-to-sprint variation there is no basis to assume velocity keeps climbing, and projecting an upward trend produces an over-optimistic date. Sound practice uses the *range and average* of observed velocity, not a hoped-for slope or a pruned dataset.
