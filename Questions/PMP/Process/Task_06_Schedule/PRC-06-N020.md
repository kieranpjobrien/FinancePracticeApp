# Question

During sprint planning, the team identifies a high-priority user story estimated at 21 points against an average velocity of 16. The product owner wants meaningful progress on this feature in the coming sprint. Which actions are appropriate for the project manager, acting as scrum master, to encourage? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate the team and product owner splitting the story along its workflow into smaller, independently valuable slices that can be sized and sequenced across sprints.
- **B**: Help the team identify a thin end-to-end slice delivering the highest-value path through the feature and pull only that into the sprint.
- **C**: Pull the whole 21-point story in as the sprint's single goal and let the team carry the remainder into the next sprint as expected spillover.
- **D**: Have the team timebox a short spike this sprint to resolve the unknowns inflating the estimate, so the story can be split and sized confidently next sprint.
- **E**: Keep the story at 21 points in the backlog and ask the product owner to wait until the team's velocity rises enough to absorb it in one sprint.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

A story larger than velocity should be made to fit the sprint cadence while protecting value and predictability. (A) workflow-based vertical splitting produces independently valuable, sprint-sized slices, the standard response. (B) pulling the highest-value thin slice ships something usable now and defers the rest, which is exactly "meaningful progress" without overcommitting. (D) when the size is driven by genuine uncertainty, a timeboxed spike resolves the unknowns so the story can be split and estimated reliably, a legitimate planning move. (C) deliberately committing a story known to exceed velocity and planning for spillover undermines the predictability sprint planning exists to create and is not a sound commitment, even though spillover sometimes happens. (E) parking a high-priority story to wait for velocity to drift upward forfeits value indefinitely and ignores that splitting, not waiting, is how oversized work is handled. The appropriate actions split, slice for value, or de-risk, rather than overcommit or stall.
