# Question

Halfway through an eight-sprint release for a mobile banking app, the team's velocity has run at 38, 26, 34, and 30 points across the four completed sprints, and the backlog remaining for the release is estimated at about 210 points. The product owner opens the release-planning checkpoint by asking the project manager point-blank: "On these numbers, how many sprints are we short, and what do we cut?" The four sprints left would deliver roughly 120 to 152 points on the spread seen so far. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Show completion as a band derived from the team's actual velocity spread, with the likelihood attached to each end, so the product owner weighs the cut against the real chance of making the date rather than against one number.
- **B**: Answer the question as asked: take the running average, state the resulting point shortfall, and work with the product owner on which lower-value items to trim to close it.
- **C**: Reforecast on the three most recent sprints, since the opening sprint's 38 reflects start-up effects unlikely to repeat and overstates the team's sustainable pace.
- **D**: Note that even the optimistic end of the range still misses the date, so recommend the product owner plan now to either add a ninth sprint or cut scope, and bring options to the next checkpoint.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The velocity series is volatile (38 down to 26), so any single figure — the average (B) or a trimmed average (C) — hands the product owner a false sense of precision about a decision that hinges on how wide the outcome really is. The product owner has asked "how short, what do we cut," but the soundest answer reframes the question rather than answering a poorly-framed version of it: express completion as a probability band so the cut is weighed against the actual chance of hitting the date (A). (B) is reasonable and may be where the room lands, but it freezes the forecast to a point and steers straight to the scope lever before the spread is visible — answering the question as asked is not the same as answering it well. (C) is a defensible refinement and dropping a non-representative sprint can sharpen a forecast, yet it still collapses the picture to one number and the call to exclude that sprint should be made openly with the team, not used to manufacture an answer. (D) jumps to the schedule and scope levers on the strength of the range's pessimistic end before the band and its confidence have been put in front of the PO, pre-committing the remedy. The fine margin is between sizing the uncertainty honestly (A) and serving any single number or lever (B, C, D).
