# Question

In a scaled agile programme, Team A's checkout feature depends on an authentication service Team B will not finish until late in the release. Both teams plan their sprints independently, and the dependency only surfaced at the programme increment review. The project manager wants to stop this recurring across the dozen-plus dependencies the teams share each release. What is the most effective action?

## Options

- **A**: Establish a joint cross-team planning event each programme increment where teams map and sequence dependencies before their sprints are committed.
- **B**: Have the teams maintain a shared, visible dependency board updated continuously so a dependency like this is seen as soon as either team identifies it.
- **C**: Add a scrum-of-scrums where representatives from each team meet frequently during the increment to raise and coordinate emerging dependencies.
- **D**: Re-order Team B's backlog so the authentication service is delivered early enough for Team A, resolving this dependency directly.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

(B), (C), and (D) are all real scaled-agile practices, so the discriminator is which best prevents recurrence across many dependencies. (A) attacks the root cause — the teams plan independently — by synchronising planning so dependencies are surfaced and sequenced before commitments are locked, which is the standard mechanism for this failure mode. (B) is valuable but reactive: a board only helps once someone has spotted a dependency, and this one was missed precisely because the teams never planned together. (C) improves in-increment coordination but, like the board, catches dependencies after sprints are already committed rather than designing them out at planning time. (D) fixes this single instance and should be done, but it does nothing to prevent the next eleven dependencies from surfacing late, so it is a point fix rather than the systemic remedy the PM is asked for.
