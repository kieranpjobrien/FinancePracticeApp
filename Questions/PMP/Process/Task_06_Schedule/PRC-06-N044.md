# Question

A predictive project is two weeks behind a hard contractual finish date. Three activities on the current critical path can each be crashed by up to two weeks: activity W at £2,000 per week, activity X at £8,000 per week, and activity Y at £3,000 per week. A near-critical parallel path has just one week of total float. The sponsor has approved spending to recover the full two weeks but wants the lowest total cost. How should the project manager crash the schedule?

## Options

- **A**: Crash one week of W, then recalculate the network before deciding the second week, since the one-week-float parallel path may become critical and change the cheapest next move.
- **B**: Crash W by its full two weeks up front, as it has the lowest cost slope, then re-baseline.
- **C**: Crash one week each of W and Y so the recovery is split across two activities and less dependent on any single one delivering.
- **D**: Crash one week of W and one week of X to spread the compression across different parts of the network and reduce concentration risk.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four start from the correct instinct of crashing low-cost-slope critical-path work, so the margin is the parallel path with only one week of float. (A) crashes the cheapest week (W) and then rechecks the network: after pulling one week off the critical path, the near-critical path with one week of float can become co-critical, at which point crashing W further may no longer shorten the finish and a different activity is needed — recalculating before the second week is what protects against wasted spend. (B) commits both weeks to W without re-checking; if the parallel path goes critical after week one, the second week of W buys nothing and the £2,000 is wasted. (C) and (D) both incur unnecessary cost — (C) uses Y (£3,000) when a second week of W (£2,000) may still be the cheapest valid move, and (D) brings in X at £8,000 with no justification — and neither re-evaluates the shifting critical path between steps.
