# Question

A predictive project depends on a government regulator issuing a permit by a week-10 milestone, after which on-site works can begin. The permit is outside the project's control and the regulator's turnaround has historically ranged from week 8 to week 14. Construction crews are contractually booked, with cancellation and standby charges, to start the day after the milestone. Which actions should the project manager take to manage this point in the schedule? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Model the permit as an external dependency on a high-risk milestone and reflect the week-8-to-14 range as schedule uncertainty rather than a single week-10 date.
- **B**: Negotiate a flexible or short-notice mobilisation clause with the construction contractor so the crew start can shift with the permit without incurring the full standby charge.
- **C**: Add a schedule buffer ahead of crew mobilisation and define a trigger date by which, if the permit is not in hand, the contingency plan for the crews is activated.
- **D**: Bring the activities that follow permit issuance forward in the plan so that, if the permit lands early, the project can capitalise on the time, while keeping the crew commitment tied to actual issuance.
- **E**: Treat the permit as a fixed week-10 constraint in the baseline and book the crews firmly against it, since planning to a single committed date gives the contractor and stakeholders certainty.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

An uncontrollable external milestone with a known turnaround range is a quantifiable risk to be modelled, buffered, monitored against a trigger, and de-risked commercially. (A) represents it as a range and a high-risk dependency instead of false single-date precision. (B) reduces the financial exposure of the costly crew booking to a slip. (C) buffers the mobilisation and sets a decision trigger for the response plan. (D) sounds prudent but is the trap: pulling the dependent works earlier cannot help because they are gated by permit issuance, and the option still implies acting ahead of the permit; capturing early-issue upside is done by keeping the successors ready, not by re-sequencing them before the gate. (E) baselining a single week-10 date in the face of a week-8-to-14 history manufactures certainty the data contradicts and books crews against a date likely to move, maximising standby cost.
