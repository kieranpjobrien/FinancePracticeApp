# Question

During sprint planning, the team works out that two members will each be away three of the ten working days this sprint, so realistic capacity is clearly below the last few sprints. The product owner argues last sprint's completed points are the fair target and presses the team to commit to the same number. The team is hesitant but does not push back. As the facilitator, what should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Point out that velocity already averages out occasional absences, so the team should hold last sprint's number unless the leave is unusually disruptive.
- **B**: Have the team forecast this sprint from its average velocity adjusted for the known reduced capacity, then pull in only the highest-priority items that fit, leaving the product owner to rank what is included.
- **C**: Reduce the commitment strictly in proportion to the missing person-days, lowering last sprint's total by the same percentage as the lost capacity.
- **D**: Let the team commit to last sprint's number as a stretch goal, with an explicit agreement that unfinished items roll to the next sprint without penalty.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The team owns the forecast and capacity has demonstrably dropped, so the question is how to right-size it without ignoring the product owner's priorities or faking precision. (B) uses observed velocity as the baseline, adjusts for the specific known capacity loss, and fills the sprint by priority, which keeps the forecast honest and the product owner deciding scope, the correct division of roles. (A) is a real consideration, but velocity only absorbs the typical background level of absence; six planned days off across a two-person gap is a known, material reduction this sprint, not noise, so holding the old number overcommits. (C) a strict pro-rata cut treats story points as linearly proportional to person-days, ignoring that the absent members may not be the constraint on every item and that velocity, not a headcount formula, is the better predictor. (D) labelling an overcommitment a "stretch goal" with planned spillover normalises missing the forecast and erodes the predictability sprint planning exists to create. Forecasting from adjusted velocity and filling by priority fits best.
