# Question

A two-year pharmaceutical facility upgrade has well-defined early phases, but late-phase work hinges on a regulatory decision not expected for about a year. The steering committee wants a published schedule now that lets them track delivery for the whole 24 months. How should the project manager structure the schedule?

## Options

- **A**: Detail the near-term phases at activity level and represent the regulation-dependent later phases as planning packages with milestone dates, refining them by rolling-wave planning as the decision approaches.
- **B**: Detail the near-term phases at activity level and model the later phases at activity level too, but tag those durations as assumption-based and subject to a regulatory decision point.
- **C**: Detail the near-term phases and, for the later work, run a quantitative schedule risk analysis across the plausible regulatory outcomes to publish a finish-date range rather than fixed activities.
- **D**: Detail the near-term phases at activity level and hold the late-phase work as a single summary bar between two milestones, elaborating it only once the regulatory decision lands.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four give the committee forward visibility and avoid pretending the unknown is known — the discriminator is how much structure is appropriate for work blocked on a decision a year out. (A) is textbook rolling-wave: planning packages plus milestones give trackable structure now and are progressively elaborated, the standard treatment for distant, information-poor work. (B) elaborates uncertain work to activity level prematurely; even labelled as assumptions, those activities will churn repeatedly and consume effort with little benefit. (C) is a valid technique for expressing finish-date confidence, but a full schedule risk analysis is heavy when the dominant uncertainty is a single binary regulatory gate, and it still leaves the committee without the trackable interim structure they asked for. (D) is defensible but coarser than rolling-wave — a single summary bar gives the committee little to monitor across a year of the project, where at least milestone-level structure is achievable.
