# Question

A predictive construction project is four weeks behind on a 30-week schedule and the sponsor wants the original finish date recovered. The project manager evaluates four candidate activities on the critical path, each with a different constraint profile. Match each activity to the schedule action that fits its constraints best.

## Items

- **1**: Structural steel erection has firm-fixed-price labour with idle crew capacity this month, a hard budget contingency still available, and a sequential successor that cannot legally begin until steel sign-off is issued.
- **2**: Interior fit-out has multiple subcontractors whose tasks were planned end-to-end purely for coordination convenience, no inspection gate between them, and a near-exhausted cost contingency.
- **3**: Site earthworks duration was carried as a single rough figure from a comparable past job, and the team has now received the geotechnical survey with measured soil volumes and the contractor's published excavation rates.
- **4**: Commissioning is sequenced after handover only because the same specialist team runs both, the activities are technically independent, and management has explicitly ruled out any further spend.

## Matches

- **A**: Re-estimate the duration from the new quantitative data before deciding whether compression is even needed.
- **B**: Crash the activity by funding the idle crew to work extended shifts.
- **C**: Fast-track by overlapping the activities and actively manage the added risk.
- **D**: Leave the sequence as a discretionary dependency that could be overlapped, but do not act here because a mandatory gate prevents it.

## Answer

1-D, 2-C, 3-A, 4-C

## Explanation

This turns on reading each activity's constraints, not reciting technique definitions. Item 1 has budget and idle crew — superficially a crashing candidate — but its successor is bound by a mandatory (legal sign-off) dependency that overlapping cannot break, so neither crashing the successor nor fast-tracking helps and the right call is to recognise the hard gate and look elsewhere (1-D). Items 2 and 4 both have exhausted/forbidden budgets, so cost-adding crashing is off the table; both have discretionary dependencies (coordination-only, shared-team-only) that can be overlapped, making fast-tracking the fit (2-C, 4-C). Item 3 is not a compression question at all — fresh quantitative data means re-estimating first, since the apparent variance may shrink once real soil volumes replace the rough figure (3-A). Option B is a plausible-looking trap for item 1 because the money and crew exist, but spending to shorten an activity whose successor is gated yields no recovery.
