# Question

A fit-out schedule is under pressure to recover four days, and the critical path runs paint walls, then a confirmed two-day paint cure, then lay carpet, then install furniture. The cure is a genuine manufacturer-mandated technical requirement. The floor is one large open-plan area that the team has so far planned to paint, cure, carpet and furnish as a single block. A faster-curing paint exists but costs more and the budget is tight. What gives the project manager the best chance of recovering time across this part of the path?

## Options

- **A**: Split the open-plan area into zones so that while the first zone's paint is curing, the crew paints the next zone, letting carpeting and furnishing of each zone follow on without the whole floor waiting on one cure.
- **B**: Model the cure as a two-day finish-to-start lag rather than a resourced activity and reassign the idle crew, so the schedule reflects the dependency accurately.
- **C**: Switch to the faster-curing paint for the whole floor and request the additional budget, shortening the cure portion of the critical path directly.
- **D**: Fast-track by overlapping the carpet installation with the back end of the paint cure, starting carpet a day before the cure completes to claw back a day.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The cure is a mandatory, fixed-length dependency that cannot itself be shortened by scheduling, so the recovery has to come from how the surrounding work flows; zoning the area converts one serial paint-cure-carpet cycle into a pipeline where curing in one zone overlaps productive work in another. (A) recovers time by parallelising the work around an immovable wait, which is the highest-leverage move and adds no cost. (B) is correct modelling and tidies the resource profile, but representing the cure as a lag changes nothing about the duration and recovers no time on its own. (C) does shorten the cure but spends scarce budget on the whole floor when the constraint could be relieved by re-sequencing at no cost, so it is not the best first lever. (D) overlapping carpet into the cure violates the confirmed technical requirement and risks laying carpet on an uncured surface, trading a day for likely rework and damage.
