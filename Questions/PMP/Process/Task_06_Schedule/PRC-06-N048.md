# Question

After building the network logically, the project manager's resource report shows the lead electrician — the only person qualified for both tasks — assigned to two concurrent critical-path activities at 200% for the same week. Both activities have zero float, and the sponsor has ruled out hiring additional electricians. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Resource-level the two activities so the electrician works them in sequence, then take the resulting finish-date impact and recovery options to the sponsor.
- **B**: Resource-smooth within available float elsewhere and shift other work to free the electrician, levelling only if smoothing cannot resolve the 200% overload.
- **C**: Split each activity so the electrician personally does the licensed portions sequentially while a qualified assistant performs the supervised tasks in parallel, compressing the overlap.
- **D**: Reorder the wider network to delay one of the two activities into a later week where the electrician is otherwise idle, avoiding any finish-date slip.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four engage resource-optimisation thinking, but the scenario's constraints decide it: both conflicting activities are on the critical path with zero float, and they are the bottleneck. (A) is correct — resource levelling sequences a constrained resource and, because the activities are critical, it extends the finish date, which the PM must surface transparently with options. (B) names the right preference order (smoothing before levelling), but smoothing only works within existing float; here the conflicting activities have none, so smoothing cannot resolve this particular overload and the PM is forced to level — making (B) a near-miss that misapplies the technique to a zero-float case. (C) is creative but assumes the work cleanly divides into licensed and supervised portions and that an assistant is available, neither of which the scenario supports. (D) presumes a later idle week exists for a critical, zero-float activity; moving a critical activity later simply pushes the finish, so it cannot avoid the slip it claims to.
