# Question

A predictive project has fallen behind because a key vendor delivered components two weeks late. The project manager has analysed options and believes a combination of fast-tracking and a modest scope deferral can recover most of the time, though the agreed completion date may still move by a few days. Which actions should the project manager take? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Develop a schedule recovery plan setting out the proposed fast-tracking and deferral, the residual date impact, and the added risks.
- **B**: Apply the fast-tracking immediately and re-baseline the schedule to the recovered dates, since the PM is authorised to optimise the schedule within the project.
- **C**: Raise a change request if the recovery still moves the baseline completion date, and route it through integrated change control.
- **D**: Defer the agreed scope items straight away under the PM's own authority, treating them as a schedule decision rather than a scope change.
- **E**: Update the issue log and risk register to capture the vendor delay and the new risks introduced by fast-tracking.

## Answer

A, C, E

## Explanation

Recovering a slip means producing a costed, risk-assessed recovery plan, processing any residual baseline impact through change control, and keeping the issue and risk logs current. (A) documents the analysed recovery and its residual impact. (C) is required because a change to the baseline completion date must go through integrated change control. (E) maintains the records for the delay and the new fast-tracking risks. (B) re-baselining is not a unilateral optimisation; a change to the approved baseline requires an approved change, so the PM cannot simply re-baseline to the recovered dates. (D) deferring agreed scope is a scope change that needs change control and stakeholder agreement, not a quiet schedule adjustment the PM makes alone.
