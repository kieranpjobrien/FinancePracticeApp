# Question

During backlog refinement for an e-commerce checkout rebuild, the team is sizing a story to integrate a new payments provider. The estimates land far apart — several developers say 8 points, several say 21 — and a few minutes of discussion has not closed the gap, with the next story still to size before the timebox ends. As the project manager coaching the team, which actions would genuinely help the team reach a sounder estimate for this story? (Choose all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Ask the developers holding the extreme estimates to surface the specific assumptions behind their numbers, since the divergence usually points to a requirement or unknown the group has not yet shared.
- **B**: Explore splitting the story along the payment flows it covers into smaller slices the team can each size with more confidence, then refine the whole later.
- **C**: Have the team compare the story against a recently completed story of agreed size to anchor the relative estimate.
- **D**: Take the midpoint of the two clusters as the story's size to stay inside the timebox, flagging it for review if it later proves too large.
- **E**: Defer sizing this story, move on to keep the timebox on track, and bring fuller information back to the next refinement session.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

A wide spread in relative estimates is signal, not noise, and the practices that actually converge it work the signal: surfacing the divergent assumptions (A) exposes the hidden requirement driving the gap, splitting along the payment flows (B) shrinks the uncertainty into sizeable pieces, and triangulating against a known reference story (C) anchors the team's scale. (D) is the seductive trap — a midpoint keeps the timebox moving but discards the very information the disagreement carries and bakes an unexamined number into the plan; "flagging for review" defers the reckoning rather than improving the estimate now. (E) is defensible time-management and is not harmful, but on its own it neither resolves nor improves *this* estimate — it only postpones the same conversation — so it is not one of the actions that genuinely helps the team size the story. The fine line is between practices that converge an estimate (A, B, C) and moves that merely manage the clock (D, E).
