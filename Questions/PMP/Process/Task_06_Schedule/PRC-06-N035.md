# Question

A Kanban-based support-and-enhancement team commits to service-level expectations rather than sprint goals. Over the last quarter its cycle time has risen from a median of 4 days to 9 days, and the cumulative flow diagram shows the "in progress" band steadily widening while arrival and departure rates are roughly equal. The project manager wants to bring delivery times back down. What is the most effective first action?

## Options

- **A**: Introduce or tighten work-in-progress limits so the team starts fewer items at once and finishes what is open before pulling new work.
- **B**: Add team members so the larger pool of in-progress work can be worked in parallel and cleared faster.
- **C**: Expedite the oldest in-progress items by prioritising them to the front, to pull the median cycle time back down.
- **D**: Break incoming work into smaller items so each one moves through the board faster.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A widening in-progress band with balanced arrival and departure rates means accumulating WIP, and Little's law shows cycle time falls when WIP is constrained, so limiting concurrent work attacks the actual cause. (A) caps started work so items finish before new ones begin, draining the swollen in-progress band. (B) adding people without a WIP limit usually just lets the team start even more in parallel, leaving the band wide and cycle time high. (C) expediting old items rescues a few cases but does nothing about the systemic over-starting that is inflating cycle time for everything. (D) smaller items help flow over time but, without a WIP limit, the team can simply start more of them at once, so the in-progress band keeps growing.
