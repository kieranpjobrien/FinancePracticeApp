# Question

A stable Scrum team has averaged 30 points per sprint over six sprints. For the next two-week sprint, two of its five developers will each be away for one of the two weeks. At sprint planning the product owner asks the team to still aim for 30 "since that's your normal velocity". What should the project manager advise the team to do?

## Options

- **A**: Forecast a reduced target this sprint scaled to the lost capacity, and explain to the product owner that velocity assumes the team's full availability.
- **B**: Let the team pull stories from the top of the backlog until it judges the sprint full at this reduced capacity, rather than committing to a top-down point number at all.
- **C**: Hold the 30-point average as the planning figure but flag the leave as a sprint risk, adjusting only if the reduced capacity actually causes a shortfall.
- **D**: Use the team's lowest historical velocity as this sprint's target, treating the reduced staffing as a worst-case capacity scenario.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four avoid blindly overcommitting, so the margin is which best matches the forecast to the known capacity reduction. Two developers each losing one of two weeks removes roughly 20% of the team-weeks, so a forecast scaled to that loss is the honest, specific target (A). (B) describes a legitimate capacity-based planning style and will tend toward a similar reduced number, but on its own it gives the product owner no forecast and skips the explanation that the 30-point expectation is unfounded this sprint — it is a mechanism, not the advice the PO needs. (C) treats a certain, quantifiable capacity cut as if it were an uncertain risk; the leave is known now, so planning to the full average and "adjusting if it bites" is planning to fail. (D) reaches a lower number but by the wrong reasoning — past low velocities reflect normal full-team variation, not this specific staffing gap, so it neither matches the actual capacity loss nor explains itself.
