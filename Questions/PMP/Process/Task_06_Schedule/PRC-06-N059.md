# Question

A project manager coaches several agile teams, each facing a different schedule problem. For each situation, match the single most useful response. Each response is used once.

## Items

- **1**: A Kanban support team commits to service-level expectations with its customer. The team is mostly stable but occasionally a ticket sits for weeks, and the customer wants confidence about how long a typical new request will take to resolve.
- **2**: A Scrum team's stakeholders keep asking when a fixed 240-point backlog will be done. The team has six sprints of history and the backlog is not expected to change much.
- **3**: A team three days into a ten-day sprint is being asked by its product owner whether the committed scope is still achievable, given a story has just been blocked.
- **4**: A team's points-per-sprint looks healthy, but a stakeholder complains that finished features still take a long time to actually appear, and the project manager suspects work is piling up between "dev complete" and "released".

## Matches

- **A**: Track and quote cycle time, including its variability, rather than a single average
- **B**: Forecast a completion range by dividing remaining scope by a band around historical velocity
- **C**: Inspect the sprint burndown together and re-plan the remaining sprint scope with the product owner
- **D**: Measure throughput and work-in-progress across board stages to expose where flow is stalling

## Answer

1-A, 2-B, 3-C, 4-D

## Explanation

Each situation points to one metric being the most useful lever, and the wrong pairings are tempting because the metrics overlap. (1-A) the customer cares about how long *a single request* takes, so cycle time — and crucially its variability, since rare multi-week tickets break a simple average — is the right commitment basis; velocity or throughput would describe aggregate output, not per-item duration. (2-B) a fixed backlog with stable history is a classic empirical-forecast case: remaining scope over a velocity *range* gives a credible completion window, whereas cycle time would answer a per-item question, not a backlog date. (3-C) mid-sprint, with a freshly blocked story, the actionable instrument is the sprint burndown reviewed with the PO to re-plan scope — throughput or velocity are too coarse to answer "are we on track *this sprint*". (4-D) healthy velocity but slow-to-appear features signals a flow problem downstream of development, so throughput plus WIP across stages reveals the bottleneck; velocity alone hid it precisely because points were being completed while items queued before release.
