# Question

A Kanban team maintaining a payments-gateway service is asked when a feature can be delivered. The feature sits fifth in the prioritised backlog, four higher-priority items are ahead of it, and the work-in-progress limit is consistently respected. The team has several months of stable, slightly right-skewed cycle-time and throughput records. A marketing lead must lock a campaign launch date against this delivery and has asked the project manager for the date to commit to externally. How should the project manager have the team produce the forecast?

## Options

- **A**: Run a Monte Carlo simulation driven by the historical throughput to forecast completion of all five items, and offer the marketing lead a date at the higher confidence level the external commitment warrants.
- **B**: Run a Monte Carlo simulation on the historical throughput and give the marketing lead the 50th-percentile completion date for the five items as the most representative single figure.
- **C**: Build the forecast from the team's average throughput, dividing the five backlog items by it to derive the number of weeks to completion.
- **D**: Take the historical cycle time at the confidence level the commitment warrants and apply it to the feature itself, since cycle time already measures how long one item takes to finish.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Two things have to be right: the forecast must span all five items ahead of the feature, and the confidence level must match the risk being taken. A Monte Carlo simulation over historical throughput models how the five queued items actually flow and yields a distribution, and because the date is an *external commitment* the PM should quote a higher-confidence percentile rather than a coin-flip date (A). (B) uses the right method but quotes the 50th percentile — a date as likely to be missed as met — which is too aggressive to commit a dependent campaign against; the simulation is sound, the percentile choice is not. (C) divides items by *average* throughput, collapsing a right-skewed distribution to a single deterministic point that silently sits near the optimistic middle and carries no confidence. (D) forecasts the feature from its own cycle time and ignores the four items queued ahead of it under the WIP limit — cycle time starts when work begins, so it answers "how long once we start it," not "when will we get to it," understating the date. The discriminator is whether the method captures queue position *and* sets confidence to the stakes, which only (A) does.
