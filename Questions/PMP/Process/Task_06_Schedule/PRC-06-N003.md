# Question

During schedule development for a pharmaceutical validation project, the only engineer who has run a comparable equipment-qualification gives a duration that swings from 8 to 20 days depending on which risks she happens to focus on that day. The activity sits on the critical path and the project manager must baseline a duration the team will commit to. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Convene two more qualified engineers and the original engineer to reach a consensus single duration before baselining.
- **B**: Capture her optimistic, most likely, and pessimistic figures together with the risk that drives each, then compute a weighted expected duration and carry the spread as known schedule risk.
- **C**: Take the most likely value she gives and protect the activity with a reserve sized to the difference between her optimistic and pessimistic figures.
- **D**: Baseline the activity at her most likely value and add it to the watch list so its actuals are tracked closely once work begins.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The signal is high genuine uncertainty in a single critical activity, not a careless estimator, so the best technique both quantifies the range and feeds it forward. (B) three-point estimating converts her own optimistic/most-likely/pessimistic inputs into a defensible expected value and preserves the spread as schedule risk for later analysis, directly addressing the variability. (A) consensus among more experts is reasonable but the scenario states she is the only one with comparable experience, so a forced single number still buries the real uncertainty. (C) a reserve sized to her range is plausible but applies it as a buffer on a point estimate rather than modelling the activity itself, and reserve is normally set after quantitative risk analysis, not in place of it. (D) baselining the most likely value records the uncertainty nowhere and leaves the critical path exposed. Modelling the range is what the situation demands.
