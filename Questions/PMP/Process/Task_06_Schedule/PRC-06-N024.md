# Question

On an agile project, several business stakeholders repeatedly email the project manager between sprints asking whether particular features will make the next release, which is six sprints away. The team already maintains a product backlog and a burn-up chart with a projected completion range in a shared tool the stakeholders have access to, and a sprint review is held every two weeks that these stakeholders are invited to but rarely attend. The product owner is comfortable with the current forecast. The project manager wants the questions to stop recurring. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Walk the stakeholders through the existing burn-up and roadmap, showing them where it lives and how to read the forecast range themselves, and reinforce the sprint review as the standing forum for these questions.
- **B**: Produce a concise written feature-status summary after each sprint review and email it to the stakeholder list, since these stakeholders have shown they engage by email rather than by attending.
- **C**: Add a standing agenda item to the sprint review where the product owner walks through the status of each stakeholder-requested feature.
- **D**: Ask the product owner to convert the burn-up's projected range into a single committed delivery sprint per requested feature and circulate that, so stakeholders get the clear answer they keep asking for.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four keep stakeholders informed, but the recurring questions come from stakeholders who have the radiator yet do not engage with it, so the durable fix is to build their ability and habit of self-serving the forecast the team already keeps, anchored to the review. (A) raises transparency and self-service and respects that an agile forecast is a range, which stops the questions at their source. (B) is a genuine option and matches the stakeholders' shown email preference, but a pushed periodic report duplicates a live artefact, goes stale between sprints, and trains stakeholders to keep asking rather than to read the forecast themselves. (C) does surface the statuses, but it spends review time re-reading information the roadmap already shows and only reaches the stakeholders who attend, which these largely do not, so it will not stop the between-sprint emails. (D) gives the clearest answer but collapses a probabilistic range into per-feature committed sprints, overstating a certainty the velocity data does not support and setting up the team to be held to dates it never had grounds to promise.
