# Question

Three days into a 10-day sprint, the sprint burndown is flat — no points have burned down — yet at standup the team reports steady progress and no blockers. The product owner worries the team has stalled. What should the project manager investigate first?

## Options

- **A**: Whether the in-progress stories are large and binary, so real effort is accruing but nothing has yet reached "done" to drop the line.
- **B**: Whether the team is updating the board and burning down points only when stories close, rather than as tasks complete, which would naturally show a flat early line.
- **C**: Whether an unspoken impediment — an environment or dependency the team is quietly working around — is slowing items without being raised at standup.
- **D**: Whether the sprint was over-committed at planning, so the flat start signals the scope will not fit the iteration.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Each is a genuine cause a competent PM would consider, but they differ in likelihood and what the evidence already rules in. The team reports real progress and no blockers, and three days is early — the most common explanation for "work happening, line flat" is that stories are too large to close partway through, which points to an actionable story-splitting conversation (A). (B) is plausible and worth confirming, but a story-points burndown that only moves on closure is normal behaviour, not a fault to fix, so it explains the chart without indicating a problem to act on. (C) is real but the team has explicitly reported no blockers; chasing a hidden impediment contradicts the stated signal and is a lower-probability first move. (D) cannot yet be concluded — a flat line on day three is equally consistent with large stories mid-flight, so jumping to over-commitment is premature before checking story size.
