# Question

A predictive systems-integration project has slipped three weeks against a firm go-live date that cannot move. Before approaching the sponsor, the project manager convenes the team to assemble recovery options. Which actions are sound schedule-recovery measures to put forward for the sponsor's decision? (Choose all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Fast-track by overlapping critical-path activities that were sequenced conservatively, documenting the added integration risk so the sponsor can weigh it.
- **B**: Crash the lowest-cost-slope critical-path activities, presenting the incremental cost and the point at which a parallel path becomes critical.
- **C**: Propose deferring lower-priority scope to a post-go-live release, after confirming with the product owner and sponsor that the deferred items are not go-live-critical.
- **D**: Reassign people from near-critical activities that still have float onto the critical path, accepting that the donor activities move closer to becoming critical.
- **E**: Run the remaining critical-path activities in parallel and add a focused contingency reserve drawn from the management reserve to cover the elevated risk.

## Answer

A, B, C, D

## Explanation

This set is deliberately close — every option is a recognised lever and four of the five are sound; the candidate must catch the one that crosses a line rather than spotting obvious throwaways. (A) fast-tracking, (B) crashing, and (C) authorised scope deferral are the canonical recovery levers, each trading risk, cost, or scope transparently. (D) is also legitimate: reallocating effort from float-bearing near-critical work to the critical path is real resource optimisation, provided the PM tracks the donor activities edging toward critical — which the option states. (E) is the trap: parallelising the remaining work is just fast-tracking again, but funding its risk from the *management* reserve is wrong — management reserve covers unknown-unknowns and is controlled by sponsors/governance for out-of-scope events, not drawn down by the PM to backstop a chosen recovery tactic; contingency for known, identified risk is the correct vehicle, so (E) is unsound as written.
