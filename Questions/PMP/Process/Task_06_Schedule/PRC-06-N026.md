# Question

A two-year hybrid infrastructure programme has firm contractual milestones at months 6, 12, 18 and 24, but the scope of the later phases is still being shaped by findings from the first phase. The PMO needs a schedule it can baseline now and report against for the whole two years. How should the project manager build it?

## Options

- **A**: Detail the imminent phase to activity level and hold the later phases at milestone or work-package level, baselining the whole and progressively elaborating the later detail as scope firms up.
- **B**: Detail all 24 months to activity level now using analogous estimates from a similar past programme, then revise heavily each quarter as real scope emerges.
- **C**: Baseline only the first phase to activity level and leave the later milestones out of the baseline until their scope is known, adding each phase to the baseline as it is planned.
- **D**: Build the full 24-month activity-level schedule by assigning placeholder durations to the unknown later activities so the network is complete and a critical path can be calculated end to end.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Rolling-wave planning details the near-term work while keeping uncertain future work at a higher level, and the contractual milestones must still appear in the baseline so the whole programme can be reported and controlled. (A) baselines a complete milestone-level view, plans what is knowable in detail, and elaborates later. (B) borrows analogous activity detail for work whose scope is genuinely undefined, manufacturing precision that will be reworked wholesale and churning the baseline. (C) leaves the firm milestones out of the baseline, so the PMO loses the whole-programme view and the committed dates are not being controlled. (D) placeholder durations produce a fabricated end-to-end critical path that misleads everyone relying on it.
