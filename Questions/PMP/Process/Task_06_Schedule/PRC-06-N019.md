# Question

On a predictive project, activity D has 5 days of total float but only 2 days of free float, because its successor activity E (which D shares with another path) starts soon after D's early finish. A resource clash means D must be delayed by 4 days. The project manager wants to understand the schedule impact before agreeing to the delay. What is the correct read of the situation?

## Options

- **A**: Delaying D by 4 days will not push out the project finish, but it will delay the early start of successor E by about 2 days because the slip exceeds D's free float.
- **B**: Delaying D by 4 days is safe end to end, since 4 days is within D's 5 days of total float and free float only matters for non-critical reporting.
- **C**: Delaying D by 4 days will push the project finish out by roughly 2 days, since the slip consumes all but 1 day of D's float.
- **D**: Delaying D by 4 days forces E onto the critical path immediately, because any use of free float makes the successor critical.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The distinction between total and free float is doing real work here, and each wrong option misapplies one of them. (A) is correct: a 4-day slip is within the 5 days of total float, so the project end is safe, but it exceeds the 2 days of free float, so it delays successor E's early start by the 2-day overrun. (B) is wrong because treating free float as merely a reporting nicety ignores that exceeding it has a concrete effect on the successor's early start, even though the finish date holds. (C) is wrong because total float, not free float, governs the project finish; with 5 days of total float a 4-day slip does not move the end date at all. (D) overstates the effect: consuming free float delays the successor's early start but does not by itself drive the successor to zero total float, so E does not automatically become critical. Reading total float against the finish and free float against the successor is the key.
