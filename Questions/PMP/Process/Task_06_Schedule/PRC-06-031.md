# Question

You notice the development team has been "gold plating" — adding small unrequested enhancements to deliverables. The work is high quality but it has consumed two weeks of schedule reserve and the deadline is now at risk. What should the project manager do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Allow the gold plating to continue because the customer will perceive higher quality at handover and praise the team.
- **B**: Discuss with the team why the enhancements are happening, restate that scope changes go through change control, and refocus on the agreed scope.
- **C**: Submit a change request to extend the deadline by two weeks to absorb the reserve consumed by the unrequested enhancements.
- **D**: Report the gold plating as a performance issue to the functional managers of the developers so corrective action can be taken.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

PMI treats gold plating as a scope-control failure that is also a coaching opportunity — well-intentioned but not authorised. (B) addresses cause and behaviour without punishing initiative, and reasserts change control. (A) accepts unauthorised scope and the risk of missing the deadline. (C) changes the baseline to cover unauthorised work rather than stopping the unauthorised work. (D) escalates a coachable behaviour into a performance issue with line managers, which damages trust and the working relationship.
