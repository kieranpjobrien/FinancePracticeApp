# Question

On a predictive pipeline-coating project the schedule links "apply protective coating" to "backfill trench" as finish-to-start with a 24-hour lag for the coating to cure. In practice the crew coats the pipe in continuous sections as it moves along the trench, and each section can be backfilled once its own 24-hour cure has elapsed, so the present link is delaying all backfill until the entire coating run is finished and then cured. The schedule is on the critical path and the project manager wants it to mirror how the work really flows. What is the most appropriate change?

## Options

- **A**: Change the relationship to start-to-start with a lag that covers a typical section's coating plus its 24-hour cure, so backfill trails coating section by section.
- **B**: Keep the finish-to-start relationship but reduce the lag, on the basis that the original 24 hours overstated the wait now that sections cure in parallel.
- **C**: Split coating and backfill each into per-section activities and link each section's pair finish-to-start with a 24-hour lag.
- **D**: Keep the single finish-to-start link with its 24-hour lag and instead reduce both activities' durations to reflect the crews working concurrently in the field.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The real flow is two crews advancing together along the trench with a fixed offset, and the question is which network construct represents continuous overlap with a maintained gap. (A) a start-to-start relationship with a lag is precisely that construct: backfill starts a set time after coating starts and shadows it down the line, capturing the section-by-section overlap and the cure within one link. (B) trimming the FS lag still forces all backfill to wait for coating to finish; it only shrinks the final wait and misrepresents the parallelism entirely. (C) per-section pairs model the overlap accurately and are arguably the most precise option, but they multiply the network into many activities and links for what is one continuously offset operation, which is heavier than the schedule needs and harder to maintain. (D) cutting durations fakes a shorter timeline without changing the logic that still serialises the two activities. A start-to-start link with a lag is the fit-for-purpose way to model trailing, overlapping work.
