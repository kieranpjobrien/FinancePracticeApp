# Question

A product owner must launch a mobile app to coincide with a marketing campaign on a fixed launch date that cannot move. At current velocity the backlog holds roughly a third more work than the team can finish by then, and the items are a mix of must-have core flows and nice-to-have enhancements. Marketing has told the team the campaign will drive heavy first-day usage. The project manager is helping plan the release. Which approach best fits the situation?

## Options

- **A**: Have the product owner define the minimum coherent set of features that delivers the campaign's value, plan the release around that set, and sequence the remaining enhancements for the next release while keeping them visible on the roadmap.
- **B**: Have the product owner rank every backlog item by value and have the team pull from the top each sprint until the date arrives, shipping whatever has reached done by then.
- **C**: Plan the full backlog into the release and protect the date by adding a short hardening sprint before launch to absorb any items that run over.
- **D**: Split the release into a smaller launch-day scope plus a fast-follow update a week later, and ask marketing whether the campaign can reference the fast-follow features as "coming soon".

## Answer

A

## Explanation

With a fixed, high-visibility date and a backlog that does not fit, the product owner should deliberately shape a coherent minimum release built around the campaign's value, rather than letting the launch contents fall out of velocity, and park the rest transparently. (A) makes scope an explicit, value-driven decision the product owner owns and ensures the date ships a complete, usable product. (B) ranking and pulling from the top sounds disciplined, but with no defined target set the launch contents are decided by whatever happens to finish, risking a date that ships half a core flow because a high-value item was only partly done. (C) a hardening sprint does not create capacity for a backlog that already exceeds velocity; it just defers the shortfall to the worst possible moment and still leaves items unfinished at a fixed date. (D) a fast-follow can be sensible, but committing to a second release a week out and renegotiating the campaign message is a larger move that should follow, not replace, first defining the minimum launch set, and it depends on marketing's agreement that is not yet given.
