# Question

A Scrum team's product owner committed to external customers that a fixed scope of roughly 180 story points will ship in a release. The team has finished three sprints at velocities of 22, 18, and 20 points, with six sprints remaining. The product owner, who controls scope and the customer relationship, asks the project manager whether the commitment is still realistic. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Present the velocity forecast and the size of the gap, then recommend the product owner descope the lowest-value stories to fit the six remaining sprints.
- **B**: Re-run the forecast using a range of plausible velocities and wait until two more sprints of data confirm the trend before raising the commitment risk.
- **C**: Forecast remaining capacity from observed velocity, show the product owner the gap against the 180-point commitment, and let the product owner decide how to adjust scope or date.
- **D**: Build the forecast and the options, and raise both at the upcoming release review where the product owner, customers, and sponsor are present together.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

All four respect empirical forecasting; the margin is who owns the decision and how soon the risk surfaces. The observed average (~20) projects about 120 points over six sprints, well short of 180, and the gap is already material, so it must be surfaced now to the person who owns scope and the customer commitment. (C) gives the product owner the data and the decision rights that are theirs. (A) is sound analysis but pre-empts the product owner's call on which stories to cut, overstepping the role. (D) delays surfacing a known risk to a larger forum and risks blindsiding the product owner in front of customers rather than briefing them first. (B) waits for more data when the trend is already clear enough to act, letting the gap grow. The project manager informs and equips the decision-owner first.
