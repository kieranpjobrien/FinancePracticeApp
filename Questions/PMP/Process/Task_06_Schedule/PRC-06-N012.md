# Question

A newly formed agile team is sizing backlog items. The most senior developer habitually says a number out loud first, and everyone else's estimates then cluster tightly around it. The project manager, facilitating, judges the estimates are echoing one voice rather than capturing the team's collective understanding. Which change should the facilitator make?

## Options

- **A**: Calibrate the team against two or three agreed reference stories of known size, then estimate new items by comparison to those references.
- **B**: Rotate the order in which people speak so the senior developer is no longer consistently first to state a number.
- **C**: Group the backlog items into buckets by relative size in a silent affinity exercise, resolving disagreements only where placements conflict.
- **D**: Have all members commit their estimate privately and reveal simultaneously, then open discussion on the items where estimates diverge most before re-voting.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The root problem is anchoring: one estimate is revealed before others form their own, biasing the rest. The fix must break that information order and then exploit disagreement. (D) simultaneous private reveal removes the anchor at the moment of estimating, and discussing the widest divergence surfaces the hidden assumptions, which is precisely how planning poker is designed to work. (A) reference-story calibration improves consistency but, if estimates are still spoken in sequence, the anchoring continues unchecked. (B) rotating speaking order spreads the anchoring around rather than eliminating sequential disclosure; whoever speaks first still anchors the room. (C) silent affinity sizing reduces verbal anchoring but loses the structured divergence conversation that turns a new team's differing views into shared understanding. Concealing estimates until simultaneous reveal addresses the bias most directly.
