# Question

A new PM joins your project and notices that the published schedule shows on-time completion but contains no schedule reserve, no buffer, and no contingency for risks identified in the risk register. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Trust the original PM's judgement and continue to report on-time completion based on the published schedule as a going-in position.
- **B**: Re-baseline the schedule immediately to add 20% contingency to every activity duration across the plan.
- **C**: Quietly add hidden buffer to internal task estimates while continuing to publish the same end date to stakeholders externally.
- **D**: Quantify the schedule risk from the register, propose explicit reserve through change control, and communicate the exposure to stakeholders.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

PMI's Plan Schedule Management process expects schedule reserves to be visible, sized to identified risks, and managed through change control. (D) does that. (A) carries forward the prior PM's blind spot. (B) pads every estimate without analysis, which inflates the schedule and hides risk inside activity durations. (C) is the "secret buffer" anti-pattern — it conceals contingency, breaks transparency, and prevents informed stakeholder decisions.
