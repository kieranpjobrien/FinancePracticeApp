# Question

On a predictive office-fit-out project, this week's schedule update shows two activities finished late: one on the critical path slipped four days, and a parallel activity with eight days of total float slipped two days. The sponsor sees both flagged red and asks the project manager to brief them tomorrow on the recovery plan. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Recalculate the schedule network to see how the four-day critical-path slip has moved the project finish date and whether the float-protected activity is still within its float.
- **B**: Build recovery options — fast-tracking and crashing candidates — for the critical-path activity so the sponsor briefing leads with a concrete plan.
- **C**: Ask both activity owners for revised duration estimates and root causes for the slip so the briefing explains why the dates moved.
- **D**: Update the dashboard's RAG thresholds so the float-protected activity reflects its remaining buffer rather than showing the same red as the binding slip.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four are reasonable, but recovery planning, root-cause gathering, and dashboard tuning all depend on first knowing the actual schedule impact. (A) recalculates the network to establish which slip truly moved the finish date and whether the parallel activity's two days are still absorbed by its eight days of float — without this, the PM cannot size the problem or scope recovery. (B) is the right next step but is premature until the network shows how much time must be recovered and on which path. (C) usefully informs the narrative but does not by itself tell the PM the end-date effect. (D) is a legitimate reporting improvement (the two reds are not equally severe), yet adjusting the display before understanding the impact risks masking a genuine problem and is lower priority than the analysis.
