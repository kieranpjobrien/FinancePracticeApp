# Question

A project manager has built the baseline using three-point estimates. For one complex data-migration activity the team has never performed, the analysis gives optimistic 8 days, most likely 14 days, and pessimistic 32 days; every other activity on the path has a tight, symmetric spread. The sponsor accepts the team's expected durations but wants the schedule contingency reserve spread evenly as a flat percentage across all activities, "to keep it simple". What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Apply the flat percentage as the sponsor asked, since an even reserve is transparent, easy to track, and the sponsor owns the funding decision.
- **B**: Size each activity's contingency from its own three-point spread, so the data-migration activity carries proportionally more reserve, and walk the sponsor through why the even split under-protects the one uncertain activity.
- **C**: Keep a single flat percentage for visibility but add a separate, named contingency line for the data-migration activity sized to its spread, leaving the other activities on the flat figure.
- **D**: Hold all activity reserves at the flat percentage and instead carry the extra data-migration exposure as a quantified risk in the project's overall schedule buffer rather than against the activity.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Contingency reserve should be sized to the quantified uncertainty it covers, so the activity with the wide, skewed spread warrants proportionally more reserve than the tight, symmetric ones, and the sponsor should be shown the reasoning rather than simply obeyed or overridden. (B) allocates reserve where the variance analysis says the risk is, across every activity, and brings the sponsor along, which is the soundest treatment of both the estimate and the stakeholder. (A) honours the request and the sponsor's authority but knowingly under-protects the genuinely uncertain activity while over-protecting the rest, accepting a worse schedule to avoid a conversation. (C) is close and defensible: it does fund the migration risk and preserves a simple headline figure, but leaving every other activity on an unjustified flat percentage still mis-sizes their reserves and is a partial fix rather than sizing each to its own uncertainty. (D) pooling the migration exposure into the overall buffer can be legitimate, but parking the one clearly activity-specific risk away from the activity, while keeping the flat split everywhere, weakens the link between the reserve and the activity whose spread drives it, making buffer monitoring less informative than sizing it at the activity.
