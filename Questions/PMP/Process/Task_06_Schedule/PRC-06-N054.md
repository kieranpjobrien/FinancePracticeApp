# Question

On a building project, "Install drywall" currently has a finish-to-start dependency on "Run electrical wiring". The project manager confirms drywall on the completed far wing can safely begin once wiring in that wing is about two days underway, while wiring continues elsewhere. The team wants to shorten the schedule with no added cost and no added risk to other activities. Which modelling change best captures this?

## Options

- **A**: Apply a two-day lead (negative lag) to the finish-to-start relationship so drywall can start before wiring fully finishes, reflecting the real overlap.
- **B**: Change the relationship to start-to-start with a two-day lag, so drywall begins two days after wiring starts.
- **C**: Decompose wiring and drywall by wing, then set a finish-to-start dependency within each wing so the far wing's drywall follows the far wing's wiring.
- **D**: Keep the finish-to-start logic but fast-track by overlapping the two activities, scheduling drywall to run partly in parallel with wiring.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four are genuine techniques and several would compress the schedule, so the test is which most precisely and safely models the specific overlap described, at no cost or added risk. (A) is the exact construct for "successor may start before predecessor fully finishes": a lead on the finish-to-start link, cost-free and bounded to the stated two-day overlap. (B) reaches a similar start time but changes the dependency's meaning — start-to-start with a lag ties drywall to when wiring *began* across the whole activity, not to the far wing's two-day point, so it can let drywall start where wiring has not yet reached. (C) is the cleanest model in principle, but it requires splitting both activities by wing; the team asked for a low-effort schedule change, and the scenario does not establish that the estimates and resources decompose neatly by wing, so it adds modelling work beyond what was requested. (D) names the right family (fast-tracking is overlapping with a lead) but is vaguer than (A) and explicitly accepts added risk by overlapping, which contradicts the "no added risk" constraint unless quantified as the precise two-day lead in (A).
