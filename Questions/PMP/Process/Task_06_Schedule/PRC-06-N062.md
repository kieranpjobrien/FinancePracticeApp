# Question

On a predictive data-centre fit-out, the network diagram shows "Install cable trays" finish-to-start into "Pull cabling" with a 5-day lag for adhesive cure on the tray brackets, and "Pull cabling" currently sits on the critical path. The cabling subcontractor proposes a faster-curing adhesive needing only 1 day, which would cut the lag by four days. The sponsor is pressing the PM to confirm a new, earlier completion date this afternoon. Before the project manager commits to an earlier date, what is the most important thing to establish?

## Options

- **A**: Whether another near-critical path would become critical once the lag is reduced, capping the actual gain on the completion date.
- **B**: Whether the faster-curing adhesive is approved against this project's bracket specification, since an unqualified material could fail and force rework.
- **C**: Whether the cabling crew can be on site four days earlier, as the saving evaporates if the subcontractor cannot start sooner.
- **D**: Whether the faster adhesive adds material cost that would require a budget change request before it can be used.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The sponsor is being promised a new completion date, and that date is only as good as the network says it is: shaving four days off this lag delivers four days on the end date only if no parallel path becomes critical first, so recalculating the schedule network is the load-bearing check before any commitment. (A) is the decisive schedule verification. (B) is a real and necessary gate — an unqualified adhesive is a quality risk — but quality sign-off does not tell the PM whether the end date actually moves. (C) is a valid resourcing dependency, yet even with a crew available the gain may not reach the finish date. (D) is a routine commercial check that is secondary to knowing whether the time saving materialises at all.
