# Question

A project manager is under pressure to recover a slipping predictive schedule and is considering fast-tracking several sequential design-and-build activities. Which statements correctly describe what fast-tracking specifically does or requires, as opposed to crashing? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: It overlaps activities that were planned in sequence, so a successor may start on a predecessor's incomplete or interim output.
- **B**: It raises the risk of rework because downstream work proceeds before its inputs are finalised, and it increases coordination effort across the now-parallel activities.
- **C**: It can compress the schedule without necessarily increasing total project cost, though it does increase risk.
- **D**: It shortens an activity's duration by adding resources such as overtime or extra crews to that activity.
- **E**: It only ever applies to activities that are not on the critical path, since overlapping critical activities is not permitted.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Fast-tracking is defined by overlapping work that was meant to run in series. (A) states that mechanism, including starting on interim outputs. (B) names its two characteristic costs: rework risk from unfinalised inputs and heavier coordination of parallel work. (C) is correct and is the key contrast with crashing: fast-tracking typically adds risk rather than cost, which is why it is often tried first when budget is tight. (D) describes crashing, which adds resources to shorten duration, not fast-tracking. (E) is false: fast-tracking is applied precisely to critical-path activities to pull in the finish date; the constraint is whether the overlap is technically feasible, not criticality. Recognising what fast-tracking is versus crashing is the point.
