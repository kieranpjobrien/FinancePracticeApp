# Question

It is day eight of a ten-day sprint on a logistics-tracking product. The team has already inspected the burndown together: two committed stories will not finish because a third-party API behaved differently than assumed, and the team agrees the sprint goal can still be met without them. The product owner is offsite at a customer site for the next two days and is reachable only by message. What should the project manager support the team in doing?

## Options

- **A**: Message the product owner now with the situation and the team's recommendation to drop the two stories back to the backlog, and proceed on that basis once acknowledged.
- **B**: Have the team drop the two stories from the sprint immediately, since the team owns the sprint backlog and can adjust its own committed work.
- **C**: Keep both stories in the sprint and let them roll over to the next sprint at sprint end, leaving the current scope untouched until the review.
- **D**: Pull two ready stories of similar size from the top of the product backlog to replace the at-risk ones and keep the team's committed point total intact.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The sprint goal is safe, so this is a scope-change decision, and changes to sprint scope are the product owner's call even when the team manages the day-to-day backlog — the PM keeps the PO in the loop rather than acting around an absent-but-reachable PO. (A) respects PO ownership of scope while not stalling, getting acknowledgement before proceeding. (B) is the subtle trap: the team owns *how* it organises the sprint backlog, but removing committed scope is a PO decision, and the PO is reachable. (C) defers a known outcome to the review and forfeits the chance to pull forward other value with two days left. (D) silently re-scopes the sprint and changes what was agreed without the PO, swapping committed work purely to protect a point total, which is not a sprint goal.
