# Question

Midway through a two-week sprint, a senior stakeholder asks the development team directly to add a "small" report needed for a board meeting in three days. The team is fully committed to its sprint backlog, and the report is genuinely valuable. Acting as scrum master, what should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Route the request to the product owner to rank in the backlog, and hold the current sprint backlog unchanged so it is picked up at the start of the next sprint.
- **B**: Facilitate the team estimating the report, and if it fits within remaining capacity without displacing committed work, let the product owner approve adding it.
- **C**: Recommend the product owner cancel the sprint and re-plan, since a new high-priority need from leadership has emerged.
- **D**: Bring the request to the product owner, who can decide with the team whether to trade something of equal size out of the current sprint to make room for the report.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

New work mid-sprint is the product owner's call, and the sprint backlog is the team's to negotiate, so the realistic question is how the change is absorbed without breaking either. (D) routes the decision correctly and recognises that capacity is already committed, so adding the report means trading comparable work out, a legitimate product-owner-plus-team negotiation. (A) is defensible but treats a three-day board deadline as if it can always wait a full sprint, ignoring that a swap may be both possible and warranted. (B) assumes free slack exists in a fully committed sprint, so "without displacing committed work" is usually wishful and risks silent overcommitment. (C) cancelling a sprint is reserved for when the sprint goal itself becomes obsolete, which one extra report does not do. Negotiating a same-size trade through the product owner fits the situation best.
