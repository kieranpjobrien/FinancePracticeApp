# Question

At the daily scrum on day six of a two-week sprint, a developer mentions she agreed to add a "small" extra report to a story already in progress after a senior stakeholder approached her directly. The team estimates the report is genuinely small but, with current commitments, finishing it would put the sprint goal at risk. The product owner is present. As Scrum Master, what should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Surface the trade-off to the product owner now — keeping the sprint goal intact versus absorbing the report — and let the product owner decide what enters the sprint.
- **B**: Facilitate a quick team conversation on whether anything in the sprint backlog can be dropped or reduced to make room for the report without threatening the goal.
- **C**: Add the report to the product backlog for ordering in a future sprint and have the developer continue with the original committed story.
- **D**: Remind the team and stakeholder of the agreement that mid-sprint scope changes are negotiated through the product owner, then address the immediate item.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Every option reflects sound agile behaviour, so the question is sequence and ownership. The sprint backlog belongs to the team, but the product owner owns what work is most valuable, and a live conflict between the sprint goal and a new request is precisely the product owner's call to make — so the PM surfaces the decision to them first (A). (B) is a healthy negotiation technique but pre-empts the product owner by assuming the report should be accommodated before they have weighed it against the goal. (C) is a reasonable likely outcome, yet deciding to defer it unilaterally still bypasses the product owner who may judge the report urgent. (D) reinforces the right channel and will matter, but coaching the process breach is secondary to resolving the immediate goal-versus-scope decision the team is blocked on.
