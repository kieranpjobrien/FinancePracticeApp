# Question

A make-or-buy analysis has concluded that a specialised testing capability, needed for about eight weeks, will be contracted to an external lab. The test scope is precisely defined and stable, the schedule is firm, and the budget is tight with little tolerance for overrun. The project manager is choosing the contract type. Which is the most appropriate for this engagement?

## Options

- **A**: A firm-fixed-price contract, since the scope is well defined and a fixed price places the cost-overrun risk on the seller.
- **B**: A cost-plus-fixed-fee contract, reimbursing the lab's actual costs plus a set fee to keep the arrangement flexible.
- **C**: A time-and-materials contract, paying for hours and materials as the testing proceeds for maximum responsiveness.
- **D**: A fixed-price-incentive-fee contract, sharing any cost savings or overruns with the lab against a target cost.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A firm-fixed-price contract suits well-defined, stable scope and a tight budget: the price is locked, and the risk of any overrun sits with the seller, which is exactly the protection a fixed budget needs. (B) cost-plus reimburses whatever the lab spends, putting overrun risk on the buyer — wrong when scope is clear and budget is tight. (C) time-and-materials is for undefined or evolving scope and offers no cost ceiling, exposing the tight budget. (D) an incentive-fee arrangement adds target-cost negotiation and shared overruns that are unnecessary complexity when the scope is fixed and a simple firm price will do.
