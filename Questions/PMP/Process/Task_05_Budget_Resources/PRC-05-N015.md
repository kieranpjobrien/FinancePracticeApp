# Question

A scope addition has just been approved through the change control board on a predictive project; it raises the expected cost of two work packages by an amount larger than the contingency reserve currently held. The project manager is updating the budget so performance measurement stays meaningful. How should the project manager incorporate the approved change?

## Options

- **A**: Have the additional funding authorised from management reserve and added to the cost baseline as part of implementing the approved change.
- **B**: Draw the additional cost from the contingency reserve and re-spread it across the two affected work packages within the existing baseline.
- **C**: Raise the two work packages' budgets to the new expected cost and re-spread the cost baseline, leaving the project's total authorised funding unchanged.
- **D**: Hold the cost baseline at its current total and record the additional approved cost as a planned variance the team will manage down over the remaining work.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

An approved change that adds scope needs an authorised funding source before the baseline moves: the money is released from management reserve (it is unforeseen additional in-scope work, and it exceeds contingency anyway), and the baseline is increased as part of implementing the change so future variance is measured against the correct plan. (B) contingency is reserved for the impact of identified risks, not approved scope additions, and the amount exceeds it — wrong source and insufficient. (C) raising the work-package budgets without authorising new funding silently consumes reserve or overstates the baseline against the same total, breaking the baseline-plus-reserve relationship. (D) freezing the baseline manufactures a standing variance that is not an overrun and distorts every later earned-value reading; an approved change should be planned in, not "managed down".
