# Question

An agile mobile-banking team runs two-week sprints against a fixed release budget. Three sprints in, the project manager confirms the average cost per sprint is running about 15% above the rate the budget assumed, although velocity is on plan. At the current burn, the funding will run out one sprint before the planned release. The backlog is prioritised and the release date is fixed. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Quantify the runway against the burn rate, then bring the projection and the scope-versus-funding options to the product owner and sponsor for a decision.
- **B**: Have the product owner trim the lowest-priority backlog items now so the funded sprints can deliver the rest, since the backlog is already prioritised.
- **C**: Raise a change request for additional funding to cover the projected shortfall before it materialises.
- **D**: Reforecast the remaining sprints assuming the burn rate returns to the budgeted rate, on the basis that early-sprint costs are often atypical.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The first move is to make the funding reality visible with a quantified runway and bring the trade-off to the people who own scope and funding while there is still time to act. (B) trimming scope is a likely outcome, but committing to it now pre-empts the product owner's and sponsor's call and assumes scope is the lever they will choose over funding. (C) requesting more money is also premature: it presumes the answer is funding rather than scope, before the owners have weighed the options. (D) assuming the burn will self-correct without evidence buries the warning the data is giving and risks discovering the shortfall too late; three consecutive sprints above rate is a trend, not a start-up blip.
