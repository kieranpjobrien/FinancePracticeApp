# Question

On an offshore wind project, a transformer with a 14-month manufacturing lead time sits on the critical path. The procurement budget is approved and a qualified supplier is selected. The required-on-site date gives just over 14 months before installation, leaving almost no margin between order placement and delivery. The project manager is finalising how this resource is handled in the plan. What is most important to build into the plan?

## Options

- **A**: Place the order at the very start to protect the thin lead-time margin, and add schedule reserve plus delivery milestones so any manufacturing slippage is detected and managed before it hits the on-site date.
- **B**: Place the order at the very start so the 14-month manufacturing time fits within the window before the required-on-site date.
- **C**: Add cost contingency sized to the transformer so funds are available if a delay forces expediting or a penalty against the supplier.
- **D**: Build a logistics and staging plan for transport, port handling, and on-site receipt so delivery is sequenced cleanly to the installation date.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The exposure is a critical-path item with almost no slack between order and need, so ordering early is necessary but not sufficient: the plan must also carry schedule reserve and intermediate delivery milestones so a manufacturing slip is seen and acted on before it consumes the on-site date. (B) ordering early addresses only the lead time and leaves the project blind to slippage during the 14 months, with no buffer if the supplier runs late. (C) cost contingency funds a response to a delay but does nothing to prevent or detect one, and money cannot recover a 14-month build that started slipping unnoticed. (D) logistics and staging matter but apply only once the unit is built and shipping; they do not protect the far larger manufacturing-lead-time risk that dominates this resource.
