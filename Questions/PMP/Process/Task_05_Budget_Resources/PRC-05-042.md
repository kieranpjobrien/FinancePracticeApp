# Question

A vendor has proposed a time-and-materials (T&M) contract for upcoming work, while the sponsor wants a firm fixed-price (FFP) for budget certainty. The scope of work is still loosely defined. What should the project manager recommend?

## Options

- **A**: Award an FFP contract because the sponsor's preference for budget certainty should take priority.
- **B**: Award a T&M contract because the vendor knows the work better and is best placed to choose the contract type.
- **C**: Use T&M for an initial discovery phase, then move to FFP once scope is sufficiently defined to be priced.
- **D**: Award a cost-plus-incentive-fee contract so risk is shared equally between the buyer and the vendor.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

PMI's contract guidance is to match contract type to scope certainty: FFP needs a well-defined scope, otherwise the buyer pays for the vendor's risk padding or absorbs change orders. (C) phases the contract — T&M for discovery to remove uncertainty, FFP for execution once scope is clear — which gives the sponsor the certainty they want where it matters. (A) signs FFP against unclear scope and invites either price padding or change-order disputes. (B) abdicates a buyer-side decision to the vendor. (D) introduces a complex incentive contract that does not solve the underlying scope-clarity problem.
