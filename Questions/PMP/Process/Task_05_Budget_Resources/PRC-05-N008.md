# Question

A product on a fixed annual budget is funded incrementally, with the steering group reviewing spend at each quarterly release. After two releases, measured adoption of the shipped features is far below the business case, while a different, unbuilt segment of the backlog targets a user group the early data suggests is far more receptive. The next release review is in two weeks. As the project manager accountable for value, what should you bring to the steering group?

## Options

- **A**: A recommendation to re-prioritise the backlog toward the more receptive segment for the next release, with the adoption evidence supporting the shift.
- **B**: A recommendation to continue the planned release sequence, on the basis that adoption often lags and more of the planned features may move the numbers.
- **C**: A recommendation to pause funding for one quarter while the team runs further analysis to confirm which segment to target before building more.
- **D**: A recommendation to hold scope but extend the timeline, spreading the approved annual budget over more releases to lower the quarterly burn.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Incremental funding exists so funders can redirect investment on evidence; the data points to a more receptive segment, so the PM recommends re-prioritising the backlog toward it and keeps delivering value within the same budget. (B) keeps spending against an assumption the evidence is already contradicting, betting on sunk-cost momentum. (C) pausing delivery to analyse forgoes a quarter of value when there is already enough signal to act and adjust on the next review. (D) stretching the timeline addresses burn rate, not the real issue — whether the work being funded is the work most likely to create value.
