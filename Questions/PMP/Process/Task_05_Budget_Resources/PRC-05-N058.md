# Question

An agile e-commerce team is partway through a release. The completed-work line on the burn-up is rising more slowly than the trajectory needed to meet the release date, while the scope line is flat and cost per iteration is on plan. The product owner owns scope and the customer relationship; stakeholders were told the full scope would land on the fixed date. The release has several iterations still to run. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the projected completion point from the burn-up to the product owner now and have them decide whether to trim scope or move the date.
- **B**: Add capacity for the remaining iterations to lift throughput toward the required line, keeping both the scope and the date intact.
- **C**: Re-run the projection across a range of recent velocities and confirm the shortfall holds over the next iteration or two before raising it, so the forecast is not called on a short, noisy trend.
- **D**: Work with the product owner to re-order the backlog so the highest-value items burn down first, protecting the release's value if the full scope does not land.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The burn-up already shows a material, sustained gap between trajectory and actuals, and the decision it forces — trade scope against date — belongs to the product owner, who owns both scope and the customer commitment; the project manager's job is to surface the projected completion point now and equip that decision (A). (C) is the strongest distractor: re-forecasting across a velocity range is good practice, but waiting for another iteration or two to "confirm" a trend that is already clear lets a known shortfall grow against a fixed date, and the existing data is enough to raise it — it loses to A only on timing. (B) adding capacity mid-release usually depresses near-term throughput through onboarding and lifts a cost base that is currently on plan, and it commits a recovery the product owner has not yet been asked to choose. (D) re-sequencing for value is a sensible hedge and may well follow, but on its own it quietly accepts that scope will be cut without putting the scope-versus-date call to the person who owns it — it acts on the consequence before surfacing the decision.
