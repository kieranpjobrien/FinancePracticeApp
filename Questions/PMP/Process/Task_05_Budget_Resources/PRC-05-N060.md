# Question

A hybrid digital-transformation programme is funded incrementally, with each tranche released at a benefits gate where measured value to date must justify the next investment. Approaching the gate, the programme has spent on plan but the benefits realised so far are materially below the business case. Part of the shortfall is a market move by a competitor that has changed which of the programme's components are now most valuable; part is slower-than-expected adoption of what has already shipped. The next tranche is large. How should the project manager prepare the gate decision?

## Options

- **A**: Present the benefits, adoption, and market data as they stand and ask the funders to decide whether to continue, re-scope, or stop before the tranche is committed, without leading them to a recommendation.
- **B**: Bring the same data with a recommended re-scoping that redirects the next tranche toward the components the market move has made most valuable, for the funders to approve or amend.
- **C**: Bring the data with a recommendation to release a smaller bridging portion of the tranche to fund adoption of what has shipped, deferring the larger commitment to the next gate once benefits respond.
- **D**: Re-baseline the business case to the current run-rate and revised market assumptions first, then take the updated case and the tranche request to the gate against the new targets.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The gate's job is to test whether the next investment is still justified, and the project manager owes the funders not just data but analysis: the shortfall has a diagnosable cause — a market shift that has re-ranked the components' value — and the most useful preparation pairs the evidence with a recommended re-scoping that redirects the tranche to where value now lies, leaving the decision with the funders (B). (A) is defensible and not wrong — surfacing the data and the options is the floor — but presenting bare options while deliberately withholding a recommendation underuses the PM's analysis when the data points to a specific re-scope; the funders own the call, yet the PM should still advise. (C) a bridging tranche to lift adoption is a credible option and may be the funders' choice, but it treats the shortfall as mainly an adoption problem and commits money before the gate has weighed the larger strategic re-scope the market move calls for. (D) re-baselining the business case before the gate is the trap: quietly resetting targets to the current run-rate ahead of the decision moves the goalposts the gate is meant to hold the programme against, and the re-baseline should follow the funders' direction, not pre-empt it.
