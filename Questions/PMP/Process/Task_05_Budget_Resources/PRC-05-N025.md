# Question

A project manager must give the finance committee a budget figure they will hold the project to within a tight tolerance, and the committee meets in two days. The WBS is decomposed to work-package level. For about 70% of the packages there are reliable productivity rates from comparable past work; the remaining 30% are first-of-a-kind packages with no historical data and significant uncertainty. Subject-matter experts are available. Which approach best produces the committed figure?

## Options

- **A**: Estimate each package at the lowest practical level — parametric rates where reliable history exists, expert three-point estimates where it does not — and aggregate, carrying contingency sized to the uncertain packages.
- **B**: Estimate every package parametrically by applying the available historical productivity rates uniformly across the whole WBS.
- **C**: Produce a single bottom-up point estimate per package from the experts and sum them into one committed total.
- **D**: Build an analogous estimate from the most similar past project and adjust it for the known differences in this project's scope.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A tightly held budget needs package-level (bottom-up) rigour, but the right per-package technique differs: parametric is most defensible where reliable rates exist, while the first-of-a-kind packages have no history, so expert three-point estimates capture their uncertainty and feed a contingency sized to it — this gives both accuracy and an honest confidence range. (B) applying historical rates uniformly forces parametric onto the 30% that have no comparable history, understating their real uncertainty. (C) single point estimates aggregated into one number hide the variance in the uncertain packages and leave no basis for contingency on a figure that will be enforced. (D) an analogous estimate is too coarse to commit to a tight tolerance when the WBS and experts already support a detailed build-up.
