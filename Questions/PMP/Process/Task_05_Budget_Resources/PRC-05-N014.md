# Question

At a stage gate, a predictive project reports CPI of 0.88 driven by sustained rework, and the trend has held for three reporting periods. The sponsor wants cost performance brought back towards budget on the remaining work. Of the responses below, which TWO should the project manager pursue first to actually improve cost performance going forward? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Run a root-cause analysis on the rework and correct the source so future work stops eroding cost performance.
- **B**: Strengthen quality assurance at the point where the rework originates, accepting some added prevention cost to cut the larger rework cost downstream.
- **C**: Re-forecast the estimate at completion using the cumulative CPI and present the realistic outlook so the sponsor can decide on scope or funding.
- **D**: Reassign the lowest-performing work to a faster supplier under a new fixed-price arrangement to lift efficiency on the remainder.
- **E**: Apply the to-complete performance index to confirm the efficiency now required, then commit the team to hitting it for the rest of the project.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

A sustained, recurring CPI of 0.88 is a process problem: the two actions that genuinely improve future cost performance are fixing the root cause of the rework (A) and reinforcing prevention where it originates so the rework stops being produced (B) — the cost-of-quality trade of more appraisal/prevention to avoid larger failure costs. (C) is sound and necessary, but a re-forecast reports the position honestly; it does not by itself bring cost back towards budget, so it is not one of the two corrective actions. (E) TCPI quantifies the efficiency required and is worth calculating, but "commit the team to hit it" is an exhortation, not a mechanism — with CPI stuck at 0.88 for three periods, demanding a higher index without changing the process is unlikely to deliver. (D) re-procuring mid-stream could in principle help but introduces transition risk, re-baselining, and supplier ramp-up that typically worsen near-term performance; it is not a first-line response ahead of fixing the known internal cause.
