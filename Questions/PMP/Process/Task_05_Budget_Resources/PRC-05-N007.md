# Question

A predictive project's RACI chart lists three engineers as Responsible for an integration deliverable and shows no one as Accountable for it. The deliverable is now two weeks past its planned completion with no progress, while each engineer has kept their own portion moving. The three are cooperative and technically capable, and the project manager has the authority to adjust role assignments. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Assign one Accountable owner for the deliverable on the RACI, leaving the three engineers as Responsible for their respective parts.
- **B**: Bring the three engineers together to agree among themselves who will lead the deliverable to completion and by when.
- **C**: Name one of the three as sole Responsible for the whole deliverable and remove the other two from it.
- **D**: Ask the engineers' functional managers to nominate which of their staff should carry the deliverable to completion.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The deliverable spans three Responsible parties but the RACI shows no Accountable owner, so no single person is answerable for driving it to done; the most direct first move is to fill that role on the chart, which both unblocks this deliverable and closes the structural gap so the pattern does not repeat. (B) is a defensible empowerment move and self-organising teams often allocate their own work well, but here the missing role is a structural defect in the artifact the PM controls — a verbal agreement to lead does not establish accountability on the RACI, so the next multi-Responsible deliverable can stall the same way. (C) collapsing to a single Responsible can work, but it discards legitimate shared execution across three capable engineers and still leaves no one formally accountable — it conflates doing the work with being answerable for it. (D) routes a decision the PM is empowered to make through functional managers, adding delay and ceding a project-role assignment that belongs to the PM. The fine margin is that A fixes the accountability structure while preserving shared responsibility, whereas B, C, and D each either skip the structure or hand the call to someone else.
