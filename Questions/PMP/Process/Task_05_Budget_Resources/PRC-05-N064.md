# Question

A newly formed agile team has been funded to deliver a product increment but has no historical velocity. The steering group is pressing the product owner and project manager for a credible cost-to-completion forecast for the release. What is the soundest basis for the first forecast?

## Options

- **A**: Run two or three sprints to establish an initial velocity range, then forecast the release cost from that range and refine it each sprint.
- **B**: Convert the full backlog to ideal hours, multiply by the team's blended rate, and present that as the release budget.
- **C**: Borrow the average velocity of a comparable team elsewhere in the organisation and forecast the release from it.
- **D**: Commit the steering group's target date and back-calculate the velocity the team will need to hit it.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

With no velocity history, a short run of real sprints yields an empirical range that is the most defensible foundation for a release forecast, and agile forecasting expects that range to tighten each iteration. (B) ideal-hours estimating ignores the variability agile explicitly plans around and tends to read as false precision. (C) another team's velocity reflects their context, story sizing, and skills, so it is an unreliable proxy. (D) starting from the desired date and reverse-engineering the required velocity is wishful planning, not forecasting, and sets the team up to fail.
