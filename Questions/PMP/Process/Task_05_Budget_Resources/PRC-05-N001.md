# Question

A water-treatment upgrade is two-thirds complete. A risk that was identified in planning and funded from contingency reserve has now occurred, and the agreed response will cost slightly more than the amount set aside for it in the reserve. The total project budget still has untouched management reserve. The project manager wants the cost record and reserve reporting to stay credible. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Fund the response from contingency up to the amount allocated, and raise the small shortfall for the sponsor to release from management reserve.
- **B**: Fund the full response from contingency reserve, since the risk was identified in planning and that is what contingency exists for.
- **C**: Fund the full response from management reserve, keeping the remaining contingency intact for risks not yet realised.
- **D**: Fund the response from the affected work packages' remaining budget and record the contingency draw once the period closes.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Contingency covers identified risks but only up to the analysed amount; once the response exceeds what was set aside, the excess is an overrun on the estimate that the PM does not control unilaterally — it is raised for the sponsor to release from management reserve. (B) overspends contingency beyond its basis, so the reserve burn-down no longer reconciles to the analysed risks. (C) bypasses the contingency that was specifically provisioned for this risk and prematurely draws management reserve, distorting both reserves. (D) hides the draw inside work-package budgets and defers recording it, which falsifies in-period cost performance — the response is correctly funded but the accounting is wrong.
