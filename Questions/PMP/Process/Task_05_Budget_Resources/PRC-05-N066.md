# Question

A developer is building the eighth near-identical distribution warehouse in a standard programme. The design is fixed and the gross floor area of this unit is confirmed. The organisation holds full final-cost records for the previous seven units, including the area and the as-built cost of each. The sponsor needs a cost figure for this unit that is both fast to produce and well grounded in what the programme has actually spent. Which estimating approach best fits the situation?

## Options

- **A**: Derive a cost-per-square-metre rate from the seven completed units' actual costs and apply it to this unit's confirmed area.
- **B**: Take the final cost of the most recent comparable unit and use it directly as this unit's estimate, adjusted for inflation.
- **C**: Decompose this unit's WBS and build a detailed bottom-up estimate from the component work packages.
- **D**: Average the total final cost of all seven previous units and use that average as the estimate for this unit.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four are real estimating moves and the data exists to attempt any of them; the situation rewards the one that is both quick and tightly grounded in actuals. (A) is parametric: a per-square-metre rate calibrated from seven actual cost-and-area records and applied to a known area is statistically grounded, fast, and scales correctly to this unit's size — the strongest fit when a reliable rate and a measured quantity both exist. (B) is analogous: defensible and quick, but it leans on a single prior unit and ignores six other data points and any area difference, so it is lower-fidelity than the rate-based approach the data supports. (C) bottom-up would be the most accurate in principle but is slow and discards the rich actual-cost history; it is overkill for an eighth standard repeat where a calibrated rate already captures the cost behaviour. (D) a flat average ignores that cost scales with area — if this unit differs in size from the average, the figure is systematically wrong; the per-area rate corrects for exactly that. The fine margin is parametric versus analogous: both are fast, but the calibrated rate uses all the data and the measured area, so it edges out the single-analogue figure.
