# Question

A Scrum team funded per release has a stable velocity over six sprints. Two sprints into a six-sprint release, the product owner brings in several new high-value stories. At the team's velocity, the new stories plus the committed backlog will not all fit the remaining sprints. The release date and the funding envelope are both fixed, and a portion of the originally planned backlog is lower in value than the new stories. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Have the product owner re-order the whole backlog by value against the team's remaining capacity, so the most valuable items — old or new — are delivered and the lowest-value items drop out of this release.
- **B**: Protect the items the team already committed to for this release and place the new stories at the top of the next release's backlog.
- **C**: Add the new high-value stories to the current sprint backlogs and let the team work out during sprint planning which committed items to displace.
- **D**: Convene the sponsor and product owner to decide whether the higher value of the new stories justifies relaxing the fixed date or funding for this release.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

With date and funding fixed, scope is the lever, and the product owner owns backlog ordering: re-prioritising the entire remaining backlog by value against capacity ensures the release delivers maximum value, displacing lower-value items whether they were planned or new. (B) freezing the existing commitment protects sequence over value and would ship lower-value planned work while higher-value stories wait a whole release — the opposite of agile's value optimisation. (C) injecting stories mid-sprint and leaving the trade-off to the team during the sprint disrupts sprint commitments and pushes a portfolio-value decision down to the wrong level. (D) reopening the fixed date or funding is premature when re-prioritising within the fixed constraints can absorb the change, which is exactly what the agile funding model is designed to do.
