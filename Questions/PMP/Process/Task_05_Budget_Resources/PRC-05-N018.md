# Question

A project has BAC = €1,500,000. At status date: EV = €600,000, AC = €700,000, PV = €650,000. Cost variance is judged typical and expected to persist. The sponsor asks by how much the project is now forecast to overrun its budget. What is the Variance at Completion?

## Options

- **A**: A €250,000 overrun, from an EAC of €1,750,000 based on the cumulative cost performance index.
- **B**: A €100,000 overrun, equal to the current cost variance carried straight through to completion.
- **C**: A €150,000 overrun, from an EAC that adds remaining budgeted work to actual costs.
- **D**: A €396,000 overrun, from an EAC that divides the budget by the product of the cost and schedule performance indices.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

With typical variance, EAC = BAC / CPI; CPI = 600,000 / 700,000 ≈ 0.857, so EAC ≈ 1,500,000 / 0.857 = €1,750,000, and VAC = BAC − EAC = 1,500,000 − 1,750,000 = −€250,000, a €250,000 overrun. (B) carrying only the current €100,000 CV assumes no further erosion, contradicting the typical-variance assumption. (C) uses the atypical EAC = AC + (BAC − EV) formula, which the scenario rules out by stating the variance is typical. (D) divides by CPI × SPI (≈ 0.857 × 0.923 ≈ 0.791), giving EAC ≈ €1,896,000 and a €396,000 overrun — that formula is for when schedule pressure also drives remaining cost, but the sponsor asked only about the cost overrun and the variance is cost-typical, so the schedule factor should not be folded in.
