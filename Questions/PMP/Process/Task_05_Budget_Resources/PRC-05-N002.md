# Question

A 10-month plant relocation has a BAC of £4,000,000. At the end of month 5, EV = £1,800,000, AC = £2,000,000, and PV = £2,000,000. The original estimating assumptions no longer hold because a one-off permitting delay caused the overrun, and the team is confident the remaining work will run to plan. Which forecast should the project manager report?

## Options

- **A**: EAC = £4,444,444, applying the cumulative cost performance index to the whole budget.
- **B**: EAC = £4,200,000, treating the overrun as atypical and adding the remaining budgeted work to actual costs.
- **C**: EAC = £4,938,272, dividing the budget by the product of the cost and schedule performance indices.
- **D**: EAC = £3,800,000, recovering the £200,000 overrun across the remaining work because the team is confident the rest runs to plan.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

When a variance is atypical (a one-off that will not recur), EAC = AC + (BAC − EV) = 2,000,000 + (4,000,000 − 1,800,000) = £4,200,000. (A) assumes the overrun is typical and will persist, contradicting the stated facts. (C) is the EAC formula used when both cost and schedule pressure continue, which does not fit. (D) tries to recover the £200,000 already spent by under-spending the remaining budgeted work — but "runs to plan" means remaining work costs its budgeted amount, not less; the sunk overrun cannot be clawed back, so this understates the EAC.
