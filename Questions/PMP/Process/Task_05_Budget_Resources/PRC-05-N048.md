# Question

During sprint planning for a games-studio team, the project manager confirms that a public holiday and two developers' pre-booked leave will cut the team's available hours for the upcoming sprint by roughly a third. The product owner has a sprint goal in mind and the team is about to pull stories based on its usual velocity. There is no fixed external deadline on this sprint's content. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Let the team forecast a reduced sprint scope sized to the hours actually available, and have the product owner confirm which goal that smaller forecast can still meet.
- **B**: Bring in a temporary contractor for the sprint so the team can pull its usual amount of work and keep the goal unchanged.
- **C**: Pull the team's normal scope and plan to carry the likely unfinished stories into the next sprint.
- **D**: Keep the usual scope but extend the sprint by the equivalent lost days so the same work still fits.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Velocity assumes typical availability, so when known absences cut capacity the team forecasts a sprint sized to the hours actually present, and the product owner reshapes the goal to what that smaller forecast can deliver. (B) adding a contractor for a single two-week sprint rarely yields net capacity once onboarding and context are accounted for, and it raises cost to protect a goal that can simply be rescoped. (C) deliberately over-committing to generate carry-over inflates apparent velocity and corrupts the forecasting data the team relies on. (D) lengthening the sprint breaks the fixed-cadence timebox that makes velocity and planning comparable across sprints; cadence is held constant and scope flexes, not the reverse.
