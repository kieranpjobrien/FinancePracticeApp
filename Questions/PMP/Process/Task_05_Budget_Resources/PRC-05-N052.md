# Question

A hybrid healthcare-portal project has a fixed budget and a fixed go-live date. With one quarter to go, the forecast shows the funds will fall short of completing all planned features at the agreed quality standard. The quality standard is tied to patient-safety requirements and cannot be relaxed. The product backlog is prioritised, and a small number of lower-value features have no regulatory dependency. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Work with the product owner to defer the lowest-value, non-regulatory features to a later release so the funded scope is completed to the full quality standard.
- **B**: Take the forecast shortfall, the safety constraint, and the scope options to the sponsor and product owner and let them decide between deferring scope and authorising more funding.
- **C**: Submit a change request for the additional funding needed to deliver the full scope at standard, since the budget shortfall is a baseline issue the sponsor must rule on.
- **D**: Rebalance the team toward the safety-critical features first, completing the must-pass scope to standard and finishing whatever lower-value work the remaining budget allows.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The constraints are explicit — budget fixed, date fixed, safety-linked quality fixed — and the backlog already flags lower-value features with no regulatory dependency, so the lever is identified: defer those items with the product owner and deliver the rest to standard (A). (B) escalating the trade-off is reasonable when the levers are genuinely unclear or funding is realistically available, but here the prioritised backlog and the hard constraints already point to the answer, so pushing the decision up is slower than acting on what the product owner owns. (C) requesting more money presumes funding is the chosen lever and that an increase is obtainable in the final quarter; with a fixed budget stated, scope is the safer first move and the change request is a fallback, not the opening play. (D) front-loading safety-critical work is sound sequencing, but "finish whatever the budget allows" leaves the partly-built lower-value features unmanaged — better to deliberately defer them with the product owner than to let the budget decide which half-done features get dropped.
