# Question

A predictive subsea-cable project has a quantified risk register with probabilities and cost impacts for each identified risk; several of the larger risks are correlated, in that a bad-weather season would trigger more than one at once. The sponsor questions whether the proposed contingency reserve is "just a round number" and wants a defensible figure. Which approach best supports this?

## Options

- **A**: Sum the expected monetary value (probability × impact) of every risk in the register and set contingency to that total.
- **B**: Run a quantitative cost-risk simulation across the register that accounts for the correlations, and set contingency from the exposure at an agreed confidence level.
- **C**: Set contingency to the simple sum of the full cost impacts of all identified risks, so the reserve covers the worst case if every risk occurs.
- **D**: Benchmark contingency against the actual reserve drawdowns of the organisation's two most recent comparable cable projects.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A simulation (e.g. Monte Carlo) models the risks together, captures the correlation the scenario highlights, and produces an exposure distribution from which contingency can be set at an agreed confidence level — the most defensible basis here (B). (A) straight EMV summation is a reasonable technique, but adding independent EMVs understates exposure when risks are correlated and move together, which is precisely this project's feature; it is the strong-but-second-best answer. (C) summing full impacts assumes every risk occurs at once, vastly over-funding the reserve and tying up capital the project does not need. (D) benchmarking against comparable projects is a legitimate sanity check and better than a round number, but it derives the figure from other projects' histories rather than from this project's own quantified register, so it is less defensible than analysing the data the team already holds.
