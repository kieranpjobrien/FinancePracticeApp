# Question

Three sprints from a major release, an agile team is consistently completing only about 80% of its committed points, defect rates are climbing, and the team has been working evenings to keep up. In sprint planning the product owner keeps loading each sprint to the team's historical velocity and asks the project manager to help the team "find a way" to deliver the committed scope. What is the most appropriate response?

## Options

- **A**: Plan future sprints to the team's recent demonstrated throughput and have the product owner re-prioritise scope to fit that smaller, sustainable envelope.
- **B**: Keep planning to historical velocity but add an explicit quality-hardening allowance each sprint so defects are fixed within the sprint rather than deferred.
- **C**: Reduce work-in-progress limits and have the team finish in-progress stories before starting new ones, lifting completion without changing the committed total.
- **D**: Protect the release scope and date, and ask the team to sustain the current evening effort for the three remaining sprints, then plan a recovery period afterward.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Falling completion plus rising defects plus evening work are the signature of over-commitment; the durable fix is to plan to demonstrated throughput and have the product owner re-prioritise scope to fit, which restores both a credible plan and a sustainable pace. (B) reserving hardening time helps quality but still plans to a velocity the team is not achieving, so it keeps over-committing the build. (C) limiting WIP improves flow and is good practice, but it does not reconcile a committed total that already exceeds capacity — completion stays short of the commitment. (D) sustaining overtime for three sprints knowingly trades quality and people's wellbeing for a fixed scope, and a recovery period afterward does not undo the defects shipped in the release.
