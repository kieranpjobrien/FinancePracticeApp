# Question

A project manager is finalising the detailed cost estimate for a hospital wing fit-out ahead of the funding gate, where the figure must be both as accurate as the available information allows and defensible under scrutiny. Several estimating choices are on the table. Which three actions most improve the accuracy and credibility of the estimate? (Select three.)

## Options

- **A**: Build the estimate bottom-up from the WBS work packages and have the trades who will do the work review their own package estimates.
- **B**: Run a quantified reserve analysis so that contingency is sized to the identified risks on each package rather than carried as one undifferentiated allowance.
- **C**: Calibrate the estimate against the organisation's actual costs and lessons learned from comparable recent fit-outs, adjusting for known differences in scope and location.
- **D**: Use a three-point estimate on every line item, including the routine packages whose cost is already well established from recent identical work.
- **E**: Have an independent cost engineer who is not on the project review the estimate before it goes to the gate.

## Answer

A, B, E

## Explanation

This question makes every option a real estimating practice; the discrimination is which three most raise both accuracy and defensibility. Bottom-up estimating validated by the performers (A) and risk-based reserve analysis that ties contingency to specific identified risks (B) both sharpen the number and make it auditable. Independent review by a cost engineer outside the project (E) is a recognised way to surface bias and error and is exactly what makes an estimate defensible at a gate. (C) is genuinely useful but is the weaker of the candidates here: comparison against past actuals is an analogous-style cross-check, valuable for sanity but lower-fidelity than the detailed build, and the stem already has a bottom-up estimate to rely on. (D) is the trap: three-point estimating is a sound technique, but applying ranges to packages whose cost is already firm from recent identical work adds effort and spurious spread without improving accuracy — three-point estimating earns its keep on the uncertain packages, not the settled ones. The closest contest is C versus E; E both improves accuracy and directly answers the "defensible under scrutiny" requirement, so C is the one to drop.
