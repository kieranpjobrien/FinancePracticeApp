# Question

A predictive project is two-thirds complete. Several identified risks in the register have occurred at close to their assessed impacts, and the project manager has drawn the matching contingency from the cost baseline to fund the responses, as planned. Roughly 70% of the contingency is now consumed with a third of the work remaining. A steering-group member asks whether these events mean the project will overrun its total authorised funding. What is the most accurate response?

## Options

- **A**: The events were funded from contingency held inside the baseline for exactly these risks, so they do not by themselves breach the total authorised funding; the open question is whether the contingency now remaining is adequate for the risks still ahead.
- **B**: The events do not breach the authorised funding because contingency, by definition, can never cause an overrun — it is what the project draws on whenever any cost pressure arises.
- **C**: The project will overrun, because 70% of contingency consumed at two-thirds complete is a run-rate that mathematically projects the reserve being exhausted before the end.
- **D**: It cannot be answered yet: only once the contingency is fully drawn and the project starts drawing management reserve does a funding overrun become possible.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Contingency is provisioned inside the cost baseline to fund identified, analysed risks; spending it as those risks occur is performing to the funded plan, not overrunning it — so the events alone do not breach the authorised funding, and the live concern is forward-looking: is the remaining contingency enough for the risks still in front of the project (A). (B) reaches the right verdict for the wrong reason — contingency is not a bottomless pool for "any cost pressure"; it is sized for specific risks and can be exhausted, so the claim that it can never cause an overrun is false. (C) misreads the data: contingency burn is driven by which risks happen to occur, not by a linear run-rate against percent complete, so projecting exhaustion from a 70%/two-thirds ratio is unsound. (D) is wrong because a funding overrun is assessed by forecasting remaining exposure against remaining reserves now, not deferred until management reserve is first touched.
