# Question

A predictive project is 40% complete. Cost performance to date shows persistent inefficiency driven by a systemic productivity shortfall that the team expects to continue for the remainder of the work. The sponsor asks the project manager for a revised forecast of total cost at completion. Which approach gives the most defensible estimate at completion in this situation?

## Options

- **A**: Forecast the remaining work at the cost efficiency experienced so far, since the inefficiency is systemic and expected to persist.
- **B**: Forecast the remaining work at the budgeted rate, assuming the team recovers to plan now that the issue is known.
- **C**: Set the estimate at completion equal to the original budget at completion, treating the variance to date as a timing difference.
- **D**: Forecast the remaining work using the combined effect of cost and schedule efficiency, to reflect total project pressure.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

When a cost variance is systemic and expected to continue, EAC = BAC / CPI applies the current cost efficiency to the remaining work — the correct model for a persistent productivity problem. (B) BAC + (BAC − EV), which assumes future work runs to plan, is only valid when the past variance is a one-off, which is explicitly not the case here. (C) resetting to BAC ignores a real, ongoing overrun. (D) the schedule-and-cost combined index (the SPI×CPI form) is used when both schedule pressure and cost inefficiency jointly drive the remaining work, not for a purely cost-driven productivity shortfall.
