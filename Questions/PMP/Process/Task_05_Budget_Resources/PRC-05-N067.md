# Question

On a predictive project, the construction phase will finish about two weeks early. A scarce, high-day-rate specialist crew that is hard to re-source on short notice will then be idle until the commissioning phase needs them in three weeks. Their day rate continues for as long as they are assigned to the project. The staffing management plan says resources are released back to the resource pool when their phase work completes. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Release the crew per the staffing management plan and rely on re-acquiring them when commissioning starts.
- **B**: Before releasing the crew, check with the resource manager whether they can be redeployed to another project for the gap and recalled for commissioning, and release them only once that recall is confirmed.
- **C**: Pull forward commissioning-phase preparation work that this crew is qualified to do, so the early finish is used to de-risk the next phase rather than leaving them idle.
- **D**: Retain the crew on the project through the gap, treating the continued day rate as the cost of guaranteeing their availability for commissioning.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

This is a judgement call among defensible options, not a "follow the plan" reflex. The staffing management plan's release rule is the default, but a scarce resource that is hard to re-source creates a real re-acquisition risk that the plan's mechanics do not by themselves resolve. (B) honours the release process while actively protecting the recall — it stops the project paying for idle time and secures the crew's return, which is the balance the situation demands. (A) follows the plan literally but ignores the stated re-sourcing difficulty, risking that the crew is unavailable when commissioning starts. (C) is tempting and sometimes right, but pulling commissioning work forward only helps if that work is genuinely ready and independent of construction sign-off; using it purely to keep the crew busy risks gold-plating or rework on unconfirmed inputs. (D) eliminates the availability risk but knowingly burns scarce budget on three weeks of non-work, which is the cost problem the manager should be trying to avoid. The fine margin is B versus A: both release the crew, but B adds the recall safeguard that the scarcity makes essential.
