# Question

An agile team's last three sprints delivered 32, 28, and 30 story points. For the upcoming sprint, two of eight developers are on leave for half the sprint and the team has committed to a two-day regulatory spike. During sprint planning the team estimates it can credibly take on about 22 points. The product owner says the release forecast assumes 30 points this sprint and asks the team to commit to 30. What should the Scrum Master do?

## Options

- **A**: Have the team commit to the 22 points it estimates it can complete, and surface the gap against the release forecast to the product owner now.
- **B**: Commit to 22 points this sprint and propose the team recover the 8-point shortfall by raising commitments in the two sprints after the absentees return.
- **C**: Commit to the 22 points and privately add a stretch goal of 8 more points so the team aims for 30 without formally over-committing.
- **D**: Commit to 26 points as a compromise between the team's estimate and the product owner's forecast to keep the release on track.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The sprint commitment must reflect the capacity the team actually has; it commits to 22 and makes the resulting gap against the release forecast visible immediately so the product owner can re-plan scope or expectations. (B) pre-commits future sprints to a recovery the team has not yet estimated, manufacturing the same over-commitment one sprint later. (C) a hidden stretch of the full shortfall is over-commitment by another name and erodes the honesty of the forecast. (D) splitting the difference to 26 commits to work the team has said it cannot do, trading a credible plan for a comfortable number — the schedule pressure does not change capacity.
