# Question

On a predictive solar-farm construction project, a key subcontractor notifies a 12% price increase on remaining works, citing a steel-index escalation clause in the original contract. The schedule depends on this subcontractor. The contingency held for commodity-price risk covers part but not all of the increase. The project manager has read the clause and it does appear to permit an indexed adjustment. What should the project manager do next?

## Options

- **A**: Reconcile the subcontractor's calculation against the contract's named index source and the specified base and current reference dates, confirming the adjustment is correctly derived before accepting any figure.
- **B**: Accept the 12% as contractually valid since the clause permits escalation, draw the available contingency, and raise a change request for the funding shortfall above it.
- **C**: Open a commercial negotiation with the subcontractor to share the steel-cost impact, on the basis that a long-term relationship should absorb part of the increase.
- **D**: Have the procurement team issue a formal notice disputing the increase and requesting full substantiation before any payment is considered.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The clause permitting an indexed adjustment is not the same as the subcontractor having calculated it correctly, so the next step is to reconcile their figure against the contract's *named* index, base date, and current reference date — escalation disputes routinely turn on the wrong index series or reference dates, and verifying the derivation is what makes the eventual funding decision sound (A). (B) is the strongest distractor because the clause is valid and acting decisively is appealing, but committing contingency and a change request to a 12% figure before checking how that 12% was derived funds a number that may itself be overstated. (C) goodwill negotiation can have a place, but asking a subcontractor to waive a charge the contract expressly grants, before even validating the amount, is a weak commercial position and risks the schedule the project depends on. (D) a formal dispute notice is disproportionate and adversarial when the clause is genuine and the figure has not yet been checked — substantiation is obtained by reconciling the calculation first, escalating to a dispute only if that reconciliation fails.
