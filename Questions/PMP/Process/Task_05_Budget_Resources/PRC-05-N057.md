# Question

Early in a predictive plant-commissioning project, the first two control-account reports show CPI trending below 1.0 and a widening cost variance. The project manager wants to respond while the impact is still small. Which THREE actions are appropriate?

## Options

- **A**: Analyse the cost-variance drivers at control-account level to find where and why the overrun is occurring.
- **B**: Update the EAC forecast using an assumption that reflects whether the variance is one-off or systemic.
- **C**: Identify and evaluate corrective actions targeting the specific drivers, and route any that change the baseline through integrated change control.
- **D**: Brief the sponsor on the current variance and the forecast EAC before any corrective action has been agreed, so expectations are managed early.
- **E**: Apply the project's contingency reserve now to absorb the variance and keep the cost performance report within tolerance.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Responding to an early cost variance means diagnosing the drivers at the level the data exposes them (A), re-forecasting EAC on a justified one-off-versus-systemic assumption (B), and selecting targeted corrective actions while routing baseline-changing ones through change control (C). (D) is tempting because transparency is good, but reporting a forecast to the sponsor before the variance is even diagnosed front-runs the analysis — the PM first understands the cause and the realistic EAC (A and B), then communicates; D inverts that order. (E) misuses contingency: that reserve funds identified risk events that occur, not a general cost-efficiency shortfall, and drawing it to keep a report inside tolerance hides the performance signal rather than correcting the cause.
