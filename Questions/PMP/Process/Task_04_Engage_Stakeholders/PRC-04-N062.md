# Question

A predictive project widening a motorway past a small village issues the residents' association the same fortnightly technical progress report sent to the client and contractors. The association has high interest but no authority over the works. Attendance at its meetings has fallen and a local councillor relays that residents feel "talked at, not consulted". The project manager believes the reports are accurate and timely, and the contract requires fortnightly progress reporting to the client. What should the project manager do next?

## Options

- **A**: Meet the association to understand how they want to be consulted and informed, and revise the communication approach in the engagement plan to match.
- **B**: Add a plain-language summary at the top of the fortnightly report that anticipates residents' likely concerns and explains upcoming disruption.
- **C**: Introduce a separate monthly community forum for two-way discussion, while continuing the existing fortnightly report unchanged.
- **D**: Ask the councillor to gather the residents' specific concerns and pass them on, so the project can respond to the points raised.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The drop in attendance and the "talked at" feedback signal that the channel and tone do not fit this stakeholder, and the only option that finds out what consultation they actually want — rather than assuming the fix — is (A), which also treats the engagement plan as the live artifact. (B) is a genuine improvement and may well be part of the answer, but it presumes a one-way report is the right vehicle, which is precisely what is in doubt. (C) adds a sensible two-way channel but commits to a specific format before checking whether a monthly forum suits residents who may want something different. (D) routes consultation through an intermediary, which keeps the project from hearing residents directly and risks the councillor filtering or colouring the input. The contract governs client reporting and does not constrain how the project engages the community, so it is not a reason to leave the approach unchanged.
