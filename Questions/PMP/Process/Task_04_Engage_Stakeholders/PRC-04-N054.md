# Question

Late in planning a national broadband rollout, the PM realises that most major decisions are quietly shaped by a senior technical adviser who holds no formal project role but whom the sponsor consults privately before approving anything. The adviser has never attended a project meeting and is not in the stakeholder register. Decisions keep changing after they appear settled. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Add the adviser to the stakeholder register, assess their influence, and establish a direct engagement channel with them.
- **B**: Ask the sponsor to bring the adviser formally into the project's governance so their input is transparent.
- **C**: Continue working through the sponsor, since the adviser has chosen to remain outside the project structure.
- **D**: Pre-brief the adviser informally before key decisions so their concerns are addressed before they reach the sponsor.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A high-influence hidden stakeholder must first be recognised and assessed so the PM can engage them deliberately rather than be blindsided by their off-stage influence — registering and opening a direct channel (A) is the foundational, in-the-PM's-control step. (B) formalising their role depends on the sponsor and adviser agreeing and may not happen; it should not be a precondition to engaging. (C) continuing through the sponsor alone leaves the decision-churn unaddressed because you never reach the real influencer. (D) informal pre-briefing is a plausible tactic, but doing it before you have assessed the adviser's interests and influence is acting without analysis — the engagement approach should follow identification, not precede it.
