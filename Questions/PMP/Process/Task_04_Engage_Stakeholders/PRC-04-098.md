# Question

A newly formed agile team is starting a school timetabling product. During the first iteration planning, the team frames its stakeholders as the developers, the product owner, and the IT department, and begins shaping the backlog around them. The PM, listening, is not convinced this is the whole picture of who the timetables will affect. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Guide the team to work through who the timetables will actually affect and surface any stakeholders the current list has missed before engagement is planned.
- **B**: Let the backlog proceed with the present list, trusting that other affected groups will emerge and be added as increments are demonstrated.
- **C**: Ask the product owner to stand in for any end users not on the list, since the product owner's role is to represent user needs.
- **D**: Log the PM's concern as a risk and schedule a fuller stakeholder-identification pass at the next iteration review.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Identifying who is affected should be as complete as practical from the outset, and the PM has reason to think materially affected groups are missing; helping the team surface them now means engagement is planned around the real set of stakeholders (A). (B) leans on a genuine agile truth — stakeholders do emerge over time — but using that to defer an identification gap the PM can already see risks building the wrong thing and reworking it once those voices appear. (C) over-extends the product owner: one person cannot authentically carry the distinct interests of every external group, and assuming so simply reproduces the blind spot. (D) treats a gap that can be closed today as a future risk to revisit, delaying the very step that fixes it. The margin is between closing the identification gap now (A) and the defensible-sounding reasons to defer or delegate it (B, C, D).
