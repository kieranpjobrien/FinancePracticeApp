# Question

On an agile insurance-quoting platform, two business units fund the team jointly. The motor-insurance sponsor and the home-insurance sponsor each press the product owner to top-rank their features every sprint, and the backlog is reshuffled so often that little is shipping. Velocity is falling. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the two sponsors and the product owner together to agree a shared, transparent way of ranking work by value across the whole platform.
- **B**: Back the product owner in setting one firm sprint backlog and declining mid-sprint reprioritisation from either sponsor.
- **C**: Propose the sponsors alternate top priority sprint by sprint, so each business unit gets its features delivered in turn.
- **D**: Raise the funding split with the executives and explore separating the work into two streams to end the shared-backlog contention.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The thrashing comes from two powerful stakeholders pulling the backlog in opposite directions with no agreed basis for who wins; the durable fix is to get them and the product owner to a shared, value-based ranking they all accept, which is engagement and alignment work the PM is placed to lead (A). (B) is a defensible agile instinct — protect the sprint, let the product owner own the order — but a product owner holding the line against two funders without an agreed value basis simply relocates the conflict and erodes sponsor support. (C) alternation gives a predictable rhythm yet ignores relative business value and can starve the more valuable work every other sprint. (D) splitting the work may be warranted eventually, but it is a costly structural answer to a stakeholder-alignment problem and forfeits the shared-platform benefits — a last resort, not a first move. The margin is between resolving the misalignment at its source (A) and managing its symptoms through discipline, rotation, or restructuring (B, C, D).
