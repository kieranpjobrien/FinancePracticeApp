# Question

A predictive payroll-system replacement is meeting resistance from the payroll team. They have stopped attending demonstrations and respond slowly to data-validation requests, putting the cut-over at risk. In one-to-one conversations the PM has heard the team is less worried about the technology than about what the new system means for their roles. Which actions would most effectively improve this group's engagement? (Choose two.)

## Options

- **A**: Arrange for HR and the sponsor to set out directly how roles will be affected and what support is available after cut-over.
- **B**: Involve members of the payroll team in user-acceptance testing so they shape and own how the new system is adopted.
- **C**: Bring the cut-over forward to shorten the period of uncertainty the team is sitting in.
- **D**: Add focused training on the new system so the team feels competent and less threatened by it.
- **E**: Hold steady on the current plan and reassess the team's engagement after the next demonstration to see whether it recovers on its own.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

The resistance is rooted in fear about roles, so the two strongest levers address that fear at its source — HR and sponsor clarity on what happens to jobs and what support exists (A) — and convert the threatened group into co-owners by involving them in acceptance testing so the change is something they shape rather than have done to them (B). (D) is the most tempting distractor: training is genuinely valuable and reduces some anxiety, but the team has told the PM their worry is about role security, not competence, so training treats the wrong fear. (C) accelerating cut-over compresses the timeline without easing the underlying concern and is more likely to deepen it. (E) waiting to see whether engagement self-corrects cedes time on an at-risk cut-over while doing nothing about a known, addressable cause.
