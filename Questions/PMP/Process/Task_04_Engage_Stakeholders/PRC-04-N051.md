# Question

On a payroll-system upgrade, a department head attends every review, nods along, and says the project "sounds fine", but never volunteers staff, never promotes it in leadership forums, and takes no action to advance it. The PM's engagement plan records her desired level as supportive because her department's cooperation is needed for the pilot. The pilot is one month away. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Identify a specific, low-cost contribution the pilot needs from her department and ask her directly to commit to it.
- **B**: Record her current engagement as neutral and design a tailored approach to move her toward supportive.
- **C**: Continue the current communications, since she attends every review and voices no objection to the project.
- **D**: Escalate to the sponsor that her department's support for the pilot is not yet secured and ask for help.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

She is aware and present but takes no advancing action — neutral, not supportive — and with the pilot imminent the most effective move is to convert passive acquiescence into a concrete commitment by asking for a specific, achievable contribution (A), which both advances engagement and tests her true stance. (B) correctly diagnoses the gap but stops at planning an approach when the timeline calls for a direct ask now; classifying and theorising is a step behind acting. (C) reads attendance and the absence of objection as support, the classic neutral-versus-supportive trap, and changes nothing as the pilot approaches. (D) escalating before the PM has made a direct request skips the low-cost step the sponsor would expect to have been tried first.
