# Question

A finance-system replacement will automate reconciliation work currently done manually by a team of eight. Their manager, a long-tenured and well-respected stakeholder, has been quietly raising doubts about the new system's accuracy in corridor conversations, and adoption commitment among the wider finance group is softening. The PM needs this manager's active support. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Meet the manager privately to understand the specific concerns behind the doubts and what would make the system trustworthy to them.
- **B**: Arrange a controlled demonstration with real reconciliation data to show the system produces accurate results.
- **C**: Ask the sponsor to reaffirm the business case and the decision to proceed at the next finance all-hands meeting.
- **D**: Identify the manager's underlying interest — likely the team's job security — and propose how affected roles will be redeployed.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The manager's resistance may be about accuracy, job security, status, or something else entirely, and you cannot select the right intervention until you know which — so a private, exploratory conversation (A) comes first. (B) a data demonstration is powerful but presumes the objection is genuinely about accuracy; if the real driver is the team's future, a flawless demo changes nothing. (D) likewise commits to a job-security hypothesis before testing it. (C) a sponsor reaffirmation answers a resistant influencer with top-down authority, which often hardens rather than softens quiet opposition.
