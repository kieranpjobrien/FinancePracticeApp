# Question

An agile team building a mobile banking app holds sprint reviews where the team walks business stakeholders through the completed stories, the stakeholders nod along, and little substantive feedback surfaces — until features reach production, where change requests then spike. The product owner is satisfied because the reviews are well-attended and finish on time. The project manager wants the reviews to catch this feedback earlier. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Put the increment on real devices in the room and have stakeholders use it themselves and react during the review.
- **B**: Send stakeholders the working build a day before the review so they arrive having already tried it and formed feedback.
- **C**: Add a facilitated round where each stakeholder is asked in turn for their specific assessment of the increment against its acceptance criteria.
- **D**: Hold short individual sessions with each key stakeholder after the review to capture the feedback they did not raise in the room.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The change-request spike means stakeholders are not truly inspecting the increment until it is in production; the most direct fix is to put a working increment in their hands during the review so reactions surface there. (A) does this and restores the hands-on inspect-and-adapt the review exists for. (B) genuinely helps — pre-review access can deepen input — but a build tried alone the day before, without the team present to probe reactions, tends to yield scattered or unspoken feedback, which is the very gap here. (C) adds useful structure and accountability, yet asking people to assess what they have only watched still rests on passive observation rather than use. (D) will collect some feedback, but moving it out of the room sacrifices the shared, real-time discussion where stakeholders build on each other's reactions, and adds per-sprint overhead. The fine margin is that only (A) changes stakeholders from watching to doing inside the review.
