# Question

A wind-farm construction project has full regulatory approval and strong sponsor backing, but a local community group opposed to the development has begun organising public meetings and contacting local media. The group has no formal authority to stop the project, yet sponsor patience and the project's social licence could erode. The PM has not yet spoken with the group. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Meet the group to hear their specific concerns first-hand before deciding how the project will respond publicly.
- **B**: Hold an open public forum to present the project's impact assessments and mitigations and answer the group's questions.
- **C**: Issue a factual public statement and FAQ rebutting the group's claims, coordinated with the sponsor's corporate affairs team.
- **D**: Commission a community-impact study and offer to share its findings with the group once it is complete.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The PM has not yet heard what the group actually objects to — it could be turbine noise, land access, visual impact, or process grievance — and the engagement response should follow that understanding, so a direct listening conversation comes first (A). (B) a public forum is genuine two-way engagement and may well be the next step, but staging it before you understand the concerns risks a confrontational set-piece that hardens positions rather than defusing them. (C) a public rebuttal, even if factual, leads with broadcasting the project's message before listening and can escalate the dispute through the media the group is already courting. (D) commissioning a study is constructive but defers any engagement for weeks while opposition mobilises, and offering findings later still substitutes information for dialogue.
