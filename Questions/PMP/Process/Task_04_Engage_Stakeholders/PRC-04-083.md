# Question

A manufacturing firm is deploying predictive-maintenance software that will change how floor technicians schedule their work. A vocal group of senior technicians is resisting, fearing the system devalues their judgement and threatens their roles. Their cooperation is essential for accurate data input. Which engagement actions are most appropriate for the project manager to pursue? (Select three.)

## Options

- **A**: Hold sessions where senior technicians shape how the system incorporates their expertise and surface their specific concerns.
- **B**: Involve respected senior technicians as pilot participants whose feedback visibly influences the configuration.
- **C**: Communicate transparently how the system is intended to support, not replace, technician judgement, with concrete examples.
- **D**: Tie continued shift assignments to demonstrated adoption of the new scheduling workflow to ensure compliance.
- **E**: Limit detailed system briefings to supervisors and roll the change down through the management line to avoid amplifying dissent.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Resistance grounded in fear of devaluation is best converted by involving the resisters, giving them genuine influence, and communicating honestly, all of which build ownership and trust. (A), (B), and (C) move the technicians along the engagement spectrum through participation and transparency. (D) coercing adoption by threatening shift assignments may force surface compliance but deepens resentment and risks deliberately poor data input from the very people whose cooperation is essential. (E) restricting information and pushing the change down the management line treats engagement as something to suppress, which typically hardens resistance and breeds distrust.
