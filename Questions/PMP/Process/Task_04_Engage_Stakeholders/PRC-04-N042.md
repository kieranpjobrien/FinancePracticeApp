# Question

On a scaled agile programme, two business sponsors fund a shared platform team. Each sponsor insists their features take priority in the next three sprints, and the single product owner is now privately accepting verbal commitments from both, leading to a backlog the team cannot possibly complete. The team is starting to disengage. What is the best action for the project manager?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate a joint session where both sponsors and the product owner agree a single prioritisation method and a transparent, ordered backlog.
- **B**: Escalate to the programme steering committee to rule on which sponsor's features take precedence over the coming quarter.
- **C**: Coach the product owner to stop making side commitments and to own a single ordered backlog visible to both sponsors.
- **D**: Have the team forecast realistic capacity for the three sprints so the sponsors can see only a finite amount of work fits.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The root problem is two funders competing through a product owner who is making incompatible promises; the durable fix is a shared, transparent prioritisation mechanism the sponsors themselves buy into, which (A) delivers. (C) addresses the PO's behaviour but leaves the sponsors' conflict unresolved, so the pressure simply returns. (D) supplies useful data but capacity alone does not decide whose work wins — it can even harden each sponsor's demand. (B) escalating removes the sponsors' ownership of a decision they are best placed to make collaboratively and should be a fallback only if facilitation fails.
