# Question

An agile team developing a logistics tracking app holds well-attended sprint reviews where the team presents polished demos. Key business stakeholders rarely say much in the session, then raise significant concerns weeks later that force rework. The PM wants the reviews to genuinely engage stakeholders and surface feedback while it is still cheap to act on. What is the most effective change?

## Options

- **A**: Have stakeholders work hands-on with the increment and put specific decisions and questions to them during the review.
- **B**: Send the agenda and the features to be shown a day ahead so stakeholders come prepared to comment.
- **C**: Record each review and share it so stakeholders who need more time can give feedback afterwards.
- **D**: Bring more stakeholders into each review to increase the range and volume of feedback gathered.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The concerns surface late because the format lets stakeholders sit back and watch; getting them to handle the working increment themselves and answer pointed questions turns the review into the two-way feedback loop it is meant to be, drawing out reactions in the moment when changes are cheapest (A). (B) preparation is a reasonable aid and may lift quality at the margins, but a presentation people arrive prepared for is still a presentation they can watch passively. (C) recording extends the feedback window in exactly the delayed way that is already causing the rework, rather than capturing reactions live. (D) more attendees without changing the format simply scales the same weak engagement and tends to make the session less focused, not more useful. The margin is between changing how stakeholders participate (A) and tweaking the inputs, timing, or audience around an unchanged passive format (B, C, D).
