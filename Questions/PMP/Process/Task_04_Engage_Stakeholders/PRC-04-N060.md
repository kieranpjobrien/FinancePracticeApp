# Question

On a city-centre transport upgrade, the government transport minister controls the project's funding but has shown little day-to-day interest, delegating attendance to junior officials and rarely reading updates. The PM has just learned that a rival department is lobbying the minister to redirect part of the budget. The funding decision is still weeks away, and the minister remains the single most powerful stakeholder. How should the PM engage the minister?

## Options

- **A**: Secure a short, senior-level briefing focused only on the project's strategic value and the funding risk, timed before the budget decision.
- **B**: Add the minister to the routine distribution for detailed progress reports so they have full visibility ahead of the decision.
- **C**: Continue engaging through the minister's officials, trusting them to relay the funding risk upward at the right moment.
- **D**: Request the minister's regular attendance at project review meetings to build their interest and ownership before the decision.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A high-power, low-interest stakeholder should be kept satisfied with concise, strategically pitched contact rather than drowned in operational detail — and with a funding threat emerging, the right move is a targeted senior briefing on value and risk, timed to the decision (A). (B) routing detailed progress reports to a stakeholder who already ignores updates mistakes volume for influence and is likely to go unread at the moment it matters most. (C) relying on officials to escalate a funding threat leaves the project's most consequential decision to second-hand relay and a timing the PM does not control. (D) trying to manufacture day-to-day interest by demanding meeting attendance over-engages a stakeholder whose interest is inherently low and risks irritating the very person whose goodwill secures the budget.
