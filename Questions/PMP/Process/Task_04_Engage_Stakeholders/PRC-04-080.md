# Question

A government-funded transport project depends on a third-party signalling vendor whose project director has become hard to reach, missing two integration coordination calls and replying slowly to queries. The vendor's work is on the critical path. The contract is managed by the procurement department, not the project manager. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Speak directly with the vendor's project director to re-establish the working relationship and understand what is driving the disengagement.
- **B**: Ask procurement to issue a formal notice citing the missed obligations under the contract's communication clauses.
- **C**: Escalate to the project sponsor that the vendor's responsiveness now threatens the critical path and request intervention.
- **D**: Reschedule the integration coordination calls to times the vendor director has historically attended, to improve turnout.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Disengagement from a critical-path partner is first a relationship problem, and the engaging response is to make direct contact to understand the cause before invoking contractual or escalation machinery. (A) re-opens the channel and may reveal a fixable issue such as competing priorities or an internal vendor problem. (B) a formal contractual notice through procurement is adversarial and premature before a conversation, and can sour a relationship you still depend on. (C) sponsor escalation may follow if direct engagement fails, but jumping to it first skips the lighter-touch step. (D) rescheduling treats a possible symptom without learning whether timing is even the real cause.
