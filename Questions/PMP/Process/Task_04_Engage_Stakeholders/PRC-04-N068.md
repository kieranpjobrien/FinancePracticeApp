# Question

Midway through a hybrid pharmaceutical manufacturing-line upgrade, a national regulator announces it will now require pre-approval of any change to validated production equipment — a rule that did not previously apply, so the project never identified the regulator as a stakeholder. The change control board has already approved several equipment modifications now in progress. As project manager, which three actions are appropriate? (Choose three.)

## Options

- **A**: Add the regulator to the stakeholder register and analyse its interest, influence, and the engagement level the project now needs from it.
- **B**: Engage the regulator to clarify the pre-approval process, timelines, and evidence required, and feed that into replanning the affected work.
- **C**: Assess the in-progress modifications against the new rule and pause any that now require pre-approval until the regulator's position is known.
- **D**: Take the new requirement to the change control board as a change request and let the board decide whether the project will comply with it.
- **E**: Hold all equipment modifications, approved or not, until the regulator has reviewed and signed off the project's entire change backlog.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

A newly relevant regulator is a newly identified key stakeholder: bring it into stakeholder management (A), engage to understand its requirements (B), and reassess the in-flight work it now affects, pausing what is exposed (C). (D) misframes the situation — an external legal requirement is not a discretionary change the board can vote to accept or reject; the board governs how the project responds and replans, but compliance itself is not optional, so treating it as a normal change request is the error. (E) over-reacts in the opposite direction: freezing every modification, including those the regulator may not even cover, and demanding sign-off on the whole backlog needlessly stalls the line and presumes a scope of regulatory review that has not been established. The trap is that (D) and (E) both sound like governance discipline, but one under-responds to a mandatory rule and the other over-responds beyond what the rule requires.
