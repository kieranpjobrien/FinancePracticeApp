# Question

On a renewable-energy grid project, a regional landowner association has moved from unaware to resistant after learning the transmission route may cross grazing land. The route itself cannot change, but the project can adjust tower siting, access timing, and compensation within the corridor. The engagement matrix records the desired level for the association as "neutral" — the project does not need their advocacy, only that they stop actively opposing. The association's opposition is already generating local press and the next consenting submission is in three weeks. The community-liaison lead asks the project manager how to direct the response. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Open a dialogue to understand the association's specific objections and adjust siting, timing, and compensation where feasible, so the active opposition has less to fuel it.
- **B**: Commission a benefits package — community fund, local hiring, site visits — aimed at building the association into advocates who will speak for the project at the consenting hearing.
- **C**: Publish a clear account of what on the route is fixed and what remains open to influence, so the association engages on the decisions it can actually affect.
- **D**: Bring the association's representatives into the project's siting and access-planning sessions as participants, giving them a standing role in shaping the within-corridor decisions.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The desired state is neutral, not supportive, so the task is to defuse active opposition using the levers the project genuinely controls — which is exactly what (A) does by surfacing the real objections and adjusting siting, timing, and compensation where it can. (B) over-shoots the recorded target: a full advocacy-building package invests effort the matrix says is not required, and converting forced-route resistance into public advocacy at a hearing is unlikely to land regardless of spend. (C) is a sound and honest move — clarifying fixed-versus-open scope is part of good engagement and may well precede (A) — but on its own it informs without engaging the objections, and "here is what you cannot change" leading the message tends to entrench resistance rather than soften it. (D) embeds the association in decision-making, which would suit a stakeholder the project wanted as a collaborative partner, but granting a standing co-design role to reach mere neutrality over-invests in the relationship and risks ceding influence over corridor decisions the project must own. The fine margin is matching the depth of engagement to the desired level: enough dialogue and adjustment to neutralise opposition, neither manufacturing advocacy (B) nor institutionalising co-decision (D), and going beyond one-way clarification (C) to actually engage the objections.
