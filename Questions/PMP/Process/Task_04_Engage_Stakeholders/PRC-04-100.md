# Question

A distributed agile team spans three time zones. The product owner and most stakeholders sit in the headquarters zone, where sprint reviews are fixed at 10:00. A key operations stakeholder nine hours behind joins near midnight her time, contributes little, and has begun declining the invitations. Her acceptance of operational readiness is required before each release. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Change how the team engages her so she can take part on fair terms, agreeing the arrangement with her.
- **B**: Have the product owner send her the review outcomes and collect her readiness sign-off by email afterwards.
- **C**: Ask her to nominate a delegate in her own time zone to attend the reviews and hold the readiness decision on her behalf.
- **D**: Keep the current schedule that suits the majority and treat her non-attendance as tacit acceptance unless she objects.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A key stakeholder is being effectively shut out by a meeting time that ignores her zone, and the fix is for the team to adapt its own practice so she can participate meaningfully — on terms agreed with her rather than imposed (A). (B) keeps her informed but reduces her to an after-the-fact signatory, which invites rubber-stamped readiness sign-offs on a release gate and is the opposite of genuine engagement. (C) is a plausible accommodation, but it pushes the burden of a problem the team created onto her and risks the readiness decision being made by someone less informed than the stakeholder who actually owns it. (D) optimises for the majority and reads silence as consent, the classic error that lets a gate-holder's concerns surface only when a release is blocked. The margin is between the team adapting to include her (A) and shifting the inconvenience or the decision onto her or onto silence (B, C, D).
