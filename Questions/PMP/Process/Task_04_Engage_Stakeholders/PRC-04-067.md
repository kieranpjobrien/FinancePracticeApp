# Question

At a steering committee, one of six members consistently disagrees with majority decisions but always acquiesces in the formal minutes. The PM senses the disagreements are substantive but unvoiced. The chair prefers consensus-style minutes. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Leave the minute-taking convention as the chair prefers, since consensus framing helps maintain steering committee cohesion over the programme.
- **B**: Approach the dissenting member privately to understand their underlying concerns before any procedural changes to minuting are raised here.
- **C**: Raise dissent documentation with the chair, proposing that formal minutes capture substantive concerns voiced during each committee meeting.
- **D**: Propose the chair allow explicit captured dissent in minutes, and check privately with the member to understand their substantive concerns.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

PMI-aligned governance values honest minutes; the PM should address the minute-taking convention and the person's concerns in parallel. (D) does both. (A) quiet dissent in "consensus" minutes produces poor decisions and damages governance trust. (B) private conversation alone doesn't fix the structural minute-taking convention. (C) changing procedure without understanding the person's concerns misses part of the picture.
