# Question

A regional water utility is upgrading its billing platform. A consumer advocacy group has low formal authority over the project but commands significant media attention and has publicly criticised the utility before. An internal finance director controls the budget but has shown little interest in day-to-day progress so far. The project manager has limited bandwidth for proactive engagement. Where should the PM concentrate tailored engagement effort?

## Options

- **A**: The finance director, because budget authority makes this stakeholder's continued support essential and disengagement there poses the greatest delivery threat.
- **B**: The advocacy group, monitoring the finance director, because a low-authority but high-influence external party can rapidly escalate into a reputational risk if left uninformed.
- **C**: Both equally, splitting available engagement time so neither high-power nor high-influence stakeholder feels neglected during the upgrade.
- **D**: The finance director through proactive engagement and the advocacy group through formal published progress notices, satisfying both without bespoke effort.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A grid that weighs only formal power misses that influence and the ability to mobilise public attention can matter more than authority lines, especially for a publicly scrutinised utility. The advocacy group is low-power but high-influence and has a track record of escalation, so it warrants active, tailored engagement, while the currently disengaged but budget-holding finance director should be kept satisfied and monitored. (B) reflects this dual read. (A) over-indexes on formal authority and ignores the asymmetric reputational risk. (C) spreading effort equally ignores that the analysis points to differentiated treatment. (D) published notices are one-way and unlikely to manage a hostile, attention-seeking party who needs genuine dialogue.
