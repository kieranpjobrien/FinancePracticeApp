# Question

Midway through delivery of a mobile banking feature, the agile team's sprint reviews are consistently attended by the same two product-side users, while the operations and compliance representatives who raised concerns at kick-off have stopped coming. The product owner says the absent groups "trust us to handle it." The next release touches a process those groups own. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Accept the product owner's read of the situation, since the product owner holds accountability for stakeholder representation and has working relationships with those groups.
- **B**: Reach out to the operations and compliance representatives directly to understand why their attendance lapsed and what would make the reviews valuable to them.
- **C**: Make sprint review attendance mandatory for all originally identified stakeholders and ask their managers to enforce it for the remaining releases.
- **D**: Record the disengagement as a risk in the register and proceed, planning to re-engage those groups when the release that affects them is closer to deployment.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Declining attendance from stakeholders whose process the next release touches is a live engagement signal, and the engaging response is to investigate the cause and restore two-way value, not to assume or coerce. (B) treats the symptom by understanding the root cause and adapting the engagement, which fits the PM's facilitation role even with a product owner present. (A) accepts a second-hand assumption about stakeholders who explicitly raised concerns earlier. (C) mandating attendance addresses the symptom with authority and breeds resentment without fixing why the reviews stopped delivering value. (D) deferring re-engagement risks the affected groups discovering an unworkable change late, which is exactly what early engagement is meant to prevent.
