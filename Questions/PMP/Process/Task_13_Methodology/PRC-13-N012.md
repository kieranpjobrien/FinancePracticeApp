# Question

Three sprints into an "agile" web project, velocity is collapsing. Investigation shows the requirements are actually fixed and fully specified in a signed statement of work, the customer refuses to engage between milestones, and every change must go through a formal change board. The team adopted Scrum because it was fashionable internally. The project manager must decide how to proceed. What is the BEST course of action?

## Options

- **A**: Keep Scrum but add more detailed up-front requirements workshops so the backlog is fully defined before each sprint.
- **B**: Escalate to the customer and insist they provide a continuously available product owner so the chosen agile approach can work as intended.
- **C**: Recommend shifting to a predictive approach, because the project's actual characteristics — fixed scope, disengaged customer, formal change control — do not suit agile.
- **D**: Persevere with Scrum for two more sprints to give the team time to mature before judging whether the method fits.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Methodology must match reality: fixed signed scope, an unavailable customer, and gated change control all contradict the conditions agile needs, so the honest move is to realign the approach to the project. (C) fixes the root mismatch rather than the symptoms. (A) bolts predictive practices onto an agile frame, producing "fake agile" overhead without the benefits. (B) tries to force the environment to fit a method that was chosen for the wrong reasons, when the constraints are largely contractual and outside the PM's control. (D) spends more time and money on an approach already shown to be misaligned, hoping maturity will overcome a structural mismatch.
