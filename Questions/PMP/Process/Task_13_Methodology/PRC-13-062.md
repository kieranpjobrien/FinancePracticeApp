# Question

A Scrum team building a retail mobile app runs two-week sprints and demonstrates working increments at each review. The single integration partner — a payments gateway provider — will only accept and certify new API integrations on the first business day of each calendar month, a window fixed by their own change-freeze policy and not negotiable. The team keeps finishing payment-related stories mid-sprint, then waiting weeks to integrate, and value sits undelivered. The product owner is frustrated that completed work is not reaching users, and a senior developer argues the team's two-week rhythm is the problem. What is the BEST adjustment?

## Options

- **A**: Extend the team's sprint length to one month so each sprint boundary lands on the partner's certification window.
- **B**: Keep two-week sprints for development and cadence, and schedule a separate monthly integration-and-release event aligned to the partner's fixed window.
- **C**: Abandon fixed-length sprints and move the team to a continuous-flow approach so payment stories release the moment they are certified.
- **D**: Hold all payment stories in the backlog until the sprint immediately before the certification date, so completed work never waits.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A core tailoring principle is that the team's development cadence and an external release cadence need not be the same; you decouple them rather than distort one to fit the other. (B) preserves the short feedback loop the team values while creating a dedicated release event on the partner's immovable date. (A) stretches the sprint to a month purely to align boundaries, lengthening every feedback loop and weakening the empirical control the team relies on — a common but costly over-correction. (C) switching to flow does not help, because the constraint is a once-monthly external gate, not the team's internal batching, so certified work would still wait. (D) deliberately delays starting payment stories, idling capacity and front-loading risk into one sprint to avoid the appearance of waiting. The discriminator is separating internal cadence from the external release window, not reshaping the team's rhythm around the gateway.
