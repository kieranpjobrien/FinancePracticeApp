# Question

An IT operations team handles infrastructure change requests that arrive continuously, vary widely in size and urgency, and get reprioritised through the day as incidents break. Work rarely sits still long enough to plan a fixed block of it in advance, and the team is often blocked waiting on one overloaded specialist while other requests pile up behind. The team's new lead wants a structured way of working that lifts throughput and surfaces where the work is getting stuck. Which approach fits this work best?

## Options

- **A**: Scrum with two-week sprints and a committed sprint backlog set at the start of each iteration.
- **B**: A Kanban system for the team's request flow.
- **C**: A predictive plan with a baselined schedule built from the year's anticipated change volume.
- **D**: Scrumban that begins with the full set of Scrum ceremonies and relaxes them once the team matures.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Demand that is continuous, variable, and reprioritised daily — with no body of work stable enough to commit to for an iteration, and a visible bottleneck on one specialist — is the canonical fit for Kanban, which manages flow and exposes blockages through a visualised workflow and work-in-progress limits (B). (A) requires committing a sprint backlog the team cannot hold stable for two weeks, so the commitment is broken almost daily and the ceremony becomes disruption. (C) presumes a foreseeable, schedulable volume of work, but genuinely unpredictable incident-driven requests cannot be baselined. (D) front-loads the heavier Scrum ceremonies that the same instability makes a poor fit, adding ritual before it adds value; starting from flow management is the lighter, better-matched move.
