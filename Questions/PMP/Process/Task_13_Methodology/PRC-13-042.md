# Question

A four-person team delivering an internal analytics tool has adopted Scrum by the book. After three sprints, the developers complain that daily stand-ups, sprint planning, a sprint review, and a retrospective each fortnight consume time that could be spent building, given how small and co-located the team is. The product owner agrees value is being delivered but wants the overhead reduced. What is the project manager's most appropriate response?

## Options

- **A**: Keep all events unchanged; reducing Scrum ceremonies breaks the framework and undermines its benefits.
- **B**: Work with the team to tailor the cadence and length of events to the team's size and context while preserving the inspect-and-adapt intent.
- **C**: Drop the retrospective, since a small co-located team can raise improvement points informally at any time.
- **D**: Switch the team to a purely predictive plan to remove agile ceremonies altogether.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Tailoring adapts a method to the team's actual context rather than applying it mechanically or abandoning it. (B) keeps the inspect-and-adapt purpose intact while right-sizing the events for a tiny co-located team, which is exactly what tailoring means. (A) treats the framework as immutable, the opposite of tailoring. (C) singles out the one event most directly tied to continuous improvement and removes it outright, losing a deliberate feedback mechanism rather than streamlining it. (D) discards an approach that the product owner confirms is delivering value, an overcorrection driven by ceremony overhead alone.
