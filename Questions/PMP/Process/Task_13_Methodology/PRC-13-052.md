# Question

A manufacturing firm wants its first-ever agile project to deliver a shop-floor scheduling tool. The team has never worked in an iterative way, no one has filled a Scrum Master or product owner role before, and management has read that agile delivers faster. The work itself does suit an adaptive approach. Considering the team's lack of experience, what should the project manager do to set the approach up for success?

## Options

- **A**: Adopt full Scrum immediately across all roles, because the work suits it and the team will learn fastest by doing everything at once.
- **B**: Start with a simple, well-supported agile approach plus coaching, and add practices as the team builds capability.
- **C**: Stay predictive for this project and revisit agile only once the team has been formally certified.
- **D**: Hire an entirely new agile-experienced team and reassign the current staff elsewhere.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The work suits agile, so abandoning it is wrong, but a team new to iterative delivery needs a graduated, supported start rather than the full framework on day one. (B) introduces a simple approach with coaching and grows the practice set as competence develops, managing the adoption risk. (A) overwhelms an inexperienced team by demanding mastery of every role and ceremony at once, a common cause of failed first attempts. (C) discards a fit-for-purpose approach over a learning curve that coaching can address, and certification is not a prerequisite for starting. (D) is a drastic, costly overreaction that throws away existing domain knowledge to solve a capability gap that mentoring would close.
