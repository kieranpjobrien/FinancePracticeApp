# Question

A project manager has just been assigned to a cross-functional initiative and must decide on the delivery approach. The organisation runs both predictive and agile projects and maintains a tailoring guide in its process assets. The project manager has read the charter, which is high level, and has not yet met the delivery team or examined the deliverables in detail. What should the project manager do FIRST when selecting the approach?

## Options

- **A**: Apply the organisation's tailoring guide to the initiative as described in the charter, and record the approach it indicates.
- **B**: Examine the initiative's own characteristics — requirements stability, technical risk, stakeholder needs and constraints — before mapping them through the tailoring guide.
- **C**: Convene the cross-functional team and key stakeholders to agree the approach together, so they are committed to the way of working from the outset.
- **D**: Review how comparable past initiatives in the organisation were delivered and what their retrospectives recorded about the approach.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A tailoring guide is only as good as the inputs fed into it; the first step is to understand the actual characteristics of this initiative so the guide is applied to real evidence rather than to the charter's thin summary. (B) does exactly that — gather requirements stability, risk, stakeholder needs and constraints, then map them through the guide. (A) is the strongest distractor and is nearly right, but applying the guide to a high-level charter before examining the work risks tailoring to an incomplete picture; the assessment of characteristics precedes running them through the guide. (C) engaging the team matters and will follow, but agreeing an approach by consensus before anyone has analysed the work invites preference-led choices rather than fit-led ones. (D) historical analogues are a useful input, yet past projects may differ in the very characteristics that drive the decision, so they inform rather than determine the first step. The discriminator is that understanding this initiative's characteristics must come before applying any guide, consensus or analogue — all of which are real early actions, but each depends on that assessment first.
