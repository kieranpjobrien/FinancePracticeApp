# Question

A government agency is digitising a paper licensing service that already runs to a settled set of rules; the agency knows in detail what the digital service must ultimately do. The sponsor is under political pressure to put working functionality in front of the public within months rather than after a single launch two years away, and is willing to release the service one capability at a time. The technical team is comfortable with either predictive or agile ways of working. Which lifecycle should the project manager choose?

## Options

- **A**: A predictive lifecycle with a single delivery at the end.
- **B**: An incremental lifecycle releasing successive usable parts of the licensing service.
- **C**: An iterative lifecycle that reworks one evolving prototype until it is accepted.
- **D**: An adaptive lifecycle driven by a continuously reprioritised backlog.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The decisive combination is scope the agency can already specify in full *and* a strong demand to put usable functionality in front of the public early and repeatedly — which is what an incremental lifecycle delivers by releasing known scope one usable part at a time (B). (A) honours the stable, well-defined scope and would be a perfectly orthodox choice if there were no early-delivery pressure — but it forces the public to wait for a single end launch, which is the constraint the sponsor cannot accept. (C) earns its keep when the solution itself is unclear and has to be refined through repeated reworking; here the requirements are settled, so iterating on a prototype solves a problem this project does not have. (D) is built for high requirements volatility and continuous reprioritisation, neither of which applies to a stable, rules-based service.
