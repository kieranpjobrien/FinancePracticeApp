# Question

A museum is commissioning a single large interactive exhibit. The overall budget and the opening date are fixed, and only the complete installation will be handed to the public — there is no value in releasing parts of it early. However, the curators are unsure what visitor experience will actually work; they expect to refine the concept substantially as they test rough versions with focus groups. The same internal team will build whatever is decided. Which lifecycle should the project manager choose?

## Options

- **A**: An incremental lifecycle for the exhibit.
- **B**: An iterative lifecycle for the exhibit.
- **C**: A predictive lifecycle, fixing the experience design up front and building it once against a detailed baseline.
- **D**: An adaptive lifecycle working from a continuously reprioritised backlog of independently shippable features.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Two facts decide it: the solution is unclear and must be refined through repeated testing with focus groups, and the exhibit is handed over once as a whole with no value in releasing parts early. Iterative delivery — repeatedly building and reworking one evolving version until the experience is right — fits both (B). (A) is the deliberate trap, and the iterative/incremental distinction is where this question bites: incremental also delivers in steps, but each step is a *usable portion of known scope* released for value sooner — and the scenario rules out both early release and known scope, so incremental is the wrong half of the pair. (C) locks in an experience design the curators openly say they cannot yet specify, discarding the learning they need. (D) presumes independently shippable features and continuous reprioritisation, neither of which fits a single integrated exhibit delivered once against a fixed budget and date.
