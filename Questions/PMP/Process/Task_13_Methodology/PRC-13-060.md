# Question

A project manager is assessing a national rail-signalling upgrade and is weighing whether a predictive approach is the right fit. Which characteristics of this project genuinely support choosing a predictive approach? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: The technical requirements are well understood and very unlikely to change once the safety case is approved.
- **B**: Regulatory and safety obligations require comprehensive up-front documentation and approval before deployment.
- **C**: The project team would prefer to avoid the discipline of detailed planning.
- **D**: The interdependencies and sequence of works are largely known and can be planned in detail in advance.
- **E**: The customer wants to change priorities every few weeks and review partial functionality continuously.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

Predictive delivery suits stable, well-understood requirements (A), heavy regulatory documentation and up-front approval needs (B), and work whose dependencies and sequence can be planned in detail in advance (D), all of which describe this signalling upgrade. (C) is not a selection criterion at all; a team's wish to avoid planning is a reason to coach, not a basis for choosing an approach, and predictive work in fact demands rigorous planning. (E) describes volatile priorities and continuous partial review, which are adaptive drivers that argue against predictive, not for it. The fit turns on stability, governance, and plannability, not on preference or appetite for change.
