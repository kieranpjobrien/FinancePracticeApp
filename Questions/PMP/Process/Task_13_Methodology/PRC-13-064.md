# Question

A three-person, co-located analytics team has adopted Scrum by the book: daily stand-up, sprint planning, backlog refinement, sprint review and retrospective, all at the textbook durations. Because the team sits at the same desk and talks constantly, the daily stand-up and much of formal refinement repeat conversations the members have already had, and two of the three have started skipping them. Throughput and quality have held steady all quarter. At the last retrospective the team asked the project manager to "stop running events for the sake of it". What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Keep all ceremonies at full duration to preserve the integrity of the framework and the discipline it brings.
- **B**: Drop the retrospective along with the daily stand-up, since a small high-performing team needs little formal process of any kind.
- **C**: Work with the team to streamline the low-value ceremonies for their context while retaining the events that still provide inspect-and-adapt value, then review the effect.
- **D**: Switch the team to a continuous-flow board and remove all time-boxed events, since fixed iterations no longer suit such a small team.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Tailoring means adjusting practices to the team's context while preserving the events that still deliver value, and doing so collaboratively and reversibly. (C) trims the ceremonies that merely restate shared knowledge while keeping inspect-and-adapt mechanisms, and checks the result before locking it in. (A) treats the framework as sacrosanct, the very "process over people" stance the situation warns against. (B) over-corrects by discarding the retrospective — the one event that drives continuous improvement and is rarely redundant even for a tight team. (D) is a wholesale method change justified only by team size, abandoning iterations that are not actually the source of the friction. The discriminator is selectively right-sizing low-value ceremonies, not all-or-nothing removal nor a needless switch of method.
