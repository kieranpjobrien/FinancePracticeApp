# Question

A single co-located team of seven is starting its first software product. Requirements are uncertain and will be refined through frequent stakeholder feedback, the customer can attend regular reviews, and there are no cross-team dependencies. Work arrives in reasonably plannable chunks rather than as a continuous unpredictable stream, and the team has no prior agile experience. Which framework is the most sensible starting point?

## Options

- **A**: Basic Scrum, giving the inexperienced team a defined cadence of planning, review, and retrospective around an engaged customer.
- **B**: Kanban, visualising the workflow and limiting work in progress so the team manages flow without committing to fixed iterations.
- **C**: Extreme Programming, adopting test-driven development, pair programming, and continuous integration from the first iteration.
- **D**: Scrum combined with the full set of XP engineering practices in place from sprint one, so quality discipline is built in from the start.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

For one small team new to agile, with refinable requirements, an engaged customer, and plannable batches of work, basic Scrum offers the simplest fit-for-purpose structure and a feedback rhythm the team can learn quickly. (A) matches the facts. (B) suits continuous, unpredictable flow with no natural iteration commitment, but the scenario states work arrives in plannable chunks and the customer attends regular reviews, so Scrum's cadence fits better. (C) brings valuable engineering practices but, on its own, gives an inexperienced team little of the planning-and-review rhythm it needs to coordinate with the customer. (D) layers a heavy set of new engineering disciplines on top of an unfamiliar process all at once, risking overload before the team has built basic habits — sound to adopt those practices later, not all on day one.
