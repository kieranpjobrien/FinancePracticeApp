# Question

A managed-services team handles a continuous stream of customer change requests of widely varying size, arriving unpredictably with no natural sprint boundary. The team currently forces the work into two-week sprints, but requests routinely arrive mid-sprint as urgent, disrupting commitments. The project manager wants a practice that improves flow and limits work in progress without pretending the demand is batchable. Which approach should the project manager introduce?

## Options

- **A**: Keep Scrum but shorten sprints to one week so urgent requests wait less time before the next planning event.
- **B**: Adopt a Kanban approach with work-in-progress limits and continuous pull, since the demand is a continuous, variable flow.
- **C**: Move to a predictive schedule, sequencing all known requests into a single plan with fixed dates.
- **D**: Keep two-week sprints but add a separate emergency team to absorb anything urgent.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Continuous, unpredictable, variable-sized demand with no natural batch boundary is the textbook case for Kanban, which manages flow with WIP limits and pull rather than fixed iterations. (B) matches the nature of the work. (A) keeps the iteration model that the demand pattern keeps breaking; shorter sprints reduce but do not remove the mismatch. (C) imposes fixed dates on work that arrives unpredictably and cannot be fully known in advance. (D) treats the symptom by quarantining urgent items into a second team, adding cost and coordination while leaving the core flow problem unaddressed.
