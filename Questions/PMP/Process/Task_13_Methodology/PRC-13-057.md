# Question

A retailer plans a project to rebuild its e-commerce checkout. Requirements are expected to evolve as A/B tests reveal customer behaviour, and the business wants to release improvements continuously. The delivery team, however, is split across three time zones with little overlap, and the organisation has weak collaboration tooling. The project manager believes the work suits an adaptive approach. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Reject the adaptive approach, because agile cannot work with a distributed team across three time zones.
- **B**: Proceed with an adaptive approach and address the distribution by establishing collaboration tooling, overlap hours, and clear communication practices.
- **C**: Choose a predictive approach instead, since the team's distribution is a bigger constraint than the requirements volatility.
- **D**: Co-locate the entire team permanently before starting any work.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The nature of the work, evolving requirements and continuous release, points clearly to an adaptive approach, and distribution is an impediment to be managed, not a reason to abandon the right method. (B) keeps the fit-for-purpose approach and tackles the real obstacle with tooling, overlap windows, and communication practices. (A) treats distributed agile as impossible, which is false; many adaptive teams work across time zones. (C) lets a manageable constraint override the dominant driver of requirements volatility, producing an ill-fitting predictive plan. (D) proposes an expensive, often infeasible reorganisation when targeted collaboration measures would suffice.
