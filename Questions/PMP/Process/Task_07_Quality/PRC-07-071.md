# Question

A pharmaceutical packaging line is meeting its quality targets: every sampled unit passes the final dimensional and label checks, and the reject rate is within plan. While walking the floor, the project manager sees operators making the same manual machine adjustment partway through each run, on every shift, to hold output inside tolerance. Output is conforming and the schedule is on track. The project manager has limited time before the next phase gate. Where should the effort go first?

## Options

- **A**: Investigate why the same mid-run adjustment is needed every shift and aim to correct the underlying process, even though current output is conforming.
- **B**: Leave the line as it is and document the adjustment as a standard operating step, since the operators have found a reliable way to keep output in tolerance.
- **C**: Tighten the sampling plan to inspect more units per run, so any drift caused by the adjustments is caught earlier at final check.
- **D**: Standardise the timing and size of the operator adjustment and train all shifts to apply it identically, removing the shift-to-shift variability.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The output is good, so every option can claim to "protect quality" — the discrimination is whether to treat the recurring intervention as a fact of life or a symptom. A correction that is needed identically every run is evidence the process is not naturally holding tolerance and is being propped up by manual rescue; quality assurance looks past conforming output to whether the process can reliably produce it, so the first effort is to find and remove the cause. (A) is correct. (B) codifies the workaround as permanent, masking the instability and leaving the line one missed adjustment away from defects. (C) adds inspection — detection, not prevention — and does nothing about why the adjustment is required. (D) is the subtle trap: standardising the workaround reduces variability between shifts but still entrenches a process that cannot run without intervention, treating the symptom rather than the cause.
