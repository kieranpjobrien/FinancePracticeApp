# Question

A data-warehouse project is in planning. The deliverable descriptions agreed so far say the loaded data must be "accurate" and the loads "timely". On the manager's last project a similar deliverable was disputed at acceptance, and a business stakeholder has just raised the same concern about this one. The project manager has the requirements traceability matrix, risk register, quality management plan, and schedule all open and can act in any of them. What is the most effective thing to do?

## Options

- **A**: Define specific, measurable quality metrics — such as the maximum tolerated data-reconciliation error rate and the load-completion deadline — and record them with their measurement method in the quality management plan.
- **B**: List "data inaccuracy at delivery" and "late data loads" as risks in the risk register with owners and response plans so the concern is tracked and revisited as the project proceeds.
- **C**: Capture each deliverable's quality requirement in the requirements traceability matrix and link it back to its source stakeholder so nothing agreed is lost.
- **D**: Add an acceptance gate to the schedule at which the stakeholder formally reviews each deliverable before sign-off, giving a defined point to settle whether it is acceptable.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The deliverables are defined only in vague, unmeasurable terms ("accurate", "timely"), which is exactly what invited the earlier dispute and is now worrying the stakeholder. The direct remedy is to convert those attributes into specific quality metrics with thresholds and an agreed measurement method, documented in the quality management plan — this is what makes "acceptable" objective in advance. (A) does that. (B) tracks the consequence of undefined quality as a risk but never defines the threshold, so the ambiguity that drives the dispute remains. (C) ensures each requirement is captured and traceable, but a traceability matrix records that a quality requirement exists and where it came from, not the measurable target that settles "how much." (D) schedules when acceptance happens but, without defined metrics to judge against, the review at that gate would still be the argument the stakeholder fears. Defining the metric is the root fix; the others depend on it.
