# Question

A logistics software team is investigating recurring data-import failures and has gathered several months of defect and operational data. The same three tools — a scatter diagram, a cause-and-effect (Ishikawa) diagram, and a Pareto chart — could each be reached for, and the analyst keeps proposing the wrong one. Match each immediate need below to the tool that genuinely answers it.

## Items

- **1**: The logs show eleven distinct failure reasons recorded against the imports, and the team's improvement time for the quarter is fixed, so they must decide which handful of reasons to tackle first to remove the most failures for the effort spent.
- **2**: For the single dominant failure reason, the team suspects it worsens as nightly batch volume climbs, and before committing to re-architecting the batch window they want evidence that failures actually track with volume rather than just assuming it.
- **3**: A failure reason that the data cannot explain on its own has appeared, and the team needs to lay out the full range of candidate contributors — people, process, tooling, environment — so nothing is overlooked before they start gathering more data.

## Matches

- **A**: Scatter diagram
- **B**: Cause-and-effect (fishbone / Ishikawa) diagram
- **C**: Pareto chart

## Answer

1-C, 2-A, 3-B

## Explanation

Each item is written so a neighbouring tool looks tempting; the match turns on the exact question being asked. Item 1 is about *prioritising among many known categories* under a fixed effort budget — that is precisely what a Pareto chart does by ranking reasons to expose the vital few (1-C); a fishbone is tempting because it also deals with "causes," but it generates and organises candidate causes, it does not rank recorded ones by frequency. Item 2 asks whether two measured variables *move together* before inferring causation, which is a scatter diagram's job (2-A); the pull toward a fishbone ("why does this fail?") is the trap — the team already has the suspected driver and needs correlation evidence, not a cause inventory. Item 3 is the one case where the data are insufficient and the need is to *enumerate possible contributors across categories*, the fishbone's purpose (3-B); reaching for Pareto here fails because there is nothing yet to rank. Swapping the fishbone and Pareto (the most common error) mismatches "rank what we know" with "explore what we don't."
