# Question

During a sprint review, the product owner rejects a completed story because the search results do not rank by relevance the way she expected, although ranking behaviour was never written into the acceptance criteria. The developers argue they delivered exactly what was agreed and the relevance work is non-trivial. The relevance ranking matters to her, but the sprint goal did not depend on it. Tension is rising in the room. What is the BEST action for the project manager to take?

## Options

- **A**: Record the story as accepted against its stated criteria and have the product owner raise the relevance-ranking behaviour as a new backlog item to be prioritised and refined for a future sprint.
- **B**: Facilitate a brief working agreement in the room — confirm the story met its criteria, log the relevance gap as new work for the product owner to prioritise, and have the team adjust refinement so behaviour like ranking is drawn out before stories are committed.
- **C**: Treat the gap as a refinement defect rather than new scope, hold the story open, and have the team add the relevance expectation to this story's acceptance criteria so it is closed only when the product owner's intent is met.
- **D**: Offer to have the team timebox a relevance-ranking spike in the next sprint to size the work before anyone decides whether it becomes a story, leaving the current story's status to the product owner.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Two things are simultaneously true: the work conforms to the written criteria, and the product owner's reaction exposes a real gap in how criteria are elaborated. The strongest move resolves the immediate standoff *and* fixes the cause: confirm the criteria were met, capture the relevance behaviour as new prioritised work, and improve refinement so latent expectations surface before commitment (B). (A) does the right transactional thing — accept against criteria, route the new need to the backlog — but it stops at processing this one story and never addresses why the expectation was missed, so the same dispute recurs next sprint. (C) is the subtle trap: reopening a story that met its agreed criteria to retrofit new behaviour rewards incomplete criteria with scope creep and erodes the meaning of "done," even though it feels responsive to the product owner. (D) is a reasonable way to size unknown work, but a spike investigates effort and sidesteps the actual problem — the refinement weakness — while leaving the team's conforming work in limbo. B is best because it is the only option that both closes the current item fairly and changes the process that produced the gap; A, C, and D each handle the symptom.
