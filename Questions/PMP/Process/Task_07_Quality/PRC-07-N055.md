# Question

On an agile team building a payments API, most defects surface late, at integration testing, where they are costly to trace. A senior engineer proposes mandatory peer code review before any merge. Some developers are concerned the reviews will add delay to every change. As project manager and servant leader, how should you proceed?

## Options

- **A**: Encourage the team to trial mandatory peer review for a few sprints and inspect its effect on escaped defects and on lead time before deciding to keep it.
- **B**: Have the team adopt mandatory peer review as a permanent standard now, since catching defects earlier is well established to lower cost.
- **C**: Run peer reviews only on changes to the most defect-prone modules, so review effort is concentrated where defects actually cluster.
- **D**: Pair the proposal with a service-level limit on review turnaround so the team commits to the practice without it becoming the new delay.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The proposal is a reasonable prevention/early-detection practice, but the team is divided and the trade-off against lead time is exactly what is in dispute. An agile team resolves this empirically: try it, measure, and let the data decide. (A) trials the practice and inspects both the quality benefit and the delay concern before committing — the servant-leader move that respects the team's self-management. (B) imposes a permanent change over the team's stated concern without evidence for this context, short-circuiting inspection and adaptation. (C) is a plausible refinement, but deciding upfront to limit review scope pre-empts the very experiment that would show whether broad review is worth its cost. (D) addresses only the turnaround worry and locks in the practice before the team has seen whether it actually reduces escaped defects here.
