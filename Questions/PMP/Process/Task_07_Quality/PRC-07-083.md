# Question

A budget-hotel chain is procuring guest-room televisions for a refurbishment. The sponsor has specified a basic small-screen model with no smart features to control cost. The procurement lead pushes back, arguing the team should source a higher-specification television because the basic one is a "low-quality" choice that will reflect badly on the project. The project manager must decide how to respond before the order is placed. What is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: Keep the specified basic model and define the quality requirements around it — reliability, defect-free units, and conformance to the agreed spec — so the low-grade choice is held to a high quality standard.
- **B**: Support the procurement lead and source a higher-specification television, because a richer feature set is what raises the quality of the deliverable and protects the project's reputation.
- **C**: Keep the basic model but lower the acceptance standard to match it, accepting a higher tolerance for minor defects since it is only an entry-level television.
- **D**: Escalate to the sponsor to reconsider the specification, since the procurement lead's reputational concern shows the basic model may not meet stakeholder expectations of quality.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The procurement lead is conflating grade with quality. Grade is the category of features a product offers; quality is the degree to which it is free of defects and meets its requirements. A deliberately basic, low-grade television is a legitimate cost-driven design choice and can be entirely high quality, so the right action is to keep the specified grade and set firm quality requirements around it. (A) is correct. (B) treats more features (grade) as more quality and overrides a deliberate sponsor decision on cost. (C) makes the real error — letting low grade justify low quality by relaxing defect tolerance, which is what quality management exists to prevent. (D) escalates on a misdiagnosis: a feature-set preference dressed up as a quality concern does not warrant reopening a sound specification, and accepting that framing concedes the grade-quality confusion.
