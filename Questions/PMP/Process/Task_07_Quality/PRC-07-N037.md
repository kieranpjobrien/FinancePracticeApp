# Question

To cut defects, a team adopted a new code-review step two sprints ago. The escaped-defect count fell in one sprint and rose in the other, and the two sprints differed in size and complexity, so the picture is muddy. The step also adds review time the team can feel. In the next planning session some members want to roll the step out more widely, others want to drop it, and the lead asks the project manager how to settle it. Which is the BEST course of action?

## Options

- **A**: Compare the two sprints' results against the pre-change defect baseline, normalising for the difference in size and complexity, and use what that shows to refine, keep, or drop the step.
- **B**: Run the step unchanged for two or three more sprints so that a larger sample makes the trend clear before any decision is taken.
- **C**: Keep the step but tighten it to review only the higher-risk changes, reducing its time cost while the team continues to gather evidence.
- **D**: Drop the step for now and reinstate it only if the defect rate worsens once it is gone.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A process change should be treated as an experiment that is evaluated against the baseline it was meant to improve. The data look muddy mainly because raw defect counts are being compared across two sprints of different size and complexity; analysing the results against the pre-change baseline and adjusting for that difference is what turns noise into a usable signal and lets the team decide on evidence (A). (B) keeps gathering data but defers the analysis — more sprints of un-normalised counts will stay just as ambiguous, and it leaves a possibly wasteful step running untouched. (C) is a reasonable adaptation, but changing the step now alters the very thing under test before its effect has been read, confounding the evidence the team still needs. (D) removing it to see if defects worsen is a slow, costly way to learn by regression and discards the data already collected. The discriminator is acting on analysed evidence now (A) versus accumulating, altering, or reversing before the existing results have actually been read.
