# Question

A bottling project monitors fill volume on a control chart. The customer's specification allows 498–502 ml. The process's calculated control limits, based on its own variation, sit at 499–501 ml. A team member proposes resetting the control limits to 498–502 ml so the chart will only alarm when a bottle actually breaches the customer's tolerance, reducing "nuisance" signals. How should the project manager respond?

## Options

- **A**: Approve the change, because aligning control limits with the specification means the chart flags only bottles that fail the customer's requirement.
- **B**: Decline; control limits reflect the process's natural variation and are used to detect when the process changes, whereas specification limits express the customer's requirement — they are not interchangeable.
- **C**: Approve the change but only if the customer formally agrees to the wider control limits in writing.
- **D**: Replace the control chart with a simple pass/fail check against the 498–502 ml specification, since the control limits add no value.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Control limits are derived from the process's own variation and signal when that process has shifted or become unstable — an early warning that can fire while output is still within the customer's tolerance. Specification limits express what the customer will accept. Widening control limits to the spec defeats their purpose: the chart would no longer detect process changes until defects are imminent. (B) is correct. (A) confuses the two limit types and discards the process-monitoring value. (C) treats control limits as a customer-negotiable parameter, which they are not — they belong to the process, not the contract. (D) abandons statistical process control entirely for end-point inspection, losing the ability to detect drift before it produces out-of-spec bottles.
