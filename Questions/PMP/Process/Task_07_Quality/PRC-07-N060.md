# Question

A project depends on a critical component from an external supplier. The first batch passed incoming inspection, but the project manager wants confidence that quality will hold across all future batches rather than relying on screening each delivery as it arrives. Which two actions best support that objective? (Select two.)

## Options

- **A**: Conduct a quality audit of the supplier's process to confirm it is capable and controlled, not just that one batch happened to pass.
- **B**: Agree quality metrics and acceptance criteria with the supplier and require process and inspection data to be supplied with each batch.
- **C**: Add a clause requiring the supplier to notify the project of any change to their materials, process, or sub-tier suppliers before shipping affected batches.
- **D**: Increase the incoming-inspection sample size for each batch to lower the chance of accepting a bad one.
- **E**: Require certificates of conformance with each batch and treat them as sufficient evidence that the batch meets requirements.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

Sustained supplier quality comes from assurance that the source process is capable and controlled (A) and from agreed metrics, acceptance criteria, and shared process data that let quality be managed at the supplier rather than only screened on arrival (B). (C) is genuinely useful and supports quality, but change-notification is a control over future risk rather than evidence the process is capable today, so it is a weaker fit for the stated objective than directly assuring and measuring the process. (D) leans on incoming inspection, which only screens what has already been produced and gives no confidence about the underlying process. (E) substitutes a paper certificate for actual process and inspection evidence; a certificate of conformance attests the supplier's own claim and, taken as sufficient, can mask a process that is not in control.
