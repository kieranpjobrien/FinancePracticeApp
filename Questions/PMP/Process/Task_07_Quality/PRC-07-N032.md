# Question

A team's retrospectives are lively and generate a long list of improvement ideas each sprint, but several of the same problems keep recurring and some members are starting to call the ceremony a waste of time. Reviewing recent retrospectives, the project manager sees that each one produced eight to ten agreed actions, almost none of which were assigned, scheduled, or revisited the following sprint. Which is the BEST way to make the retrospectives effective?

## Options

- **A**: Bring a structured technique, such as start-stop-continue, to focus the discussion and surface deeper issues each retrospective.
- **B**: Have the team pick one or two of the actions, give each an owner, and pull them into the next sprint where their progress is visible.
- **C**: Begin each retrospective by reviewing the previous sprint's improvement actions and the data on whether the targeted problems have changed.
- **D**: Track every agreed action on a dedicated improvement backlog so none of the ideas raised is lost between sprints.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The diagnosis is that the team has no shortage of insight or actions — it generates eight to ten a sprint — but the actions are never owned, scheduled, or carried out, so nothing changes and cynicism grows. Continuous improvement converts insight into a small amount of owned, completed change: limiting to one or two actions, assigning an owner, and pulling them into the next sprint creates accountability and visible progress (B). (A) improves how problems are surfaced, but generating ideas is already the team's strength, not its gap. (D) preserves every idea, but capturing more actions that still nobody executes deepens the backlog without changing behaviour; in fact it is the volume of unactioned items that is breeding the cynicism. (C) is the closest rival — reviewing prior actions and their effect is good practice and would expose the follow-through gap — but on its own it inspects the failure each sprint without creating the ownership that makes the next action actually happen; B supplies the missing mechanism, and C naturally follows once actions are being owned.
