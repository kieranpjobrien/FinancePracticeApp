# Question

A project manager is developing the quality management plan for a bridge-monitoring sensor system and wants the plan to genuinely drive quality during execution. Several proposed entries are all defensible quality work, but not all of them belong in the quality management plan itself. Which three entries belong in the quality management plan? (Select three.)

## Options

- **A**: The quality metrics, with target values and acceptable tolerances, against which the key deliverables will be measured.
- **B**: The standards and regulations the deliverables must comply with, and the method by which conformance to each will be verified.
- **C**: The quality control and assurance activities for the project, including who performs each and at what point in the life cycle.
- **D**: The list of specific defects found during testing and the status of each one's rework, kept current as execution proceeds.
- **E**: The detailed test scripts and step-by-step inspection instructions the testers will execute on each deliverable.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

The quality management plan describes *how* quality will be managed: the measurable metrics and tolerances (A), the standards plus how conformance will be verified (B), and the QC/QA activities with owners and timing (C) — together these let quality be planned in and monitored deliberately. (D) is a quality-control *result*, not a plan: a live defect-and-rework list is logged output produced during execution and belongs in quality reports or an issue/defect log, not in the plan that defines the approach. (E) is genuine and necessary, but detailed test scripts and inspection instructions are procedural work products that implement the plan; the plan identifies the activities and standards, while the executable scripts sit at a lower level as supporting procedures. The trap is that both (D) and (E) are real quality artefacts — but neither is content of the quality management plan itself.
