# Question

A food-processing project manager is analysing quality data and keeps seeing colleagues reach for the wrong technique. Four distinct needs have arisen, and the team must match each to the quality tool that actually answers it rather than one that merely looks related.

## Items

- **1**: Packaging weights are being recorded across the shift and the team wants to see the shape of the distribution — whether weights cluster, are skewed, or show two separate peaks suggesting two machines behaving differently.
- **2**: A finished-goods inspection log lists nine categories of reject; with limited improvement budget the team must identify which few categories account for the bulk of the rejects so effort goes where it removes the most.
- **3**: The team suspects reject rate rises with line speed and, before slowing the line, wants evidence of whether the two variables actually move together.
- **4**: A new and unexplained contamination defect has appeared, and the team needs to brainstorm and organise the full range of possible contributors across people, machines, materials, methods, and environment before collecting more data.

## Matches

- **A**: Pareto chart
- **B**: Histogram
- **C**: Cause-and-effect (Ishikawa) diagram
- **D**: Scatter diagram

## Answer

1-B, 2-A, 3-D, 4-C

## Explanation

Each item is framed so a neighbouring tool tempts. Item 1 wants the distribution's shape — clustering, skew, bimodality — which is a histogram's job (1-B); a Pareto chart is the trap because it also uses bars, but it ranks categories, it does not show a continuous distribution. Item 2 prioritises the vital few among many known reject categories under a fixed budget, the definition of a Pareto chart (2-A). Item 3 tests whether two measured variables move together before inferring causation, which is a scatter diagram (3-D); a fishbone tempts ("why do rejects rise?") but the driver is already suspected and correlation evidence is what is needed. Item 4 must enumerate possible causes across categories for an unexplained defect with no data yet, the cause-and-effect diagram (4-C); Pareto fails here because there is nothing recorded to rank. The classic errors — swapping histogram and Pareto (both bar charts) and swapping scatter and fishbone (both about relationships/causes) — are exactly what the wording guards against.
