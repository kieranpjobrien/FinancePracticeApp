# Question

A pharmaceutical client's quality assurance group has scheduled a quality audit of your project midway through the build of a clinical trial data platform; attendance is not optional under the contract. Several developers tell you they read the audit as a sign management distrusts their work and they want you to register the team's objection with the client before it goes ahead. The audit window overlaps the current sprint. How should the project manager proceed?

## Options

- **A**: Pass the team's objection to the client's QA group and ask them to confirm the audit's purpose in writing before the team engages with it.
- **B**: Engage with the audit as scheduled, and separately work with the team to set up the records and process evidence the auditors will sample so the sprint is disrupted as little as possible.
- **C**: Ask the QA group to narrow the audit to the deliverables and let the test lead present the passing test results, since clean inspection records are the strongest demonstration that the work is sound.
- **D**: Ask the QA group to move the audit to just after the sprint closes so the team can prepare process evidence properly and respond without losing sprint capacity.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A quality audit examines whether project *processes* conform to organisational and project policy; in regulated pharma it is a contractual, improvement-focused activity the PM facilitates, not a verdict on individuals. The PM's job is to enable it while shielding the team's flow, which is exactly supporting the audit and preparing the process evidence it will sample (B). (A) treats a contractual audit as something the team can gate behind a registered objection and a demand for written justification, which positions the PM against the client rather than enabling a legitimate review. (C) is the subtle trap: it quietly converts a *process* audit into a *product* inspection — passing test results show the deliverables conform but say nothing about whether the processes follow the plan, which is what the audit assesses. (D) sounds accommodating but defers a review designed to catch process issues while there is still time to correct them, and trades the audit's timing for sprint convenience. The discrimination is between facilitating the audit on its own terms (B) and three responses that each push back on, narrow, or postpone it.
