# Question

A medical-device project manager is preparing the cost-of-quality section of the quality management plan and must classify several budgeted activities so the sponsor can see how money is split between conformance and non-conformance work. Match each expenditure below to the cost-of-quality category it belongs to.

## Items

- **1**: Calibration of test rigs and an end-of-line functional test that screens every assembled unit before it is released to packaging.
- **2**: Replacing units that failed in the field, plus the cost of handling the resulting customer complaints and regulatory reporting.
- **3**: Designing the assembly fixtures to prevent mis-orientation, and training operators on the standard work before production begins.

## Matches

- **A**: Prevention cost
- **B**: Appraisal cost
- **C**: External failure cost

## Answer

1-B, 2-C, 3-A

## Explanation

The categories turn on when and why the money is spent. Item 1 — calibration and screening every unit — is appraisal cost: it measures and inspects to find defects, not to prevent them, so it maps to B. Item 2 — replacements, complaints, and regulatory reporting after units fail at the customer — is external failure cost, incurred because defective product escaped, mapping to C. Item 3 — fixture design that stops errors occurring and operator training before production — is prevention cost, spent up front to keep defects from arising, mapping to A. The common trap is confusing appraisal (detecting defects, item 1) with prevention (stopping them, item 3); both are conformance costs, but only prevention removes the cause, while appraisal finds what slipped through. External failure (item 2) is unambiguously non-conformance because the failure reached the customer.
