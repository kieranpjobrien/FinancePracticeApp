# Question

An executive asks the PM why the quality metrics for your mobile app project look "weaker" than an industry benchmark report they've just read. The benchmark report uses different definitions and sample populations. The PM believes the comparison is misleading. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Adopt the benchmark report's definitions so the next report is directly comparable to the industry figures cited.
- **B**: Produce a side-by-side chart showing your metrics alongside the benchmark, with footnotes on where definitions diverge.
- **C**: Ignore the benchmark and continue reporting your metrics because the comparison is apples-to-oranges and unhelpful.
- **D**: Explain the definitional differences, recommend a tailored benchmark set with peers, and agree reporting with the executive.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Benchmarks have value only when definitions and populations are comparable; the PM educates the executive and negotiates a tailored comparison set. (D) is correct. (A) adopts external definitions blindly. (B) is a useful tactic but does not address that the benchmark itself is unsuitable. (C) dismisses a legitimate stakeholder interest.
