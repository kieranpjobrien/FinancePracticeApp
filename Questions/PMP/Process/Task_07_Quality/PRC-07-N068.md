# Question

A PMO quality audit of a pharmaceutical packaging project finds that the team has been consistently skipping a documented line-clearance step. The team explains the step duplicates a check the automated vision system now performs, so they dropped it to save time; the audit also notes the team's own changeover log as a good practice worth sharing. The project manager wants to respond appropriately to the audit's findings. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Reinstate the documented line-clearance step immediately to restore compliance, then raise a change request to retire it if it is genuinely redundant.
- **B**: Raise a change request to update the procedure to reflect the vision-system check, and continue running without the manual step in the meantime.
- **C**: Record the team's reasoning in the audit response, keep the current practice since the vision system covers the check, and share the changeover log.
- **D**: Treat the deviation as a training gap, retrain the team on the existing procedure, and monitor for further skipped steps.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The audit confirms a real non-conformance: the team is not following an approved procedure, and in a regulated pharmaceutical context the controlled process is authoritative until it is formally changed, regardless of whether a step looks redundant. (A) restores compliance with the current approved procedure now and uses change control to retire the step properly if the redundancy is validated — it respects both quality governance and the team's legitimate improvement idea. (B) leaves the process out of compliance while the change is pending, accepting an uncontrolled deviation in a regulated environment. (C) endorses the team's unilateral decision to drop a controlled step, which is exactly the governance failure the audit surfaced, even though sharing the good practice is appropriate. (D) assumes the team did not know the procedure, but the issue is a deliberate, reasoned deviation, not a knowledge gap, so retraining misdiagnoses the cause. The discriminator is honouring change control — comply with the approved process first, then change it formally — rather than ratifying or merely re-teaching an uncontrolled deviation.
