# Question

A sponsor reviews the prototype of an internal expense-tracking tool and is unhappy: it has fewer features and a plainer interface than a commercial product she has seen, and she says the team has "let quality slip." The tool meets every documented requirement, has no open defects, and was scoped deliberately as a lightweight internal utility. The sponsor wants a plan to close the gap. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Raise a change request to add the additional features and a richer interface so the tool matches the commercial product the sponsor referenced.
- **B**: Ask the sponsor which of her users' tasks the current tool fails to complete, and feed any genuine gaps into reprioritisation rather than matching the commercial product feature-for-feature.
- **C**: Confirm the tool already satisfies its agreed requirements and is defect-free, and propose closing the review on that basis.
- **D**: Commission a benchmarking study comparing the tool against the commercial product across every feature so the size of the gap is quantified before deciding.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The tool is high quality in the technical sense (it conforms to requirements and is defect-free); what the sponsor is reacting to is a lower grade — fewer features and a plainer interface — which was a deliberate scoping choice. Grade is legitimately changeable, but only through validated need, not by matching a competitor. (B) tests the complaint against actual fitness for use and routes any real gaps through reprioritisation, which respects both quality and value. (A) treats "match the commercial product" as the requirement and pulls in potentially unneeded scope on the sponsor's say-so. (C) is technically correct but dismisses an accountable stakeholder's concern without exploring whether a real usability gap exists. (D) is disproportionate analysis that frames feature parity with the commercial product as the goal, which it is not for a deliberately lightweight internal tool.
