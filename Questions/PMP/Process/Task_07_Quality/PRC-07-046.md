# Question

A manufacturing project shows warranty-claim costs are dominating the quality budget. A cost-of-quality analysis reveals prevention spending is at 5 percent while external failure is at 60 percent of the quality budget. Leadership wants to reduce the warranty cost. What should the PM recommend?

## Options

- **A**: Invest in prevention activities such as training, design reviews, and process capability studies to reduce future failure costs.
- **B**: Increase appraisal activities including extra inspection and testing to catch defects before they reach the customer base.
- **C**: Negotiate with customers to reduce warranty coverage so the warranty-claim cost base shrinks and looks better on reports.
- **D**: Accept the external-failure cost as the cost of doing business and focus resources on new product development instead.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Cost-of-quality theory holds that prevention is cheaper than appraisal, which is cheaper than failure; rebalancing toward prevention reduces total cost of quality. (A) is correct. (B) is useful but lower-leverage and still downstream. (C) shifts cost to the customer and erodes trust. (D) abandons continuous improvement.
