# Question

A project manager on a hybrid logistics-tracking platform is defining quality metrics for the quality management plan so that "acceptable" is objective rather than disputed at delivery. The team has proposed several candidate metrics. Which proposals are sound quality metrics — specific, measurable, and with a defined measurement method? (Choose three.)

## Options

- **A**: Defect density measured as confirmed defects per thousand lines of delivered code, captured from the defect-tracking tool each release.
- **B**: Percentage of shipment-status updates delivered to the customer portal within sixty seconds of the source event, measured over a rolling week.
- **C**: Mean time to restore service after a Sev-1 incident, calculated from the incident log's detection and resolution timestamps.
- **D**: The platform should be "highly reliable and responsive" to the satisfaction of operations staff.
- **E**: The team should "do its best" to keep the number of customer complaints low throughout the project.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

A usable quality metric states exactly what is measured, sets a quantifiable basis, and names how it is captured. Defect density per KLOC from the defect tracker (A), percentage of status updates within sixty seconds measured weekly (B), and mean time to restore from incident-log timestamps (C) all meet this — each is specific, measurable, and has a defined source. (D) is a vague attribute ("highly reliable and responsive" to "satisfaction") with no threshold or measurement method, which is exactly the ambiguity that invites delivery disputes. (E) ("do its best", "low") is an aspiration, not a measurable target, with no defined number or method. The discrimination is between concretely measurable metrics (A, B, C) and unmeasurable qualitative statements dressed up as metrics (D, E).
