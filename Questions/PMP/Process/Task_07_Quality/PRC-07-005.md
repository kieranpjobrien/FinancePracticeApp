# Question

On a high-rise construction project, recent concrete pours have repeatedly failed independent strength tests. Investigation shows the supplier is delivering exactly what the contract specifies, but those specifications were drafted three years ago against a now-superseded code. Updating the specification will trigger a change request, additional cost, and a likely two-week schedule slip. What should the project manager do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Continue accepting the supplier's deliveries because the contract is being met and the project must protect cost and schedule.
- **B**: Direct the supplier verbally to upgrade the mix to current code at no additional cost and revisit the contract at the next renegotiation point.
- **C**: Capture the specification gap as a lesson learned for future projects and continue with the current pours so this project is not delayed.
- **D**: Submit a change request to update the specification to current code, accept the cost and schedule impact, and have the supplier perform to the corrected specification.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

PMI's Code of Ethics treats compliance and safety as non-negotiable, ahead of cost and schedule. (D) follows the proper change-control path to bring the project into compliance. (A) prioritises contract conformance over fitness for purpose and accepts safety and liability risk in a structural element. (B) bypasses contract change control through informal direction, which is unenforceable and exposes both parties. (C) defers a current safety problem to "future projects" while continuing to build with non-conforming material.
