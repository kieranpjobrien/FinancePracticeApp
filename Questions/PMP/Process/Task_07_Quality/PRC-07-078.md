# Question

A scheduled quality audit on an aerospace-components project reports that the team has been logging inspection results in a local spreadsheet of its own, with several fields left blank, instead of the organisation's controlled records system. The parts themselves have all passed inspection and shipped good. The team lead, under schedule pressure, asks the project manager to close the finding as low priority because the product conformed and reworking the records helps no deliverable. How should the project manager treat the finding?

## Options

- **A**: Treat it as a process non-conformance to be corrected now, fixing the records practice and addressing why the controlled process was bypassed, because audit findings stand on process adherence independent of whether the product passed.
- **B**: Accept the team lead's reasoning and close it as low priority, since a quality audit's purpose is to give confidence in the deliverables and the deliverables conformed.
- **C**: Log it as an improvement suggestion for the next project's lessons learned, leaving the current project's records as they are given the schedule pressure.
- **D**: Escalate to the customer at once, because incomplete inspection records mean the conformance of the shipped parts can no longer be relied upon.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A quality audit evaluates whether the project's processes and records comply with policy and standards; its findings are about process adherence and are not nullified by the product happening to pass — and in aerospace the inspection records are themselves part of demonstrating conformance and traceability. A bypassed, incomplete records process is a genuine non-conformance to correct now, with its cause addressed. (A) is correct. (B) collapses process audit into product inspection and lets a standards breach close because the parts were good. (C) defers a current-project non-conformance into a future project's lessons learned, leaving the gap unremedied where it occurred. (D) overreaches in the other direction: a records-process gap warrants internal corrective action and a traceability assessment, not an immediate declaration that conforming parts are now suspect — that escalation may follow only if the traceability review shows the parts cannot be substantiated.
