# Question

A consumer-appliance manufacturer is setting the quality budget for a new dishwasher line. Two competing proposals are on the table for the same fixed quality budget. One funds supplier-component checks and operator training before production; the other funds a larger end-of-line test bay that screens every finished unit. The line uses a new sealing process the plant has not run before, and the cost of a field recall on this product is high. The sponsor asks which split the project manager recommends and why. What is the best recommendation?

## Options

- **A**: Weight the budget toward the up-front supplier checks and operator training, because on an unproven process the cheapest defects to absorb are the ones never created, and that reduces the recall exposure more than catching units at the end.
- **B**: Weight the budget toward the larger end-of-line test bay, because screening every finished unit is what actually stops a defective dishwasher reaching a customer and triggering the recall.
- **C**: Split the budget evenly between the two, since prevention and appraisal are both conformance costs and neither should be favoured without defect data from the line.
- **D**: Hold most of the budget back as a reserve and fund neither heavily yet, releasing it to whichever activity the first production run shows is needed.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four options are plausible uses of a conformance budget, so the choice turns on the situation, not on naming a category. On a process the plant has never run, defect modes are unknown and a single escaped defect carries high recall cost; prevention spending — supplier checks and operator training — attacks defects before they are built, and avoided defects are cheaper than detected ones, so it cuts recall exposure most. (A) is correct. (B) over-relies on appraisal: end-of-line screening detects rather than prevents, lets the unstable process keep producing scrap, and a sampling-based bay can still miss escapes. (C) sounds balanced but defaults to a 50/50 split with no rationale; the unproven process and high failure cost specifically argue for prevention, and waiting for defect data means defects must first occur. (D) defers the decision and funds quality reactively on a product where the failure cost is exactly what you cannot afford to discover in production.
