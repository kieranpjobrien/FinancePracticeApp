# Question

On a predictive water-treatment construction project, the last two concrete pours each developed the same honeycombing defect, which the crew patched. Ten pours remain. The site engineer notes the vibration equipment, the mix supplier, and the crew were the same on both pours, and that ambient temperature was high on both days. The project manager wants the eleventh pour to come out sound. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Convene the crew and engineer to analyse the two defective pours and identify why honeycombing is occurring before changing anything.
- **B**: Have the crew increase vibration time and consolidation effort on the next pour, since insufficient vibration is the usual cause of honeycombing.
- **C**: Bring in a different concrete mix with better workability for the high-temperature conditions seen on both pour days.
- **D**: Add a hold point requiring engineer sign-off on consolidation immediately after each remaining pour before it is patched.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Several plausible causes co-vary across the two pours — vibration, mix, crew, and temperature — and acting on any single one before confirming the cause risks fixing the wrong thing and wasting a pour. (A) is root-cause analysis: determine the assignable cause from the available evidence first, which then tells you whether B, C, or some other change is warranted. (B) assumes the cause is under-vibration and may be right, but jumping to one suspected cause without confirmation is exactly the guess the data does not yet support. (C) likewise commits to the temperature/workability hypothesis prematurely. (D) is an inspection hold point that would detect the defect after it forms again rather than preventing it, so it does not protect the next pour. All four are reasonable construction-quality actions; the discriminator is sequence — diagnose the cause before selecting the corrective change.
