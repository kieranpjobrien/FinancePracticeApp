# Question

A construction project completes a poured-concrete foundation. Quality control inspection records that the surface finish is outside the specified tolerance, though the structural strength tests pass. The customer's documented acceptance criteria explicitly include the finish tolerance. The contractor maintains the finish is cosmetic and the slab is sound, and remediating it would delay the next pour. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Record the result as a non-conformance against the acceptance criteria and convene the team to determine corrective action before approaching the customer.
- **B**: Notify the customer of the finish non-conformance now and jointly agree how it will be dispositioned before any acceptance decision.
- **C**: Have the contractor remediate the finish to bring it within tolerance, then re-inspect against the criterion before seeking acceptance.
- **D**: Raise a change request to revise the finish tolerance, on the basis that the strength tests demonstrate the slab meets its essential structural purpose.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The finish is an explicit acceptance criterion, so failing it is a non-conformance regardless of the contractor's view of what matters — every option here accepts that and none simply waves the slab through, so the decision is about sequence. (A) is the controlled first step: formally record the non-conformance and work the disposition internally (repair, rework, or a justified request for concession) so that whatever is later put to the customer is a considered position. (B) is right that the customer must be involved, but going to them before the team has determined the disposition surfaces a raw problem with no recommended path and risks prematurely framing it as the customer's call when the team may simply remediate. (C) commits to one disposition — full remediation — before it has been confirmed as the appropriate response; rework may well be the answer, but jumping to it skips deciding among the options and pre-empts any customer concession. (D) leaps to changing the agreed standard as the opening move, asking to lower the bar rather than first handling the conformance gap on the terms that were agreed.
