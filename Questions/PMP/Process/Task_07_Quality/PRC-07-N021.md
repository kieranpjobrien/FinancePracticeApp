# Question

Six sprints into a payments integration, about a third of each sprint is now consumed fixing defects found in staging, and feature velocity has fallen. The defects are spread across many different stories with no single failing component. When the team looks back, several developers say they often start a story unsure how unusual inputs and error paths are meant to behave, and fill the gaps with their own assumptions. The team already runs a sprint review and a retrospective. The project manager has capacity to support one change. Which is the BEST move?

## Options

- **A**: Expand the automated regression suite so that defects of this kind are caught by the pipeline before they reach staging.
- **B**: Have the team and product owner work the edge cases and error behaviour into acceptance criteria during backlog refinement, before a story is pulled into a sprint.
- **C**: Introduce a peer code-review gate so a second developer checks each story's handling of unusual inputs before it is merged.
- **D**: Add a hardening sprint after every few delivery sprints to absorb the defect-fixing so feature velocity is protected.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The clue is not handed over directly: defects are scattered, there is no single faulty component, and developers describe starting work without a shared understanding of edge cases and inventing their own — a sign that ambiguity is entering at story start. The cheapest place to fix that is upstream, by elaborating edge cases and error behaviour into acceptance criteria in refinement so the ambiguity never reaches development (B). (A) and (C) are both genuine quality controls, but they are appraisal — they detect defects after they have been created rather than removing the source, so the team keeps creating and then catching them. (D) protects velocity optics but institutionalises the rework instead of preventing it. All four are real practices a competent team uses; only B addresses where the defects are actually being introduced.
