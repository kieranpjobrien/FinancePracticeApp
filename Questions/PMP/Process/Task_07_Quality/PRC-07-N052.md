# Question

A medical-device assembly line has seen a spike in rejected units across many defect types. The project manager wants the team to work out where to focus and why, using data, before committing to any corrective action. Which two analytical steps best fit that objective right now? (Select two.)

## Options

- **A**: Build a Pareto chart of the defect types to identify which few categories account for most of the rejects.
- **B**: Use a cause-and-effect diagram to structure the team's investigation into the likely sources of the dominant defect category.
- **C**: Plot a control chart of the overall reject rate to confirm the process is producing within its control limits.
- **D**: Draw a scatter diagram of total units built against total rejects per shift to show that more output means more rejects.
- **E**: Produce a histogram of all defect types together to display the full spread of defects on one chart.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

With many defect types, the team first needs to find the vital few categories driving the spike (A, Pareto) and then structure root-cause investigation of the dominant one (B, fishbone) — focus, then diagnose. (C) misuses a control chart: the chart shows stability over time, and here the reject rate has already visibly spiked, so confirming "within limits" neither locates the focus nor explains the cause. (D) correlates output volume with raw reject counts, which will trivially rise together and reveals nothing about defect causes; a scatter diagram is only useful against a candidate causal variable. (E) a histogram shows the distribution of a single variable but, lumping all defect types together, it does not rank them the way a Pareto does, so it does not direct the team's focus.
