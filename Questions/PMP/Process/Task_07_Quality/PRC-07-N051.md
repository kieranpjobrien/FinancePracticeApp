# Question

Over the last three sprints an agile team's defect-escape rate has crept upward. At the retrospective the team converges on a likely cause: stories are entering sprints with ambiguous acceptance criteria, so developers fill the gaps with assumptions that later prove wrong. Several improvement ideas are floated. Which should the project manager encourage the team to commit to?

## Options

- **A**: Trial a criteria-clarity check as a shared entry condition for stories next sprint, then review at the next retrospective whether escapes fall.
- **B**: Ask the product owner to write fuller acceptance criteria for every future story, since criteria quality is the product owner's responsibility.
- **C**: Add an extra review of completed stories against their criteria before the sprint review, to catch misread requirements before they escape.
- **D**: Agree that developers will raise a question whenever acceptance criteria seem unclear, rather than proceeding on an assumption.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The team has identified a cause and should commit to a concrete, owned improvement it can trial and then inspect — the essence of continuous improvement. The cause is ambiguity entering the sprint, so the highest-leverage experiment is a shared entry condition that stops unclear stories being started. (A) is specific, team-owned, testable next sprint, and reviewed for effect. (B) assigns the fix unilaterally to the product owner and addresses only the writing of criteria, not the team's shared agreement on readiness; clarity is a collaborative responsibility. (C) adds a downstream inspection that catches problems after the assumptions are already built, rather than preventing ambiguous work from starting. (D) is a sound individual habit but is an informal intention with no shared standard or check, so it is unlikely to hold under pressure or be reviewable.
