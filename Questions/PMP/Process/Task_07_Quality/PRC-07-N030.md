# Question

During acceptance of a reporting module, the customer notices a configurable dashboard and several animated transitions that are not in the agreed scope. A senior developer had added them in spare time; they passed all tests and caused no delay. The customer is uneasy: the features behave differently from the rest of the product and were never reviewed against the requirements. What should the project manager do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Reassure the customer that the additions came at no cost or schedule impact and ask them to accept the module as delivered.
- **B**: Confirm with the customer and team that the extra features sit outside the agreed and validated scope, and treat their inclusion as the issue to resolve before acceptance proceeds.
- **C**: Have the developer remove the unrequested features so the delivered module matches the agreed scope exactly, then resubmit it for acceptance.
- **D**: Raise a change request to bring the added features into scope so the work the team already did is not wasted.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The features are gold plating — unrequested scope that was never validated against requirements or run through change control, which is why the customer is uneasy even though nothing broke. The first step is to name the situation honestly and agree how to handle the unvalidated additions before acceptance, because accepting on either premise prejudges the outcome. (B) reframes the real problem (uncontrolled scope) and opens the decision rather than forcing it. (A) pressures the customer to absorb change they did not ask for and validates the practice. (C) jumps to removal before anyone has decided whether the features are wanted — if the customer does value them, that effort is wasted twice. (D) leaps to formalising the scope addition to justify sunk effort, which is the wrong reason to widen scope. Both C and D are plausible later steps, but each presupposes the disposition decision that B exists to make.
