# Question

A newly formed agile team keeps disputing at sprint reviews whether items are finished: some count code-complete as done, others expect tests and documentation. The disputes are eating into reviews and two members have asked the project manager to "just give us the rule". The product owner offers the Definition of Done from her previous team, which is well-tested and could be in place today. The next sprint starts tomorrow. As project manager, what should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Run a short working session for the whole team to agree its own Definition of Done before the sprint starts, drawing on the product owner's version as a candidate to adapt.
- **B**: Put the product owner's tested Definition of Done in place for tomorrow so the team is working to one clear standard immediately, and revisit it at a later retrospective.
- **C**: Have the two members who asked for a rule draft a Definition of Done from the product owner's version and circulate it to the rest of the team for objections.
- **D**: Defer setting a Definition of Done until the team has delivered a few sprints together, and for now resolve each disputed item at the review as it arises.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The recurring disputes exist because this team has never agreed a shared standard, and a Definition of Done holds only if the people held to it own it; whatever is adopted must be the team's agreement, not a standard handed to them. The discriminator is not "team versus imposed" in the abstract — every option here can produce a written standard quickly — but which route produces one the whole team actually owns without surrendering that ownership to save a day. (A) gets the entire team to agree its own DoD before the sprint, using the proven version as a head start, so the standard is both fast and owned. (B) is the genuine tension — a tested standard in place tomorrow is tempting and "revisit later" sounds reasonable — but it installs an unowned rule and bets the team will later re-open something already governing their work; the same members who reject "code-complete as done" have bought into nothing. (C) delegates to a subset, so the rest are consulted rather than agreeing, and "circulate for objections" yields compliance, not the shared commitment that ends the disputes. (D) leaves the work with no completion standard and keeps relitigating each item at the review, which is the very symptom to be cured.
