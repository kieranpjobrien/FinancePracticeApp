# Question

On a precision-machining programme, bore-diameter rejects have climbed over the past month and are now threatening the delivery schedule. A Pareto chart already shows diameter drift accounts for most of the scrap. A senior machinist is convinced the drift comes from the spindle heating up through each shift and wants to fit a coolant upgrade that would take the cell offline for two days. An inspector instead points to a scatter plot he ran last week of bore diameter against spindle temperature, which shows only a weak, scattered relationship. The project manager must decide the next step before committing the two-day stoppage. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Approve the coolant upgrade now, since spindle heating is the machinist's experienced judgement and diameter drift is consistent with a thermal cause.
- **B**: Treat the weak scatter result as ruling temperature out, and instead build a cause-and-effect diagram with the team to surface other candidate drivers of the drift before changing anything.
- **C**: Plot the rejected diameters on a run chart against time-within-shift and against tool-change and material-lot boundaries to see what the drift actually tracks, then target the confirmed driver.
- **D**: Tighten the in-process inspection frequency so drifting parts are caught and reworked sooner while the team keeps investigating the cause.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The team has prioritised the problem (Pareto) but the one relationship anyone has tested — diameter against spindle temperature — came back weak, so committing a two-day thermal fix on the strength of a hunch the data does not support is acting on an unconfirmed cause. (C) is the right next step: look at how the drift behaves over time and against the other things that change during a shift — tool changes, material lots — to identify what it genuinely tracks before spending the stoppage, since the obvious thermal explanation is not holding up. (A) backs experience over the available evidence and risks taking the cell down for two days to fix the wrong cause. (B) over-reads the weak correlation as a clean elimination — a weak scatter does not prove temperature is irrelevant, and jumping straight to brainstorming fresh causes discards the time-pattern evidence already in hand. (D) is an appraisal response that catches drifted parts sooner but does nothing to find or remove the cause, so the scrap and schedule pressure continue. All four are legitimate quality actions a competent team might take; the discriminator is grounding the next move in what the drift actually tracks rather than committing a costly fix to an unconfirmed cause, dismissing evidence outright, or merely inspecting harder.
