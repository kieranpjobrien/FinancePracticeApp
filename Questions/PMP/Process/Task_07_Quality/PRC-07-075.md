# Question

An agile team maintaining a high-traffic payments platform is slowing down: shortcuts taken under earlier deadlines now mean each feature takes longer and ships more defects, velocity is falling, and escaped-defect rates are climbing. The product owner accepts the debt is real but wants to keep delivering features and is wary of spending capacity on refactoring that shows no new function. The project manager is deciding what to put to the team and product owner. Which approach is best?

## Options

- **A**: Make the debt explicit on the backlog and allocate a steady, agreed share of each sprint to paying it down in the areas where it most degrades quality and flow, alongside continued feature delivery.
- **B**: Let the team refactor opportunistically, cleaning up debt in any module they happen to touch while delivering features, without reserving dedicated capacity for it.
- **C**: Hold features steady and wait for a quieter delivery period, then run a single dedicated remediation effort to clear the accumulated debt in one focused push.
- **D**: Quantify the debt's cost in lost velocity and defects and take it to the sponsor to decide whether to fund a remediation initiative before continuing.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The debt is already measurably cutting velocity and raising escaped defects, so it is an active quality problem, and the durable, proportionate response is to make it visible on the backlog and pay it down steadily where it hurts most while still delivering value — keeping the product owner in the prioritisation. (A) is correct. (B) sounds disciplined but, with no reserved capacity, opportunistic clean-up is the first thing dropped under feature pressure and rarely reaches the worst-affected areas, so the decline continues. (C) bets the fix on a quiet period that on a high-traffic payments platform may never come, letting the debt keep compounding until one large, risky remediation. (D) defers an in-team, ongoing engineering practice to a sponsor funding decision; quantifying the cost is useful, but routine paydown is the team's to plan continuously, not a one-off initiative to seek approval for before acting. The margin is between continuous, reserved, prioritised paydown (A) and approaches that leave it unfunded, postponed, or escalated.
