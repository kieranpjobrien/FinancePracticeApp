# Question

On a patient-records product, operations rejected the latest release because the accessibility and security scans had not been run, even though the product owner had accepted every story at the sprint review. Reviewing the last few sprints, the project manager finds the team has begun treating those scans as a final pre-release step that gets squeezed when the release date is tight, and lately skipped. The product owner accepts each story against the acceptance criteria she writes for it, and the team holds a short retrospective every sprint. The team wants the scans to stop being the thing that slips. What should the project manager encourage the team to change?

## Options

- **A**: Make passing the accessibility and security scans a condition the team builds into its definition of done, so a story cannot be called complete until the scans pass for it.
- **B**: Have the product owner add accessibility and security as explicit acceptance criteria on every story she writes, so she withholds acceptance at review until they are met.
- **C**: Move the accessibility and security scans earlier into the sprint and assign a team member to own running them each sprint, so they are not left until the release crunch.
- **D**: Agree at the retrospective to protect time for the scans before each release and track in following retrospectives whether the team is honouring that commitment.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The failure is that work is being declared "done" and released without the scans, and the scans get traded away under deadline pressure. The durable fix is to make passing them part of what "done" means, so the gate is structural rather than a step that can be deferred — that is precisely the purpose of the definition of done (A). (B) is plausible because acceptance criteria do gate the product owner's acceptance, but they sit per story in her hands and depend on her remembering to add the same two criteria to every story; one omission reopens the gap, and acceptance at review is downstream of where the work is built. (C) improves sequencing and ownership but adds no completion gate, so under the next tight deadline the scans can still be squeezed exactly as before. (D) is a behavioural commitment policed by inspection after the fact; it relies on diligence rather than making incomplete work impossible to call done. All four are real agile practices a competent team uses; the discriminator is that only the definition of done makes the scans an inseparable part of completion for every story rather than a separable step.
