# Question

An organisation is engaging an external development partner to build a product whose features will be discovered iteratively over many sprints. Procurement policy requires an approved contract value before any supplier starts, and the finance director wants the firm's exposure capped at that approved figure. The development leadership wants the partner to stay genuinely responsive to a backlog that will be reordered every sprint. The project manager is choosing the contracting structure. Which approach best fits?

## Options

- **A**: A capacity-based agreement funding the partner's team for a fixed period at a fixed total budget, with the work each sprint drawn from the current prioritised backlog.
- **B**: A time-and-materials contract with a not-to-exceed ceiling set at the approved value, billed against actual effort as the backlog is delivered sprint by sprint.
- **C**: A fixed-price-per-sprint (incremental) contract at an agreed price each iteration, with the backlog for that iteration fixed at its sprint planning and the engagement endable at any sprint boundary.
- **D**: A target-cost contract at the approved value with a shared pain-and-gain mechanism, so the partner is incentivised to deliver the discovered scope within the target.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four bound the firm's exposure at the approved figure, so the choice turns on which structure keeps the partner genuinely responsive to a backlog reordered every sprint while honouring a capped, pre-approved value. A capacity-based agreement (A) funds a stable team for a fixed period and total budget and lets scope be pulled from the live backlog with no per-item renegotiation, which is the structure that most cleanly reconciles a hard cost cap with continuous reprioritisation. A T&M-with-ceiling (B) does cap exposure and flexes with effort, but billing against actuals shifts the focus to hours consumed and offers the buyer less certainty of what the fixed budget buys than a funded-capacity commitment; it is the closest alternative and the main discriminator. Fixed-price-per-sprint (C) caps cost and allows exit at each boundary, but pricing and fixing the backlog at every sprint planning injects a mini-negotiation into each iteration, which dampens the very sprint-to-sprint responsiveness the leadership wants. A target-cost pain-and-gain arrangement (D) is a legitimate capped structure, but it presumes a definable scope to set a meaningful target against and to measure under- or over-run, which sits awkwardly with scope that is still being discovered. The margin is between funding capacity (A) and metering effort (B): both respect the cap, but only A leaves feature content fully adaptive without re-pricing.
