# Question

While integrating procurement activities into the overall project plan, the project manager finds that a subcontracted deliverable lies on the critical path, and the combined time for the buyer's source selection process plus the supplier's lead time exceeds the window available before the deliverable is needed. The procurement has not yet been issued. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Fast-track the source selection by shortening the solicitation period and running evaluation in parallel with bid clarifications.
- **B**: Reconcile the procurement timeline against the project schedule, quantify the shortfall, and evaluate options such as fast-tracking the solicitation, compressing the schedule, or revising the procurement strategy.
- **C**: Split the procurement so a long-lead portion of the supplier's work can be ordered early ahead of the full award.
- **D**: Escalate the timing conflict to the sponsor and steering committee with the options and a request for a decision on schedule or budget relief.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The conflict has only just been discovered and not yet been sized or analysed, so the first step is to reconcile the procurement timeline with the schedule, quantify the shortfall, and lay out the candidate remedies before committing to any one of them (B). Fast-tracking the solicitation (A) is a plausible remedy, but choosing it before the gap is quantified risks compressing the process more or less than needed and degrading bid quality. Splitting out a long-lead order (C) is another legitimate option that may emerge from the analysis, yet acting on it first presumes a solution without weighing it against the alternatives. Escalating to the sponsor (D) may be necessary if the options require schedule or budget relief, but escalating before the project manager has analysed the shortfall and framed the choices hands leadership a problem without the analysis they need to decide. The discriminator is that B is the analyse-and-frame step that must precede selecting, executing, or escalating any specific remedy.
