# Question

A project depends on a critical specialised component available from only one qualified supplier with a long lead time. Partway through delivery the supplier discloses financial difficulties that could threaten completion, though it is still delivering for now. The deliverable sits on the critical path. What should the project manager prioritise first?

## Options

- **A**: Request the supplier's recovery plan and financial assurances, and increase monitoring of its performance and stability.
- **B**: Assess the schedule and cost impact if the supplier fails, and develop response options including qualifying an alternative source.
- **C**: Negotiate a contractual safeguard such as source-code or tooling escrow and step-in rights so the buyer can continue if the supplier collapses.
- **D**: Place a larger forward order now to build a buffer of the component before the supplier's situation deteriorates further.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A newly elevated continuity risk on a single-source, long-lead, critical-path item must first be understood before it can be treated, so assessing the impact of failure and developing response options, including qualifying an alternative source, is the priority that frames everything else (B). Requesting a recovery plan and tightening monitoring (A) is a sound mitigation and will likely follow, but it manages the existing supplier without yet protecting the project against the failure scenario, and monitoring alone buys no fallback. Negotiating escrow and step-in rights (C) is a valuable continuity safeguard, yet it is one specific response option that should be selected on the basis of the impact assessment rather than reached for blindly first. Placing a larger forward order (D) ties up funds with a financially shaky supplier and could increase exposure if it fails to deliver anyway, so it is a risky move to make before the analysis. The discriminator is that assessing impact and options precedes committing to any single mitigation, however reasonable each looks.
