# Question

A Scrum team includes a specialist supplier engaged under a firm fixed-price contract for one defined deliverable. Two sprints in, discovery shows a different deliverable of comparable effort would be far more valuable, and the supplier is well placed and willing to build it instead. The original deliverable is not yet started. The supplier's account manager notes the new work is outside the signed statement of work and asks how the buyer wants to handle it. The contract includes a change-for-free style clause permitting swaps of equivalent-sized work. What should the project manager do next?

## Options

- **A**: Have the product owner and supplier confirm the new and dropped items are of equivalent effort, then process the swap under the existing change clause and update the SOW.
- **B**: Raise a formal contract change request through the change control process to substitute the scope, holding the supplier on the original item until it is signed.
- **C**: Let the product owner reprioritise the shared backlog so the supplier simply pulls the new item next sprint, treating it as routine agile adaptation.
- **D**: Convene the buyer's contracts officer and the supplier to renegotiate price for the new deliverable before any redirection.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The contract already contains a change-for-free mechanism for equivalent-sized swaps, so the fastest defensible path is to confirm equivalence and invoke that clause, updating the SOW to reflect it (A). Raising a fresh formal change request and freezing the supplier on the old item (B) is the right instinct when no such clause exists, but here it ignores the pre-agreed mechanism and needlessly delays value. Pure backlog reprioritisation (C) governs the team's own capacity but cannot by itself alter what a fixed-price supplier is contractually bound to deliver. Renegotiating price (D) is unnecessary because the swap is of equivalent effort and the clause holds the price; reopening price invites delay and erodes the collaborative intent. The discriminator is recognising that the contract has already provided the route, so the move is to use it, not to build a new one.
