# Question

A project manager is drafting the statement of work for a fixed-price contract to design and install an HVAC system in a new laboratory building. To reduce the chance of disputes and unpriced assumptions later, which THREE elements are most important to include explicitly in the statement of work itself? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: The specific deliverables and the quantities, locations, and performance specifications they must meet.
- **B**: The acceptance criteria and the inspection or testing the buyer will use to determine that work is complete.
- **C**: The seller's internal staffing plan and the daily working hours of its installation crew.
- **D**: Applicable standards, codes, and any period-of-performance or scheduling constraints the seller must work within.
- **E**: The buyer's source selection scoring weights used to choose this seller over the other bidders.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

A statement of work defines what is to be delivered, so it must specify the deliverables with their quantities, locations, and performance specifications (A), the acceptance criteria and inspection or testing that determine completion (B), and the applicable standards, codes, and schedule or period-of-performance constraints the work must satisfy (D); together these close the gaps that breed disputes and unpriced assumptions under a fixed price. The seller's internal staffing plan and crew hours (C) are the seller's means of delivery, not the buyer's requirement, and dictating them in the SOW intrudes on how the seller manages its own fixed-price work. The source selection scoring weights (E) belong to the buyer's evaluation process and have no place in the SOW that governs the awarded work. The discriminator is requirement-of-work content the seller must deliver and be measured against (A, B, D) versus seller-internal means (C) and buyer-internal evaluation mechanics (E) that do not define the work.
