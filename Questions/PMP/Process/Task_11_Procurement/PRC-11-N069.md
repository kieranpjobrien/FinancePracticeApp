# Question

An insurer running a hybrid delivery model is selecting a software vendor to embed alongside its own agile teams for an eighteen-month engagement with emergent, evolving requirements. Several shortlisted vendors are comparable on technical capability and price. Which TWO selection criteria are most predictive of success specifically in this agile, embedded context? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: The vendor's demonstrated ability to work iteratively, integrate into a shared cadence, and adapt to changing priorities.
- **B**: The vendor's depth of certified expertise in the specific technology stack the insurer has standardised on.
- **C**: The vendor's track record of transparent collaboration, frequent demonstrable delivery, and direct product-owner interaction.
- **D**: The vendor's financial stability and ability to retain the assigned staff for the full eighteen months.
- **E**: The maturity of the vendor's documented delivery methodology and its record of producing thorough up-front design documentation.

## Answer

A, C

## Explanation

In an embedded, emergent-requirements engagement, the criteria that most predict success are the vendor's proven ability to work iteratively, share a cadence, and adapt to changing priorities (A) and its track record of transparent collaboration, frequent demonstrable delivery, and direct product-owner interaction (C); both bear directly on how well the vendor will function day to day inside an agile team facing shifting requirements. Deep certified expertise in the standardised stack (B) is genuinely valuable and would sharpen any shortlist, but it is closer to the technical-capability dimension on which the vendors are already comparable and says little about how the vendor behaves inside an iterative, changing engagement. Financial stability and staff retention (D) reduce real continuity risk over eighteen months and are a legitimate criterion, yet a stable, well-funded vendor can still be poor at iterative collaboration, so retention predicts continuity rather than fit. A mature methodology built on thorough up-front design documentation (E) signals discipline but a heavyweight, document-first approach can actively work against emergent requirements, making it a weak predictor of success here. The discriminator is iterative-collaboration evidence that bears directly on embedded agile delivery (A, C) versus criteria that are valuable but either overlap the already-equal technical axis (B), address continuity rather than ways of working (D), or favour a predictive style at odds with the engagement (E).
