# Question

A hospital is rolling out a new patient-records platform. The integration component is core to how the hospital will work for the next decade, and the in-house development team has the skills to build it and would retain the knowledge, but they are fully committed to a regulatory release due the same quarter and pulling them off it would breach a compliance deadline. A capable external systems integrator can deliver the component on the required date at a price the project can afford. The CIO asks the project manager to recommend build or buy. What should most strongly shape the recommendation?

## Options

- **A**: That the internal team cannot take the work this quarter without breaching the regulatory deadline, so the buy option is what actually lets both pieces of work be delivered on time.
- **B**: That the component is strategically core and the in-house team would retain the integration knowledge, which favours building it despite the current capacity clash.
- **C**: That the external integrator can hit the date at an affordable price, making buy the lower-risk path to the committed go-live regardless of the knowledge trade-off.
- **D**: That the recommendation should fall out of a make-or-buy comparison of cost, capacity, risk, and strategic value, with no single factor settling it ahead of the analysis.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A make-or-buy decision does weigh cost, capacity, risk, and strategic value, but here one factor is not merely heavy, it is a hard constraint: the only window in which the internal team could build the component is already committed to a regulatory release whose deadline cannot be breached. That removes "build now" as a feasible option this quarter, so the recommendation to buy is driven by the binding capacity-and-compliance constraint (A). Strategic value and knowledge retention (B) are real and would matter if the team were available, but they cannot override an immovable compliance deadline this quarter; they argue for capturing the integration knowledge later, not for pulling the team now. Cost and the integrator hitting the date (C) are genuine inputs and point the same way, but framing buy as simply "lower-risk regardless of the knowledge trade-off" understates what is given up and is not the decisive reason. Defaulting to "run the full analysis with no factor settling it" (D) is the textbook framing and is tempting, but it is wrong for this scenario precisely because a factor does settle it: when one option is infeasible against a hard constraint, the analysis is dominated by that constraint rather than balanced evenly across all four. The fine margin is between treating this as an even four-factor weigh-up (D) and recognising that the compliance-bound capacity clash is decisive (A).
