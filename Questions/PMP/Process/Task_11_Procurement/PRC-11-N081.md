# Question

A retail company is engaging an external development house to build a customer loyalty platform. Early releases will go to real customers and the product owner anticipates reshaping the feature set as usage data comes back. Procurement requires the agreement to define what the supplier is being bought to do and to give the company a basis for paying the supplier through the engagement. The project manager must decide how to write the statement of work and pair it with a contract type. Which approach best fits the situation?

## Options

- **A**: Write a deliverable-based statement of work listing the agreed screens and features, procure it firm-fixed-price, and route reprioritisations through contract change control.
- **B**: Write a performance-based statement of work specifying the service levels the platform must sustain in production, and procure it against those measurable outcomes.
- **C**: Write a functional statement of work describing the platform's capabilities and end purpose, and procure the work on a time-and-materials basis with prioritised backlog items drawn down each iteration.
- **D**: Write a functional statement of work describing the platform's capabilities, and procure it firm-fixed-price against that functional description to cap the company's spend.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

A functional statement of work fixes the *purpose and capabilities* the buyer needs without freezing the feature list, and pairing it with time-and-materials lets the supplier be paid for backlog items actually delivered each iteration, which suits a scope the product owner intends to reshape as data returns (C). A deliverable-based SOW priced firm-fixed (A) nails down every screen now and forces every reprioritisation through contract change control, recreating the rigidity that conflicts with an evolving feature set. A performance-based SOW (B) suits buying an ongoing service against sustained service levels, but the company is buying iterative *development* of the product, not steady-state operation, so production service metrics cannot yet be the basis of award. The closest trap is D: a functional SOW is right, but pricing it firm-fixed against a description the buyer expects to keep changing pushes the supplier to pad the price for unknowns or treat each reprioritisation as a change order, defeating the flexibility the functional/T&M pairing exists to provide. The discriminator is matching the contract type to the payment basis the buyer needs, not just getting the SOW form right.
