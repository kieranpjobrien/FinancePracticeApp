# Question

A research agency is contracting a supplier for a multi-year development effort on a cost-reimbursable basis. The agency does want the supplier to keep costs disciplined, but it has accepted that the work is too exploratory for a meaningful target cost to be set up front, and what it relies on most for success is the supplier's responsiveness, the quality of its collaboration with the agency's scientists, and the inventiveness of the solutions it brings, none of which it can capture in a formula. The agency has the staff and the appetite to evaluate the supplier against agreed standards each period and adjust the fee accordingly. Two advisers disagree on the fee structure. Which best fits the agency's intent?

## Options

- **A**: Cost plus award fee, where a fee pool is earned period by period according to the agency's evaluation of the supplier against agreed performance criteria that include cost stewardship alongside the qualitative behaviours.
- **B**: Cost plus incentive fee, with a target cost, target fee, and sharing ratio, so the supplier has a concrete financial stake in controlling cost and the qualitative goals are pursued through close contract administration.
- **C**: A cost-reimbursable contract carrying both an award-fee pool for the qualitative behaviours and a separate cost-incentive sharing line, so each objective has its own dedicated fee mechanism.
- **D**: Cost plus fixed fee, reimbursing costs and paying a set fee, with the desired behaviours written into the statement of work as contractual obligations the agency enforces through performance reviews.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The deciding facts are that no meaningful target cost can be set and that the qualities the agency most depends on are judgemental rather than formulaic, which is exactly what an award-fee pool is built to reward, and cost stewardship can sit inside the award criteria alongside the behaviours so the agency's secondary cost concern is still captured (A). Cost plus incentive fee (B) is the strongest distractor because the agency genuinely wants cost discipline, but CPIF needs a credible target cost the scenario says cannot be set, and it routes the reward through a cost formula while leaving the behaviours the agency cares most about to mere administration. Running both an award-fee pool and a cost-incentive line together (C) is tempting as a way to "have both", but bolting a cost-sharing formula on still requires the target cost that does not exist and adds an instrument that dilutes and complicates the award-fee judgement that the agency is well placed to make. Cost plus fixed fee (D) can name the behaviours as obligations, but its fee never moves with performance, so it provides no reward lever for the very responsiveness and inventiveness the agency wants to incentivise. The fine margin is that A folds the cost concern into the periodic judgement the agency can actually exercise, whereas B and C insist on a formulaic cost mechanism the work cannot support.
