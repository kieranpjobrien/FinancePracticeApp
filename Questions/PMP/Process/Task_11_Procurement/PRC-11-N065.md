# Question

A local council needs to buy 500 off-the-shelf office chairs. Its facilities team has already published a detailed specification covering the exact model class, dimensions, and warranty, and several established suppliers can all meet it from stock. The council wants to place the order within a fortnight and keep the evaluation light. Which solicitation document best fits this purchase?

## Options

- **A**: A request for proposal (RFP), so suppliers can propose differentiated approaches and be scored on technical merit and price.
- **B**: A request for quotation (RFQ), since the requirement is a defined commodity and price is the deciding factor.
- **C**: A request for information (RFI), to first survey the market about what chairs are available.
- **D**: A request for proposal (RFP) with a heavily price-weighted evaluation, to keep the option of technical scoring open.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A published, detailed specification that several suppliers can already meet from stock signals a defined commodity where the only remaining variable is commercial terms, and a request for quotation is built for exactly that: it asks compliant suppliers for a price against a settled spec and supports a quick award with minimal evaluation (B). A full RFP (A) is built for differentiated solutions scored on technical merit, which adds proposal and scoring effort the council explicitly wants to avoid when the chairs are interchangeable. An RFI (C) merely gathers market information and produces no award-ready offer, so it is a preliminary step that is redundant once the specification is settled and the suppliers are known. A price-weighted RFP (D) dresses up the right intent in the wrong, heavier instrument; the technical-scoring machinery it preserves is pointless when the goods are undifferentiated and the council wants a light evaluation. The fine margin is reading the cues, settled spec, off-the-shelf supply, light and fast evaluation, as the commodity condition that makes an RFQ, not an RFP or RFI, the fit.
