# Question

A contractor on a fixed-price building contract submits a claim for additional payment, arguing that a buyer-issued design change forced rework beyond the agreed price. The buyer's project manager believes the change was minor and within the original scope, and the contractor has continued working under protest while the claim is open. The contract sets out a claims procedure and, failing that, escalation to mediation. The buyer wants the matter resolved without conceding an unfounded claim or provoking an avoidable dispute. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Compare the disputed design change against the contract's documented scope and the change records, then respond to the claim through the contract's claims procedure and seek a negotiated settlement.
- **B**: Refer the claim straight to mediation as the contract allows, so an independent third party resolves the scope question without the two entrenched parties negotiating first.
- **C**: Acknowledge the claim, then defer any assessment until the disputed work is finished, so the full cost and schedule effect of the rework can be quantified before the buyer responds.
- **D**: Issue a change order accepting the rework as new scope and adjust the price, treating the dispute as a difference over whether the change was in scope rather than over the facts.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The claims procedure begins by establishing the facts, comparing the change against the documented scope and the change records, and then working the claim through the contractual process toward a negotiated settlement (A); that is what tests the claim's merit before any money moves and keeps the relationship workable. Going straight to mediation (B) is permitted but premature: the contract makes mediation the fallback if the claims procedure fails, and skipping the direct step forfeits a cheaper, faster resolution and signals the buyer has given up on settling. Deferring all assessment until the work is complete (C) is superficially prudent but lets the claim grow unmanaged, weakens the buyer's position by delaying the scope determination, and risks the cost being quantified before the question of entitlement is even addressed. Issuing a change order that accepts the rework as new scope (D) concedes the very point in dispute, the buyer believes the change was within the original scope, so it pays an unassessed claim and rewards the contractor's interpretation without testing it. The fine margin is that B, C, and D are each real procedural options, but only A resolves entitlement first and uses the contract's own first-tier mechanism before escalating or conceding.
