# Question

Ahead of proposal submission for a large infrastructure contract, several bidders have independently raised similar questions about an ambiguous requirement, and the questions suggest the bidders are interpreting it in different ways. The project manager wants every bidder working from the same understanding and wants to hear whether further confusion remains before bids are priced. Which mechanism fits best?

## Options

- **A**: Hold a bidder conference where all bidders can raise and hear questions, with the questions and answers recorded and distributed to every bidder.
- **B**: Issue a written addendum to all bidders that clarifies the ambiguous requirement and resets the question deadline.
- **C**: Compile the questions received so far and publish a consolidated written Q&A to all bidders through the tender portal.
- **D**: Extend the submission deadline and invite any bidder with remaining questions to submit them in writing for a collective response.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The project manager wants not only to clarify but to surface and resolve divergent interpretations interactively and confirm no confusion remains, which is exactly what a bidder conference provides: bidders raise and hear questions together, and the recorded answers are distributed to all, giving both shared understanding and a live read on residual ambiguity (A). A written addendum (B) clarifies the requirement fairly and is a strong option, but it is one-directional and cannot reveal the further confusion the project manager explicitly wants to detect before pricing. A consolidated written Q&A (C) shares the same information with everyone and is fair, yet it likewise lacks the interactive element to draw out lingering misinterpretations. Extending the deadline and inviting written questions (D) buys time and keeps things fair but defers resolution and still relies on bidders articulating in writing what a conference would expose directly. The fine margin is that all four preserve fairness, but only the bidder conference combines equal-footing clarification with the two-way exchange needed to confirm understanding before bids are finalised.
