# Question

A manufacturer's project depends on a single specialist supplier for a critical custom component with a long lead time and no ready alternative. The project manager is updating the procurement risk response and wants measures that genuinely reduce the buyer's exposure. Which of the following are sound responses to this single-source dependency? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Qualify a second source in parallel so a usable alternative exists if the primary supplier fails.
- **B**: Negotiate contract terms that include performance guarantees and remedies for late or non-delivery.
- **C**: Build schedule and inventory buffer around the long-lead component to absorb a delay.
- **D**: Pay the supplier a large unconditional advance on signing so they prioritise the order ahead of their other customers.
- **E**: Accept the dependency and raise the project's overall cost contingency to cover the financial impact if the supplier fails.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Sound responses reduce or cushion the exposure itself: qualifying a second source (A), strengthening the contract with guarantees and remedies (B), and buffering schedule or inventory against the long lead time (C). A large unconditional advance (D) feels like it buys priority but it increases the buyer's exposure and weakens its leverage if the supplier underperforms, whereas payments tied to delivery milestones would be the sound version. Raising cost contingency (E) is a legitimate funding step, but on its own it only pays for the failure after the fact and does nothing to lower the probability or the schedule impact, so it is not a substitute for treating the dependency. The valid responses all lower the risk the single source creates rather than merely financing its consequences.
