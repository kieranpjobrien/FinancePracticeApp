# Question

A buyer's design change to a fixed-price contract will require the seller to do additional work. The seller has acknowledged the change verbally and started the extra work to avoid delaying the schedule, and the project manager has verbally agreed that the seller will be paid fairly for it. The project manager wants the arrangement to be sound. What is the most important action now?

## Options

- **A**: Issue a written contract change order documenting the revised scope, price, and schedule impact, signed by both authorised parties.
- **B**: Record the agreed change in the project's issue log so there is a dated entry of what was decided.
- **C**: Note the verbal agreement in the next status report so stakeholders are aware additional work is underway.
- **D**: Wait until the additional work is finished, then negotiate and document the price based on the actual effort the seller expended.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Changes to a contract are only binding and enforceable when made through the formal contract change control process, so the essential action is a written, authorised change order capturing the revised scope, price, and schedule impact, which converts a risky verbal understanding into a sound contractual amendment (A). Logging the change in the issue log (B) creates an internal record but has no contractual force and does not amend the agreement the parties are operating under. Mentioning it in a status report (C) informs stakeholders but likewise carries no contractual weight and leaves the seller's entitlement undocumented. Waiting until the work is finished to negotiate the price (D) lets the seller perform unpriced work and bargains from a weak position afterwards, exactly the dispute exposure a contemporaneous change order avoids. The fine margin is that B, C, and D all produce some record or eventual settlement, but only a formal, signed change order makes the change contractually sound at the time the work is authorised.
