# Question

A project depends on a single specialist supplier for a critical custom component with no ready alternative on the market. The project manager is assessing how to reduce the continuity risk this dependency creates. Which TWO actions most directly mitigate the risk of being left without the component? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Negotiate source-code or design-escrow and rights to the component's specifications so a second supplier could be qualified if needed.
- **B**: Hold a strategic buffer stock of the component sufficient to cover the lead time to stand up an alternative.
- **C**: Award the supplier a longer contract term to strengthen the relationship and secure goodwill.
- **D**: Increase the frequency of routine performance reporting from the supplier.
- **E**: Add a liquidated-damages clause for late delivery to the existing contract.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

The exposure is being left without the component if the sole supplier fails, so the actions that most directly mitigate it are securing design or escrow rights so a second supplier could be qualified (A) and holding a strategic buffer stock that covers the time needed to stand up an alternative (B); both create a genuine fallback. A longer contract term (C) deepens dependence on the same single source rather than reducing the consequence of its failure, so it can worsen the exposure. More frequent performance reporting (D) improves visibility of how the supplier is doing but provides no alternative source of supply if it collapses. Liquidated damages for late delivery (E) compensate for delay but do not put the component in the project manager's hands, so the project is still stranded if the supplier cannot deliver at all. The discriminator is creating an actual alternative or buffer (A, B) versus measures that tighten the relationship, increase visibility, or recover money (C, D, E) without removing the single-point-of-failure.
