# Question

A bank engaged a vendor under a fixed-price contract to deliver a defined set of mobile-banking features. Three sprints in, customer feedback from early releases has made the product owner want to swap two backlog features for two newly higher-priority ones of similar size and effort. The vendor is willing in principle but notes the swap touches the agreed scope baseline. The project manager wants the change handled in a way that keeps the contract sound and the relationship collaborative. What is the best course of action?

## Options

- **A**: Treat the feature swap as informal backlog reprioritisation within the existing fixed price, since the new features are of similar size to those removed.
- **B**: Have the vendor add the two new features now and defer any decision on removing the original two until the end of the contract.
- **C**: Leave the original scope intact, deliver it as contracted, and raise the new features in a separate follow-on procurement.
- **D**: Process the swap through the contract change control process and document it as an agreed amendment to the fixed-price scope.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Under a fixed-price contract the scope is contractually fixed, so even a like-for-like feature swap alters the agreed baseline and must go through the contract change control process and be captured as a documented amendment, which protects both parties and keeps the relationship clean (D). Treating it as informal backlog reprioritisation (A) borrows an agile habit that is valid only inside flexible-scope contracts; applied to a fixed-price deal it creates undocumented scope drift and a future dispute about what was actually owed. Adding the two new features while postponing removal of the originals (B) silently expands scope beyond the fixed price and stores up exactly the cost and scope argument the project manager wants to avoid. Refusing the change and deferring the new features to a separate procurement (C) honours the contract but needlessly denies the product owner a valuable, low-impact swap that change control could accommodate now. The fine margin is that A and B both improvise around the contract and C over-corrects by rejecting a reasonable change, whereas only D makes the change properly through the mechanism the contract provides.
