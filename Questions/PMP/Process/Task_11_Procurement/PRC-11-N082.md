# Question

A pharmaceutical manufacturer is preparing a new product launch and must establish a specialised cold-chain logistics capability to support it. The launch date is set and the firm does not operate this capability today. A finance-led analysis shows that, counting direct costs only, building the capability in-house is marginally cheaper over a three-year horizon than contracting an established third-party provider; established providers carry accreditation for temperature-controlled handling that the firm would have to build and validate itself. The sponsor asks the project manager which factor should carry the most weight in the make-or-buy recommendation. Which factor should it be?

## Options

- **A**: The three-year direct-cost comparison, since over that horizon the cheaper option delivers the better return to the firm.
- **B**: Whether standing up and validating the capability in time would draw the firm's limited specialist effort away from launch-critical work it cannot outsource.
- **C**: Whether owning the capability gives the firm a durable strategic asset it can reuse across future product launches rather than renting it each time.
- **D**: Whether the firm can negotiate strong enough service-level and liability terms with a provider to hold it accountable for handling failures.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A make-or-buy decision weighs cost against capacity, schedule, and risk together. Here the binding constraint is the fixed launch date set against a capability the firm must build and validate from nothing, so the factor that should carry the most weight is whether doing so consumes the scarce specialist effort the launch itself depends on (B) — that is what most threatens delivery and is hardest to recover if it slips. The three-year direct-cost edge (A) is the trap: a marginal saving computed on direct costs alone ignores opportunity cost, the schedule risk of building under a deadline, and the validation burden, so it should not drive the call. The strategic-asset argument (C) is genuinely valid and is the real reason a firm might choose to *make* despite the pressure — but reusability is a longer-horizon benefit that does not address the immediate launch constraint, so it ranks below it here. Provider accountability terms (D) matter to executing a buy decision well, yet they presuppose the buy choice rather than determining whether to make or buy. The discriminator is that one defensible consideration (C) pulls toward make and another (D) toward buy, so the question turns on which constraint is actually binding, not on naming a principle.
