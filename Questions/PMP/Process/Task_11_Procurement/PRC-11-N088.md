# Question

A logistics firm has contracted an external supplier to deliver part of a product increment, while the firm's own teams build the rest. The internal teams work in two-week iterations with a shared backlog that is reprioritised each iteration. The project manager is setting up how the supplier engagement will run. Which TWO arrangements most directly support managing this supplier within the firm's agile delivery model? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Have the supplier deliver working, integrated increments on the same cadence as the internal teams, with the buyer reviewing and accepting each increment.
- **B**: Structure the agreement so the buyer can reprioritise the remaining backlog and adjust or end the engagement at defined points without penalty.
- **C**: Have the supplier maintain its own separate backlog and deliver against a fixed feature list agreed at contract signature, reporting percentage complete each iteration.
- **D**: Appoint a dedicated contract administrator to capture every supplier interaction and process inter-team questions through a weekly change-control board.
- **E**: Have the supplier integrate and demonstrate its work in a hardening sprint scheduled before each major release milestone.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

Agile supplier management depends on the vendor delivering working, integrated increments on the same cadence the internal teams use, with the buyer reviewing and accepting each one so value and integration are proven continuously (A), and on commercial terms that let the buyer reprioritise the backlog and adjust or exit at defined points as priorities move (B). Maintaining a separate supplier backlog against a fixed feature list with percentage-complete reporting (C) reverts to predictive tracking and severs the supplier's work from the shared, reprioritised backlog the internal teams run. Routing inter-team questions through a weekly change-control board with a dedicated administrator (D) is the strongest trap: governance and a contract administrator are reasonable on a contracted engagement, but funnelling routine collaboration through a weekly gate inserts latency between teams that need direct, frequent contact. Integrating only in hardening sprints before each milestone (E) is more incremental than a single big-bang at the end and therefore looks defensible, but it still batches integration into periodic events rather than proving it every iteration, letting integration defects accumulate between milestones. The discriminator is continuous, flexible, integrate-every-iteration practices (A, B) versus arrangements that batch scope, communication, or integration into periodic gates (C, D, E).
