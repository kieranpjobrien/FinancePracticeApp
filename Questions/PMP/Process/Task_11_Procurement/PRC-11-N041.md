# Question

A utility is contracting a vendor to develop a bespoke control system using a novel technical approach. A rough order-of-magnitude target cost can be agreed, but the final cost is genuinely hard to predict. The buyer is willing to reimburse legitimate costs, expects the design to mature as work proceeds, and wants the vendor financially motivated to keep the final cost near the target. The CFO will not approve a structure where the vendor's fee rises as costs rise. Which contract structure fits best?

## Options

- **A**: Cost plus award fee, with a discretionary fee pool the buyer awards each period based on overall performance.
- **B**: Fixed price incentive fee, with a target cost, target profit, and a ceiling price the vendor cannot exceed.
- **C**: Cost plus incentive fee, with a sharing ratio splitting the variance between target and actual cost.
- **D**: Cost plus fixed fee, with the fee fixed as a dollar amount set at the target cost.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The buyer accepts cost reimbursement and wants a direct, formula-based link between final cost and vendor reward, which a cost plus incentive fee delivers through its sharing ratio on the target/actual variance (C). Cost plus award fee (A) ties the fee to subjective periodic judgement rather than to the cost outcome the CFO cares about, so the cost-control incentive is weaker and disputable. Fixed price incentive fee (B) imposes a ceiling price the vendor cannot exceed, which a vendor cannot rationally accept when the scope is still maturing and the final cost is genuinely unpredictable. Cost plus fixed fee (D) reimburses costs but the flat fee creates no incentive to reduce them, failing the explicit motivation requirement. The CFO's objection rules out a percentage-of-cost fee but not the incentive structure in C, whose fee is anchored to the target, not driven up by overruns.
