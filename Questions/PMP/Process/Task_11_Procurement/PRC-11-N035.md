# Question

A buyer must engage specialist consultants to investigate an intermittent production fault within days. The effort needed is unknown until the cause is found, but a procurement manager objects that an open-ended arrangement gives the buyer no cost ceiling and little protection if the diagnosis drags on. The project manager still needs work to start almost immediately. Which contracting choice best balances the urgency with the buyer's cost-exposure concern?

## Options

- **A**: A time-and-materials engagement with agreed rates, a not-to-exceed ceiling, and a review point at which scope and continuation are reassessed.
- **B**: A pure time-and-materials engagement at agreed rates with no ceiling, relying on the consultants to stop once the fault is found.
- **C**: A firm-fixed-price contract for a defined diagnostic deliverable so the buyer's cost is capped from the outset.
- **D**: A cost-plus-fixed-fee contract reimbursing the consultants' costs plus a set fee for the investigation.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The work must start at once and its extent is unknown, which points to time and materials, but the buyer's specific worry is cost exposure, so the right form is T&M bounded by a not-to-exceed ceiling and a review point that caps and re-scopes the open commitment (A). Pure uncapped T&M (B) starts quickly but leaves exactly the unlimited exposure the procurement manager objected to. Firm fixed price (C) cannot be priced honestly when neither party knows how much investigation is required, and forcing it either inflates the price or invites disputes. Cost plus fixed fee (D) reimburses cost without the rate-based simplicity or the ceiling that addresses the buyer's concern, and it still leaves total cost open-ended. The fine margin is between unbounded and bounded T&M, and the buyer's cost concern makes the ceiling the deciding feature.
