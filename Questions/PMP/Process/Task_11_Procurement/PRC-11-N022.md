# Question

A government agency must replace an ageing data-analytics platform. The sponsor has approved a budget and wants a competition launched this quarter to keep the programme on track. The procurement lead, however, cannot yet describe the solution in enough detail to evaluate competing offers fairly: the agency does not know which architectures the current market actually supports or what they realistically cost, and getting that wrong would lock in the wrong requirement. What should the procurement lead do first?

## Options

- **A**: Issue a request for information to the market to learn the available approaches and indicative costs, accepting that the formal competition starts after the requirement is shaped.
- **B**: Issue the request for proposal now against the agency's best-available requirement, and refine scope through clarifications and addenda as suppliers raise questions during the bid period.
- **C**: Run a structured market analysis from analyst reports and the agency's own subject-matter experts to define the requirement, then go straight to a request for proposal.
- **D**: Release a draft request for proposal for industry comment, inviting suppliers to critique the requirement before the agency finalises and re-issues it for award.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The deciding facts are that the agency genuinely does not know what the market offers or what it costs, and that defining the requirement wrong is the main risk. A request for information targets exactly that gap by gathering capabilities and indicative pricing directly from the suppliers who would bid, which is the soundest basis for then shaping the requirement (A). Issuing the RFP now and refining via clarifications (B) commits the competition to a requirement the agency has already judged it cannot specify, and clarifications cannot repair a fundamentally mis-framed requirement without unfairly moving the goalposts mid-bid. A desk-based market analysis (C) is a reasonable input but draws on second-hand sources and the agency's own people, the very knowledge the scenario says is missing, so it is weaker than asking the market itself. A draft-RFP-for-comment (D) does engage the market, but it presupposes the agency can already draft a near-final requirement to critique, when the problem is that it cannot yet; it is a refinement tool, not a discovery one. The fine margin is between engaging the market to discover (A) and engaging it to refine or validate (C and D): at this stage the agency needs discovery.
