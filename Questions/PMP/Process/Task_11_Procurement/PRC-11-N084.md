# Question

A media company has selected an external agency it expects to use for a series of small, related digital projects over the next two years. The agency's commercial and legal terms are acceptable to the company, but the work to be done in each project will only become clear close to the time. The project manager is deciding how to structure the contractual relationship so each new project can start quickly. Which contracting approach best fits?

## Options

- **A**: Negotiate a master services agreement covering the common terms once, then authorise each project under a lightweight statement of work or work order beneath it.
- **B**: Negotiate a single firm-fixed-price contract now that covers all the anticipated projects across the two years.
- **C**: Negotiate one cost-reimbursable contract now with a not-to-exceed ceiling covering the full two years of expected work.
- **D**: Negotiate a fresh standalone contract for each project once its scope is defined, reusing the agency's terms as the starting draft.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A master services agreement settles the durable legal, liability, IP, and rate terms once, and then each individual project is authorised by a short statement of work or work order beneath it — the efficient pattern for a stream of related engagements that share terms but whose individual scope is not yet known (A). A single firm-fixed-price contract for everything (B) cannot be priced fairly, because the scope of the later projects is undefined, and it locks both parties to commitments they cannot yet specify. A single cost-reimbursable contract with a ceiling (C) is the closest trap: it does cover the whole period under one agreement, but folding many distinct small projects into one open contract blurs which costs belong to which project and weakens the company's ability to scope, accept, and close each one on its own terms. A fresh standalone contract per project (D) works, but it re-opens the full agreement each time, reintroducing the negotiation overhead the structure is meant to avoid. The discriminator is separating durable common terms (the MSA) from per-project scope (the work order), rather than choosing one contract type to carry both.
