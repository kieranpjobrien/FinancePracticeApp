# Question

During evaluation of proposals for a major consultancy contract, one panel member privately tells the project manager that the principal of a bidding firm is a close personal friend, though the panel member insists this will not affect their scoring. Scoring has not yet been finalised. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Accept the panel member's assurance of impartiality and allow them to continue scoring, since they disclosed the relationship in good faith.
- **B**: Disqualify the friend's firm from the competition to remove any possibility of bias.
- **C**: Have the panel member recuse themselves from scoring that bidder and document the disclosure and the action taken.
- **D**: Replace the entire evaluation panel and restart scoring from the beginning to be safe.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

A disclosed conflict of interest must be managed to keep the evaluation demonstrably fair, and the proportionate, defensible step is to recuse the conflicted panel member from scoring that bidder and document both the disclosure and the action taken (C). Accepting the assurance and letting them continue (A) leaves an undisclosed-to-the-record conflict influencing the outcome and exposes the award to a fairness challenge however sincere the panel member is. Disqualifying the friend's firm (B) punishes a bidder for a relationship it may not have engineered and unfairly narrows the competition, when the problem lies with the evaluator, not the bidder. Replacing the whole panel and restarting (D) is a disproportionate overreaction that wastes effort and discards valid scoring by unconflicted members for the sake of one member's single conflict. The fine margin is that A under-reacts, B and D over-react in different directions, while only C neutralises the specific conflict and preserves an auditable, fair evaluation.
