# Question

A buyer is confident it needs an enterprise scheduling solution and roughly what business outcomes it must achieve, but it does not yet know which technical approaches or suppliers exist, and its detailed requirements are still soft. It intends to run a competitive bid later but wants market input to firm up the requirements first, without implying any commitment to award. Which approach should the project manager take now?

## Options

- **A**: Issue a request for information to gather supplier capabilities and market approaches, then use the responses to refine requirements before soliciting bids.
- **B**: Issue a request for proposal stating the business outcomes and asking suppliers to propose and price their own solution approaches.
- **C**: Run a request for quotation against the firmest version of the requirements the buyer can write today to get comparable prices early.
- **D**: Pre-qualify a shortlist of suppliers through an expression-of-interest stage, then go straight to bid with that shortlist.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The buyer's requirements are still soft and it explicitly wants market input with no commitment, which is exactly the purpose of a request for information: survey capabilities and approaches, then refine the requirements before any competitive bid (A). A request for proposal asking suppliers to propose and price solutions (B) can suit outcome-based buying, but here it is premature and risks non-comparable bids and wasted bidder effort while the requirements are still forming. A request for quotation (C) needs a firm specification to price against, which the buyer admits it does not have, so it would produce prices for the wrong thing. An expression-of-interest pre-qualification (D) screens who may bid but does not gather the technical and market knowledge needed to shape the requirements, so it skips the step the buyer actually needs. The discriminator is that the immediate need is to learn the market and sharpen requirements, not to solicit, price, or shortlist.
