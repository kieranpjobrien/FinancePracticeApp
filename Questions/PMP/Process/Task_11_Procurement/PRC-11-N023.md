# Question

Three vendors have responded to an RFP for a critical telemetry subsystem. The selection committee has scored all three against the weighted criteria, and Vendor Y has the highest total score. During the readout, two committee members argue that Vendor Z's design is clearly the strongest engineering and that the scoring under-weighted technical merit, while a third notes Vendor X's price is materially lower and worth a second look. The project manager must take the committee to a decision. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Confirm the award to Vendor Y, the highest-scoring bid under the criteria, while recording the committee's technical and price observations as risks to manage in the contract.
- **B**: Re-weight the criteria to give technical merit more emphasis, re-score the three bids, and award to whichever bid now leads.
- **C**: Open clarifications with Vendor Z on its design and Vendor X on its price, then have the committee re-score all three with the new information.
- **D**: Take the three scored bids to the sponsor and ask the sponsor to choose, since the committee cannot reach consensus.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The criteria were weighted and agreed before bids were opened, the committee has already scored against them, and Vendor Y leads; the award follows that result, and the committee's misgivings are handled as risks to manage rather than reasons to overturn the scoring (A). Re-weighting and re-scoring after the scores are known (B) is the most dangerous option precisely because it looks rigorous: changing the weights once you can see which bid each change favours retro-fits the model to a preferred winner and destroys the defensibility the pre-agreed weighting existed to provide. Re-opening clarifications (C) is legitimate only to resolve a genuine ambiguity or deficiency in a proposal, not to give two bids a second bite because committee members prefer them, and using it that way reopens a settled competition and risks unequal treatment. Escalating the tie to the sponsor (D) misreads the situation: there is no tie, there is a clear top score that some members simply dislike, and handing the pick upward substitutes authority for the agreed objective basis. The fine margin is that B and C both dress up overturning the result as further analysis, whereas the sound move is to stand on the scoring and carry the concerns forward as managed risk.
