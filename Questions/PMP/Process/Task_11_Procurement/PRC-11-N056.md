# Question

A buyer needs a quantity of standard rack-mount servers from a published catalogue, to a specification that will not change, delivered to a date that is already fixed by a downstream commissioning slot. Quotes for this hardware have not moved in the past year, the whole purchase will be complete within three months, and the procurement team is small and already stretched. The category manager asks which contract type to put in the RFP. Which is the best fit?

## Options

- **A**: Fixed price incentive fee.
- **B**: Firm fixed price.
- **C**: Fixed price with economic price adjustment.
- **D**: Firm fixed price with an early-delivery bonus and late-delivery penalty.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The facts describe a defined commodity with a stable specification, flat prices, a short horizon, and limited capacity to administer anything elaborate, and firm fixed price matches all of them: the seller carries any cost overrun and there is nothing extra to manage (B). Fixed price incentive fee (A) introduces a target cost and a sharing ratio; an incentive mechanism earns its keep only where there is performance the buyer wants to motivate, and a fixed-spec catalogue buy offers none, so the administration is pure overhead. Fixed price with economic price adjustment (C) exists to index the price to economic movement over a contract's life, which protects against volatility that this market and three-month horizon simply do not present. Firm fixed price with delivery bonus and penalty clauses (D) is still fixed price but bolts on incentive terms to manage, and with the date already locked by the commissioning slot and the team stretched, those clauses add effort to chase an outcome the schedule already secures. The discrimination is mapping the facts to mechanics: every alternative carries an indexing or incentive feature whose triggering condition is absent here, leaving plain firm fixed price as the one that fits without surplus machinery.
