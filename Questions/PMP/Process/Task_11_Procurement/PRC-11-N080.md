# Question

A telecommunications company has contracted an external team to deliver components that will be integrated into its own product each iteration. Early on, the vendor's increments pass the vendor's own tests but repeatedly fail to integrate cleanly with the in-house build, and disputes arise over whether a given increment is genuinely "done". The project manager wants to prevent recurrence. Which TWO actions best address the underlying problem? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Agree a shared, explicit definition of done that includes successful integration with the in-house build and the company's own acceptance tests.
- **B**: Establish continuous integration of the vendor's increments into the shared build so integration problems surface immediately rather than at hand-off.
- **C**: Require the vendor to deliver larger, less frequent increments so there are fewer integration events to manage.
- **D**: Have the company accept the vendor's increments on the vendor's own test evidence to avoid further disputes about completion.
- **E**: Withhold all vendor payment until the entire contracted scope has been delivered and integrated at the end.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

The recurring failure is a gap between the vendor's notion of "done" and what integration into the buyer's product actually requires, so the two actions that address the root cause are agreeing a shared definition of done that includes successful integration and the buyer's acceptance tests (A) and setting up continuous integration so integration defects surface immediately rather than festering until hand-off (B). Delivering larger, less frequent increments (C) hides integration problems for longer and makes them costlier to diagnose, worsening the issue rather than fixing it. Accepting increments on the vendor's own test evidence (D) entrenches the very mismatch causing the disputes, since the vendor's tests are exactly what is failing to predict clean integration. Withholding all payment until the end (E) is a blunt commercial lever that neither clarifies "done" nor reveals integration faults early, and it damages the collaborative relationship without solving the technical root cause. The discriminator is closing the definition-of-done gap and shortening the integration feedback loop (A, B) versus measures that defer integration, accept the flawed standard, or apply commercial pressure (C, D, E) without fixing why increments fail to integrate.
