# Question

A buyer and a development supplier have agreed a cost-plus-incentive-fee contract for a package whose scope is reasonably understood but not fully fixed. They must now set the terms of the incentive. The buyer wants the supplier genuinely motivated to control cost, but also wants the supplier to keep raising legitimate problems early and to choose sound engineering over corner-cutting. The supplier, for its part, will not sign up to terms that expose it to a cost outcome it cannot influence. Which approach to structuring the incentive best serves the buyer's intent?

## Options

- **A**: Set the target cost from a jointly reviewed estimate and apply a moderate buyer/supplier sharing ratio on both underruns and overruns, so the supplier shares the gain and the pain but is not exposed to the full swing.
- **B**: Set an ambitious target cost below the supplier's estimate and a high supplier share of any underrun, so the reward for beating the target is large enough to drive aggressive cost control.
- **C**: Apply the sharing ratio to underruns only and shield the supplier from any share of an overrun, so the supplier is rewarded for savings without being penalised when costs rise.
- **D**: Set a generous target cost the supplier is very likely to beat and a standard share, so the supplier reliably earns extra fee and stays motivated and cooperative across the package.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A jointly grounded target cost with a moderate two-sided sharing ratio gives the supplier a real stake in beating the target while capping its exposure to the part of the outcome it cannot fully control, which is what both keeps it motivated to control cost and keeps it willing to surface problems and engineer soundly rather than hide cost to protect its fee (A). An ambitious below-estimate target with a high underrun share (B) looks like the strongest cost driver, but a target the supplier privately believes is unachievable demotivates rather than motivates and tempts it to cut corners or suppress bad news to chase an out-of-reach reward, working against the very behaviours the buyer named. Sharing underruns only and shielding the supplier from overruns (C) removes the downside that makes the incentive bite, so the supplier carries no real cost discipline on the overrun side and the structure drifts toward cost-plus behaviour. A deliberately generous, easily-beaten target (D) guarantees the supplier extra fee but rewards the appearance of a saving against a soft baseline rather than genuine cost control, so the buyer pays incentive fee for performance it would have got anyway. The fine margin is that B, C, and D each optimise one feeling about the incentive, while only A balances motivation, shared risk, and the honesty-and-quality behaviours the buyer asked for.
