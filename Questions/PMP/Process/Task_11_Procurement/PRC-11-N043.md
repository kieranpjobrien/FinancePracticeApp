# Question

A manufacturer needs a custom analytics module for a new product line that must launch within a narrow market window. The module is not core to the firm's competitive advantage and carries no sensitive data. Internal engineering could build it in about nine months; two established vendors sell configurable modules that would be ready in about three. A make-or-buy analysis has been completed and shows total cost of ownership is broadly similar either way, with buy modestly cheaper. The internal team has the capacity and would prefer to build it. Which recommendation should the project manager put forward?

## Options

- **A**: Build internally, because the team has capacity and retaining the capability strengthens the firm for future product lines.
- **B**: Build internally, because in-house development gives tighter control over quality and integration with the firm's other systems.
- **C**: Buy the vendor module, because the schedule advantage is decisive for a non-core component when cost and risk are otherwise comparable.
- **D**: Buy the vendor module, and additionally negotiate source code escrow and exit rights to remove long-term dependency concerns.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

With the analysis showing comparable cost and risk, the deciding factor is what the scenario stresses: a non-core component on a narrow market window, where the vendor's six-month schedule advantage directly protects the launch and the strategic case for owning the capability is weak (C). Building internally for capacity or capability reasons (A) optimises for a benefit the firm does not need on a non-core module and forfeits the time-to-market that matters most. Choosing build for control over quality and integration (B) is a legitimate consideration in general but does not outweigh the schedule risk to the launch when the component is non-core and the analysis already shows risk is comparable. Adding escrow and exit rights (D) is sound contracting hygiene but is a refinement of the buy decision, not the recommendation itself, and is not the discriminator the question asks for. The fine margin is recognising that schedule, not cost or capability, governs this specific decision.
