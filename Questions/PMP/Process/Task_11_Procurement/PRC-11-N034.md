# Question

A regulatory update has altered the requirement under an active fixed-price contract, so the buyer must change the seller's scope of work. The change is genuinely new scope, it is urgent, and the buyer wants the contract to stay enforceable and the relationship sound. Which actions should form part of handling this contract change correctly? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Assess the cost, schedule, and risk impact of the change before agreeing it with the seller.
- **B**: Process the change through the contract's change control system and capture it as a written amendment before the new work proceeds.
- **C**: Negotiate an equitable adjustment to price and schedule that reflects the changed requirement.
- **D**: Authorise the seller to start the new work now on a documented interim instruction, with the priced amendment to follow, to protect the regulatory deadline.
- **E**: Have the seller proceed on a verbal go-ahead given the urgency, and formalise the amendment at the next monthly contract review.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Correct handling impact-assesses the change first (A), runs it through the contract change control system and records it as a written amendment before the work proceeds (B), and reflects it in a negotiated equitable adjustment to price and schedule (C). Authorising work on a "documented interim instruction" (D) sounds disciplined but it commits the buyer to undefined cost and scope ahead of agreement and pre-empts the negotiation, which is why the amendment must be settled before proceeding rather than promised afterwards. A verbal go-ahead formalised weeks later (E) is the same exposure in a weaker form, leaving what was authorised disputable precisely on urgent, high-stakes scope. Urgency is handled by expediting the formal change, not by starting ahead of it.
