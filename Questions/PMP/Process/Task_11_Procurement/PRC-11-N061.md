# Question

A retailer's product team runs two-week sprints and has just brought in an external development house to build a payments module alongside the in-house squad. The vendor delivers good code but works to its own monthly release rhythm, and in the first two sprints its increments arrived too late to be reviewed in the team's sprint reviews, leaving the product owner unable to give timely feedback. The contract is a master services agreement with statements of work per release. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Amend the next statement of work to require the vendor to deliver a demonstrable increment by each sprint review date, making the cadence a contractual commitment.
- **B**: Work with the vendor to align its delivery to the team's sprint cadence so its increments are demonstrable and reviewable each iteration.
- **C**: Have the in-house squad review the vendor's code asynchronously between sprints so feedback no longer depends on the sprint review.
- **D**: Extend the iteration length for the combined effort so the vendor's monthly rhythm fits inside a single iteration.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The core problem is a cadence mismatch starving the product owner of timely feedback, and the first move is to work with the vendor to integrate its delivery into the team's iteration rhythm so its increments are inspected and adapted every sprint (B); collaboration ahead of contractual escalation is how agile procurement keeps a new vendor relationship productive. Amending the statement of work to mandate sprint-aligned increments (A) is a legitimate tool and may follow, but reaching for a contractual instrument before even trying to solve a two-sprint-old delivery rhythm jumps the gun, hardens the relationship, and still does not address why the vendor's process produces late increments. Asynchronous between-sprint review (C) creates a parallel, less transparent feedback loop that bypasses the very ceremony designed to give the product owner and stakeholders a shared look at working software. Stretching the iteration length to a month (D) bends the whole team to the vendor's rhythm, slowing feedback for everyone and discarding the fast inspect-and-adapt loop the retailer benefits from. The fine margin is that A is the right idea applied too soon and too formally, while C and D accommodate the vendor's existing rhythm; only B fixes the rhythm collaboratively so feedback flows on the team's cadence first.
