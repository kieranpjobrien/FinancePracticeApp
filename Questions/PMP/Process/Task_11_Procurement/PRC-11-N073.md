# Question

A vendor has delivered the final increment of a hardware-supply contract and asked to be paid the retention and have the contract closed. Before authorising closure, the project manager wants to be sure nothing essential is left undone. Which step is most important to complete first?

## Options

- **A**: Verify that all deliverables have been formally inspected and accepted against the contract's acceptance criteria, with the results documented.
- **B**: Obtain the project sponsor's verbal agreement that the vendor performed well overall.
- **C**: Archive the vendor's invoices and correspondence so a complete financial record exists.
- **D**: Capture lessons learned from the procurement for use on future contracts.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Procurement closure hinges on confirming that the contracted work is actually complete, so the essential first step is verified, documented acceptance of all deliverables against the contract's acceptance criteria, because that is what justifies releasing retention and formally closing the contract (A). A sponsor's verbal impression of overall performance (B) is no substitute for objective acceptance evidence and cannot establish that every deliverable met its criteria. Archiving invoices and correspondence (C) is a legitimate part of closure, but records are completed around and after acceptance, not before it, and archiving alone does not prove the work was accepted. Capturing lessons learned (D) is valuable for future procurements yet is an organisational-process improvement step, not the gate that determines whether this contract can be closed. The fine margin is that B, C, and D are all genuine closure activities, but only formal verification and documented acceptance directly confirms the deliverables are complete and the contract can rightfully be closed.
