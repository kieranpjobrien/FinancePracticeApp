# Question

A buyer has a complex, outcome-based requirement and has shortlisted three capable suppliers who each understand the problem well but have proposed materially different solution approaches in early discussions. The buyer wants the winning supplier to commit to the outcome at a firm, competed price, and wants the evaluation to weigh each proposed approach alongside cost. Because the approaches differ so much, the procurement lead is unsure the requirement can be specified tightly enough for like-for-like bids in a single pass. How should the solicitation be run?

## Options

- **A**: Run a single-stage RFP with one fixed specification and weighted technical and cost criteria, scoring whichever firm-priced proposal best meets the stated requirement.
- **B**: Run a two-stage RFP: a first stage that refines the requirement through the suppliers' differing approaches without price, then a priced second stage in which all three bid firm prices against the settled basis.
- **C**: Run a single-stage RFP but defer pricing, selecting the strongest approach first and then negotiating a firm price with that single supplier alone.
- **D**: Run the RFP and let each supplier bid its own approach at a firm price in one pass, then normalise the differing bids during evaluation so they can be compared on cost.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The buyer wants approach and price both evaluated and a firm competed commitment, which is the RFP family, but the deciding complication is that the approaches differ so much the requirement cannot yet be specified for clean like-for-like firm bids. A two-stage RFP resolves exactly that: the first stage uses the suppliers' approaches to settle a common basis, and the second stage takes firm competed prices against it, preserving both competition and comparability (B). A single-stage RFP on one fixed specification (A) is the standard answer and would be right if the requirement were already specifiable, but here forcing one specification up front either suppresses the differing approaches the buyer wants to evaluate or yields bids that are not truly comparable. Deferring price and negotiating with one supplier (C) abandons the firm competed price the buyer requires by collapsing the competition to a single source before price is tested. Letting each supplier bid its own approach in one pass and "normalising" afterwards (D) is the most tempting trap, because it looks like it keeps the competition intact, but normalising fundamentally different firm-priced solutions during evaluation is subjective and contestable and does not give the buyer the comparable basis a settled requirement would. The fine margin is between a single-stage RFP (A) and a two-stage RFP (B): the unspecifiable, divergent approaches are what make the staged route the better fit.
