# Question

A portfolio manager is matching contract types to four procurements, each with a different scope certainty and risk-sharing need. Match each procurement situation to the contract type whose risk and incentive profile fits it best.

## Items

- **1**: Requirements will emerge continuously and the buyer wants a vendor team to follow shifting priorities, while keeping annual spend predictable for budgeting.
- **2**: Scope is well defined and stable, and the buyer wants the seller to bear all cost-overrun risk for a single agreed price.
- **3**: Scope is broadly understood but not fully fixed, and the buyer wants the seller to share savings and overruns within a ceiling price it cannot exceed.
- **4**: Scope is genuinely uncertain and exploratory, and the buyer will reimburse actual effort but wants a cap beyond which work pauses for authorisation.

## Matches

- **A**: Firm fixed price
- **B**: Capacity-based (dedicated team) contract
- **C**: Time and materials with a not-to-exceed ceiling
- **D**: Fixed price incentive fee

## Answer

1-B, 2-A, 3-D, 4-C

## Explanation

A capacity-based dedicated-team contract funds a stable team's capacity at an agreed annual rate while the product owner redirects priorities, matching emergent scope with predictable spend (1-B). Firm fixed price places all overrun risk on the seller for one agreed price and suits well-defined, stable scope (2-A). Fixed price incentive fee combines a target cost, a sharing ratio, and a ceiling the seller cannot exceed, fitting broadly understood scope where the buyer wants shared savings and overruns under a cap (3-D). Time and materials with a not-to-exceed ceiling reimburses actual effort for genuinely exploratory work while capping exposure and pausing for authorisation at the ceiling (4-C). The traps are that situations 1 and 4 both involve uncertainty, so the discriminator is a funded standing team for continuously shifting priorities (capacity-based) versus capped reimbursement for finite exploratory work (T&M NTE); and situations 2 and 3 are both fairly defined, separated by who bears overrun risk, all to the seller (FFP) versus shared under a ceiling (FPIF).
