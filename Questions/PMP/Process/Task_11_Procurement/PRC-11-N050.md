# Question

Under a fixed-price contract, a seller submits a claim asserting that a buyer-issued design clarification mid-contract constituted extra work and seeks additional payment. The buyer's technical team believes the clarification merely restated what the original specification already required. The contract sets out a tiered dispute resolution procedure: good-faith negotiation, then mediation, then binding arbitration. Both parties have an ongoing working relationship and more deliverables to come. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Convene a joint technical review to compare the clarification against the original specification line by line, then decide whether the claim has merit.
- **B**: Open good-faith negotiation with the seller to reach a mutual position on whether the clarification changed scope, escalating to mediation only if that fails.
- **C**: Issue a written determination explaining why the buyer considers the clarification within the original scope and invite the seller to dispute it formally.
- **D**: Refer the claim to mediation now so a neutral third party can assess the merits before positions harden.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The contract prescribes good-faith negotiation as the first tier, and with an ongoing relationship the cooperative, lowest-cost route is to negotiate toward a shared position before invoking any external step (B). A joint technical review (A) is a useful input and would likely feature within the negotiation, but framed as a standalone next step it is the buyer assembling evidence to judge merit rather than engaging the contractual process of resolving the claim with the seller. Issuing a written determination and inviting a formal dispute (C) is an adversarial opening that pushes the parties toward the later tiers prematurely and can sour a relationship that still has work to run. Going to mediation now (D) skips the negotiation tier the contract requires first and incurs third-party cost before the parties have tried to settle it themselves. The fine margin is recognising negotiation as the mandated first move (B) over evidence-gathering, determination, or jumping a tier.
