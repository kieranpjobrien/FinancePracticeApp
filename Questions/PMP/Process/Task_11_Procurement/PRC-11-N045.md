# Question

A government agency is preparing a tender for the construction of a data centre. The requirements are stable and fixed by regulation, but past tenders by the agency have been plagued by mid-delivery arguments over whether particular work was actually included in the contracted scope. The project manager is deciding where to focus effort in the procurement package to prevent a repeat. Which element will most directly reduce those scope-inclusion disputes?

## Options

- **A**: A well-defined formal change control procedure in the contract for handling scope adjustments after award.
- **B**: A requirements traceability matrix linking each contracted deliverable back to a regulatory obligation.
- **C**: Detailed acceptance criteria and a clear scope baseline in the procurement statement of work, defining exactly what is and is not included.
- **D**: An integrated change-and-claims clause specifying how disputed scope is escalated and adjudicated.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The disputes are about what was included in the agreed scope, so the most direct preventive measure is to remove the ambiguity at source: a precise scope baseline and explicit acceptance criteria in the SOW define the boundary the seller prices and delivers against (C). A change control procedure (A) governs scope that changes after award, not disagreement over what the original scope was, so it manages the consequence rather than preventing the cause. A traceability matrix (B) is a useful planning and verification aid and may sharpen the requirements, but it documents linkage rather than contractually fixing the inclusion boundary the seller is bound to. A change-and-claims clause (D) provides a route to resolve disputes once they arise, which is valuable but reactive; the question asks what most directly prevents the dispute, and only C removes the underlying ambiguity before delivery begins.
