# Question

A pharmaceutical company is building a clinical-trials data platform. One part is a data-ingestion and analytics engine that encodes the trial-design approach the company's scientists have refined over years; the rest is user authentication, hosting, and notifications, all readily available from vendors. Budget and schedule are both tight, and an external firm has quoted attractively to build the whole platform end to end, faster than the company could staff the work itself. The project manager is running the make-or-buy analysis. Which question should most shape the decision on the analytics engine specifically?

## Options

- **A**: Whether the firm's end-to-end quote, delivered sooner, beats the fully loaded internal cost and schedule for that part.
- **B**: Whether handing the engine and the commodity parts to one vendor would cut integration risk and the number of contracts to manage.
- **C**: Whether the in-house team has the specialist capacity to build the engine without delaying the schedule the budget assumes.
- **D**: Whether outsourcing the engine would place the company's distinctive trial-design approach in an external firm's hands and loosen the company's control of it.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Make-or-buy weighs cost, schedule, and capacity, but for a capability that embeds the organisation's own hard-won, differentiating know-how, the decisive question is whether buying it surrenders that know-how or control of it, because that loss can outlast and outweigh any price or schedule gain (D). The cheaper, faster end-to-end quote (A) is a real input and rightly drives the decision on the commodity parts, but allowing it to govern the engine outsources the differentiator on price and speed alone. Consolidating to one vendor to cut integration risk and contract count (B) is a genuine convenience, yet it is a thin reason to give an outside firm the company's distinctive approach. Internal capacity and schedule (C) are legitimate and may well justify buying the commodity components, but a capacity gap that can be staffed or sequenced is a weaker consideration than the lasting loss of a strategic capability. The fine margin is that A, B, and C are each valid and even decisive for the commodity components, so the question turns on recognising that the engine is the one element where strategic-control risk should outrank cost, convenience, and capacity.
