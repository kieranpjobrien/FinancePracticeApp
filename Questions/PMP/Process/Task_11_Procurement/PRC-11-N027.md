# Question

A logistics company has outsourced warehouse fit-out to a contractor under a fixed-price contract. Three months in, the buyer's operations team complains the contractor is behind and quality is slipping, but the project manager has been relying on the contractor's monthly self-reported status. What should the project manager do to regain control of the engagement?

## Options

- **A**: Issue a formal notice of default to the contractor to compel them to recover the schedule.
- **B**: Withhold the next progress payment until the contractor demonstrates the work is back on track.
- **C**: Replace the contractor, since slipping schedule and quality this early signal they cannot deliver.
- **D**: Begin actively administering the contract by verifying progress against agreed deliverables and inspecting work, not relying on self-reporting alone.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Effective contract administration means the buyer independently monitors and verifies seller performance against the contract rather than passively accepting self-reported status (D). A default notice (A) is a serious escalation that should follow evidence, which the project manager does not yet have because they have not been verifying performance. Withholding payment (B) may be contractually available but is premature and adversarial before progress has been objectively assessed. Replacing the contractor (C) is disproportionate and disruptive on the strength of unverified complaints. The first move is to establish the real position through active administration.
