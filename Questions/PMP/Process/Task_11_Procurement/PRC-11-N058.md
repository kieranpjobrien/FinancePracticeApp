# Question

A buyer wants to procure development of a new digital service from an external vendor experienced in iterative delivery, but is wary of committing the full budget up front when requirements are expected to evolve. The buyer wants to fund the work as it proves value and retain the ability to stop. Which contracting approach best balances that protection with incremental delivery?

## Options

- **A**: A single fixed-price contract for the full anticipated scope, with a money-for-nothing clause letting the buyer terminate early and pay a defined fraction of the remaining value.
- **B**: A capped time-and-materials contract with a not-to-exceed ceiling, releasing funded work to the vendor sprint by sprint.
- **C**: A series of fixed-price increments, each scoped and priced one iteration ahead, with the buyer able to stop after any increment.
- **D**: A long-term master agreement with the vendor under which individual statements of work are issued and funded as requirements firm up.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Fixed-price increments scoped and priced one iteration ahead let the buyer fund small, bounded commitments as value is proven and walk away cleanly after any increment, which most directly matches a wary buyer's wish to limit exposure while delivering incrementally (C). A single full-scope fixed price with a money-for-nothing exit (A) is genuinely agile-friendly and protects against being locked in, but it still forces premature definition and pricing of the whole evolving scope up front, the very thing the buyer wants to avoid, and the early-exit payment can be contentious. A capped time-and-materials contract (B) bounds total spend and supports iterative funding, but the buyer carries the cost risk within the ceiling and gets less price certainty per increment than fixed-price increments give. A master agreement with SOWs issued as requirements firm up (D) is a sound vehicle for flexible engagement, yet on its own it specifies no pricing or exit discipline and depends entirely on how each SOW is written. The fine margin is that C builds protection and exit points into the structure itself, whereas A, B, and D each leave one of scope-definition, cost risk, or pricing discipline less well controlled for this buyer's specific concern.
