# Question

Midway through a two-year facilities-management contract, a project manager notices the vendor is consistently meeting the letter of its service levels but response quality is slipping and minor issues are recurring. The contract includes monthly performance reviews and a defined set of key performance indicators. Nothing has yet breached a threshold. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Use the monthly performance review to raise the emerging quality trend with the vendor, agree corrective actions, and document the discussion.
- **B**: Record the slippage as a formal written performance concern under the KPI regime, creating a contractual paper trail before raising it in conversation.
- **C**: Issue a formal cure notice now to put the vendor on notice that performance is unsatisfactory and protect the buyer's position.
- **D**: Hold off on raising it until the next data point confirms a sustained trend rather than normal month-to-month variation.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Contract administration is proactive and collaborative: the monthly performance review exists precisely to surface an emerging trend, agree corrective action, and document the exchange before a soft decline hardens into a breach (A). Logging a formal written performance concern first (B) builds a contractual record but leads with a paper trail rather than a conversation, signalling distrust over a trend the review forum is designed to resolve jointly and risking the relationship before any remedy has been tried. A formal cure notice (C) is a heavier, adversarial escalation that is disproportionate when no threshold has been crossed and a routine review discussion has not yet been attempted. Waiting for another data point to confirm the trend (D) is tempting given month-to-month noise, but the recurring issues are already visible and the next review is the natural place to test and address them rather than letting another cycle of erosion pass. The fine margin is that B and C reach for contractual formality too early and D defers engagement, while only A uses the collaborative review process to tackle the problem at the right level and the right time.
