# Question

Midway through delivery, a contracted supplier and the buyer disagree about whether load-testing of the new interface is included in the engagement; each side genuinely believed it was the other's responsibility. The project manager reviews the procurement statement of work with both parties and they agree the relevant clause is ambiguous and that load-testing must happen for the interface to go live as planned. The supplier is willing to do the work but says it is extra scope; the buyer's interpretation is that it was always included. The schedule has little float. What should the project manager do next?

## Options

- **A**: Work with the supplier to agree who performs the load-testing and on what commercial basis, then record the outcome as a written contract amendment before the work starts.
- **B**: Ask the supplier to begin the load-testing immediately under a documented interim instruction to protect the go-live date, and settle whether it is extra scope and at what price in parallel.
- **C**: Refer the ambiguous clause to the buyer's contracts function for a formal interpretation of what the wording requires, and have the parties proceed on that ruling.
- **D**: Have the buyer's own team perform the load-testing now and pursue recovery of the cost from the supplier afterwards if the clause is later read in the buyer's favour.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Both parties already agree the clause is ambiguous and that the work is needed, so the open question is no longer interpretation but reaching a bilateral agreement on who does it and at what cost, captured as an amendment that removes the ambiguity for the rest of the engagement (A). Starting under an interim instruction (B) is tempting because the schedule is tight and it keeps work moving, but it commits the buyer before the commercial basis is agreed and is the weaker move precisely when the parties are only one conversation away from a settled amendment; expediting the agreement protects the date without taking on undefined cost. A formal contractual interpretation (C) is a real escalation route, but seeking a one-sided ruling on wording both sides accept is ambiguous invites a claim and forfeits the faster negotiated fix the situation is ripe for; it is what you do if the parties cannot agree, not before they have tried. Having the buyer perform the work and chase recovery later (D) keeps the schedule but leaves entitlement unresolved, converts a scope ambiguity into a future cost dispute, and presumes the buyer's reading without testing it. The fine margin is that B, C, and D each get the testing done, but only A resolves the ambiguity by agreement before committing anyone, which is what an ambiguous SOW calls for.
