# Question

A media company wants an external vendor to help evolve its streaming product over the coming year. The product roadmap shifts month to month as the company reacts to viewing data and competitors, and the company's board has asked for an annual figure it can budget against and report on. In early talks the vendor says it can assign experienced engineers but only if its revenue from the engagement is stable; it does not want to carry whatever scope the roadmap throws up at a price fixed in advance. Which contracting approach best fits both parties?

## Options

- **A**: A firm-fixed-price contract for a defined feature backlog agreed up front, re-baselined each quarter as priorities change.
- **B**: A time-and-materials contract with no ceiling, billed on actual hours as features are pulled from an evolving backlog.
- **C**: A capacity-based contract that funds a dedicated team for the year at an agreed rate, with the product owner directing the backlog sprint by sprint.
- **D**: A series of fixed-price contracts, one per quarter, each scoping the features expected in that quarter.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The company needs flexible, emergent scope, predictable annual cost, and a vendor willing to commit senior staff, which is exactly what a capacity-based (dedicated-team) contract delivers: it pays for a stable team's capacity rather than a fixed deliverable list, so the product owner can redirect priorities each sprint while spend stays predictable and the vendor's revenue is secure (C). A re-baselined firm-fixed-price backlog (A) contradicts itself, because fixing a price requires fixing scope, and constant re-baselining turns into perpetual change negotiation rather than smooth reprioritisation. Uncapped time-and-materials (B) gives full flexibility but destroys the budget predictability the board requires, since spend floats with hours consumed. Quarterly fixed-price contracts (D) improve predictability over annual fixed price but still force scope to be pinned each quarter and reintroduce fixed-scope rigidity and repeated re-scoping friction four times a year. The fine margin is that A and D chase predictability by fixing scope the company cannot fix, and B preserves flexibility at the cost of predictability, while only C reconciles emergent scope, predictable spend, and vendor comfort.
