# Question

A project manager is running a competitive procurement for a manufacturing automation package and faces four separate situations across the process. Each situation calls for one specific procurement document or artefact. Match each situation (left) to the document that fits it (right).

## Items

- **1**: The cell needs a robotic palletiser that must hit a stated throughput and footprint, but the panel has seen three integrators pitch very different mechanisms to get there, and the sponsor wants the build method scrutinised, not just the headline figure.
- **2**: The line needs ISO-standard pneumatic valves to a drawing the design authority has frozen; three approved-vendor-list suppliers all stock the identical part, and finance has asked the project manager to come back with a number by Friday.
- **3**: The board has only released seed funding to investigate whether vision-guided assembly is even viable for this product; no budget for the package itself has been approved, and the team cannot yet say what it would ask a supplier to build.
- **4**: Two evaluators on the last tender scored the same submission eight points apart, and before the next round opens the project manager wants the weighting of safety record, lead time, and price agreed and documented so every panellist marks to the same yardstick.

## Matches

- **A**: Request for information (RFI)
- **B**: Request for quotation (RFQ)
- **C**: Request for proposal (RFP)
- **D**: Source selection criteria

## Answer

1-C, 2-B, 3-A, 4-D

## Explanation

In situation 1 the integrators are offering materially different ways to meet the outcome and the sponsor wants the *method* judged, not just the price — that is a request for proposal, which invites sellers to propose how they would deliver and lets the panel weigh approach and capability (1-C). In situation 2 the part is frozen to a drawing, the candidates are interchangeable, and the only open question finance has posed is the number — a request for quotation, the price-focused instrument for a defined, interchangeable item (2-B). In situation 3 the board has funded only an investigation, no purchase is yet authorised, and the team cannot state what it would buy — a request for information, the no-commitment market scan, fits; reaching for a solicitation here would imply a buying intent that does not exist (3-A). In situation 4 the symptom is two evaluators diverging on the same bid, and the fix the project manager wants in place before responses arrive is an agreed, documented weighting — the source selection criteria (4-D). The live discrimination is between situations 1 and 2: both are real purchases, and the cue is whether competing solutions must be judged (RFP) or an interchangeable item is simply being priced (RFQ); situation 3 is the trap for anyone who treats any market approach as a solicitation when no commitment to buy yet exists.
