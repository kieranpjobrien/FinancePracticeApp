# Question

A project manager is building the schedule for a construction project that depends on a custom-fabricated steel structure with a known sixteen-week manufacturing lead time. The procurement to award the fabrication contract has not yet started. Where should this dependency primarily be reflected and managed?

## Options

- **A**: In the procurement management plan and the project schedule, so the solicitation and award are timed to meet the fabrication lead time.
- **B**: In the risk register only, recorded as a threat that the steel may arrive late.
- **C**: In the quality management plan, as a specification governing the fabricated steel.
- **D**: In the issue log, to be tracked once the steel actually becomes overdue.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A long procurement lead time is a planning dependency that must be reflected in the procurement management plan and integrated into the project schedule so the solicitation, evaluation, and award are sequenced early enough for the sixteen-week fabrication to finish on time (A). The risk register (B) might also note the threat of late delivery, but recording it only there does nothing to schedule the procurement and manage the lead time, which is the substantive action required. The quality management plan (C) governs the steel's specifications and acceptance, not the timing of its procurement, so it is the wrong home for the lead-time dependency. The issue log (D) captures problems that have already occurred, whereas a known future lead time is a planning matter to be managed proactively, not a present issue. The fine margin is that B, C, and D each touch a related artefact but none of them times the procurement against the lead time, which is exactly what the procurement plan and schedule are for.
