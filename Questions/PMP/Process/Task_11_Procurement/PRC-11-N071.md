# Question

On a fixed-price systems-integration contract, the seller submits a claim for additional payment, asserting that work it performed exceeded the agreed scope. The buyer's project manager believes the work was within scope. Both parties want to avoid litigation and preserve the working relationship for the remaining year of delivery. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Set up a joint working session with the seller to talk through the disputed work and look for a mutually acceptable resolution before consulting the contract in detail.
- **B**: Review the contract terms and the claim against the documented scope, then seek to resolve it through negotiation per the contract's dispute resolution clause.
- **C**: Send the seller a written response stating the buyer's position that the work was in scope, then invite the seller to substantiate its claim in reply.
- **D**: Refer the claim to a neutral third party for mediation under the contract's alternative dispute resolution provisions.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A claim is a contested assertion to be resolved on the evidence and through the contract's own machinery, so the first step is to ground the buyer's understanding by reviewing the contract terms and the claim against the documented scope, then pursue resolution by negotiation under the dispute resolution clause, which is both the contractually correct sequence and the route most likely to preserve the relationship (B). Convening a joint working session before consulting the contract (A) is collaborative and may well happen, but negotiating the disputed work without first establishing what the contract and scope baseline actually say risks conceding or contesting points on impression rather than evidence. Stating the buyer's position in writing and inviting substantiation (C) is a reasonable administrative step, yet leading with the buyer's conclusion before the project manager has tested that conclusion against the documentation pre-judges the merits and can entrench positions. Referring the matter to mediation (D) is a legitimate part of the dispute resolution ladder, but escalating to a third party before the parties have attempted direct negotiation skips the rung the contract expects them to try first. The fine margin is that A, C, and D are each defensible moves at some point, but only B does the evidence review that must precede negotiation and follows the contract's own resolution sequence from the correct first rung.
