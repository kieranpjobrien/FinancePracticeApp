# Question

Near the end of a fixed-price contract, the seller submits a claim for additional payment, asserting that several buyer-requested clarifications during execution amounted to extra scope beyond the agreed deliverable. The buyer's delivery team is adamant the clarifications fell within the original specification, and a manager on that team is pressing the project manager to reject the claim and move on. Both parties value the relationship for future work and want to avoid litigation. The contract sets out a dispute-resolution clause that escalates through structured negotiation and then mediation, and the seller's claim letter cites the relevant requirement and the dispute clause. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Compile the contract, the original specification, the change log, and the records of each clarification, and assess the claim's merits before responding to the seller.
- **B**: Open structured negotiation with the seller, as the contract's dispute clause directs, to work toward a settlement.
- **C**: Invoke the mediation step in the dispute clause now, bringing in a neutral third party to move the matter forward while the relationship is still cordial.
- **D**: Issue a without-prejudice settlement offer for part of the claimed amount to resolve the matter quickly and protect the ongoing relationship.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Claims administration begins with establishing the facts: before the project manager can negotiate or settle credibly, they must assess what the contract and the records actually show about whether the clarifications added scope, so assembling the specification, change log, and clarification records and evaluating the claim's merits comes first (A). Opening structured negotiation (B) is the right *forum* and the strongest trap, because the dispute clause does direct the parties there — but negotiating before understanding the claim's basis means bargaining blind, and the analysis in A is what equips that negotiation to succeed. Invoking mediation now (C) skips the negotiation step the clause places ahead of it and escalates to a third party prematurely, adding cost and formality the situation does not yet warrant. Making a partial settlement offer (D) concedes value before the project manager knows whether the claim has any merit, risks setting an unfounded precedent, and may be indefensible to the buyer's own governance. The discriminator is sequencing within claims administration: understand the claim, then negotiate, then escalate only if needed — not jumping to the right forum before doing the homework.
