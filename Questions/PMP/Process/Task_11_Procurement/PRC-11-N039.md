# Question

A five-year IT outsourcing contract is ending and the service is being brought back in-house. The project manager is planning a controlled procurement closure and transition. Which actions are essential to closing the procurement properly? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Verify all contracted deliverables and services have been provided and formally accept them against the contract.
- **B**: Transfer operational knowledge, documentation, and system access from the supplier to the in-house team.
- **C**: Settle all outstanding payments, claims, and financial obligations under the contract.
- **D**: Release the supplier's final payment on the contract end date so the financial relationship closes cleanly, with any open claims resolved separately afterwards.
- **E**: Treat the project's closure report, once signed by the sponsor, as acceptance of the supplier's deliverables to avoid duplicating sign-off.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

A controlled close-out verifies and formally accepts what was delivered (A), transfers the operational knowledge, documentation, and access the in-house team needs to run the service (B), and settles all outstanding payments and claims under the contract (C). Releasing final payment while leaving claims to be resolved afterwards (D) surrenders the buyer's main leverage to settle those claims and treats financial closure as a date rather than a condition, so payment should follow settlement, not precede it. Relying on the sponsor-signed project closure report as contractual acceptance (E) conflates project-level closure with procurement acceptance; the deliverables still require verification and formal acceptance against the contract itself. The essential actions secure acceptance, continuity, and complete financial settlement before the contract is closed.
