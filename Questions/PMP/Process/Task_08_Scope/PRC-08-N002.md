# Question

Midway through a two-week sprint on an agile mobile-banking programme, the product owner returns from a steering meeting where two new high-profile features were promised to executives "for next sprint". The current sprint goal is on track and the team is at capacity. The product owner asks the Scrum Master how to handle the two features. What should the Scrum Master advise?

## Options

- **A**: Have the product owner write and order the two features in the product backlog now, and decide their place against everything else at the next planning, where the team forecasts what fits.
- **B**: Have the product owner add the two features to the top of the next sprint's scope immediately so the team can commit to them at planning as promised.
- **C**: Bring the two features to the team now for sizing so the product owner can confirm to the executives whether both will be done next sprint.
- **D**: Ask the team whether they can take the two features into the current sprint alongside the existing goal, since there is still over a week left.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The product owner owns ordering the product backlog, but how much is committed is the team's forecast made at planning against the whole ordered backlog; the right move now is simply to capture and order the items, not to pre-commit them. (A) preserves both the product owner's authority over order and the team's authority over capacity. (B) placing them "at the top of next sprint" pre-commits scope before planning and before the team forecasts capacity, overriding the team's role and ignoring whatever else may rank higher. (C) is the subtle trap: sizing now seems diligent, but promising the executives a fixed outcome ("both done next sprint") still commits the team before planning and before the backlog is weighed as a whole, converting a forecast into a fixed promise. (D) injecting work mid-sprint to "use up" remaining time disregards the existing commitment and the reason capacity is reserved, even though slack appears to exist.
