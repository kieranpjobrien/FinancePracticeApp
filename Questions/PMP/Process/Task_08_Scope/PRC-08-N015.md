# Question

On an agile retail-loyalty app, a marketing director who sits above the product owner emails the development team during a sprint asking them to build a new promotions feature "this sprint", saying a campaign launch depends on it. The product owner is on leave for two days. The team has begun the work informally to be helpful and is now mid-build. The Scrum Master learns of this. What should the Scrum Master do first?

## Options

- **A**: Have the team pause the promotions work and resume the committed sprint backlog, and ask the director to bring the request to the product owner on their return so it can be ordered against everything else.
- **B**: Let the team continue the promotions feature this sprint given the director's stated launch dependency, and brief the product owner on the change as soon as they return so the backlog can be reconciled.
- **C**: Reach the product owner remotely to decide whether the promotions feature should displace committed work, holding the team on the original sprint goal until that decision comes back.
- **D**: Convene the team to decide together whether the promotions feature is worth taking on this sprint, since the team owns its sprint commitment and can choose to adjust it.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The contested decision is whether work should enter the sprint, and that ownership belongs to the product owner — so when a genuine launch dependency is asserted, the first move is to get the product owner to make the call rather than have the Scrum Master, the team, or the director's seniority decide it. (C) reaches the absent-but-contactable product owner to adjudicate while protecting the sprint goal in the meantime, which honours both the role and the possibility that the request is genuinely urgent. (A) is defensible and protects the sprint, but it treats the product owner's two-day absence as a hard wall and defers a possibly time-critical decision the product owner could make remotely; it risks missing a launch the business actually needs. (B) lets the team's informal start harden into a committed scope change made by no one with backlog authority — exactly the uncontrolled-intake problem to avoid — and reconciles only after the fact. (D) hands a business-prioritisation decision to the team; the team decides *how* to build and may choose to adjust its commitment, but *which* features outrank others is the product owner's call, not a team vote. The margin is fine because A, B and D are all plausible, but only C puts the decision where it belongs without either stalling on the absence or letting scope change by default.
