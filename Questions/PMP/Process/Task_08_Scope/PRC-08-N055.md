# Question

A start-up's product owner has a 120-item backlog for a new booking app and three weeks of runway before the next funding decision. The founders' single open question is whether real customers will complete a paid booking through the app at all; everything else is secondary. The product owner asks the project manager how to scope the first release given that question. Which approach best fits?

## Options

- **A**: Release a thin end-to-end slice that lets a real customer search, select, pay for and confirm one booking, then instrument it and learn from live usage.
- **B**: Build a clickable prototype of the full booking journey and run moderated usability sessions with target customers to gauge their intent to book.
- **C**: Deliver the highest-value third of the backlog as the first release so customers experience a credible product when they judge whether to book.
- **D**: Ship the payment and booking-confirmation components first, since they carry the most technical risk, and add the search and selection journey in the following release.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The founders' hypothesis is specifically that customers will complete a paid booking, so the scope must be the smallest slice that exercises that whole transaction with real money and real users and produces behavioural evidence. (A) is a walking skeleton of the core journey instrumented to answer exactly that question. (B) is genuinely lean and cheaper, but a prototype measures stated intent in a moderated setting, not actual completed paid bookings, so it cannot confirm the hypothesis the founders care about. (C) builds far more than the question needs and still may omit a working end-to-end payment path, spending scarce runway on breadth rather than evidence. (D) front-loads the riskiest components but, until the search-and-select steps exist, no customer can travel the full journey, so it yields no booking-completion data within the runway. The margin is between a complete-but-minimal live transaction and three plausible lean alternatives that each fail to test the actual hypothesis.
