# Question

A project manager on a predictive enterprise payroll replacement is finalising requirements. The finance director insists the new system "must do everything the old one does", but cannot list the old system's functions and the original vendor documentation is missing. Several long-serving payroll clerks rely on undocumented workarounds. The PM needs requirements precise enough to baseline scope. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Run facilitated workshops and job-shadowing sessions with the payroll clerks to document the actual current-state processes, including the workarounds.
- **B**: Interview the finance director and senior managers to capture the functions they believe the old system performs, and baseline from that account.
- **C**: Issue a questionnaire to all payroll staff asking them to describe everything the old system does.
- **D**: Reverse-engineer the legacy system's database schema to infer the functionality that must be replicated.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The requirement "match everything" is not verifiable until the current state is actually understood, and the real knowledge lives with the clerks and their workarounds, not in missing documents. Workshops plus observation (job-shadowing) surface tacit knowledge that staff cannot articulate in the abstract, which is exactly the gap here. (B) interviewing the director and managers captures a believed, second-hand picture; they are not the ones operating the undocumented workarounds, so baselining from their account would enshrine the very gaps and misconceptions that triggered the problem. (C) relies on the same clerks being able to self-report processes they perform unconsciously, which the scenario says they cannot. (D) captures data structures but not business rules, exception handling, or the manual workarounds, so it would miss the most important undocumented behaviour.
