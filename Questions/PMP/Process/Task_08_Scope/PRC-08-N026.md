# Question

A predictive logistics-tracking project has collected requirements from operations, drivers, and IT. They conflict: operations wants GPS pings every 30 seconds, IT has demonstrated the network cannot sustain that volume, and drivers object to continuous tracking on privacy grounds. The project sponsor has set a clear primary objective — reduce delivery exceptions — and the project charter names operations as the business owner of the tracking capability. The project manager needs to converge on a baseline. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Establish the ping interval the network can reliably sustain, then take that feasible ceiling to the group as the basis for agreeing the requirement.
- **B**: Facilitate a joint session that tests each conflicting requirement against the exception-reduction objective and the network constraint, and have the group agree the ping interval that best serves the objective within what is feasible.
- **C**: Have the operations business owner, as the chartered owner of the capability, set the ping interval, then ask IT and drivers to raise any blocking concerns for resolution.
- **D**: Convene the group to surface and document each party's underlying interest — exception reduction, network load, and privacy — before any specific interval is proposed.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Conflicting requirements are reconciled by weighing them against the project's objective and hard constraints until the group agrees a feasible set — and here the sponsor has handed the PM the deciding yardstick (reduce exceptions) and a real ceiling (the network limit). (B) drives directly to convergence using both, which is what "move toward a baseline" demands. (D) is the strongest distractor and is genuinely good practice — surfacing interests is how a skilled facilitator opens the session — but it is a preparatory step that stops short of resolving anything; on its own it does not move the project toward the baseline the question asks for. (A) lets a technical constraint define the requirement before the business objective has been weighed, anchoring the group on feasibility rather than value. (C) over-reads the charter: naming operations as business owner makes them accountable for the capability, not entitled to override a demonstrated technical limit and a legitimate privacy objection by fiat.
