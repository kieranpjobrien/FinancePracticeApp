# Question

On a predictive avionics project, a stakeholder submits a change request to drop a navigation requirement they now consider unnecessary. The requirement was approved months ago and the project maintains a populated requirements traceability matrix. Before the project manager recommends a disposition, what is the most important thing to do with this change?

## Options

- **A**: Use the traceability matrix to identify every deliverable, design element and test linked to that requirement, then assess what removing it affects.
- **B**: Confirm with the originating stakeholder that the requirement is genuinely obsolete and was correctly approved in the first place.
- **C**: Check whether the requirement maps to any regulatory or contractual obligation that would prevent it being dropped.
- **D**: Estimate the schedule and cost saved by removing the requirement and its associated work.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The danger in removing an approved requirement is the hidden web of dependent deliverables, designs and tests that may break or become orphaned; the traceability matrix exists precisely to expose those forward and backward links, so tracing them is the foundation every other assessment rests on. (A) establishes the full footprint of the change before any judgement is made. (C) is a real and serious check, but you cannot reliably tell which obligations are touched until the matrix reveals what the requirement connects to. (D) quantifies one consequence (saving) but ignores the downstream breakage that determines whether the saving is real. (B) validates the request's premise yet still leaves the impact across the design unknown. Tracing first turns B, C and D into informed checks rather than guesses.
