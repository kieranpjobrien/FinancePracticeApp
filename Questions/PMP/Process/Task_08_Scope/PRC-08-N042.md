# Question

Midway through a sprint on a Scrum project building a hospital appointments app, a senior engineering director who is not the product owner tells the development team an SMS-reminder feature must ship in the current release for a key client, and asks them to start it now. The product owner is on leave for the next three working days. The sprint goal is unrelated to reminders, and the team has spare capacity this sprint. The team asks the scrum master how to respond. What should the scrum master advise?

## Options

- **A**: Have the team begin the SMS feature now using the spare capacity, and brief the product owner to formally prioritise it the moment they return.
- **B**: Have the team capture the director's request and the client and release context, and hold it for the product owner to assess against the backlog and sprint goal when they return.
- **C**: Arrange for the director and the team to agree provisional acceptance criteria for the SMS feature now, so it is ready to pull in as soon as the product owner confirms priority.
- **D**: Ask whoever is covering the product owner's decisions during the leave to rule on whether the SMS feature enters the current sprint.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The pull here is real — a senior stakeholder, a named client, a live release, and idle capacity all argue for moving now — but the product owner is the single accountable point for what enters the backlog and the sprint, and a three-day wait does not justify routing scope around them. (B) preserves that channel and loses little: the request is captured with its context so the product owner can decide quickly on return. (A) is the strongest distractor because spare capacity and a real client deadline make starting now look efficient, but committing the team to unprioritised work and asking the product owner to ratify it afterwards inverts prioritisation and pressures them to rubber-stamp a fait accompli. (C) agreeing acceptance criteria with the director rather than the product owner quietly lets the wrong person shape what "done" means and presumes the item is in. (D) only holds if the organisation actually delegated product-owner authority during the leave; absent that explicit delegation, inventing a stand-in decider for backlog priority is the same bypass as letting the director decide. The margin is that A, C and D each find a way to progress the item now, whereas only B keeps the accountable decision with the role that owns it.
