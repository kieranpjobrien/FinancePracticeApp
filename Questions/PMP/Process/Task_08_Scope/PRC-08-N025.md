# Question

A hybrid pharmaceutical-launch project has a regulatory submission date that cannot move and a fixed budget. Mid-project, analysis shows the agreed scope will overrun the deadline by an estimated four weeks. The team has already mapped which features are mandatory for a compliant submission and which are enhancements, and the sponsor has told the project manager to "do whatever it takes to make the date work." The product owner, the regulatory lead, and the sponsor are all available this week. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Take the team's existing mandatory-versus-enhancement split, cut the enhancements to fit the date, and present the reduced scope to the sponsor for sign-off.
- **B**: Reorder the remaining backlog so the mandatory submission features are completed first, deferring the decision on enhancements until nearer the deadline when the picture is clearer.
- **C**: Convene the sponsor, product owner, and regulatory lead to confirm the minimum scope that constitutes a compliant submission and agree which enhancements defer to a post-submission release.
- **D**: Ask the regulatory lead to confirm exactly which features are mandatory for compliance, since that floor determines how much scope can safely be cut.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Time and cost are fixed, so scope must give — but *how much* and *which parts* is a decision the people who own value, compliance authority, and funding must make together, not one the PM pre-decides from the team's draft. (C) brings the three deciding parties together to ratify the compliant floor and formally agree the deferrals, which is what makes the reduced scope authorised and defensible. (D) is the strongest distractor: confirming the regulatory floor is genuinely necessary input, but acting on the regulatory lead alone omits the sponsor's and product owner's call on the enhancements and treats one input as the whole decision. (A) lets the PM unilaterally cut scope on the strength of an internal draft and reduces the sponsor to a rubber stamp, when the trade-offs are theirs to own. (B) sequencing the mandatory work is sensible execution, but deferring the scope-reduction decision wastes the week the deciders are available and risks discovering too late that the deferral set was never agreed.
