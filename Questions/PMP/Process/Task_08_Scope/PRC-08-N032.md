# Question

On an agile loyalty-rewards platform, the team consistently finishes fewer stories than forecast. Reviewing the last three sprints, the Scrum Master sees that stories are pulled in with one-line titles and no acceptance criteria; developers interpret each story differently, and roughly a third are sent back at review as "not what was meant". The product owner is engaged but stretched across two products. What should the Scrum Master do first?

## Options

- **A**: Establish that stories are refined with clear acceptance criteria and meet the team's definition of ready before they are eligible to enter a sprint.
- **B**: Lower the sprint forecast to match the team's recent actual throughput so commitments become reliable again.
- **C**: Ask the product owner to attend the daily scrum so developers can clarify each ambiguous story as they pick it up.
- **D**: Have developers write the acceptance criteria themselves for any story that arrives without them, to keep work flowing.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The rework is caused by under-refined stories entering the sprint without agreed acceptance criteria, so the first move is to gate entry on readiness — criteria defined and shared understanding reached — which attacks the root cause. (A) does that. (B) makes the forecast honest but accepts the rework as permanent rather than removing it, so value throughput stays depressed. (C) clarifying live in the daily scrum patches symptoms story-by-story, pulls the stretched product owner into the wrong ceremony, and still lets ambiguous work start. (D) developers can help draft criteria, but the product owner owns what "acceptable" means; letting developers set it unilaterally risks building the wrong thing and reproduces the misinterpretation. The margin between A and C is fine — both improve clarity — but only A prevents unready work from being committed in the first place.
