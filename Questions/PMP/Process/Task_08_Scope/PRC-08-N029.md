# Question

On a predictive prison-refurbishment project, an approved change adds a new perimeter-security deliverable. The project manager must update the scope baseline so the change is properly reflected and future work is controlled against it. Which updates are part of correctly revising the scope baseline for this change? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Add the new deliverable and its boundary, including what it explicitly does not cover, to the project scope statement.
- **B**: Insert the new deliverable into the WBS, decomposing it down to manageable work packages.
- **C**: Write the WBS dictionary entries describing the new work packages, their acceptance criteria and assumptions.
- **D**: Adjust the cost baseline by control account to fund the added work before the scope baseline is considered updated.
- **E**: Re-sequence the schedule and re-level resources so the new work packages have planned dates before the scope baseline is considered updated.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Revising the scope baseline means revising its three components together so they stay consistent: the scope statement (the deliverable and its exclusions), the WBS (the decomposition), and the WBS dictionary (the work-package detail). (A), (B) and (C) are those three updates. (D) and (E) are necessary downstream — an approved change normally does flow into cost and schedule — but the cost baseline and schedule baseline are separate performance baselines; updating them is not what makes the *scope* baseline correct, and treating them as a precondition for the scope-baseline update conflates three distinct baselines that are controlled in parallel. The distractors are real PM actions, but they belong to other baselines, which is the trap.
