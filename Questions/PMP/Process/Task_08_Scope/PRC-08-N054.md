# Question

At a sprint review for a retail loyalty platform, a stakeholder notices the increment includes a discount-tiering capability nobody recognises. Investigation shows a developer added it after overhearing a sales manager mention it in a corridor; it was never a backlog item and the product owner never saw it. The capability works and demos well. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Have the product owner decide the item's fate as a backlog candidate, and reinforce with the team that only product-owner-prioritised work enters an increment.
- **B**: Accept the capability into the released increment, since it works, was demonstrated, and a sales manager clearly wanted it.
- **C**: Direct the developer to remove the capability from the increment and log the time spent as a process lapse.
- **D**: Ask the sales manager to confirm the requirement so the capability can be retained without disrupting the sprint.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The real failure is that work entered the increment outside the single prioritisation channel the product owner owns; the fix both routes this item to its proper decision-maker and closes the channel that let it bypass control. (A) preserves a potentially valuable idea while restoring scope discipline. (B) ratifies unrequested, unprioritised work on the strength of a corridor remark, which is gold-plating and undermines the product owner's authority. (C) is closer to correct on discipline but discards an idea that may hold value without giving the product owner the chance to weigh it. (D) lets an overheard comment from someone who is not the product owner legitimise scope, substituting the wrong authority for the right one.
