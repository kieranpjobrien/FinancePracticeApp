# Question

On a predictive bridge-inspection software project, the team has built a deliverable. A junior colleague is unsure how the Validate Scope review they are about to run with the client differs from the Control Quality work already done. Which statements about that upcoming Validate Scope review are correct? (Choose two.)

## Options

- **A**: It normally takes place after Control Quality, because deliverables should be verified as correct internally before being presented to the customer for acceptance.
- **B**: Its primary output is formal acceptance, recorded as accepted deliverables, rather than a list of defects to fix.
- **C**: It is the point at which the team measures the deliverable against quality metrics and tolerances to detect defects.
- **D**: It can be skipped when Control Quality has already confirmed the deliverable conforms, since conformance implies the customer will accept.
- **E**: It is performed by the internal quality function on the team's behalf, without the customer present.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

Validate Scope formalises customer acceptance and typically follows Control Quality, so the deliverable is internally verified before it is shown to the customer; this makes (A) and (B) correct, and they describe genuinely different facets (sequence/relationship, and the acceptance output). (C) describes Control Quality, which measures conformance and finds defects, not Validate Scope. (D) is the trap: conformance to specification does not guarantee acceptance, because the customer may still reject on acceptance criteria or expectation grounds, so the validation step cannot be skipped. (E) is wrong because Validate Scope is conducted with the customer or sponsor, whereas the internal-only inspection is Control Quality.
