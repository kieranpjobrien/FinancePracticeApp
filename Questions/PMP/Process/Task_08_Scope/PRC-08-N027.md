# Question

Midway through a predictive hospital-EHR rollout, a department head insists that automated discharge-summary generation "was always part of the project" and should be delivered at no extra cost. The project manager finds the feature was discussed in early workshops and appears in the requirements documentation as a candidate, but the team never built it and the sponsor signed off the deliverables some months ago. The department head is adamant and influential. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Check the approved project scope statement to see whether the feature is listed as an in-scope deliverable or sits within its stated exclusions.
- **B**: Check the requirements documentation, since the feature is recorded there and that establishes it as committed scope.
- **C**: Check the requirements traceability matrix to confirm the feature traces to an approved requirement and a design element.
- **D**: Raise a change request to add the feature now, given the department head's seniority and the dispute it is causing.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The boundary question — is this delivered work or not — is settled by the approved scope statement, which records the agreed deliverables and explicit exclusions and is the baseline against which "always part of it" claims are tested. (A) goes to the artefact that actually adjudicates in-scope versus out. (B) is the trap: appearing in the requirements documentation as a captured or candidate requirement does not mean it was baselined as a deliverable; the documentation collects what was elicited, not what was committed. (C) the traceability matrix shows linkage for requirements that were carried into the baseline, so it presupposes the very question being asked and is the wrong first reference. (D) jumps to a change request before establishing whether the work is already owed under the baseline or is genuinely new scope, which changes who bears the cost.
