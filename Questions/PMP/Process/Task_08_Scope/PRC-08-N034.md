# Question

A predictive defence-radar project has a signed, baselined requirement stating the system shall provide "fast and reliable target detection". As integration testing nears, the contractor and client disagree sharply over whether the delivered system meets it; the project manager realises the requirement was never quantified. Both parties want to keep testing on schedule, and the client is uneasy that any new numbers might quietly redefine what was promised. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Have the systems-engineering team propose measurable thresholds derived from the original operational need and comparable radar standards, and circulate them to the client and contractor for agreement.
- **B**: Refer to the requirements traceability matrix to recover the source need and design basis the requirement traces from, and use that to anchor a measurable definition.
- **C**: Facilitate the client and technical stakeholders agreeing measurable criteria for "fast" and "reliable", then put the clarification through change control so the baseline is updated with traceability.
- **D**: Convene the client and contractor to confirm the operational intent behind "fast" and "reliable" — what the radar must actually achieve in service — before any numeric thresholds are drafted.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The requirement is unverifiable, so it cannot be objectively accepted until it is made measurable *and* that clarification is formalised against the baseline — otherwise the client's fear of a quiet redefinition is well founded. (C) is the complete first move: it reaches agreed criteria with the right parties and routes the clarification through change control, which is what keeps the change authorised and traceable rather than slipped in. (D) is the strongest distractor and is genuinely good practice — confirming operational intent should inform the criteria — but on its own it stops at shared understanding and produces no verifiable requirement or controlled update, so testing still has nothing objective to test against. (A) drafting thresholds from engineering judgement and circulating them risks anchoring acceptance on the contractor's frame and, crucially, omits the change-control step that protects the baseline. (B) the traceability matrix is a useful input to recover the source need, but consulting it is a supporting action, not the resolution; a recovered design basis still has to be turned into agreed, baselined criteria. The fine margin is that A, B and D each supply one ingredient, whereas only C both reaches agreement and controls the change.
