# Question

A hybrid government grants-portal project runs an agile build inside a fixed-price contract with a defined funded scope. During a sprint the team designs a better workflow that would add two screens outside the funded scope but would meaningfully improve citizen outcomes. The product owner is enthusiastic and wants to keep momentum. How should the project manager handle the idea?

## Options

- **A**: Capture it in the product backlog, then raise a change request to the contract's change authority with the benefit and impact so funded scope is adjusted before the screens are built.
- **B**: Capture it in the product backlog and let the product owner prioritise it for an upcoming sprint, since within agile the product owner owns what the team builds next.
- **C**: Have the team prototype the two screens within the current sprint's slack as a spike, then present the working prototype to the change authority to support the change request.
- **D**: Raise the change request to the change authority, but pause the idea entirely until the authority decides, keeping it out of the backlog so the team is not tempted to start it.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

In a hybrid arrangement, agile prioritisation operates inside the contractual funding and scope controls; an addition beyond funded scope is a contract change that must be authorised before the work is built. (A) captures the idea (agile intent) and routes the funding decision to the body that owns it (governance) before any build. (B) is the trap: the product owner's authority over backlog order is real, but it does not extend to authorising work outside the funded contractual scope, so prioritising it for a sprint would commit unfunded work. (C) building a prototype "within slack" is still performing unfunded contractual scope before approval; calling it a spike does not change that it consumes effort on out-of-scope work ahead of the decision. (D) correctly routes the change but needlessly refuses to even record the idea in the backlog, which is where candidate work belongs; transparency, not suppression, is what keeps the team from starting prematurely.
