# Question

During refinement for an agile insurance-claims platform, the team cannot converge on the size of a story titled "Process all claim types end to end". Estimates range from small to enormous. Discussion reveals two separate reasons for the spread: the story plainly bundles several claim types and processing stages, and the team is also genuinely unsure how a third-party fraud-check API will behave under load. The product owner wants tangible progress on this story in the next sprint. What is the most effective action?

## Options

- **A**: Run a timeboxed spike next sprint to investigate the fraud-check API, then re-estimate and slice the story once that unknown is resolved.
- **B**: Re-estimate the story as a group using reference stories of known size to anchor the discussion and reach a shared number the team can commit to.
- **C**: Split the story by claim type and processing stage into thin, independently valuable slices the team can size and finish, and isolate the fraud-check uncertainty into its own slice or spike.
- **D**: Decompose the story into per-claim-type slices and have the product owner rank them, so the highest-value slice is sized and delivered next sprint.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The estimate spread has two distinct causes — bundled scope and a genuine technical unknown — and the most effective move handles both: slice the bundle into shippable pieces *and* quarantine the unknown so it does not hold the valuable, well-understood slices hostage. (C) does exactly that and lets real value ship next sprint. (D) is the strongest distractor and is largely right — splitting plus value-ranking is sound — but it silently folds the fraud-check unknown into a claim-type slice, so the slice carrying that risk is still hard to size and may not finish. (A) over-indexes on the unknown: a spike defers all delivery for a sprint when several slices have nothing to do with the fraud API and could ship now, which fails the product owner's need for progress. (B) better estimation technique will not rescue a story that is genuinely too big to finish in a sprint; anchoring produces a more confident number for work that still cannot be completed.
