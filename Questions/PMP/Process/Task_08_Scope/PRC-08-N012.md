# Question

A product owner on an agile telehealth booking product is applying MoSCoW to a time-boxed release that goes live to real patients. Three features are debated at refinement. Match each to the MoSCoW category the product owner should assign, given how the release is actually used.

## Items

- **1**: A self-service appointment-reschedule screen that users badly want; until it ships, patients can still reschedule by phoning the clinic line, which staff already operate.
- **2**: SMS appointment reminders. The clinic's regulator does not mandate them, but the business case for the whole release rests on cutting the no-show rate, and reminders are the single biggest lever for that outcome.
- **3**: A consent-capture step. The legal team confirms an existing signed paper consent on file remains valid for this release, and the digital version is planned for next quarter.

## Matches

- **A**: Should have
- **B**: Could have
- **C**: Must have

## Answer

1-B, 2-C, 3-A

## Explanation

MoSCoW turns on whether the release is *viable and achieves its purpose* without the feature, not on how strongly it is wanted. (2) is a "Must have": although nothing legal compels reminders, the release's justifying outcome (reducing no-shows) collapses without them, so omitting them defeats the release. (1) is a "Could have": it is strongly wanted, but a workable manual alternative (phoning the clinic) already exists, so its absence neither breaks the release nor undermines its goal, making it droppable under pressure. (3) is a "Should have": digital consent is important and its omission is genuinely painful, but a valid paper-consent path keeps the release lawful and operable for now, so it is not release-breaking for this iteration. The trap is assuming anything labelled "consent" is automatically a Must have; here an existing legal mechanism removes that necessity, while the no-show driver that actually carries the business case is the true Must.
