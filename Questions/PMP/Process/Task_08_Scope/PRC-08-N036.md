# Question

Midway through a predictive university student-records migration, the project manager notices scope is quietly expanding: small "while you're at it" additions keep appearing in the build without approvals. Some additions look genuinely useful; others duplicate planned work. The project manager wants to bring scope back under control while treating each addition fairly. Which actions are appropriate? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Compare the current build against the scope baseline to identify exactly what has been added without authorisation.
- **B**: Raise each unauthorised addition as a change request so it receives a formal accept, reject, or defer decision through integrated change control.
- **C**: Reinforce with the team and stakeholders the protocol that scope changes must be requested and approved before any work begins on them.
- **D**: Direct the team to stop further work on the unauthorised additions until each has been assessed and dispositioned through change control.
- **E**: Update the scope baseline now to incorporate the additions that look genuinely useful, and have the team strip out the ones that duplicate planned work, so the build is realigned without delay.

## Answer

A, B, C, D

## Explanation

Bringing scope back under control means detecting the variance, halting further uncontrolled work, dispositioning each addition through the proper channel, and preventing recurrence. (A) detects what was added by comparing the build to the baseline. (B) routes each addition through integrated change control for a controlled accept, reject, or defer decision rather than the PM reflexively keeping or deleting it. (C) addresses the behaviour driving the creep so it stops happening. (D) stopping work on unapproved additions pending disposition prevents further uncontrolled investment without discarding work that might be approved. (E) is the trap, and it is tempting because it promises a fast realignment: but it pre-empts integrated change control by absorbing the "useful" additions into the baseline before any board has approved them, and by unilaterally deleting the "duplicate" ones before they have been formally dispositioned — both are exactly the uncontrolled decision-making that caused the creep, merely reversed in direction. The baseline is updated *after* change control approves an item, not on the PM's own read of which additions look worthwhile, and the affected schedule and cost baselines are then realigned alongside it. The discipline is that the PM detects and halts, but the change authority decides.
