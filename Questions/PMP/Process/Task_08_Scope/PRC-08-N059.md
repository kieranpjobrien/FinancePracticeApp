# Question

A vendor project manager is delivering an agile project under a fixed-price, capped-budget contract. The client's product owner has been reshaping the backlog each iteration, which the PM has welcomed. The newly added items, taken together with the original feature list the client still expects in full, now project total effort well beyond the cap. The client values the new items highly and has not signalled any willingness to drop the originals. The next iteration plans tomorrow. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Show the product owner that total committed scope now exceeds the cap, and ask them to re-order the whole backlog so that, within the cap, the highest-value items are delivered and lower-value ones drop below the line.
- **B**: Continue delivering by highest priority and let the lower-value items naturally fall away as the cap is reached, raising the overrun formally only if the client later disputes what was not delivered.
- **C**: Raise a contract change request to increase the cap to cover the additional items, supported by the projected effort, since the client clearly wants the extra scope.
- **D**: Keep accepting the changes for now to sustain the relationship, and present the full picture of scope versus cap at the next scheduled steering review.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Under a fixed cap the lever is scope: the disciplined move is to make the over-commitment visible now and have the product owner consciously re-prioritise so the most valuable work fits within the cap and the rest falls below the line as an explicit, owned decision. (A) does exactly that and keeps the trade-off with the person entitled to make it. (B) relying on lower-value items to "naturally fall away" without an explicit agreement leaves the client still expecting the originals plus the additions, so the gap surfaces as a dispute at the worst time rather than a decision made together. (C) a cap increase may eventually be warranted, but jumping to a contract change pre-empts the cheaper scope trade-off and asks the client to pay more before they have been shown they can instead re-prioritise within the existing cap. (D) continuing to absorb changes to protect the relationship and deferring the conversation lets the over-commitment grow and stores up exactly the conflict the PM should be heading off now.
