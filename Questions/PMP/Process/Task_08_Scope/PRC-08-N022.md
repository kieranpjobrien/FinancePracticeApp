# Question

On a predictive government grants-management system, a senior business sponsor emailed the lead developer directly asking for "a small tweak" to add an extra approval step, and the developer implemented it the same day. The project manager discovers this a week later when testers flag that the workflow no longer matches the signed-off specification. The sponsor still wants the extra step, and an audit of the deployed build is due shortly. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Have the sponsor raise a formal change request for the extra approval step so the addition enters change control and can be evaluated and approved.
- **B**: Assess the deployed change against the scope baseline to establish exactly what was altered and its effect on dependent components, before deciding how to disposition it.
- **C**: Document the deviation in the change log and issue log so the unapproved change is recorded and visible ahead of the audit, then convene the change control board.
- **D**: Revert the deployed code to the signed-off specification so the build matches the baseline again, then put the requested step through change control as new work.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

All four are legitimate responses to an uncontrolled change, but the first thing the PM needs is facts: until the deployed change is compared against the baseline, the PM cannot size its impact on dependent components, cannot brief the change control board meaningfully, and cannot judge whether reverting is even safe. (B) establishes that basis. (A) is a correct and necessary step, but raising the change request before understanding what was actually altered means the board evaluates a request with no impact data, and it ignores that the change is *already live* and may have downstream effects that need handling now. (C) recording the deviation is right and will follow, but logging precedes analysis only in form; the audit is better served by an assessed, dispositioned change than by a bare log entry, and convening the board still requires the impact picture B produces. (D) reverting is a candidate disposition, not a first move: it may discard a change the sponsor will approve anyway and could itself destabilise the build, a risk that can only be judged after the impact assessment. The fine margin is sequencing — A, C and D are all things the PM will likely do, but each presupposes the impact analysis in B.
