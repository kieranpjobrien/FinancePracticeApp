# Question

A Scrum team building a consumer banking feature demonstrates a story at the sprint review. The product owner rejects it: they expected multi-currency support, while the developers built single-currency only and point out that multi-currency was never written into the story's acceptance criteria. The team's definition of done is met in full. This is the third sprint running where the increment satisfies the definition of done yet the product owner says it is not what they asked for. What should the project manager help the team strengthen?

## Options

- **A**: The clarity and completeness of each story's acceptance criteria, so what the increment must do is agreed with the product owner before development starts.
- **B**: The definition of done, by adding a check that the product owner has confirmed the story meets their expectations before it is called done.
- **C**: The backlog refinement cadence, so stories are broken down and detailed further ahead of the sprint they are pulled into.
- **D**: The sprint review format, so the product owner inspects increments against expectations earlier and more rigorously.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The recurring failure is a gap between what the product owner wants and what the acceptance criteria state: the definition of done is being met, so the missing element is per-story agreement on *what the story must actually do*. Strengthening the acceptance criteria so expectations are pinned down with the product owner before build closes the gap at its source (A). (B) is the trap: the definition of done is a team-wide completeness bar that applies equally to every increment, so loading story-specific "does it match expectations" into it conflates two different controls and still would not capture the specific functional content each story needs. (C) better refinement helps, and clearer acceptance criteria are part of refinement, but cadence alone does not guarantee the criteria capture the product owner's intent; the deficiency is in the criteria's content, not the timing. (D) catching the mismatch earlier at review reduces wasted time but still detects the problem after build, rather than preventing it by agreeing scope up front.
