# Question

An agile team building an e-commerce checkout demonstrated a completed payment feature at the sprint review, and the product owner accepted it. Two weeks later it failed in production: it had never been load-tested or security-reviewed, and the team had not regarded either as part of finishing a story. In the retrospective the team agrees the non-functional checks matter, but notes they have neither the load-test environment nor the security skills in-house, and that adding the checks will reduce how many stories fit in a sprint. What should the Scrum Master do first?

## Options

- **A**: Add load-testing and security-review as explicit acceptance criteria on every story from now on, so each story is verified before the product owner can accept it.
- **B**: Have the product owner add a recurring non-functional hardening story to each sprint that load-tests and security-reviews the features built that sprint.
- **C**: Arrange access to a load-test environment and a security specialist first, since the team cannot strengthen its completion standard around checks it has no means to perform.
- **D**: Facilitate the team and product owner in revising the definition of done to include the non-functional checks the product requires, and make explicit how the team will meet them.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The defect is that the team's shared notion of "done" omitted checks the product genuinely needed, so the durable fix is to revise the definition of done — and to do it knowingly, surfacing the environment and skills gap so the new standard is one the team can actually meet rather than an aspiration. (D) does both, which is why it leads. (A) putting the checks on every story as acceptance criteria is defensible, but story-level criteria describe what a *particular* story must do, whereas a blanket non-functional standard that applies to all relevant work belongs in the definition of done; scattering it across acceptance criteria is more fragile and easy to omit. (B) a separate hardening story re-creates the original failure pattern — work declared done before it is verified — and decouples verification from the stories themselves. (C) securing the environment and specialist is a real and necessary enabler, and the team raised it, but it is a means to satisfy the standard, not the standard itself; resolved first, the team could still leave "done" undefined and repeat the omission. The margin is fine because A, B and C are all things the team will plausibly do, but only D fixes the completion criteria that gate every story.
