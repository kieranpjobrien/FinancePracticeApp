# Question

A predictive telecoms rollout is two-thirds complete. The project manager finds the team has informally delivered several "minor enhancements" over the past month, requested directly by a friendly client contact who is not a member of the change control board. None went through change control, the work is already built, and the cumulative effort has pushed the project two weeks behind the schedule baseline. The client is delighted with the extras and is expecting more of the same. The PM has confirmed exactly what was built and the effort it consumed. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Submit the already-delivered enhancements as a change request so their cost and schedule impact is formally analysed and the additions are authorised or rejected by the board.
- **B**: Meet the client contact to reset expectations, explaining that future enhancements must come through change control, before doing anything about the work already delivered.
- **C**: Notify the sponsor and client that the project is two weeks behind because of the informal additions, and propose recovering the schedule by descoping lower-priority baselined work.
- **D**: Update the schedule baseline to absorb the two-week slip caused by the enhancements so the plan reflects the team's actual progress.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The PM has already established the facts, so the immediate task is to put the uncontrolled additions back under the change-control process that decides whether scope and the baselines change. Submitting them as a change request triggers the impact analysis and a recorded authorise-or-reject decision (A); everything else depends on that decision. (B) resetting the client's expectations is necessary and will have to happen, but managing the channel does not adjudicate the scope and schedule impact already incurred, and the question asks for the first action on the substantive problem. (C) proposing to descope baselined work pre-judges the outcome before the enhancements' impact has been formally assessed and authorised, and trades away approved scope to fund changes that may not even be approved. (D) silently re-baselining the schedule to absorb the slip ratifies the unauthorised scope and bypasses the very change control that must evaluate it first.
