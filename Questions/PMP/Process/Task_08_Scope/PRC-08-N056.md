# Question

A scrum team's backlog refinement sessions are not leaving the team confident about the scope it pulls into each sprint, and the project manager is helping them improve. All of the practices below sound reasonable to the team. Which three most genuinely strengthen refinement at this stage? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Splitting large near-term items into smaller, independently valuable slices.
- **B**: Adding acceptance criteria and clarifying ambiguity on the items most likely to be pulled next.
- **C**: Removing or deferring items that no longer reflect current priorities.
- **D**: Producing a relative-size estimate for every item in the backlog before any item may be refined further.
- **E**: Having the product owner pre-write the full technical solution for each upcoming story so developers can start without discussion.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Effective refinement concentrates effort on the near-term items the team will actually pull and keeps the backlog healthy. (A) makes imminent items small enough to flow through a sprint. (B) reduces ambiguity on the items coming next so the team can commit confidently. (C) prunes stale items so the backlog stays relevant. (D) is plausible because estimation is part of refinement, but requiring a full estimate of every item before any deeper refinement front-loads effort onto distant work that will change, inverting progressive elaboration and consuming the time that near-term clarity needs. (E) is plausible because ready stories help, but the product owner dictating the full technical solution removes the developers' design ownership and the collaborative clarification that refinement depends on, and pre-writing solutions for all upcoming stories is exactly the speculative detailing refinement should avoid. The margin is between practices that focus and maintain the backlog and well-intentioned practices that over-invest in distant or over-specified work.
