# Question

On an agile e-commerce checkout rebuild, the team has an agreed definition of done that includes automated tests, accessibility checks, and documentation. At sprint reviews the product owner keeps rejecting stories the team presents as complete because those items are unfinished, even though the visible feature works. Reported velocity is high but little is genuinely releasable. The Scrum Master wants to address the cause. What should the Scrum Master do?

## Options

- **A**: Raise the gap at the retrospective so the team examines why stories are being called done before the definition of done is met, and adjusts how it tracks completion.
- **B**: Add the definition-of-done items as explicit acceptance criteria on every story so they are checked before the story is pulled into the sprint.
- **C**: Introduce a check at the daily scrum where the team confirms each in-progress story against the definition of done before it is moved to done.
- **D**: Schedule a dedicated session to rewrite and strengthen the definition of done so the tests, accessibility, and documentation requirements are unambiguous.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A definition of done already exists and is clear; the dysfunction is that the team is declaring stories done without applying it, and the fix is to surface and correct that behaviour with the team, which is what the retrospective is for. (A) targets the actual cause: the team is not honouring the agreed standard. (B) is the strongest distractor: acceptance criteria define what a *specific* story must do, while the definition of done is the team-wide standard for *any* story; bolting the standing DoD onto each story's criteria confuses two different artefacts and still relies on the team applying them, which is the very thing being skipped. (C) adding a status-check ritual treats a discipline problem as a missing checkpoint and risks turning the daily scrum into a status inspection. (D) rewriting the definition assumes it is unclear, but the scenario shows it is understood and simply not being applied, so sharper wording changes nothing.
