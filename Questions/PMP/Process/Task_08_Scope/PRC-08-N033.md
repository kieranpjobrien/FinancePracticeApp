# Question

An agile team is building a new ride-hailing app under intense pressure to reach the market and start learning from real users. The product owner has listed 60 features as "all essential for launch" and is reluctant to drop any. The project manager believes a smaller first release would launch sooner and generate feedback. How should the project manager best help shape the scope of the first release?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate the product owner ranking all 60 features by business value, then draw the release line at the capacity the team can deliver by the target date.
- **B**: Have the team identify which of the 60 features are technically independent and could ship first without waiting on the others, and release that set.
- **C**: Group the 60 features into themed releases of roughly equal size and ship the first themed release, holding the rest for subsequent dates.
- **D**: Coach the product owner to define the smallest coherent feature set that lets a rider actually complete a trip and yields meaningful market feedback, and sequence the remaining features into later increments.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The goal is an early release that is both usable and able to validate the concept, so the scope of release one should be defined by the minimum that delivers end-to-end value and learning, with everything else deferred. (D) anchors the cut on viability and feedback, which is what makes an early launch worthwhile. (A) is the strongest distractor: value-ranking and cutting to capacity is reasonable, but a pure top-N-by-value slice can omit a feature that is low-value in isolation yet essential to complete a trip, producing a release that ranks well but cannot actually be used. (B) lets technical independence, not customer value, decide the release — the convenient features may not form a usable product. (C) equal-sized themed releases impose arbitrary symmetry rather than identifying the smallest viable first cut, delaying the very feedback the early launch is meant to capture.
