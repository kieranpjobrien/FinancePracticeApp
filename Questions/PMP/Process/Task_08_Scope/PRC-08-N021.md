# Question

Four sprints into an agile mobile-banking build, the product owner reorders the backlog every few days, each time citing whichever stakeholder she spoke to most recently. Velocity is stable, but two near-complete features were dropped late last sprint, and in retrospective the developers said they have stopped investing in refining upcoming items because nothing survives long enough to be worth it. The product owner says she is simply responding to the business, as agile expects. What should the Scrum Master do first?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate a conversation between the product owner and her stakeholders to agree a single intake point for new priorities, so requests stop arriving through ad-hoc side channels.
- **B**: Help the team and product owner adopt a working agreement that the sprint backlog is fixed once committed, while the product backlog stays free to reorder.
- **C**: Coach the product owner to make her ordering decisions against explicit shared criteria such as value, risk and dependency, so the team can see why an item moved.
- **D**: Coach the team to refine only the one or two items most likely to be built next, so churn lower in the backlog stops wasting their effort.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The developers' lost trust traces to one thing: reordering that looks arbitrary because the rationale is invisible, so the first move is to make the ordering criteria explicit and shared, which lets the product owner keep responding to change while the team can see and trust the logic. (C) attacks that root cause. (A) is a reasonable improvement and a plausible later step, but a clean intake channel does not make the *ordering* defensible; the same value-blind reshuffling could still arrive through one door. (B) protecting the sprint commitment is sound and would have saved the two dropped features, yet it addresses only mid-sprint thrash and leaves the underlying product-backlog churn and the trust problem untouched. (D) reducing refinement depth treats the symptom the team raised by lowering their investment further, accommodating the churn rather than resolving why items move. The margin is fine because A, B and D are all legitimate agile coaching, but only C repairs the visibility of prioritisation that the erosion of trust depends on.
