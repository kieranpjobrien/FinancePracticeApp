# Question

On a predictive municipal library construction project, a stakeholder argues that a requested fit-out addition was "always part of the agreed scope" and points to a line in the approved requirements documentation describing the desired feature. The PM disagrees that it was committed work. To determine authoritatively whether the addition is inside the agreed scope, against which reference should the PM check the claim?

## Options

- **A**: The approved scope statement, work breakdown structure, and WBS dictionary.
- **B**: The requirements documentation, since it is where stakeholder needs are formally recorded.
- **C**: The project charter, since it authorises the project and states its objectives.
- **D**: The requirements traceability matrix, since it links each requirement to its source and deliverable.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Whether work is committed is judged against the scope baseline, which is the approved scope statement, WBS, and WBS dictionary; only work decomposed into the WBS and described in the dictionary is in agreed scope. (B) is the trap: requirements documentation captures stated and desired needs, but a need being *recorded* does not mean it was *baselined as committed work*; many recorded requirements are deferred or excluded. (C) the charter gives high-level objectives, not the detailed in-scope/out-of-scope definition needed to settle this. (D) the traceability matrix shows where a requirement came from and where it maps, but it traces requirements rather than defining the approved scope boundary, so it cannot by itself confirm the item was committed.
