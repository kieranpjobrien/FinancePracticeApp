# Question

A hybrid government grants-portal programme has a legally fixed go-live date and a fixed budget, and the full feature set the policy team requested cannot be built in time. The sponsor confirms the date cannot move and no more funding is available. The policy team insists every feature is mandatory, but the PM knows some features are needed to meet the statute on day one while others merely improve service. The PM has decided the first release must be reduced. How should the PM decide what goes into that release?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate a session with the policy team and sponsor to classify features against the statutory go-live obligations, agree the minimum set that satisfies those obligations, and sequence the rest for later releases.
- **B**: Ask the policy team to rank all features themselves and take the top-ranked set that fits the date and budget, since they own the policy outcomes.
- **C**: Have the sponsor and PM agree the release scope against the statutory and budget constraints, then present it to the policy team with the rationale for what was deferred.
- **D**: Have the delivery team estimate every feature and build the largest set of high-ranked features that fits within the fixed date and budget, deferring the rest.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Scope is the only movable variable, so the reduction must be anchored to what the release legally has to achieve and agreed with both the people who own the policy and the person accountable for the budget. Facilitating a joint session that classifies features against the day-one statutory obligations and agrees the minimum compliant set (A) produces a release that is defensible against the legal driver and owned by the stakeholders who must defend it. (B) letting the policy team rank in isolation keeps ownership but, because they currently treat everything as mandatory, their ranking may not distinguish statutory from nice-to-have and excludes the sponsor who controls the trade-off against budget. (C) a sponsor-plus-PM decision respects the budget authority and is not autocratic in form, but settling scope without the policy team in the room risks misjudging which features are genuinely statutory and loses their buy-in for what was cut. (D) estimating and fitting the largest high-ranked set lets size and effort drive selection, which can ship several cheaper service improvements while dropping a single expensive but legally required capability.
