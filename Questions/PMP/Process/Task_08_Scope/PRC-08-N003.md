# Question

On a predictive highway-signage installation contract, the project manager's team has completed a deliverable that fully meets the documented specification. At the formal review the client's representative refuses to sign acceptance, saying the signs "don't look like what we imagined" although they match the approved drawings and tolerances. The PM has the signed specification and inspection records. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Agree to rebuild the signs to the client's new mental picture to preserve the relationship, absorbing the cost as goodwill.
- **B**: Walk the client through the approved specification and inspection results against the deliverable, and if the work conforms, treat any desired changes as a change request rather than rework.
- **C**: Escalate immediately to the sponsor and contracts team to enforce acceptance before discussing anything with the client.
- **D**: Log the client's reaction as a defect, return the signs to the team for a redesign that better matches what the client now describes, and re-present them.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Validate Scope is about confirming deliverables against the agreed acceptance criteria; conformance to the approved specification is the objective benchmark, so the PM first demonstrates that the work meets what was agreed. Where the deliverable conforms, the gap is a new desire, which belongs in integrated change control, not unpaid rework. (A) rewards undocumented expectations and invites repeated scope creep at the project's expense. (C) jumps to contractual enforcement before the simple, relationship-preserving step of reviewing the evidence with the client. (D) is the trap: treating conforming work as a "defect" mislabels a change of mind as a quality failure and commits the team to unpaid redesign against criteria the deliverable already meets, which is exactly the scope-creep-as-rework outcome that demonstrating conformance and routing the change is meant to prevent.
