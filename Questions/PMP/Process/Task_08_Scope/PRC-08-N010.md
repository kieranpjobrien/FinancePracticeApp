# Question

An agile insurance-claims platform team already holds a backlog refinement session each week and has a written definition of ready. Even so, stories keep stalling mid-sprint: acceptance criteria turn out vague, dependencies surface late, and stories prove far larger than their points suggested. At refinement the product owner walks the team through new stories, but the developers mostly listen and the session is run by the product owner alone. What should the Scrum Master change first?

## Options

- **A**: Have the whole team actively examine each upcoming story at refinement against the definition of ready, splitting and re-sizing it and naming dependencies before it can enter a sprint.
- **B**: Move refinement to the day before planning so the detail is fresh and fewer dependencies are missed by the time the team commits.
- **C**: Have the product owner attach detailed acceptance criteria and a dependency list to each story before refinement so sessions can confirm readiness faster.
- **D**: Add a second weekly refinement session so there is more total time to clarify, size, and split the upcoming stories.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Refinement and a definition of ready already exist; the defect is that refinement is a product-owner monologue, so the people who build and estimate the work are not the ones examining, sizing, and splitting it, which is why criteria stay vague, dependencies are missed, and sizes are wrong. (A) fixes the cause by making refinement a working session the whole team owns against the definition of ready. (B) timing the session closer to planning keeps the same passive process and the same blind spots. (C) the product owner pre-writing criteria and dependencies still leaves one person doing the analysis the team needs to do collectively, and it is precisely the product owner's solo view that has been missing complexity. (D) more refinement time repeats an ineffective format at greater length rather than changing who does the thinking.
