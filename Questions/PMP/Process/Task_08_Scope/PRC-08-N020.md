# Question

While collecting requirements for a predictive university student-records system, the project manager finds the registry and finance departments have submitted directly contradictory rules for how a fee-status change affects an enrolment record. Both cite their own departmental policy and insist their version is mandatory. The project manager convenes a joint workshop, but after a long discussion neither department will move and no agreed rule emerges. The requirements must be baselined this week. What should the project manager do next?

## Options

- **A**: Trace each rule back to its governing policy and the regulatory or business obligation behind it, and bring that analysis to the sponsor as the decision owner to set the authoritative rule.
- **B**: Reconvene the same workshop with a neutral facilitator and more time, since reconciliation by the two departments is preferable to escalation.
- **C**: Record both rules as competing requirements with a note that the conflict is unresolved, and baseline the rest of the scope to stay on schedule.
- **D**: Adopt finance's rule, because fee status is a financial matter and finance therefore has the stronger claim to own it.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Facilitation has already been tried and has failed to produce agreement, and the requirements cannot be left contradictory at baseline; the next step is to bring the evidenced conflict, traced to each rule's underlying policy and obligation, to the sponsor, who has the authority to make a binding cross-departmental decision. (A) does this. (B) is the trap: facilitation is the right *first* move, but repeating a stalled workshop when both parties are immovable and a deadline looms simply burns the week; a decision authority is now needed. (C) baselining an unresolved contradiction pushes a business decision into the build and guarantees rework, which is precisely what defining scope is meant to prevent. (D) deciding it yourself on a plausible-sounding ownership argument substitutes the PM's judgement for the sponsor's authority and may breach a registry or regulatory obligation the PM has not weighed.
