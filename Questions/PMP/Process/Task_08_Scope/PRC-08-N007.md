# Question

A start-up's agile team is building a new freight-tracking product with a hard, externally fixed market window in eight weeks. The product owner has 90 stories and sponsors want "all of it" launched. Average velocity suggests roughly a third of the backlog is achievable. The product owner asks the team how to settle the first release. Which approach best serves the goal?

## Options

- **A**: Rank stories by business value and capacity, then agree the smallest coherent end-to-end product that is genuinely usable in the window, treating the rest as later releases.
- **B**: Rank the full backlog by value alone and pull stories down the list until the eight weeks are full, accepting whatever subset that produces.
- **C**: Time-box a discovery spike to validate the highest-value stories with users first, then commit the release scope once the findings are in.
- **D**: Hold a weighted-shortest-job-first session to order the backlog by cost of delay, then deliver in that order until the window closes.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

With a fixed window and scope exceeding capacity, the goal is a usable product at launch, not merely "the most valuable stories we can fit". (A) explicitly aims for the smallest *coherent, end-to-end usable* slice, which is what makes the release viable. (B) ranks by value but pulling down the list can leave a set of high-value yet disconnected features that do not function together as a usable product. (C) a discovery spike is valuable but burns part of a tight eight-week window and still does not decide what forms a shippable product; it defers the very decision asked about. (D) WSJF is a sound ordering technique, but ordering by cost of delay optimises sequence rather than guaranteeing the delivered subset is a coherent usable whole by the deadline.
