# Question

A predictive municipal-website redesign is ahead of schedule and under budget, with about three weeks of slack remaining. The lead designer proposes using the slack to add animated transitions and a chatbot the client never requested, arguing the team has genuine evidence these would improve resident engagement. The acceptance criteria mention neither feature, and the client has been responsive throughout. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Log the idea in lessons learned and the project's recommendations register for the client to consider in a future phase, leaving the current scope unchanged.
- **B**: Build a quick prototype of the two features within the slack and demonstrate it to the client, so their decision is informed by something concrete rather than a description.
- **C**: Take the proposal, with its supporting evidence, to the client as a potential scope change for them to decide, and add the features only if they approve it through the agreed change process.
- **D**: Decline the proposal as gold plating, since the available slack exists to protect the committed scope against risk, not to expand what is delivered.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Slack does not license the team to expand scope on its own, but the team has real evidence of value, and the client owns scope — so the right move surfaces the opportunity to them now, through the change process, rather than either smuggling it in or burying it. (C) does exactly this and lets the client make an informed, authorised call while there is still time to act on it. (B) is the strongest distractor: a prototype would inform the decision well, but committing slack to building unrequested features *before* the client has agreed to even consider them risks the very over-delivery and expectation problems at issue, and presupposes the change rather than proposing it. (A) parking a time-sensitive, evidenced opportunity in lessons learned needlessly forfeits value the client could capture in this phase while the slack exists. (D) is defensible discipline and correctly names the risk, but reflexively refusing forgoes a genuine, evidenced benefit that the client — not the PM — is entitled to weigh.
