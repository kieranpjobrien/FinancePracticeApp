# Question

On a predictive medical-device documentation project, a developer demonstrates an analytics dashboard she built over the weekend that was never in the requirements, arguing it makes the deliverable "more complete". It works well, the regulated client has not seen it, and the next delivery to the client is in four days. The project manager must decide how to proceed. What is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: Keep the dashboard out of the imminent delivery, and submit it to the client as a change request with the developer's rationale so they can decide whether to bring it into scope.
- **B**: Demonstrate the dashboard to the client at the upcoming delivery as an optional extra they can take or leave, without changing the contracted deliverables.
- **C**: Hold the dashboard back and route it into the project's continuous-improvement log for consideration in a later phase, leaving the current delivery untouched.
- **D**: Have the quality team verify the dashboard against the project's standards first, so that if the client wants it the validation evidence already exists.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The dashboard is gold-plating — unrequested function outside the approved baseline — and in a regulated context it carries validation and regulatory exposure, so it cannot simply travel with the delivery. But the developer has a genuine value case, and the client, not the PM, owns scope. (A) does both correct things: it keeps the unauthorised feature out of the imminent delivery and formally offers it to the client through change control, where an authorised in-or-out decision can be made. (B) is the strongest distractor: showing it as "optional" sounds collaborative, but presenting unbaselined regulated functionality at a delivery without a controlled change blurs what was contractually delivered and invites informal acceptance of unvalidated work. (C) burying a time-sensitive, evidenced idea in an improvement log needlessly forfeits value the client could weigh now, while the developer's effort is fresh. (D) spending regulated verification effort on the feature before the client has agreed even to consider it commits cost to a change that has not been authorised and presupposes the outcome. The margin is that B, C and D each mishandle either the timing or the authority of the decision, whereas only A protects the baseline and gives the client a clean, authorised choice.
