# Question

In an agile marketing-automation project, the product owner has been quietly accepting small "while you're in there" additions from stakeholders into each sprint. Velocity has held steady, but the release first forecast for eight sprints is now projected at fourteen, and stakeholders are angry about the slip they did not see coming. What is the best action for the project manager to take?

## Options

- **A**: Escalate to the sponsor to have the additional requests formally rejected as scope creep.
- **B**: Impose a freeze on all new backlog items until the original eight-sprint scope is delivered.
- **C**: Ask the team to raise their commitment each sprint to absorb the extra items and recover the original date.
- **D**: Make the cumulative impact visible by reviewing the added items at the next backlog refinement and forecasting the release with and without them, so stakeholders can decide what stays.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Steady velocity with a slipping date is a classic symptom of unmanaged scope growth; the fix is transparency and a value-based re-decision, not blame. (D) surfaces the trade-off and returns the prioritisation decision to the product owner and stakeholders with real forecasts. (B) is rigid and may block genuinely higher-value work that has emerged. (C) pressures the team to inflate commitments, harming sustainable pace and quality. (A) treats legitimate emerging needs as something to police rather than prioritise, and escalation precedes any attempt to make the impact visible.
