# Question

On a predictive water-treatment automation project, the control software has passed every internal quality inspection and the team is confident it is complete. The contract requires the client's operations representative to formally accept each deliverable. That representative attended the internal test demonstration, said verbally she was "happy with what she saw", but has not run the deliverable through her own acceptance procedure or signed the acceptance record. The sponsor is pressing to mark the deliverable done and release the team to the next phase. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Record the deliverable as accepted on the basis of the representative's verbal approval at the demonstration, then continue obtaining her signature in parallel.
- **B**: Arrange for the client representative to carry out her own acceptance procedure against the agreed criteria and record the result formally before the deliverable is reported as complete.
- **C**: Mark the deliverable as complete because it has passed all internal inspections and the contractual acceptance is now a formality.
- **D**: Issue the internal inspection reports to the representative and ask her to sign acceptance based on them, sparing her a separate procedure.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Internal inspection confirms the work is built correctly; contractual completion turns on the customer formally accepting it against the agreed criteria, and the contract specifies *her* procedure and a signed record — neither of which has happened. (B) performs the actual acceptance step, the only thing that closes the scope defensibly. (A) is the strongest distractor: verbal approval at a demo is encouraging, but recording acceptance before it is real lets the project report a deliverable as done that the client could still reject under her own procedure, leaving the PM exposed. (C) treats a contractual acceptance gate as a rubber stamp and substitutes the team's confidence for the client's judgement. (D) asks the client to accept on the strength of the contractor's own reports, which is not the independent examination her acceptance is meant to provide and weakens any sign-off obtained.
