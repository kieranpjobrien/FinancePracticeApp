# Question

Late in a manufacturing-line installation, you discover a member of your team quietly cut a documented commissioning test to keep to schedule and recorded it as passed. The line is working and the customer is satisfied. The team member is otherwise reliable and clearly acted under the schedule pressure you all felt. What should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Ensure the skipped test is properly performed and the record corrected, then address the falsification directly with the team member.
- **B**: Address the conduct firmly with the team member and reinforce that records must be accurate, since the line is functioning correctly anyway.
- **C**: Quietly arrange for the test to be run now and the record fixed, avoiding a confrontation that could demoralise a usually reliable person.
- **D**: Examine the schedule pressure that drove the shortcut and adjust the plan so the team is not forced into such corners again.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Two distinct duties are in play — the integrity of the deliverable and the integrity of the record — and a leader must address both, regardless of the fact that the line currently works. (A) does both: it makes the product genuinely conform by running the omitted test and corrects the record, then confronts the falsification, which is non-negotiable on professional-responsibility grounds. (B) handles the conduct but leaves an untested commissioning step accepted as passed, which is a real quality and safety exposure that "the line works" does not retire. (C) fixes the technical gap but ducks the falsification entirely; sweeping a dishonest record under the carpet sets a corrosive precedent. (D) addressing the systemic pressure is valuable and should follow, but it cannot substitute for correcting this specific breach and record now. Both the deliverable and the honesty of the documentation must be put right, then the conduct addressed and the root cause tackled.
