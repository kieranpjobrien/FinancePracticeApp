# Question

A junior analyst on your data-migration project quietly fixed a defect over the weekend that would otherwise have blocked Monday's user acceptance testing. She did not raise it; you heard it from the test lead. The team is in a sustained crunch and several members look worn down. You want to recognise her, and you are also aware that an unreported weekend fix going straight into the build sidesteps your normal review and signals a fatigue problem. What is the best leadership response?

## Options

- **A**: Recognise her contribution privately in the way she values, and separately raise with the whole team that weekend or after-hours fixes should still go through review, framing it as protecting both quality and people's time.
- **B**: Recognise her contribution to the team in a way she is comfortable with, then take the fatigue you are seeing to a conversation about the crunch and the team's pace.
- **C**: Thank her directly and ask her to walk the test lead through the fix so it is properly reviewed, then keep watching the team's workload before raising the pace issue more widely.
- **D**: Acknowledge the save visibly so the team sees the impact, and use the moment to open a discussion on why people feel they must fix things off the clock.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

All four recognise the analyst and all four engage a real secondary concern, so the margin is fine and turns on what the leader should prioritise. The dominant signal is a tired team in sustained crunch; the unreviewed weekend fix is a symptom of that pressure, so (B) recognises her appropriately and then addresses the root cause — the unsustainable pace — which is the leadership priority here. (C) is sound on the review gap but defers the pace conversation and narrows the response to process, leaving the fatigue driving the behaviour unaddressed. (A) splits the message well but leads the team-facing part with a process correction off the back of someone's good deed, which can read as rebuking the very contribution being praised. (D) treats a visible call-out as the recognition; for a junior who deliberately stayed quiet, a public spotlight may not be how she values being recognised, and pairing praise with a "why are you working off the clock" discussion in the same public moment is awkward. Recognise in the manner that fits the person, then deal with the systemic pace issue.
