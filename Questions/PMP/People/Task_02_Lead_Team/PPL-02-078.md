# Question

A respected engineer on your aerospace-components project made a judgement call to expedite a part that later failed inspection, causing a minor rework cost and a short delay. At the project review, with the team and two functional managers present, one manager asks you pointedly, "So whose mistake was this?" The engineer is in the room. The decision was reasonable given what was known at the time. How should you respond in the moment?

## Options

- **A**: Explain that the decision was a sound judgement given the information available, treat it as a team learning point, and move the discussion to what you will adjust going forward.
- **B**: State plainly that as the project manager you are accountable for the outcome, so the responsibility rests with you.
- **C**: Decline to assign blame in the meeting and offer to discuss the specifics of the decision with the manager separately afterwards.
- **D**: Describe the facts of how the decision was made and let those present draw their own conclusions about accountability.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

How a leader handles a reasonable decision that turned out badly, in public, sets the team's psychological safety for every future call — the goal is to protect the individual, normalise honest mistakes, and redirect to learning. (A) does all three: it defends the quality of the decision, reframes blame-hunting as learning, and steers forward, which is the strongest response. (B) is well-intentioned and the accountability point is true, but absorbing it as "my mistake" still concedes the framing that a mistake needing an owner occurred, when in fact a sound decision met bad luck. (C) avoids a public blaming but, by deferring, leaves the engineer exposed in the room and implicitly validates that someone is at fault. (D) reciting facts without an interpretation lets the loaded "whose mistake" framing stand and abandons the engineer to others' judgements. Actively reframing the event as a reasonable decision and a learning opportunity best preserves trust and safety.
