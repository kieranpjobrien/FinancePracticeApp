# Question

A senior stakeholder who genuinely depends on your product keeps coming to your scrum team's area mid-sprint to ask individual developers for quick data pulls and status updates. The requests are real and sometimes time-sensitive, but they keep pulling developers off committed sprint work, and the developers feel unable to refuse someone so senior. As the scrum master, what is the best leadership action?

## Options

- **A**: Establish with the stakeholder and the product owner a single intake route for their requests, so urgent ones can be triaged into the sprint properly and the rest queued, protecting the team's focus without cutting the stakeholder off.
- **B**: Step in and tell the stakeholder directly that they must stop approaching developers during the sprint and route everything through you.
- **C**: Allocate a fixed slice of each sprint's capacity as a buffer for the stakeholder's ad hoc requests so the team can absorb them without derailing commitments.
- **D**: Coach the developers to acknowledge the stakeholder politely but redirect them to the product owner, so the team learns to protect its own focus.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The stakeholder's need is legitimate, so the leadership task is to protect the team's focus while still serving that need, which means building a proper channel rather than simply blocking or absorbing the interruptions. (A) routes requests through the product owner for triage so genuinely urgent items are prioritised into the work and the rest do not ambush developers mid-sprint; it serves both the team and the stakeholder. (B) protects focus but treats a legitimate stakeholder bluntly and centralises everything on you, straining the relationship and making you a bottleneck. (C) reserving buffer capacity can be a sound tactic, but committing fixed capacity to undefined ad hoc work before triaging it risks institutionalising the interruptions and sizing the buffer wrong. (D) rightly involves the product owner but pushes the redirecting onto junior developers, leaving them to manage a senior stakeholder the scrum master should be handling.
