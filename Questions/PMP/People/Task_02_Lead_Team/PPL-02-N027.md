# Question

On a pharmaceutical packaging line upgrade, a quiet automation technician consistently solves the hardest problems on the project but never volunteers in meetings. In a one-to-one he lights up describing the elegance of a control routine he built, and the only frustration he voices is that "the clever part is invisible — it just works, so nobody realises what was actually involved." He turned down an earlier offer to act as team lead. How should you lead this person to keep him motivated and engaged?

## Options

- **A**: Put him forward for a "technical excellence" award decided by the leadership team and announced at the company all-hands.
- **B**: Arrange for him to walk a few senior engineers and peers through how his toughest solutions work, in a forum where people who grasp the craft can engage with it.
- **C**: Single him out with warm praise in the regular team meeting so the whole team hears how valuable his contribution is.
- **D**: Hand him the most demanding work package coming up, signalling that you trust him with the hardest problems.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

He has not stated a reward he wants; you have to read his driver from behaviour. The clues are consistent — pride in mastery, declining the lead role, and a frustration that is specifically about the difficulty of his work being unseen by people who could appreciate it. The leadership task is to satisfy that intrinsic driver. (B) gives his work a competent audience that can actually engage with the craft, which is the precise recognition he is missing. (A) is genuine recognition and tempting, but an award badged and announced by leadership is status-and-visibility recognition aimed at a broad audience — close, yet it does not give him what he values most: knowledgeable peers understanding the substance of what he did. (C) is well-meant but generic praise from a non-expert room does not convey the technical difficulty and may discomfit a markedly private person. (D) keeps him challenged, which sustains him, but it answers a need for stimulation rather than his expressed need to have the hard part of his work seen and understood. The margin between A and B is fine; B wins because it targets comprehension by experts rather than visibility to the crowd.
