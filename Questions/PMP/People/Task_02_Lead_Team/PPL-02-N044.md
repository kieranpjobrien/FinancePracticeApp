# Question

Midway through a sprint, a marketing director repeatedly contacts individual developers on a scrum team with urgent ad-hoc requests unrelated to the sprint goal. Developers are context-switching and the sprint goal is now at risk. The product owner is on leave for the week with no delegate appointed. As the team's leader, what should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Take the marketing director's requests on yourself as a single point of contact, capturing them for the product owner to prioritise on return, while the team stays on the sprint goal.
- **B**: Engage the marketing director directly to understand the urgency, explain the sprint commitment, and agree how genuinely urgent work will be handled so the team can stay focused.
- **C**: Add the marketing director's requests to the current sprint so the work is visible and tracked alongside the committed items.
- **D**: Convene the developers to decide collectively which of the marketing requests they can absorb without missing the sprint goal.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A servant leader protects the team's focus by dealing with the source of the disruption rather than passing the burden to the team, and engaging the stakeholder also surfaces any genuine urgency that must not simply be parked. (B) addresses the director causing the interruptions, preserves the sprint commitment, and establishes a path for real urgency — covering the absent product owner's prioritisation role appropriately. (A) is close and well-intentioned: shielding the team by absorbing the intake is sound, but unilaterally deferring everything to the product owner's return can leave a truly time-critical request stranded for a week, which is why engaging the director to test urgency is the stronger move. (C) injects unplanned scope mid-sprint and undermines the sprint commitment. (D) leaves the team to triage interruptions that leadership should be shielding them from.
