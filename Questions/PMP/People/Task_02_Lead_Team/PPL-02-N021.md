# Question

Three sprints into building a hospital scheduling product, the development team is delivering working increments to a good standard, but in retrospectives people describe their work as "just closing tickets" and energy is low. The product owner keeps the backlog tidy, prioritised, and annotated with the business reason for each item, and the team can recite those reasons. The team has never met a clinician or seen the wards their software will run on. As the project manager, what is the most effective way to lead the team out of this?

## Options

- **A**: Arrange for the team to spend a shift on a ward, sitting with nurses who wrestle with the current scheduling system, so they experience first-hand the people their increments serve.
- **B**: Invite a charge nurse to the next sprint review to react to the increment and tell the team what difference it makes on the ward.
- **C**: Open a retrospective on what would make the work feel meaningful, then act on what the team says it needs to feel connected to the outcome.
- **D**: Bring the team into backlog refinement so they help shape upcoming items around clinician outcomes rather than receiving them as ready-made tickets.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The team already has the stated reasons for each item yet still feels disconnected, so more articulated purpose — written, presented, or discussed — is not the gap; lived contact with the people they serve is. (A) gives the team direct, sensory experience of the users behind the increments, which is the servant-leadership move that converts abstract reasons into felt meaning, and it is the only option that does so. (B) brings the user to the team, which helps, but a single second-hand reaction in a review is a pale substitute for being on the ward and does not let the team see the work in context. (C) is sound facilitation and may well surface the need for user contact, but as an action it stops at talking about meaning rather than supplying the experience that creates it. (D) deepens ownership of the backlog, yet shaping items around clinician outcomes the team has still never witnessed leaves the same missing ingredient. All four are reasonable leadership moves; A is best because it alone closes the specific gap the scenario describes.
