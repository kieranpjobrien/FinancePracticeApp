# Question

On a three-month cybersecurity uplift, engineers repeatedly ask "who actually decides?" before making any meaningful move. Your sponsor wants speed; your architect wants control; your security lead wants consensus. The team is stalling on design choices. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Make it clear that the sponsor has the final say on all decisions so engineers can act quickly without ambiguity and the programme can hit its three-month delivery target.
- **B**: Reserve every meaningful decision for yourself as the PM so that the architect, security lead and sponsor are no longer pulling the team in three different directions on design choices.
- **C**: Co-create a lightweight decision-rights matrix with the sponsor, architect and security lead — what's delegated, what's consulted, what's escalated — and publish it to the team.
- **D**: Ask engineers to escalate any unclear decision to all three — sponsor, architect and security lead — so that every call has visibility and agreement from every involved leader before action.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

(C) solves the root cause — unclear decision rights — with a published, co-owned matrix that unblocks the team and keeps the three leaders aligned. This is the PMI governance-clarity pattern. (A) centralises authority in a sponsor who is not close enough to most calls and marginalises the architect and security lead. (B) creates a PM bottleneck and removes ownership from people who should have it. (D) multiplies the coordination tax on every decision and is exactly the pattern producing the stall.
