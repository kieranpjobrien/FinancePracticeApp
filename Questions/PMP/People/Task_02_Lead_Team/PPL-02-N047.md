# Question

On a scrum team, the product owner is new to the role and keeps directing developers on how to implement stories during the sprint, not just what outcome is needed. Developers are frustrated because implementation choices are theirs to make, and two have complained privately to the PM, who is acting as scrum master. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Coach the product owner privately on the boundary between the "what" they own and the "how" the developers own, and help them express needs as outcomes.
- **B**: Facilitate a session where the team and product owner together define and agree their respective decision rights, so the boundary is owned by everyone rather than handed down.
- **C**: Coach the developers to engage the product owner in the moment — acknowledge the intent, then explain that implementation is their call — so they learn to hold the boundary themselves.
- **D**: Bring the pattern to the next retrospective for the team to inspect and adapt as a working-agreement issue, the standard scrum mechanism for resolving team process problems.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A scrum master leads by coaching team members into their roles while protecting the team's self-management, and here the cause is one new colleague who does not yet understand a role boundary. Every option is a legitimate agile facilitation move, so the margin is fine and turns on what fixes the root with the least collateral. (A) addresses the specific person whose behaviour is the problem, privately and developmentally, which is the most direct and least exposing way to correct a coachable misunderstanding and is what a new PO most needs. (B) co-defining decision rights is genuinely valuable, but convening the whole team to formalise a boundary that mainly one person is crossing risks feeling like a collective correction aimed at the PO and is heavier than the situation needs as a first step. (C) developing the developers to hold their own boundary is a good longer-term aim, but asking frustrated developers to push back on a new PO in the moment can inflame the friction before the PO even understands the line they are crossing. (D) the retrospective is the right venue for team process problems in general, but routing a single new colleague's role confusion through a group ceremony is slower and more public than a quiet coaching conversation, and risks the PO feeling ganged up on. The fine margin is coaching the individual at the root (A) versus escalating to a team-level mechanism (B, D) or shifting the burden onto the developers (C) before the PO has simply been helped to see the boundary.
