# Question

You are the project manager for an agile team building a mobile banking feature. During the daily stand-up, a developer mentions she has been blocked for two days because she cannot get access to a test environment owned by another department, and she has not wanted to "make a fuss." The team is otherwise running well. What is the most appropriate leadership response?

## Options

- **A**: Remind the team at stand-up that anyone who is blocked should raise it immediately so the impediment can be cleared the same day.
- **B**: Take ownership of escalating to the other department's manager today to secure the developer's access, then follow up that it is resolved.
- **C**: Ask the developer to log the blocker on the impediment board so it is visible and prioritised at the next backlog refinement.
- **D**: Coach the developer through who in the other department she could approach herself, so she builds the confidence to clear such blockers in future.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A servant leader's core duty is to remove impediments the team cannot clear on its own, and a cross-departmental access barrier is exactly that — it sits outside the team's authority and is already two days old. (B) acts now to unblock the person, which is the immediate priority. (A) addresses the reporting culture but leaves today's blocker unresolved, so the developer stays stuck. (C) defers a live, two-day-old blocker to a future refinement session, which is too slow for something already costing delivery. (D) builds long-term capability and is worth doing later, but coaching the person to solve it herself does not discharge the leader's responsibility to clear an impediment that is currently outside her reach. Removing the obstacle comes first; reinforcing the culture and building capability follow.
