# Question

Your scrum team delivers each sprint's backlog items reliably and to a good standard. The product owner hands work over as isolated features with no narrative, and when developers hit small design forks they make safe, literal choices and ship exactly what was asked. A competitor has just launched a rival product, and the sponsor wants the team moving faster and smarter on the trade-offs that differentiate you. The product owner is stretched thin and rarely explains where the product is heading. As project manager, what is the best leadership action?

## Options

- **A**: Work with the product owner to share the product vision and two-quarter roadmap with the team and keep it visible, so developers can weigh everyday design forks against where the product is going.
- **B**: Ask the product owner to enrich each backlog item with fuller acceptance criteria and design intent so developers have clearer guidance on each feature.
- **C**: Set up a recurring sync where developers bring design forks to the product owner for a decision, so trade-offs are made by the person who owns priorities.
- **D**: Run a competitor teardown with the team so they understand the new threat and can respond to it in their work.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The team makes literal, low-leverage choices because it lacks a shared sense of direction, not because individual tickets are underspecified; equipping people with the vision is what lets them make sharper autonomous trade-offs. (A) gives the team the roadmap and vision so each small decision serves where the product is heading, which is the durable, scalable fix. (B) adds detail feature by feature but more acceptance criteria still do not convey strategic direction, and it leans harder on an already-stretched product owner. (C) routes every fork back to the product owner, which keeps the team dependent and bottlenecks decisions on the one person with no time. (D) supplies one slice of context, but a single teardown is a snapshot of one rival rather than the ongoing product vision the team needs to reason with sprint after sprint.
