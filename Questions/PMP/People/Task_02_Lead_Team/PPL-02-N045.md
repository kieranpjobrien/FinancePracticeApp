# Question

On a construction project, one engineer consistently misses internal deadlines, forcing teammates to cover his deliverables to keep the schedule. The team has not complained openly, but the PM has noticed rising resentment and two strong performers have started disengaging in meetings. The engineer is technically capable and personable. What should the PM do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Hold a private conversation with the engineer to understand the cause of the missed deadlines and agree clear expectations and support before the pattern erodes the team further.
- **B**: Speak first with the two disengaging strong performers to understand how much the situation is costing them and reassure them you are dealing with it, then address the engineer.
- **C**: Rebalance the upcoming deadlines so the engineer's commitments better fit a realistic pace, on the basis that consistent slippage often signals an overloaded or poorly sequenced plan.
- **D**: Raise slipping internal commitments with the whole team as a shared working-practice issue, without singling anyone out, so the norm is reset for everyone at once.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Leadership requires addressing underperformance directly and early, because unaddressed it corrodes the team's fairness and morale — and the FIRST move should go to the source while the cause is still unknown. Each option targets a real part of the problem, so the choice turns on sequence and on who the PM speaks to first. (A) goes straight to the engineer privately, diagnoses the cause, and sets clear expectations with support — respecting the individual while getting to the root, which is what must happen before any other step makes sense. (B) the strong performers' disengagement is real and will need attention, but reassuring them before you have even spoken to the engineer risks taking sides on an unconfirmed cause and can read as managing the audience rather than the issue. (C) replanning may well be warranted, but adjusting deadlines before understanding why he misses them assumes overload is the cause and could simply lower the bar for a different underlying problem. (D) a team-wide norm reset avoids singling him out, but addressing one person's specific pattern through a general team message is indirect, lets him hide in the group, and signals to the others that the real issue is being skirted. The fine margin is going privately to the individual first (A) versus addressing the affected peers (B), the plan (C), or the team norm (D) before the cause is known.
