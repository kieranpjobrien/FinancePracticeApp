# Question

Two months from a major automotive launch, your project manager radar tells you the team is fraying: standups have gone quiet, two engineers snapped at each other yesterday, and a normally upbeat tester has become withdrawn. Nothing has appeared in the issue log. Schedule pressure is high. As the leader, which two actions best demonstrate the emotional intelligence the situation calls for right now? (Choose two.)

## Options

- **A**: Hold low-key one-to-ones to understand individually how people are coping and what is driving the tension.
- **B**: Name what you are sensing to the whole team in the next standup, acknowledge the strain openly, and invite them to talk about how the team is doing.
- **C**: Read the team's mood and adjust your own demeanour and pace to steady them rather than letting your own pressure spill into the room.
- **D**: Sit the two engineers who snapped at each other down together to talk through and repair the friction before it spreads.
- **E**: Take the schedule pressure to your sponsor to seek relief, on the basis that the strain is being driven by an unrealistic timeline.

## Answer

A, C

## Explanation

Every option here is a defensible, well-intentioned leadership act, so the question turns on which two best fit emotional intelligence at this moment — perceiving the team's state and managing yourself before acting outwardly. (A) gathers the real, individual picture the issue log will never show and is the necessary first read; withdrawal and snapping have different personal causes you can only learn one-to-one. (C) is self-management and social awareness in action — regulating your own behaviour so you steady rather than amplify the room — and it requires no new information to start. (B) is tempting and not wrong in principle, but naming the strain to the whole group before you understand individual causes can expose a withdrawn person publicly and put people on the spot under pressure; it is better sequenced after (A). (D) addresses one visible symptom, yet forcing a repair conversation before you understand what is actually driving the two engineers risks misreading a flare-up that may be about overload rather than each other. (E) may even be correct about the root cause and worth doing, but it is an upward, structural move rather than the immediate emotional-intelligence response to a team you have not yet read. The fine margin is that A and C are the EI-grounded actions you can and should take first; the others are reasonable but either premature or aimed elsewhere.
