# Question

A previously co-located team building a logistics dashboard is being split: half stay in the head office and half move to a new development centre two time zones away. The work stays the same; only the team's distribution changes. Members worry that decision-making will favour whoever is in the room with you and that the remote half will lose influence. As the leader, which actions best maintain a single, cohesive team across the two locations? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Establish working agreements that define which decisions are made synchronously versus asynchronously, and default to making key decisions visible to both locations.
- **B**: Nominate a senior remote-site member as a decision delegate who joins your head-office discussions, so the remote half always has a voice where decisions are taken.
- **C**: Rotate the timing of recurring meetings so the burden of inconvenient hours is shared rather than always falling on the remote site.
- **D**: Be deliberate about including remote members in informal communication and recognition, not only formal meetings, to counter "out of sight, out of mind".
- **E**: Move most coordination to a shared written channel and trim synchronous meetings, so progress no longer depends on finding overlapping hours across the two zones.

## Answer

A, C, D

## Explanation

The fear is that proximity to the leader will translate into influence, so the strongest actions are the ones that make process fair and keep the remote half present. (A) makes the decision process explicit and transparent to both sites, directly countering co-located dominance. (C) shares the cost of time-zone overlap fairly, a recognised practice for sustaining trust on dispersed teams. (D) attacks proximity bias where exclusion actually creeps in — the informal and recognition channels. (B) sounds inclusive but routes the remote site's influence through a single proxy and still locates "where decisions are taken" at head office, entrenching the imbalance rather than dissolving it. (E) is a reasonable efficiency response to time zones, but stripping synchronous interaction from a team that has just been split removes the real-time contact it most needs to rebuild trust and shared context — convenience bought at the cost of cohesion.
