# Question

You are mentoring a new project manager on a hybrid wind-farm programme. She runs an immaculate operation: the schedule is re-levelled weekly, the RAID log is current, status reports are flawless. Her team delivers exactly to plan and no more, raises few ideas, and a recent engagement pulse scored low on "I understand why our work matters" and "I have room to grow here". She is puzzled, telling you she has "every process under control" and asks you what she is missing. What is the most useful thing to help her see?

## Options

- **A**: That the same disciplined attention she gives the plan now needs to go into the people — their sense of purpose, their development, and their input into how the work is done — because that is what the pulse is telling her is absent.
- **B**: That a team scoring low on purpose and growth usually signals individuals who feel stuck, so she should start by having career conversations with each person and finding them stretch opportunities.
- **C**: That tightly specified plans can leave a capable team little discretion, so she should deliberately under-specify some work and let the team shape the approach, which will lift both engagement and ideas.
- **D**: That flawless reporting and re-levelling consume hours the team would value spending differently, so streamlining the administrative cadence will free her to be more present with them.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Both pulse signals — purpose and growth — point the same way: she is controlling the mechanics expertly while the human side of leading (meaning, development, voice) is going unattended, and the most useful thing is to help her see that whole gap, not just one slice of it. (A) names the shift in where her energy must go and covers all three levers the data implicates, which is why it is the most useful single insight. (B) is a genuine and correct action, but it responds to only one of the two signals (growth) and skips purpose and team voice, so it is a part of the answer presented as the whole. (C) is also valid and addresses engagement through autonomy, yet on its own it reframes the problem as purely a planning-discretion issue and still leaves purpose and development untouched. (D) identifies a real efficiency point, but freeing time is only an enabler; time alone changes nothing unless she also changes what she does with the team, so it mistakes a precondition for the insight.
