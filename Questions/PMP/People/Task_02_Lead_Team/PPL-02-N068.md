# Question

You lead three sub-teams on a utility billing transformation. Each has delivered competently in the past, so none is a true beginner — the difference between them is subtler. For each situation, match the leadership response that fits it best. Each response is used once.

## Items

- **1**: A capable sub-team that has met its commitments for a year suddenly starts asking you to approve routine decisions it used to make alone, after a release it built was rolled back in front of the client.
- **2**: A capable sub-team that is moving into a part of the platform it has never touched; the people are strong and willing, but neither they nor you have done this specific integration before, and they keep asking how to begin.
- **3**: A capable sub-team that has quietly absorbed a new toolchain on its own over the last two iterations, is meeting its goals, and has stopped raising questions in your check-ins.

## Matches

- **A**: Step back from approvals, confirm the outcomes you need, and stay reachable for blockers — let them run it.
- **B**: Stay close and make the "how" explicit for this work — agree the approach step by step until they find their feet in the new area.
- **C**: Re-involve yourself supportively — talk through their decisions with them and point to what they still clearly do well, to rebuild the confidence the setback knocked out.

## Answer

1-C, 2-B, 3-A

## Explanation

All three sub-teams are competent, so the surface cue "they're asking for help" does not by itself fix the style — the cause behind it does. (1) is a confidence dip, not a skill gap: the rollback knocked their belief and they have regressed to seeking approval, so the fit is supportive re-involvement (C) that rebuilds confidence rather than re-teaching skills they still have. (2) is the genuine competence gap — strong, willing people in unfamiliar territory where even you lack the specific experience — so they need close, explicit direction on the "how" for this work (B), despite their general seniority. (3) looks like disengagement but is actually self-sufficient mastery — they have taught themselves the new toolchain and gone quiet because they no longer need you — so the fit is to delegate and stay available (A); imposing direction here would signal mistrust of a team that has earned autonomy. The trap is matching on the loudest surface signal (asking for help, or going silent) instead of the underlying competence-versus-confidence diagnosis.
