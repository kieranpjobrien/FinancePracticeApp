# Question

A PM leads a mixed team delivering a hospital records migration on a firm cutover date. A new graduate analyst joined two weeks ago and owns reconciling patient identifiers across two legacy databases — a task she has never done. She is enthusiastic but has twice produced reconciliation files with errors caught in review, and the review queue is filling up. A separate senior analyst handles a comparable reconciliation with no oversight but is already fully loaded. How should the PM lead the graduate analyst on this task?

## Options

- **A**: Coach her closely on the reconciliation method now — walk through it together and review her output frequently — then taper the oversight as she demonstrates she has it.
- **B**: Have the senior analyst spend a focused session teaching her the method and reviewing her first few files, then have her continue on her own once she is up to speed.
- **C**: Give her a written checklist and worked examples of the reconciliation steps so she can self-check against the standard before her files reach review.
- **D**: Keep her on the task but split off the trickiest identifier edge cases to the senior analyst for now, leaving her the routine reconciliations to build a track record on.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Effective leadership is situational: the style is matched to the individual's competence and commitment on the specific task. The graduate is high-commitment but low-competence here, which calls for a directing-and-coaching style that tapers as she grows — and the question is which way of delivering that coaching the PM should own. Each option is a plausible development tactic, so the margin is fine. (A) puts the PM's own hands-on coaching and review against her current level and deliberately reduces it as she improves, building her independence while protecting quality — the response situational leadership prescribes. (B) is genuinely tempting and peer coaching is effective, but the senior analyst is already fully loaded, and handing the PM's leadership and development responsibility to a stretched colleague risks both delivery and a shallow handover. (C) a checklist is a useful aid but not leadership; tools alone do not coach judgement, and a novice who has already missed errors may not catch what the checklist assumes she understands. (D) protecting the cutover by carving off the hard cases is a reasonable delivery hedge, but it narrows her learning to the routine and quietly signals limited trust, developing her more slowly than direct coaching would. The fine margin is the PM coaching her directly (A) versus delegating the coaching (B), substituting a tool for it (C), or trimming her scope instead of building her capability (D).
