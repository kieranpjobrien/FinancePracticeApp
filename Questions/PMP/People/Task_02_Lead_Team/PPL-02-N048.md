# Question

A data science team is split on which of two modelling approaches to use for a fraud-detection product. The team's most senior data scientist favours approach A on the strength of past experience; the rest of the team, after analysis, favour approach B and have data supporting it. The two camps have already exchanged their reasoning in stand-ups and neither has shifted; the disagreement is now stalling the work. The team turns to the PM to move it forward, and the deadline allows a few more days. What is the best leadership action?

## Options

- **A**: Convene the two camps to agree the evaluation criteria that should decide the choice, then have them score both approaches against those criteria together and proceed on the result.
- **B**: Ask the team to run a quick time-boxed spike on each approach and let the comparative results, rather than further debate, settle which one they take forward.
- **C**: Weigh the evidence both sides have already presented and make the call yourself, so the team can stop debating and start building without losing the remaining days.
- **D**: Have the senior data scientist and the lead proponent of approach B reconcile their positions between them and bring back a single agreed recommendation to the team.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Both sides have already argued their case and not moved, so simply repeating the debate (or asking the same two people to keep debating offline) will not converge it; the team needs an agreed basis for deciding, not more discussion. (A) shifts the disagreement from "whose judgement wins" to "what criteria should decide," which the whole team can own and which turns the existing evidence into a comparable result — converging the split while keeping the decision the team's. (B) is a strong move and a spike can generate decisive evidence, but it spends some of the limited buffer building throwaway prototypes when the deciding factors may be evaluable from the analysis already done; it is the right step only if criteria-based comparison cannot separate them. (C) restores momentum but removes a decision a competent team is well placed to make, trading their ownership for speed the schedule does not actually require. (D) hands the deadlock back to the two most entrenched individuals with no new basis for agreement, so it is likely to return either the same stalemate or a private compromise the rest of the team did not shape.
