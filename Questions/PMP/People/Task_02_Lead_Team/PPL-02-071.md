# Question

Two senior engineers on your bridge-refurbishment project disagree sharply over the structural design approach, and the disagreement has spilled into design reviews where each now openly dismisses the other's input. The rest of the team has started taking sides, and review meetings are becoming tense and unproductive. Both engineers are technically excellent and respected. What should you do first?

## Options

- **A**: Bring both engineers together privately to surface the substance of the disagreement and agree how design decisions between them will be resolved going forward.
- **B**: Ask an independent senior structural engineer to adjudicate the technical dispute and have the team adopt that ruling so the project can move on.
- **C**: Reaffirm the team's design-review ground rules so that dismissive behaviour stops and reviews regain their professional tone.
- **D**: Make the design decision yourself based on the project constraints, since the deadlock between two experts is now harming the team.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The presenting problem is a relationship conflict between two capable experts that is now degrading the team, and the leader's first move should be to confront it collaboratively at its source rather than treating the symptoms or seizing the decision. (A) brings the parties together to address the actual disagreement and establish how they will resolve such clashes, which targets the root cause. (C) tackles only the visible incivility; even if reviews become polite, the unresolved conflict and the deadlock remain. (B) may eventually be needed if the technical question genuinely cannot be settled internally, but outsourcing the decision before the two have been brought together skips the leadership step and can deepen the rift. (D) imposes a solution and undermines two respected experts, likely worsening the relationship and the team split. Confronting and resolving the conflict directly with those involved comes before escalation or imposition.
