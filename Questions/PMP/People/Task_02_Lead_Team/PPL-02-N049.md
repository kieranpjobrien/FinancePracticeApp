# Question

Three months ago a PM's team agreed two ground rules: working sessions start on time, and laptops stay closed unless a session needs them. The rules worked well at first. Over the last few weeks attendance has drifted later and several members now keep a laptop open during planning, occasionally answering email. In the same period the PM, under pressure from a parallel project, has started arriving a few minutes late to stand-ups and clearing messages during sessions. A retrospective is due tomorrow. What is the most effective way for the PM to lead the team back to the agreed standards?

## Options

- **A**: Name the slippage at the retrospective, acknowledge that the PM's own lateness and device use have contributed, recommit alongside the team, and from that point hold to the rules consistently.
- **B**: Put the erosion of the ground rules to the team at the retrospective and let them decide together whether to recommit to the current rules or agree more realistic ones.
- **C**: Raise the drift at the retrospective and ask the team to recommit to the rules they agreed, keeping the focus on the team's behaviour rather than singling out individuals.
- **D**: Quietly correct the PM's own conduct first — arriving on time and keeping the laptop closed — and let the improved example pull the team back without making it a discussion topic.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A team's adherence to its norms tracks the leader's own conduct, so the most effective correction both repairs the example and re-engages the team's shared ownership of the rules. (A) does both: the PM owns their part openly, which gives the recommitment credibility, and brings the team into renewing the standard rather than imposing it. (C) is reasonable but asks the team to recommit while staying silent on the PM's own slips, so the call to recommit lands less credibly when the leader's behaviour has visibly drifted too. (D) fixes the example but relies on modelling alone to reverse a norm that has already eroded across the team, and forgoing any conversation is slower and leaves the shared standard unspoken. (B) reopens whether the rules should hold at all, which legitimises the drift instead of leading the team back to a standard they found valuable when they kept it.
