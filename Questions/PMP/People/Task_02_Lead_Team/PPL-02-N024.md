# Question

Midway through a critical sprint for an e-commerce replatforming, the team is blocked because a shared staging environment owned by another department keeps being wiped by that department's nightly jobs. The developers are frustrated and have started building elaborate workarounds that are eating into sprint capacity. Two engineers ask you, the project manager, what they should do. What is the best leadership action?

## Options

- **A**: Help the team document the recurring conflict precisely and coach them to take it to the owning department themselves, so they build the muscle to clear their own cross-team blockers.
- **B**: Escalate to your sponsor immediately and ask them to direct the other department to stop wiping the environment.
- **C**: Engage the owning department's manager directly to secure the team a protected environment or change the job schedule, removing the impediment so the team can get back to the work.
- **D**: Add the staging instability to the risk register and bring mitigation options to the next sprint review for stakeholders to weigh.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

A servant leader clears the path so the team can focus on delivering value, and this is a live, capacity-draining blocker on a critical sprint that sits with another department the team has no leverage over. (C) goes straight to the person who can fix the root cause and removes it now, which is exactly what the moment demands. (A) is a legitimate long-run move to grow the team's self-sufficiency, but coaching junior engineers to negotiate access with another department mid-sprint is too slow for an active blocker and leaves them spending energy on the wrong thing today; clearing it yourself first, then building that capability later, serves the team better here. (B) reaches for executive escalation before a normal peer-level conversation with the owning manager has even been tried, which is heavy-handed and can sour the inter-team relationship. (D) reclassifies a present, certain impediment as a future risk and defers it to a ceremony, leaving the team stuck for the rest of the sprint. All four are things a real PM might do; the fine margin is matching the response to a here-and-now external blocker.
