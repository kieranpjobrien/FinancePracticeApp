# Question

A PM newly assigned to lead an established team needs the team to adopt a different testing approach that the PM is convinced will reduce escaped defects. The team is competent and has used its current approach for years; a few members are openly sceptical that the change is worth the disruption. The PM has formal authority over the project and was, in a previous role, recognised across the company as a strong test engineer, though this team does not know that history yet and has no prior relationship with the PM. To lead the team to genuinely adopt the new approach, which is the most effective basis for the PM to influence them?

## Options

- **A**: Direct the team to switch to the new approach on the authority of the PM's formal role, then monitor compliance through the test metrics.
- **B**: Walk the sceptics through the defect data and the reasoning behind the new approach, demonstrating the depth of testing knowledge behind the recommendation.
- **C**: Invest first in building personal rapport and trust with the team so that, over the coming weeks, members adopt the change out of respect for the PM.
- **D**: Offer to recognise and reward the team members who adopt the new approach earliest, creating an incentive for the others to follow.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The PM has several power bases available and must choose the one that produces genuine adoption from competent sceptics, not mere compliance. The scepticism is substantive — "is this worth the disruption?" — so it is best met with expert power: the evidence and reasoning that demonstrate the change is sound. (B) engages the doubt on its merits and earns durable buy-in. (A) leans on positional power, which can compel surface compliance but breeds resentment in an experienced team and will not change their genuine conviction. (C) draws on referent power, which is powerful but the PM has no relationship yet, so it cannot be the basis for influence now and the change is time-sensitive. (D) is reward power, which incentivises the appearance of adoption rather than persuading the team the approach is actually better. Expert and referent power build the most durable influence, but only expert power is both available and matched to the nature of this resistance.
