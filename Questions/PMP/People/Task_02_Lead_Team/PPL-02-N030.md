# Question

Your agile team's working agreement says everyone joins the daily standup on time and prepared. Over the last fortnight you, the scrum master, have arrived late to three standups because a recurring stakeholder steering meeting overruns into the slot, and team punctuality is now slipping too. You have already privately apologised to the team for being late. You cannot move the steering meeting, which is fixed by senior leadership, and the standup time was chosen by the team to suit their own working day. What should you do next?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the conflict to the team and let them decide whether to move the standup, keep the slot and accept you will sometimes join a few minutes late, or adjust the agreement, then hold everyone including yourself to whatever they choose.
- **B**: Reschedule the standup yourself to a time that no longer clashes with the steering meeting and tell the team the new time, since the agreement is unworkable as it stands.
- **C**: Ask a team member to facilitate the standup on the days the steering meeting runs long, so it starts on time and you join when you can, keeping the rest of the agreement intact.
- **D**: Recommit publicly to the existing agreement and reorganise your schedule to leave the steering meeting early enough to reach standup on time.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The standup is the team's agreement on the team's chosen slot, so the durable fix is to return the genuinely conflicting constraint to the team and let them re-decide ownership of their own ritual, then apply the result to everyone equally. Each option is a defensible move, and the choice turns on which actually resolves the clash rather than working around it. (A) preserves the team's ownership and settles the structural conflict openly, which is the leadership move once a private apology has already been given. (B) imposes a unilateral change to an agreement the team owns, undermining the very ownership you want them to feel — even if the team might well choose this, deciding it for them is the error. (C) rotating facilitation is a sound agile practice and keeps the event running, but quietly engineering around your own lateness leaves the underlying conflict and the "prepared and on time for everyone" norm unaddressed, and your continued absence still models the slippage. (D) sounds principled, but walking out of a leadership-fixed steering meeting early to honour a self-imposed slot subordinates a real business obligation to a schedule the team can simply choose to change, and recommitting without resolving the clash invites the same breach next week. The fine margin is resolving the conflict with the team (A) versus deciding for them (B), patching around it (C), or sacrificing a business obligation to it (D).
