# Question

On a hybrid software programme a developer who has shipped solid work for two years is given a security-hardening task using a threat-modelling method she has used twice before, alongside a tester she has never worked with. In your first one-to-one she is upbeat, says she is "pretty sure" she has the method covered, and asks only that you stay out of her way. Her first deliverable on the task misses two findings that the method should have caught, and she does not flag any difficulty. Schedule is tight. How should you lead her on this task?

## Options

- **A**: Increase coaching on the threat-modelling method specifically — review her approach together and work through the gaps — while leaving her free to run the parts of the task she has clearly mastered.
- **B**: Delegate the task to her fully as she requested, trusting that a developer with two strong years will self-correct once she notices the misses.
- **C**: Move to a directing style for the whole task, giving her step-by-step instructions and checking each step, to make sure nothing else is missed under schedule pressure.
- **D**: Take the threat-modelling portion off her and assign it to the tester, letting her keep the implementation work she is proven on.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Her general track record is high, but on this specific method she has shallow experience and an inflated read of her own competence, shown by missed findings she did not flag; situational leadership matches style to competence and commitment on the actual task, not on her overall reputation. (A) targets coaching at the genuine gap (the method) while respecting her mastery elsewhere, which both fixes the quality risk and develops her. (B) accepts her self-assessment at face value when the deliverable has already contradicted it, leaving security gaps to chance. (C) over-corrects into full directing across work she demonstrably owns, which would feel like distrust and waste her capability. (D) removes her development opportunity and burdens an unfamiliar tester, avoiding rather than leading the situation.
