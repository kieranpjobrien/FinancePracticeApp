# Question

Three weeks into a six-month agile delivery, a new senior developer joins your established, high-performing team. She is technically excellent but has begun pushing to replace several of the team's working practices with ones she prefers, and a couple of long-standing members have grown noticeably quiet and resistant in ceremonies. Delivery has not yet suffered, but the atmosphere has cooled. What should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Talk with the new developer about integrating into the team's established ways first and channelling improvement ideas through the team's own retrospective.
- **B**: Welcome the fresh perspective and ask the team to trial her suggested practices, since challenging the status quo can prevent complacency.
- **C**: Reassure the long-standing members privately that the team's established practices are not under threat.
- **D**: Facilitate a session where the team and the new member jointly review which practices to keep and which to change.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A newcomer overriding a high-performing team's norms before earning trust is straining cohesion, and the leader's first task is to guide her integration while preserving the legitimate route for improvement. (A) does both — it asks her to integrate before reforming and points her ideas into the retrospective, the team's own change mechanism, which respects the existing team while not dismissing her input. (D) a joint review of practices is attractive and may follow, but convening it now, before she has integrated and while the established members are already defensive, can entrench the very stand-off you want to defuse and reward the premature push. (B) endorsing wholesale trialling of her practices three weeks in undercuts a working team and is likely to deepen the resistance. (C) privately reassuring the old guard addresses their feelings but leaves the new developer's behaviour unaddressed and may quietly position you against her. Guiding her integration and routing change through the retrospective best balances cohesion with openness to improvement.
