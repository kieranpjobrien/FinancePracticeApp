# Question

During the go-live cutover of a national payments switch, a critical data migration fails at 02:00 and the rollback window closes in ninety minutes. Two of your six engineers each propose a credible recovery path and are defending them firmly; the other four are split between the two. Your incident commander is on a flight and unreachable, and you, the project manager on the bridge call, understand the system well enough to follow both arguments. What leadership approach is most appropriate right now?

## Options

- **A**: Hear the two proposals once each, decide between them yourself, and direct the team to execute your choice within the window.
- **B**: Have the two proposers each take a small parallel slice of the window to prove their path, then commit fully to whichever shows results first.
- **C**: Put the choice to a quick team vote, since most of the engineers have a view and a backed majority decision will execute faster than an imposed one.
- **D**: Ask the two proposers to spend ten minutes converging on a single combined approach they both trust before the team commits to anything.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Leaders flex their style to context, and a time-boxed crisis with two viable but competing paths and no commander present calls for the PM to take the decision after the briefest possible input. The hard part is that every option here is a recognised, defensible technique — the choice turns on what the ninety-minute window can actually afford. (A) spends the least time and converts understanding into a single directed action, which is what a closing rollback window demands. (B) parallel-proving is a sound de-risking method with time and spare hands, but splitting six tired engineers across two live recovery attempts inside ninety minutes risks doing neither well and muddying which change caused what. (C) a backed majority can execute fast, but voting surrenders an accountable technical judgement to a count and can entrench the very split you need to break. (D) forcing two firm proposers to merge paths under extreme time pressure tends to produce a slow, lowest-common-denominator compromise, and there may be no coherent hybrid. The fine margin is decisiveness now (A) versus three techniques that are each valid in calmer conditions but too slow or too risky for this window.
