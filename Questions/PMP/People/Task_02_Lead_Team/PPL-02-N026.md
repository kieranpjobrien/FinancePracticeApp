# Question

A capable agile team in a telecom company is four months old and delivering well. As the scrum master, you have noticed that you assign each task in standup, decide who pairs with whom, and adjudicate every technical disagreement. The team never proposes solutions; they bring problems to you and wait. You want to build their capacity to self-organise without the quality or pace slipping while they find their feet. Which approach is most likely to grow durable self-organisation?

## Options

- **A**: In standup, when work needs picking up or a disagreement arises, ask the team how they want to handle it and have them decide, stepping in only to coach or unblock when they are genuinely stuck.
- **B**: Make the team's priorities, constraints, and definition of done fully transparent and agree with them which classes of decision are now theirs to make without you, widening that scope as their judgement proves out.
- **C**: Continue making the calls yourself but explain your reasoning aloud each time, so the team absorbs how you weigh decisions before you ask them to make their own.
- **D**: Rotate a different team member each sprint into a lead role who owns task allocation and arbitrates disagreements, so ownership passes around the team rather than resting on you.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Self-organisation is durable only when the team has both the authority to decide and the shared context to decide well; the deepest leadership move is to install that context and explicit, expanding decision rights rather than to manage the symptoms of dependency. (B) does exactly this — transparency plus agreed, widening decision boundaries removes the structural reason the team keeps coming to you, and scales beyond any single ceremony. (A) is a sound coaching habit and genuinely helpful, but it changes behaviour only inside standup and still leaves you as the implicit owner of decisions outside it; it is a tactic within (B), not as complete. (C) keeps you as the decision-maker — better-informed dependence is still dependence — so it builds understanding without transferring ownership. (D) distributes the bottleneck and develops individuals, but routing all allocation and arbitration through a rotating single leader recreates a mini command structure rather than growing the whole team's capacity to self-organise.
