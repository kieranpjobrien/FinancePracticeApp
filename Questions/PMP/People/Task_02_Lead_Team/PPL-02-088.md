# Question

You lead four workstream leads on a rail-signalling upgrade. Each is at a different point in their development, and the right leadership response differs for each. For each lead, match the most fitting response. Each response is used once.

## Items

- **1**: A lead who is new to leading a workstream, enthusiastic and eager, but unsure how to plan or sequence the work and asking for a lot of guidance.
- **2**: A lead who has the skills to run the workstream but has lost confidence after a recent setback and is now hesitant to commit to decisions.
- **3**: A lead who is fully capable, consistently delivering, and visibly ready for more autonomy and responsibility.

## Matches

- **A**: Delegate ownership and the authority that goes with it; set the outcomes and step back, staying available if needed.
- **B**: Direct closely — provide clear instruction and structure on how to plan and sequence, and check in frequently while skills build.
- **C**: Coach and support — work alongside them on decisions and rebuild belief by reinforcing what they already do well.

## Answer

1-B, 2-C, 3-A

## Explanation

Effective leaders adapt their style to each person's development level rather than applying one approach to all. (1) is high willingness but low competence — an eager novice who needs clear direction and structure (B) until the skills are in place. (2) is competent but low in confidence after a setback, so the fit is a supportive, coaching response (C) that rebuilds belief rather than re-teaching skills already held. (3) is high competence and high confidence, ready for autonomy, so the leader should delegate genuine ownership and authority (A) and get out of the way. The common error is to misread the cues — directing the capable-but-shaken lead (which insults their competence) or delegating to the eager novice (which sets them up to flounder). Matching the response to the underlying competence-and-confidence diagnosis is what distinguishes the fits.
