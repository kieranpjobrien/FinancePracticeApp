# Question

Your agile team has consistently met its sprint goals for six months and is proud of it. In the last two sprints, however, you have noticed people working late, skipping the retrospective to "save time," and quietly carrying over polished-but-incomplete stories so the burndown still looks clean. Velocity is steady and the product owner is delighted. What is the best leadership action?

## Options

- **A**: Reinstate the retrospective as non-negotiable, since dropping it is removing the team's main mechanism for self-correction.
- **B**: Reduce the scope the product owner can pull into the next sprint to bring the team's load back to a sustainable level.
- **C**: Raise what you have observed with the team and ask them to examine, in their retrospective, whether their commitment and pace are sustainable.
- **D**: Speak privately with the individuals you have seen working late to check on them and understand what is driving the long hours.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The team is self-organising and successful, but it is masking strain in ways that threaten sustainability, and the leader's role is to make the issue visible and let the team own the correction rather than impose one or handle it person by person. (C) surfaces the observation and routes it into the team's own improvement loop, respecting self-organisation while addressing sustainable pace. (A) is right that skipping retrospectives is dangerous and is part of the cure, but mandating the ceremony alone treats one symptom and does not put the underlying overcommitment in front of the team to resolve. (B) reducing intake may well become appropriate, but the leader unilaterally capping scope pre-empts the team's agency and is premature before the team has even examined the problem. (D) checking on individuals is humane and may be warranted, but the carried-over stories, dropped retrospective, and overtime are a team-level pattern of overcommitment, and addressing it one-to-one misses the shared dynamic and the team's own ability to adjust it. Naming the pattern and engaging the whole team to address it is both more respectful of self-organisation and more durable.
