# Question

On a co-located game studio's agile team, two senior designers dominate every planning and refinement session. Three newer engineers who joined last quarter rarely speak and have privately told the scrum master they feel their ideas "won't land". Estimates end up reflecting the two seniors' view because they speak first and most forcefully, and the quieter members then simply agree. You decide a concrete facilitation change is needed rather than a quiet word. Which change is most likely to surface the missing input?

## Options

- **A**: Switch estimation to planning poker, where everyone commits a private estimate and reveals simultaneously, and have the outliers — not the seniors — speak first to explain their thinking.
- **B**: Start each refinement with a few minutes of silent individual note-taking, then go round-robin so every person voices a point before open debate begins.
- **C**: Appoint the newer engineers, in rotation, as the session's facilitator so they run the discussion and naturally hold the floor.
- **D**: Ask the two senior designers to deliberately speak last in sessions, giving the quieter members the first opportunity to set the direction.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Every option is a real facilitation technique aimed at the same goal, so the question turns on which most directly breaks the specific mechanism described: the seniors anchor the estimate by speaking first and forcefully, and the others then converge on it. (A) attacks that mechanism head-on — simultaneous private reveal removes the anchor entirely, and having outliers explain first puts the quieter, differing views into the room with status before the seniors can frame the answer. (B) is genuinely strong and close to correct; silent writing plus round-robin does surface every voice, but a verbal round-robin can still let the seniors' turn dominate the subsequent debate and does not specifically neutralise the first-mover anchoring on the *estimates*, which is the stated problem. (C) develops the newer members and gives them the floor, but facilitating a session is a heavy, exposing demand on people who already fear their ideas "won't land", and a facilitator does not necessarily get their own ideas weighted. (D) helps by reordering who speaks, yet it still relies on the seniors' compliance and on the quieter members being willing to go first into a vacuum — weaker than a structural change that makes independent input the default. The fine margin is between A and B; A wins because it targets the anchoring on estimates precisely.
