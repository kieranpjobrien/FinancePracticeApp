# Question

You took over a stalled two-year defence systems integration programme three weeks ago. There is a clear, board-endorsed vision statement for the integrated system, and you have already run a well-received programme-wide session walking every sub-team through it. Yet the sub-teams keep optimising their own components and clash at the integration points: when you probe, each lead says they "agree with the vision" but cannot say how their current technical trade-offs serve it, and each is being measured by their own functional manager purely on component delivery. What should you do next to lead the programme effectively?

## Options

- **A**: Re-run the vision session with more compelling framing and richer examples, since the message clearly has not yet landed deeply enough for the teams to act on it.
- **B**: Work with the functional managers to change how the sub-teams are measured so their goals reward integrated outcomes, not just component delivery.
- **C**: Translate the vision into shared, programme-level success measures and integration acceptance criteria that each sub-team's day-to-day trade-offs are explicitly weighed against.
- **D**: Establish a cross-team integration forum where leads must justify significant design decisions against the programme outcome before committing to them.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The vision exists and has been communicated and accepted, so the gap is not awareness — it is that nothing translates the vision into concrete criteria people can steer everyday decisions by, which is why "I agree with the vision" coexists with component-first choices. (C) bridges abstract vision and daily action by giving every sub-team shared measures and acceptance criteria to test their trade-offs against, which is the leadership step that actually changes behaviour. (B) addresses a real misaligned-incentive driver and may be necessary, but reshaping functional managers' measurement systems is slower and outside your direct control, and it still leaves teams without an operational definition of "good for the whole"; it supports the fix rather than being the most effective next step. (D) creates useful accountability, but a forum that judges decisions has nothing objective to judge them against until the shared criteria in (C) exist, so it presumes the very thing that is missing. (A) repeats an intervention that has already worked at the awareness level; saying the vision again louder does not convert agreement into aligned trade-offs.
