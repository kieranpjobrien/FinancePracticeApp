# Question

Your agile team spans Manila, Berlin, and São Paulo. The team debates in a fast, interruption-heavy style that the Berlin members enjoy. The Manila members rarely challenge ideas in the live sessions, and you learn in a one-to-one that openly contradicting a colleague in front of the group feels disrespectful to them, so they stay silent even when they disagree. Decisions are losing valuable dissent, and you want a change that keeps the energy of live debate while making sure the quieter input actually reaches the decision. What should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Add an asynchronous written channel where concerns and dissent can be raised before and after live debate, and fold what surfaces there into the decision.
- **B**: Privately brief the Manila members before each session on the points up for debate and offer to voice their concerns on their behalf when they signal them to you.
- **C**: Run the live debate as now, but appoint a rotating facilitator who explicitly goes round the table and invites each person to speak before any decision is taken.
- **D**: Agree a team norm that ideas are critiqued in writing first, so the most contentious decisions are debated on the document rather than live in the room.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Inclusive leadership adapts the team's process so people with different communication norms can all contribute, without forcing anyone to abandon their cultural comfort or stripping the team of what works. Every option here is a real inclusion technique, so the choice turns on which best preserves the live energy while reliably capturing the missing dissent. (A) adds a parallel channel that suits the quieter members and feeds their input into the decision, while leaving the live debate the Berlin members value intact — the broadest, least disruptive fix. (B) surfaces some input but routes it through you, which keeps the Manila members dependent on a proxy, can distort their meaning, and does not build their own standing on the team. (C) round-robin facilitation is genuinely useful, but being put on the spot to critique a colleague aloud in the moment is exactly the public confrontation they find disrespectful, so it may still produce silence or bland agreement. (D) debating in writing first would include everyone, but making it the default for contentious decisions sacrifices the fast live debate the team is built around, over-correcting towards one group's comfort. The fine margin is adding an inclusive channel alongside the live forum (A) versus options that either use a proxy, still force live confrontation, or suppress the live debate altogether.
