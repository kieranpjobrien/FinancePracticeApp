# Question

You led a now high-performing team closely and directively through a turbulent predictive delivery, which succeeded. The organisation has since moved the team to a hybrid model with substantial iterative work, and you have carried your hands-on directive style over out of habit. The team is experienced and capable but seems stifled, and two of your strongest people have hinted at leaving. You have decided you need to move towards a more empowering style. What is the best way to make that shift?

## Options

- **A**: Announce that you are moving to a hands-off approach and, from the next sprint, stop directing the team's day-to-day work so they have room to take ownership.
- **B**: Agree explicit decision rights with the team and your management — what they now own outright, what they decide and inform you of — and visibly hold to those boundaries as they take them up.
- **C**: Begin delegating the most significant, high-visibility decisions to the team first, so they immediately feel the trust you are extending and the change is unmistakable.
- **D**: Keep your current involvement but ask the team for regular feedback on where they would like more autonomy, and loosen your grip in the areas they raise.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A real shift from directive to empowering leadership is delivered through clear, jointly-understood decision rights, not a mood or an announcement; the team needs to know precisely what is now theirs and to see you honour it. (B) makes the new operating model explicit and credible — it gives experienced people genuine, bounded authority and removes the ambiguity that an inherited directive habit creates, which is what re-engages them. (A) swings to abrupt abdication; a sudden "hands-off" announcement on an iterative programme can read as withdrawal and leaves the team guessing where authority now sits. (C) is well-intentioned but front-loading the highest-stakes decisions before boundaries are agreed risks a costly early stumble and a retreat to old habits; autonomy is better widened from a clear baseline. (D) keeps you as the gatekeeper deciding when to loosen, so it concedes autonomy reactively and slowly while the very people you are trying to retain are already signalling they may leave.
