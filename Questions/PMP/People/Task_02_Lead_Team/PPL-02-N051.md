# Question

A newly formed agile team of eight, drawn from three previously separate departments, is in its second sprint. Stand-ups have become tense: members challenge each other's estimates, argue over what "done" means, and group along old departmental lines. Velocity is erratic, and a couple of members have asked the PM privately whether the team mix is right. The PM, acting as team lead, sees competent people who have not yet found a common way of working. Which leadership action will most help the team settle into productive collaboration?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate the team to agree its own working norms and a shared definition of done now, then coach members to hold each other to them as friction arises.
- **B**: Run a facilitated session that surfaces the estimate and definition-of-done disagreements directly, work the team to consensus on each, and record the outcomes as the team's standard.
- **C**: Run a team-building offsite focused on relationships and trust across the three former departments, so the personal tension behind the grouping eases before tackling the process disputes.
- **D**: Pair members from different former departments on shared stories for the next sprint, so collaboration is built through the work itself rather than through a separate norming exercise.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The friction is what a new team predictably goes through before it gels, and the fastest route through it is to build the shared agreements that resolve the very things being clashed over — while growing the team's own capacity to enforce them. (A) does both: it establishes norms and a definition of done as a lightweight, team-owned baseline and then coaches the team to self-police, so the team keeps maturing without the PM as referee. (B) tackles the same substance and is close, but driving every disagreement to full consensus in one facilitated push is heavier and slower than agreeing a workable baseline and letting the team refine it through coaching as real cases arise — consensus on everything up front is not required to move forward. (C) can ease relationships but treats the symptom (interpersonal tension) ahead of the cause (no shared way of working), and rapport built offsite often dissolves back at the stand-up if the working agreements still do not exist. (D) builds cross-department ties through real work, a sound complementary tactic, but on its own it leaves the explicit estimate and definition-of-done disputes unresolved, so the same arguments recur inside the pairs.
