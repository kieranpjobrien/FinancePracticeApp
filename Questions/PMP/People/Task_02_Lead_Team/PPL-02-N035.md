# Question

You are leading a predictive construction-design project and want to embody servant leadership with your engineering team. Each option below is a genuine, well-meant action a caring project manager might take. Which three best reflect a true servant-leadership approach to leading the team rather than a benevolent but controlling one? (Choose three.)

## Options

- **A**: Spend your time clearing the permitting and resourcing obstacles that are slowing the team, then report back what you have unblocked.
- **B**: Learn each engineer's development goals and steer suitable stretch assignments and exposure their way so they grow.
- **C**: Take on the difficult client conversations yourself and feed the team a clean, simplified version so they are not unsettled by the politics.
- **D**: Build a climate where engineers raise problems and admit mistakes early, and respond to bad news with support rather than blame.
- **E**: Personally review and sign off every drawing before it leaves the team, because you care deeply about getting the quality right for them.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

Servant leadership serves the team's growth and capacity to do its best work, which means clearing obstacles, developing people, and building psychological safety. (A), (B), and (D) do exactly that. (C) is protective in intent but filtering the client reality denies the team the context and stakeholder exposure it needs to mature, so it serves the leader's comfort with control more than the team. (E) springs from genuine care for quality, yet making yourself the mandatory sign-off on everything keeps the team dependent and signals you do not trust their work; a servant leader builds the team's own quality ownership instead. The trap in (C) and (E) is that good intentions can still produce controlling behaviour.
