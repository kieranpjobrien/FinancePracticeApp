# Question

A test analyst who has delivered strong, independent work on your predictive aerospace programme for a year has recently gone quiet and her output has slipped. In a one-to-one she is subdued and tells you that a defect which escaped to production was pinned on her in front of her peers, even though the root cause was not hers, and she now second-guesses work she used to sign off without a thought. She is clearly still technically sharp. With a tight schedule ahead, how should you lead her back to form?

## Options

- **A**: Give her clear assignments but stay close — frequent encouragement, visible backing in front of the team, and involving her in decisions — while leaving the technical execution, which she is fully capable of, in her hands.
- **B**: Tighten supervision of her work for a while: detailed instructions and step-by-step checkpoints, so there is no ambiguity and she cannot put another foot wrong under the schedule pressure.
- **C**: Demonstrate your trust by handing her a stretching, high-visibility work package and stepping fully back, so a clear win reminds her and everyone else what she is capable of.
- **D**: Address the cause head-on by correcting the record publicly about the defect, then let her carry on with her normal workload as before.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

She is technically capable but shaken in confidence, so the leadership need is morale and reassurance, not more control or more instruction. (A) supplies encouragement, visible backing, and involvement while still trusting her to do the work she plainly can — it meets the actual gap without implying she has lost her skills. (B) reads a confidence wound as a competence problem; close supervision and checkpoints would feel like distrust and is likely to deepen the very self-doubt you are trying to heal. (C) is genuinely tempting — a public vote of confidence and a win could help — but stepping fully back from someone currently fragile and loading on high-visibility pressure risks a stumble that confirms her fears; support, not abandonment-as-trust, is what she needs first. (D) is worth doing and addresses the injustice, but correcting the record alone does not rebuild day-to-day confidence, and "carry on as before" offers no active support through the recovery; clearing her name helps, yet on its own it is the weaker leadership response. The fine margin is between supporting her closely (A) and either over-controlling (B), over-trusting (C), or under-supporting (D).
