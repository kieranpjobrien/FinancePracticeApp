# Question

Three sprints into building a hospital scheduling platform, your Scrum team consistently meets its sprint goals. In today's daily scrum, two developers disagree about whether to refactor a shared module now; the disagreement turns on whether other teams' components depend on it, which neither developer is sure about. The exchange has run several minutes and the rest of the team has gone quiet. The sprint goal does not hinge on resolving this today. As the project manager acting as servant leader, what should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Note that the question needs the dependency facts the team doesn't yet have, and ask the two developers to take it offline, gather that information, and bring a recommendation back to the team.
- **B**: Ask the team to park the refactor as a backlog item for the product owner to order against other work, since changing a shared module is a scope decision rather than a daily-scrum topic.
- **C**: Let the two developers keep working it in the daily scrum until they agree, since the rest of the team can speak up and a self-organising team should resolve technical questions in the open.
- **D**: Suggest the team timebox five more minutes now to decide a direction, so momentum is not lost waiting for a separate session and the wider group can weigh in while everyone is present.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four are plausible facilitation moves, so the question turns on what the situation actually needs. The disagreement is blocked on missing information (the dependency facts), not on willpower, and it is consuming the whole team's timebox for a decision that does not affect today's goal — so (A) routes it to the two owners to resolve with the facts and report back, the servant-leader move that protects both the event and self-organisation. (D) keeps the whole team in a discussion that cannot conclude without information nobody in the room has, so the extra timebox likely ends unresolved. (C) lets an unbounded deep-dive consume the event, which is exactly what the "take it offline" norm prevents. (B) wrongly reframes a technical "how" decision the team owns as a product-owner scope call; the PO orders value, not internal refactoring approaches.
