# Question

A scrum team three sprints into a new product has fallen into a pattern where developers wait for the project manager to assign each backlog item at the start of every sprint. Velocity is stable but the team rarely raises ideas in retrospectives and defers most technical decisions upward. The product owner is satisfied with delivery but the PM is concerned the team is not maturing. What should the PM do to lead the team toward self-organisation?

## Options

- **A**: Stop assigning items and ask the team to pull their own work in sprint planning, then use each retrospective to coach them on owning the technical decisions they currently defer.
- **B**: Keep facilitating sprint planning as now, but bring a recurring technical-design discussion into the team's working week so the decisions they defer get debated and owned by the group.
- **C**: From the next sprint, hand the team full ownership of both pulling work and making technical calls, and step back from those decisions so the space to self-organise is unmistakable.
- **D**: Rotate a different developer each sprint to run planning and arbitrate the technical decisions, so ownership is distributed and someone other than the PM always holds the call.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Leading an agile team means growing its capacity to self-organise, and the PM's job is to create that space while staying present enough to coach the team into it. Every option here genuinely pushes toward self-organisation, so the choice turns on pace and on whether the PM keeps coaching or just withdraws. (A) moves ownership of both the pull and the technical decisions to the team while using the retrospective as the coaching vehicle, which grows maturity at a sustainable pace without abandoning them. (B) is sound and surfaces the deferred technical decisions, but if the PM keeps running planning the team still does not own how work is selected, so it fixes only half the dependency. (C) gets the destination right but hands over everything at once and then steps back; a team that has deferred upward for three sprints is likely to flounder or quietly stall without the coaching that makes the handover stick. (D) distributes the role mechanically, yet rotating a single arbiter each sprint recreates a mini-PM in turn rather than building shared ownership, and the deciding still sits with one person rather than the team. The fine margin is graduated handover with active coaching (A) versus a partial shift (B), an abrupt full handover (C), or rotating the dependency onto individuals (D).
