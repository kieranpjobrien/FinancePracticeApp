# Question

A normally enthusiastic member of your logistics-systems project has become withdrawn over the past fortnight: she contributes little in meetings, her output has slowed, and she leaves promptly each day. You do not yet know why. As her leader, which actions are appropriate early responses to re-engage her? (Choose three.)

## Options

- **A**: Speak with her privately and without judgement to understand what has changed and whether something is affecting her.
- **B**: Reassure her that her work is valued and ask what support, if any, would help her at the moment.
- **C**: Assign her a high-visibility task to reignite her motivation and signal your confidence in her.
- **D**: Quietly observe her interactions with the rest of the team for another fortnight before raising anything, to be sure the dip is real.
- **E**: Review whether her current work, workload, or working conditions may have contributed to the change.

## Answer

A, B, E

## Explanation

A sudden, unexplained behaviour change calls for a leader to find the cause and respond to the person, not to act on assumptions. (A) opens a supportive, non-judgemental conversation that is the only way to learn what is actually going on; (B) signals value and offers tailored support, which sustains engagement; (E) examines whether the work or conditions are a contributing factor, addressing causes within your control. (C) and (D) are the tempting distractors. (C) hands a disengaged person a high-pressure, visible task before you understand why she is struggling, which can overwhelm rather than motivate and may worsen things if the cause is, say, burnout or a personal issue. (D) deliberately waiting another two weeks while she clearly declines abdicates the leader's duty to engage early; observation is not a substitute for a caring conversation. A, B, and E address the person and the cause; C and D act prematurely or passively.
