# Question

A capable analyst on your infrastructure project has asked for more responsibility and is clearly ready for it. You decide to delegate ownership of the stakeholder-reporting workstream to her. A month later you notice the reports are accurate but formatted differently from how you would do them, and one stakeholder has mentioned the change in passing without complaint. What is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: Leave the workstream with her and let the new format stand, since the reports are accurate and no real problem has arisen.
- **B**: Ask her to revert to the previous format to keep reporting consistent with the rest of the programme.
- **C**: Check in with her on how she feels the workstream is going and whether she has what she needs, without dictating the format.
- **D**: Resume reviewing each report before it is issued until you are confident the new approach is landing well with stakeholders.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Genuine delegation means transferring ownership and accepting that a capable person may do the work differently, intervening only where there is a real problem rather than a difference of style. The reports are accurate and the only stakeholder to remark on the change did so without complaint, so there is no substantive issue to fix. (A) is reasonable on the facts, but doing literally nothing forgoes the light-touch support that keeps delegation healthy. (C) preserves her ownership while staying available and alert to any genuine difficulty, which is the strongest leadership posture for a newly delegated, capable owner. (B) overrides her on a matter of preference and signals that the delegation was not real, undermining the growth she asked for. (D) reinstates a control gate over accurate work, which is micromanagement and the clearest way to kill the initiative you were trying to develop. Supporting without seizing back control is the essence of effective delegation.
