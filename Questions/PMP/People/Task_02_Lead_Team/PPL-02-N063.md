# Question

Your cross-functional team has been pulled together to rebuild a retailer's loyalty programme using an iterative approach. The members come from three departments, have not worked together before, and each describes the project's purpose differently when you speak with them one-on-one. Morale is fine but you sense no shared sense of direction. As the leader, which actions most directly build a common purpose for this team? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate a chartering session where the team co-creates a concise vision statement and the working agreements that support it.
- **B**: Ask each department lead to brief their own members on how this programme serves that department's objectives, so each person hears the purpose from a familiar, trusted voice.
- **C**: Connect the upcoming iteration goals explicitly to the programme vision in your first few iteration planning sessions.
- **D**: Circulate the programme vision and business case the PMO has already documented, and ask each member to read it before the next session so everyone is working from the same written reference.
- **E**: Hold short one-on-ones to learn what motivates each member and where their strengths lie, then reflect that back into how you frame the shared goal.

## Answer

A, C, E

## Explanation

The deficit is a single shared purpose across people who currently hold three different ones, so the test is which actions converge the team into co-owning one direction rather than merely transmitting a "why" to them. (A) has the team itself articulate one vision and the norms around it, creating shared ownership. (C) keeps that vision live by tying each iteration to it, reinforcing direction continuously. (E) lets you connect each person's motivation to the collective aim so individuals buy in, not just comply. (B) is a reasonable communication step but routes purpose through three separate departmental lenses, reinforcing the very fragmentation causing the problem — each member still hears a different "why" framed around their own function. (D) distributes an authoritative, consistent statement, which beats three conflicting versions, but it is passive consumption of a purpose authored elsewhere; reading a document does not build the shared ownership a brand-new cross-functional team needs to internalise a common direction, and people who already describe the purpose differently will read the same words through their existing frames. The actions that build common purpose are the ones the team helps shape, not the ones handed to it.
