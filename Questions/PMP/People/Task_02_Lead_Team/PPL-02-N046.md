# Question

A small software team has just delivered a difficult release after several weeks of sustained effort that included a number of long days. The release went live cleanly and the customer is delighted. The next phase of work begins almost immediately. The PM wants the team to stay strong through it and has room to shape how the transition is handled. Which leadership action is most likely to sustain the team's performance into the next phase?

## Options

- **A**: Acknowledge the team's effort and the specific results they achieved, build a short period of lighter load into the start of the next phase, and bring them into planning it.
- **B**: Hold a retrospective on the release so the team converts the hard push into lessons that make the next phase smoother, then move into it.
- **C**: Recognise the team's achievement publicly to senior stakeholders so the wider organisation sees their contribution and the team feels valued going into the next phase.
- **D**: Bring the team straight into shaping the next phase while the success is fresh, using their enthusiasm from the win to build commitment to the new goals.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

After a sustained push, sustaining performance depends on both recognising the effort and allowing recovery before the next demand; energy that is not restored does not carry forward. (A) combines specific recognition, built-in recovery, and engagement in planning, addressing the human cost of the push as well as motivation. (B) is genuinely useful — capturing lessons is good leadership — but a retrospective is more cognitive work and does nothing to relieve a team that is running on empty. (C) provides recognition but only the external kind; without any recovery it leaves the fatigue unaddressed, and visibility to seniors matters less to a tired team than rest and direct acknowledgement. (D) capitalises on momentum but ignores depletion entirely, which is how a successful team is tipped into burnout just as the next phase starts.
