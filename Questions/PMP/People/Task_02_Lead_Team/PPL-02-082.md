# Question

You lead a project in a weak matrix organisation, so your team members report to functional managers and are assigned to you part-time. One key contributor has started prioritising his functional line work over your project, missing your deadlines because his manager keeps loading him up. You have no formal authority over him or his manager. What is the best leadership approach to regain his commitment?

## Options

- **A**: Redistribute the contributor's project tasks to other team members so the project no longer depends on his uncertain availability.
- **B**: Escalate to your sponsor to have the project's claim on the contributor's time formally reinforced with his functional manager.
- **C**: Understand what matters to the contributor and his manager, then show how the project's success serves their interests, building willing commitment.
- **D**: Agree a clear, documented commitment of the contributor's hours with his functional manager and hold both to it.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

With no formal authority in a weak matrix, a leader's primary lever is influence — understanding others' motivations and aligning the project's goals with what they and their managers care about. (C) builds genuine, willing commitment through influence, which is the most durable solution when you cannot direct people. (D) negotiating an explicit time commitment is a sensible complementary step, but a documented agreement imposed without underlying buy-in tends to erode the moment line pressures resurface, so it treats the symptom more than the cause. (B) escalating to the sponsor may sometimes be needed, but leading with authority you do not personally hold can sour the relationship and breeds compliance rather than commitment. (A) routing around the contributor abandons the relationship entirely, may overload others, and concedes the influence challenge instead of meeting it. Leading through influence by aligning interests is the strongest first approach in a low-authority context.
