# Question

On an agile programme delivering a transport ticketing app, the product owner has begun joining the team's internal design discussions and steering them toward specific technical solutions, not just desired outcomes. A senior developer tells you privately that the team feels its autonomy over the "how" is being eroded and engagement is dropping, but no one has said this to the product owner, who believes she is simply being a responsive, engaged stakeholder. As the leader, what is the most appropriate first action?

## Options

- **A**: Coach the team to push back in the moment — to accept the product owner's outcomes while holding the line on solution choices themselves — so the boundary is defended where it is actually crossed.
- **B**: Put the team's loss of design autonomy on the next retrospective agenda as a process topic, so the team and product owner inspect and adapt the working relationship together.
- **C**: Have a candid conversation with the product owner to reaffirm the boundary between owning the "what" and the team owning the "how," then reinforce it through how you run upcoming events.
- **D**: Reaffirm with the team the working agreement that they own technical design, and ask the product owner to bring desired outcomes to the team rather than proposed solutions.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The real issue is a blurred "what versus how" boundary held by a more senior stakeholder who does not realise she is crossing it and whom no one has told. (C) addresses it at the source through a direct, candid conversation, then makes it stick via how events are run — protecting both self-organisation and the collaboration. (A) loads the confrontation onto developers in the moment against a senior stakeholder they are already reluctant to challenge, which is likely to fail or breed friction. (B) is a legitimate inspect-and-adapt venue, but surfacing the grievance to the product owner for the first time in a group retrospective risks an ambush dynamic before she has had any private signal. (D) is half the answer — it reinforces the boundary with the team and even nudges the product owner, but doing it through a working-agreement reminder rather than a direct conversation can feel like a public correction and skips the candid exchange that resolves the misunderstanding. Speak to the product owner directly first, then reinforce.
