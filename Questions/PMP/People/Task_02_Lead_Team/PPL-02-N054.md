# Question

The day before a steering committee, the sponsor tells the PM that the team's two failing acceptance tests should not be raised as a quality concern in the report. The sponsor's reasoning is that the root cause is likely a flaky test environment rather than the product, the team expects to clear it within the week, and a "red" flag now would trigger a disruptive board review over what will probably be a non-issue. The PM agrees the environment is the more likely cause but cannot yet rule out a genuine defect. The whole team knows the tests are failing. How should the PM lead in this situation?

## Options

- **A**: Present the failing tests as an open quality risk with the current hypothesis, the investigation underway, and the expected resolution date, and discuss the framing with the sponsor before the meeting.
- **B**: Defer to the sponsor's read of the situation and leave the failures out of this report, on the basis that the sponsor owns the board relationship and judges the likely cause the same way the PM does.
- **C**: Report the quality area as on track but tell the PM's own line manager separately about the failing tests, so the information is on record somewhere without alarming the board.
- **D**: Present the failing tests as a confirmed defect requiring board attention, since until the cause is proven otherwise the PM should report the most cautious interpretation.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The team is watching how the PM handles pressure to soften a status, so leading with integrity means the material fact reaches the people accountable for the project — but integrity does not require overstating the problem either. (A) discloses the failing tests truthfully, frames them proportionately as a risk under investigation rather than a confirmed defect, and aligns with the sponsor on framing first, which respects the sponsor's board relationship without concealing anything. (B) suppresses a material item the board needs; the sponsor's preference and a shared hypothesis do not make an unconfirmed cause safe to omit, and complying teaches the team that status can be shaded under pressure. (C) tells the board a falsehood and routes the truth to someone with no decision authority over this project, which is concealment dressed up as a record. (D) is honest but overstates an unproven cause as a confirmed defect, triggering exactly the disruptive overreaction the situation does not yet warrant — accuracy includes representing uncertainty as uncertainty.
