# Question

Midway through a fixed-length release, your sponsor approaches your agile team's members directly and asks several of them to "just quickly add" two features she has thought of, bypassing the product owner. The developers, wanting to please her, begin doing so, and the sprint goal is now at risk. As the team's leader, what should you do first?

## Options

- **A**: Have the product owner assess the two requested features and, if valuable, prioritise them properly into the backlog for a future sprint.
- **B**: Speak with the sponsor about routing change requests through the product owner and the backlog, and explain how direct asks destabilise the team's commitments.
- **C**: Remind the developers that work entering the sprint must come through the product owner, and ask them to pause the unplanned additions.
- **D**: Convene the team and sponsor to renegotiate the sprint goal so the new features can be accommodated transparently.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The root cause is the sponsor circumventing the agreed intake mechanism, and a servant leader protects the team by addressing that behaviour at its source while shielding the team from the disruption. (B) tackles the origin of the problem and safeguards the team's process and commitments, which is why it is the right first move. (C) is a reasonable and necessary step with the developers, but redirecting the team without addressing the sponsor leaves the underlying behaviour intact and puts junior people in the awkward position of refusing their sponsor. (A) is the correct downstream handling of the features themselves but does nothing about the bypass that is destabilising the current sprint. (D) renegotiating mid-sprint is sometimes legitimate, but doing so in response to an out-of-process ambush rewards the bypass and undermines the discipline that makes commitments meaningful. Protecting the team means dealing with the sponsor's behaviour first, then handling the requests through the proper channel.
