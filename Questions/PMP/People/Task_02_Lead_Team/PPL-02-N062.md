# Question

You inherit a predictive infrastructure project mid-execution. The previous PM led with detailed task assignments and daily check-ins. The four engineers are highly experienced; they told the sponsor they felt micromanaged, and on-time delivery has slipped two reporting periods running. You do not yet know whether the slippage stems from the management style, from external dependencies, or from genuine under-capacity. How should you lead this team going forward?

## Options

- **A**: Give the engineers ownership of their work packages with agreed outcomes and a light blocker cadence, and work through the slippage with them to find what is actually driving it.
- **B**: Shift to delegating their work packages in full now, then run a focused root-cause review of the missed dates as a separate workstream once the team feels the change in style.
- **C**: Keep a daily check-in but reframe it as a team-run stand-up they own, so oversight on the slipping dates continues while the engineers regain day-to-day control of how they work.
- **D**: Agree with the team that you will hold the current cadence for two weeks only while you and they jointly diagnose the cause, then set the ongoing style based on what the diagnosis shows.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Every option moves away from micromanagement and toward diagnosis, so the margin is fine. (A) does both at once and without delay — it matches a delegating style to a high-competence team and treats the slippage as something to investigate with them rather than a problem with an assumed cause; the light cadence still surfaces blockers. (B) sequences delegation before the diagnosis and quarantines the root-cause work into a separate stream, so the dates keep slipping while the engineers settle in. (C) keeps a daily touchpoint that, however reframed, still reads as the watched behaviour the team flagged, and "team-run stand-up" on a predictive package is cadence theatre rather than restored ownership. (D) holds the disliked cadence for a fixed window, which prolongs the friction and defers ownership the senior team is already ready for. The best move grants autonomy and diagnoses concurrently.
