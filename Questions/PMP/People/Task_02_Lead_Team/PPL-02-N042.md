# Question

A two-year infrastructure programme to replace a national rail signalling system has just entered its delivery phase. The PM has noticed that the four discipline leads each describe the programme's purpose differently when speaking to their own teams: one emphasises cost savings, another safety, another schedule, and another regulatory compliance. Delivery is on track, but cross-discipline decisions are increasingly contested and each lead defends their own priority. What is the PM's best leadership response?

## Options

- **A**: Agree with the sponsor a clear priority order for the four objectives, publish it, and have the leads use it as the tie-breaker for future cross-discipline decisions.
- **B**: Stand up a cross-discipline decision forum where the leads bring contested trade-offs and you, as PM, make the call when they cannot agree, so decisions stop stalling.
- **C**: Have each lead present their discipline's priority to the others so everyone understands the competing pressures, then let the leads negotiate trade-offs case by case as they arise.
- **D**: Facilitate a session with the four leads and the sponsor to agree a single shared statement of the programme's purpose and the trade-off principles that follow from it.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

When team members hold divergent interpretations of purpose, contested decisions are a symptom of an absent shared vision, and the leader's durable job is to build that shared frame so the leads can resolve trade-offs themselves. Each option is a legitimate alignment or decision mechanism, so the choice turns on which actually removes the root cause rather than managing the symptom. (D) co-creates a shared purpose and the trade-off principles that let the leads settle future conflicts consistently, which scales as the programme grows. (A) a sponsor-backed priority order is more defensible than a PM directive and gives a real tie-breaker, but a fixed ranking rarely fits the specifics of each trade-off and, imposed rather than understood, secures compliance without the shared conviction that prevents the friction recurring. (B) a decision forum is genuinely useful, yet routing every contested call to the PM makes you the arbiter and keeps the leads dependent, treating the decision friction rather than the missing alignment beneath it. (C) airing each discipline's priority builds mutual understanding, but stopping at case-by-case negotiation with no agreed purpose means the same battles are re-fought on every decision. The fine margin is building a shared purpose with trade-off principles (D) versus imposing a ranking (A), centralising the decisions (B), or relying on repeated ad hoc negotiation (C).
