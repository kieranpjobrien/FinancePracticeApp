# Question

Leadership has decided to migrate your retail analytics platform from an ageing monolith the team built and is proud of, to microservices, over the next year. The technical plan is sound and was communicated clearly in a town hall, where leadership laid out the strategic reasons for the move. Two sprints on, velocity has dropped and several engineers quietly call the rewrite "change for change's sake". As project manager, what is the most effective leadership action to re-engage the team?

## Options

- **A**: Relay leadership's strategic rationale again to the team, more thoroughly than the town hall did, so the reasons for the migration are unmistakably clear.
- **B**: Involve the team in shaping how the migration is approached — service boundaries, sequencing, and the standards they will work to — so they help author the change rather than receive it.
- **C**: Acknowledge the loss of something they built well, and connect the migration to concrete outcomes the engineers themselves care about, so the purpose becomes theirs and not just leadership's.
- **D**: Break the migration into short, visible deliverables and celebrate each one, so steady wins rebuild momentum and confidence in the new direction.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The team has heard the rationale yet still rejects the purpose; resistance of this kind is rarely an information deficit and usually an ownership and emotional one — they have no felt reason to commit and are grieving something they were proud of. (C) does the core leadership work: it honours the loss and converts an organisationally-owned "why" into one anchored to outcomes the engineers value, which is what actually re-engages people during change. (B) is powerful and often part of the answer because participation builds ownership, but engaging them on the *how* while they still reject the *why* risks well-run delivery of a goal they do not believe in; purpose has to land first. (A) repeats what has already been communicated and treats conviction as a comprehension problem — saying the rationale again does not make it theirs. (D) builds mechanical momentum and is a fine supporting tactic, but visible wins towards a destination they find pointless do not supply the missing belief in the destination itself.
