# Question

You are leading a newly formed agile team that you want to grow into a self-organising, high-performing unit. The team is capable but still looks to you for direction on most things. Which leadership behaviours genuinely move the team towards self-organisation, rather than merely appearing to? (Choose three.)

## Options

- **A**: Make the goals, constraints, and the definition of success transparent so the team has the context to make sound decisions on its own.
- **B**: Resist solving problems the team can solve, instead asking questions that help it reach its own answers.
- **C**: Step back almost entirely from the team's day-to-day work so members are compelled to organise themselves out of necessity.
- **D**: Foster a safe environment where members can take measured risks and surface mistakes without fear of blame.
- **E**: Approve the team's significant decisions yourself for now to safeguard quality while the team matures.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

Self-organisation is cultivated by giving teams context, building their judgement, and making it safe to act — not by either abandoning them or retaining control. (A) supplies the shared understanding that lets the team decide without you; (B) develops the team's own problem-solving rather than fostering dependence; (D) creates the psychological safety without which people will not take the ownership self-organisation requires. (C) and (E) are the plausible traps in opposite directions. (C) abdication-by-absence is not empowerment — a young team dropped without support tends to flounder or fragment, and necessity alone does not build capability. (E) keeping yourself as the approval gate, however well-intentioned about quality, directly reinforces the dependence you are trying to dissolve; better-informed dependence is still dependence. A, B, and D build real autonomy; C abandons, and E tethers.
