# Question

You lead a predictive infrastructure programme delivering a new water treatment plant. A senior commissioning engineer with a strong track record has been assigned to a work package built on a control technology she has not used before. She is keen, treats the gap as minor, and says she will pick it up as she goes. The package is on the critical path with no float, and she is the only commissioning resource currently allocated to it. How should you lead her on this package?

## Options

- **A**: Keep the package with her but set up close direction and structured check-ins on the unfamiliar technology, while still leaning on her judgement for the parts that fit her existing expertise.
- **B**: Keep the package with her and arrange a vendor specialist in the control technology to pair with her through the unfamiliar parts, with you tracking progress against the critical-path dates.
- **C**: Move the package to an engineer already fluent in the control technology, and give her a package that plays to her proven strengths, protecting the critical path.
- **D**: Get her onto an accredited training course on the control technology before she starts the package, so she comes to the work properly qualified.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The right leadership style follows competence and commitment on this specific task, not general seniority. She is highly committed but a genuine novice on this technology, so a directing/coaching style with structured support fits, and keeping her on the package lets you still draw on her deep experience for the parts that match it. (A) does this and keeps the accountability for steering the critical-path work with you. (B) is the closest competitor and a reasonable move — a vendor specialist is exactly the right knowledge source — but pairing her with the vendor substitutes their direction for yours and can leave you a step removed from a critical-path risk; specialist input is better added underneath your own close direction (A) than in place of it. (C) protects the schedule by removing the risk, but writes off developing a capable senior engineer and may be infeasible if she is the resource you have, and it treats reassignment as the default rather than supporting her to succeed. (D) is sound for a non-urgent skills gap, but front-loading a full course on a critical path with no float is unlikely to be affordable in time, and on-the-job coaching can build the same competence without stopping the clock. The margin is between coaching her through it yourself (A) and the options that delegate the coaching out (B), avoid it (C), or defer the work for it (D).
