# Question

You have just become the project manager of an agile team that previously had a directive, command-and-control lead. The team is capable but cautious: it escalates trivial decisions to you and waits for permission before acting. You want to lead as a servant leader and grow the team's autonomy over the coming sprints, without letting quality or delivery wobble during the handover. Which actions best serve that intent? (Choose three.)

## Options

- **A**: When the team brings you a decision it is equipped to make, respond with coaching questions and ask them to make the call, then back the outcome.
- **B**: Make the team's purpose, priorities, constraints, and definition of done transparent so they have the context to decide well without checking with you.
- **C**: Keep approving decisions yourself for now but explain your reasoning each time, so the team absorbs your judgement before you let go.
- **D**: Agree explicit decision boundaries with the team — what they can now decide alone, what to decide and inform you, and the few things to escalate — and widen them as confidence grows.
- **E**: Tell the team you will personally shield them from any fallout if an early decision goes wrong, so they feel safe enough to start acting without your sign-off.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

Servant leadership grows autonomy by building the team's own judgement, supplying context, and clarifying where authority now sits. (A) transfers real decisions while staying available to coach; (B) gives the shared understanding that lets them act without permission; (D) replaces "ask first" with explicit, expanding boundaries, which is how a dependent team safely takes ownership during a handover. (C) and (E) are both genuinely plausible and well-meant, which is what makes the choice hard. (C) keeps you as the approval gate — better-informed dependence is still dependence — so it reinforces the very behaviour you are trying to break. (E) is aimed at psychological safety, which matters, but framing it as the leader absorbing all fallout teaches the team that acting is safe only because you backstop them, which breeds reliance on you rather than genuine ownership; real safety comes from clear decision rights and a no-blame response to honest mistakes (closer to B and D), not from a promise to take the hit. A, B, and D build owned autonomy; C and E, however kindly meant, keep the team tethered to you.
