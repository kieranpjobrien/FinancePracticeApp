# Question

On your agile data-migration team, one developer is by far the most productive person on the team and delivers excellent code, but he frequently grabs the highest-value stories before others can, reworks teammates' code without asking, and answers most questions in refinement before anyone else can contribute. Velocity is strong, yet two quieter members have stopped volunteering for anything and the team feels increasingly like a one-person show. What should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Raise team ownership and shared contribution as a topic in the next retrospective for the whole team to discuss.
- **B**: Introduce a work-in-progress limit and a pull policy so stories are taken up more evenly across the team.
- **C**: Pair the strong developer with the quieter members so his knowledge spreads and they gain confidence through working alongside him.
- **D**: Speak with the developer privately about the effect his style is having on the team and enlist him in creating room for others to contribute and grow.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The dynamic is being driven by one individual's behaviour, and while it is unintentional, the most direct and respectful first step is a private conversation that makes him aware of his impact and brings him into the solution. (D) addresses the actual cause and preserves his standing while protecting the team. (C) pairing can help knowledge flow but, applied to someone who already reworks others' code unbidden, risks reinforcing his dominance and the others' passivity rather than easing it. (B) WIP limits and pull policies are sound mechanics but treat a behavioural and interpersonal issue as a process problem; he can still dominate refinement and rework code within any such rules. (A) airing it in retrospective without first speaking to him risks a public call-out that puts him on the defensive and embarrasses the quieter members further. Tackling the root cause one-to-one, then using process or team forums as reinforcement, is the sounder sequence.
