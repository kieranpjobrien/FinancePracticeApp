# Question

A project manager building teams across a pharmaceutical R&D portfolio faces four team-formation situations in the same fortnight. Each could plausibly be read more than one way, so the task is to match each situation to the response that addresses its real underlying need rather than a similar-looking one. Match each situation on the left to the most appropriate response on the right.

## Items

- **1**: A new agile squad has the right people and clear roles, yet members keep tripping over each other — two will not proceed without a decision the other thinks is already made, and "done" means different things to different people.
- **2**: A forming team's backlog needs laboratory-automation work that no current member can do, the skill is scarce in the organisation, and the project manager is weighing whether to grow it internally or bring it in.
- **3**: On a predictive team where everyone is allocated and roles are set, a regulatory submission was missed and a test environment built twice, each time because several disciplines touched the item and each assumed another discipline owned it.
- **4**: A capable team keeps over-committing and slipping because two members are repeatedly planned into weeks when they turn out to be tied up on a parallel clinical trial.

## Matches

- **A**: Conduct a make-or-buy capability decision and acquire the missing skill.
- **B**: Facilitate the team to co-create its working agreement.
- **C**: Build a resource calendar reflecting each person's real availability.
- **D**: Develop a responsibility assignment matrix (RACI) for the deliverables.

## Answer

1-B, 2-A, 3-D, 4-C

## Explanation

Each situation is matched to the response targeting its actual cause. Situation 1 is members lacking agreed decision rules and a shared definition of done — norms the team itself needs to set, the purpose of a co-created working agreement (1-B), not a roles or scheduling fix. Situation 2 is a genuine capability shortfall the team cannot fill internally with the strategic choice of building versus buying, resolved by a make-or-buy decision (2-A). Situation 3 reads like a ground-rules problem but the missed submission and duplicated environment stem from undefined accountability for specific cross-discipline deliverables, which a RACI fixes by naming who is accountable (3-D). Situation 4 is not a roles dispute but people being scheduled into windows when they are unavailable, which a resource calendar showing real availability prevents (4-C). The traps are reading situation 3 as a working-agreement need or situation 4 as a RACI need; matching on the true cause yields the correct set.
