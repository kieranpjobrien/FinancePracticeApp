# Question

On an agile payments-platform product, three engineers are each split 50% between the product team and a separate fraud-operations rota. Velocity swings widely and sprint goals are routinely missed because the engineers are pulled into live fraud incidents mid-sprint. The product owner asks the project manager to fix the staffing model. What should the project manager pursue FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Propose that the three engineers serve fraud operations in whole-sprint blocks, alternating so the product team always knows who is fully available that sprint.
- **B**: Negotiate with the operations manager to dedicate the three engineers to the product team, with a separate, ring-fenced on-call rotation covering fraud incidents.
- **C**: Negotiate to swap the three split engineers for two engineers dedicated full-time to the product, accepting slightly lower nominal capacity for predictable focus.
- **D**: Establish a formal interrupt buffer in each sprint sized to typical fraud demand, so commitments reflect the interruptions rather than being blown by them.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The root cause is divided attention and mid-sprint context-switching, so the strongest first move is to restore focus by dedicating the engineers and isolating operational demand behind a separate on-call arrangement (B). (A) reduces switching within a sprint but still loses whole people to operations and leaves the product chronically short-handed in alternating sprints. (C) trades headcount for focus and could work, but it concedes capacity unnecessarily when the demand can instead be ring-fenced; it is a fallback if dedication cannot be negotiated. (D) plans around the interruptions rather than removing them — sometimes pragmatic, but it institutionalises the very dysfunction the product owner asked to fix, and steadily depresses throughput.
