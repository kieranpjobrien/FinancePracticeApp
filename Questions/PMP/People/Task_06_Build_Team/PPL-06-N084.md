# Question

A project manager is assembling an agile team to build a renewable-energy trading platform from scratch. Leadership has approved headcount but left composition to the project manager. The product spans real-time market data, complex pricing logic, a trader-facing UI, and strict regulatory reporting. The project manager wants a team that can deliver a working increment every sprint without depending on outside groups. Which THREE principles should guide how the project manager builds this team? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Compose the team so it collectively holds every skill a feature needs from idea to done — data, pricing, UI, and compliance — without routine hand-offs to external groups.
- **B**: Size the team to the work and keep it small enough to communicate effectively, rather than filling all the approved headcount because the budget allows it.
- **C**: Put the scarce regulatory-reporting capability on the team and plan to spread it, so the most specialised knowledge is not locked in one person.
- **D**: Structure the team around durable specialist roles, with each member clearly owning their discipline so deep expertise is protected and accountability for each area is unambiguous.
- **E**: Staff with the most senior people available across every discipline, so the team carries the depth a complex trading platform demands from the outset.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

A team that delivers a working increment each sprint without external dependence must be cross-functional, collectively holding every skill a feature needs end to end (A); it must be right-sized so communication stays effective rather than inflated just because headcount is approved (B); and because regulatory reporting is both essential and risky to concentrate, that capability must be on the team with a deliberate plan to share it so it is not a single point of failure (C). Building the team around fixed specialist roles sounds disciplined, but locking each person to one discipline forfeits the cross-skilling that lets the team flex and creates exactly the single points of failure principle C guards against — clear accountability does not require permanent silos (D). Loading up on the most senior specialists optimises individual depth, yet a team of deep specialists who do not cross-skill is brittle and bottlenecks whenever one is absent or overloaded; seniority is not the organising principle, collective capability and the right size are (E). The correct principles are end-to-end capability, sensible sizing, and embedded-yet-shared critical expertise (A, B, C); D and E are plausible-sounding but lead to silos and fragility.
