# Question

A project manager is assembling a software-localisation team whose strongest specialists sit across three time zones and have never worked together. There is enough budget for exactly one of: a one-week in-person kickoff, or a year's investment in high-quality collaboration tooling, or a recurring quarterly cadence of shorter in-person meet-ups through the project. The team will work remotely throughout. Leadership leaves the choice to the project manager. Which is the best use of the budget to build this team?

## Options

- **A**: The one-week in-person kickoff, to build the relationships and shared agreements up front that a never-before-worked-together team most needs, then rely on existing tooling.
- **B**: The quarterly in-person cadence, so relationships are renewed and recalibrated repeatedly across the life of a long distributed project rather than only at the start.
- **C**: The collaboration tooling, since the team works remotely every day and better day-to-day tools compound over the whole project.
- **D**: The kickoff, but run as working sessions producing the team charter, definition of done, and first plan, rather than primarily as relationship-building.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

For a brand-new distributed team, the highest-value early investment is the face-to-face time that builds the trust and shared understanding virtual collaboration then sustains; front-loading it gives every later interaction a foundation to stand on (A). (B) is genuinely strong for a long engagement, but deferring all in-person contact until the first quarterly meet-up leaves the team forming remotely at the very point relationships matter most. (C) equips the team to communicate yet skips the relationship-building a never-met group needs first, and good tooling cannot manufacture trust. (D) captures real value but over-indexes on artifacts; producing a charter without first establishing the human relationships tends to yield agreements the team does not truly own.
