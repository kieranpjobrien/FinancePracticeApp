# Question

On a predictive plant-relocation programme, the project manager met the functional manager last week to request two senior controls engineers for the cutover. They explored phased release and backfill together, but the functional manager has now confirmed in writing that operational demands mean he can release only one senior engineer plus a junior, and that this is his final position. The cutover is ten weeks out, the controls activities have no float, and a capability shortfall there would delay the whole programme. What should the project manager do next?

## Options

- **A**: Accept the one senior and one junior, and pair the junior closely with the senior so the senior carries the critical decisions during cutover.
- **B**: Escalate to the sponsor, framing the staffing gap as a programme-level schedule risk and asking them to weigh in with the functional manager.
- **C**: Re-open the negotiation with the functional manager, offering to adjust the cutover window so his senior engineer is needed for a shorter, defined period.
- **D**: Bring in an external contract controls engineer for the cutover to cover the second senior position.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The resource owner has negotiated in good faith and given a firm final answer; the gap now threatens the programme's schedule, so the legitimate next move is to escalate the constraint to the sponsor who owns the trade-off between operations and the programme (B). (A) silently absorbs a capability shortfall on a zero-float critical activity by relying on one stretched engineer, accepting programme-level risk locally. (C) re-opens a negotiation the manager has explicitly closed and offers a schedule concession the PM may not be able to make. (D) is a plausible fallback but commits unbudgeted spend and an unknown contractor to critical work before the sponsor has been given the chance to free up the named senior engineer.
