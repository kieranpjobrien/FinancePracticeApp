# Question

On a predictive clinical-trial systems project in a strong matrix, the project manager negotiated named resources at 80% with three functional managers and holds the agreed resource management plan. A deliverable has just slipped, and investigation shows two of the managers have been allocating their people at 30%, not the 80% in the plan, with no notice. There are no integrated reports that would have flagged the drift earlier. The sponsor is supportive and the trial is strategically important. What is the best action?

## Options

- **A**: Meet the two functional managers to confirm what each actually committed, quantify the gap against the slipped deliverable, and agree corrective allocation before deciding whether to escalate.
- **B**: Escalate to the sponsor with the deliverable impact and ask the sponsor to reconfirm the committed allocations directly with the two functional managers.
- **C**: Establish a weekly resource-utilisation report against the plan so any future deviation surfaces immediately, then take the assembled evidence to the managers.
- **D**: Re-baseline the schedule to the demonstrated 30% availability and formally notify stakeholders that the original dates assumed capacity that is not being provided.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Commitments were genuinely made and then quietly broken, so the project manager should first reconnect with the resource owners, establish the facts against the evidenced slippage, and seek correction — this preserves the working relationships in a matrix where the project depends on those managers, and often recovers the capacity without escalation (A). (B) is the right escalation to the right person, but going to the sponsor before the project manager has even confirmed the discrepancy with the managers can convert a fixable misunderstanding into a reported breach and damage relationships the project still needs; escalation is the fallback if A fails. (C) fixes the real monitoring blind spot and is worth doing, but installing a report does not recover the capacity already lost on a strategic deliverable — control is a follow-on, not the first corrective act. (D) treats the broken commitment as the new baseline and re-plans around it, surrendering agreed capacity rather than first attempting to restore it.
