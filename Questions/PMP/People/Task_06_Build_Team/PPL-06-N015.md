# Question

A project manager is forming a globally distributed team drawn from three countries and two external vendors for an 18-month predictive aerospace programme. Each member's technical skills are already confirmed. Which THREE actions matter most to building *this particular* team — distributed, multi-vendor, and long-running — into an effective one? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Establish how decisions, hand-offs, and escalation will work across time zones and organisational boundaries.
- **B**: Confirm contractual terms, export-control clearances, and tooling access for vendor staff before they begin work.
- **C**: Negotiate reasonable overlapping working hours across the time zones and agree which interactions must be synchronous versus asynchronous.
- **D**: Assign every work package to a single country or vendor so each piece has one clear owner and cross-boundary collaboration is minimised.
- **E**: Run a co-located kick-off in week one to set norms, then hold all subsequent coordination through the project's written status reports.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

For a distributed, multi-vendor, long-running team, the decisive enablers are agreeing how the team operates across boundaries (A), clearing the contractual, clearance, and access prerequisites so vendor members can actually contribute (B), and engineering workable overlap with a deliberate synchronous/asynchronous split (C). (D) sounds tidy but partitioning all work by organisation hardens silos and forfeits the integration a single programme team needs; clear ownership is achievable without walling off collaboration. (E) starts well — an in-person kick-off is valuable — but then collapses ongoing coordination onto status reports alone, which cannot sustain an 18-month effort across vendors and time zones; the kick-off must be followed by living cross-boundary working agreements, not a reporting cadence.
