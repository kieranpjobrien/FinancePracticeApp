# Question

A games studio is forming a feature team for a six-month live-ops content programme delivered in two-week iterations. The backlog's mix of art, design, and engineering work will swing sharply from sprint to sprint, and every story must be playable and integrated by each sprint review. The hiring manager has already confirmed a team of generalising specialists — each person deep in one discipline with working ability in one or two others — and the roster is set. Reviewing it, the project manager notices the team's combined secondary skills are thin in exactly the two disciplines the early sprints lean on hardest, and those two disciplines are scarce studio-wide. The programme starts in two weeks. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Map the team's actual primary and secondary skills against the next two or three sprints of backlog, then work with the hiring manager to adjust the roster or the early sprint sequence so the heavy disciplines are covered.
- **B**: Keep the roster and trust the team to self-organise around the swinging mix each sprint, since a cross-skilled team is built precisely to rebalance to whatever work is heaviest.
- **C**: Start a cross-training push immediately so members broaden their secondary skills into the two thin disciplines before those sprints arrive.
- **D**: Reorder the backlog to pull the work in the two scarce disciplines later, giving the team time to grow into it while it delivers the disciplines it already covers.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A generalising-specialist team is the right model for a swinging backlog, but this team has a concrete, evidenced coverage gap precisely where the early sprints are heaviest, so the project manager's job is to verify the gap against the actual upcoming work and then close it by adjusting either the people or the sequencing with the person who owns the roster (A). (B) is the strongest distractor because the cross-skilled rationale is genuinely sound — but self-organisation can only rebalance capacity the team actually has; trusting it to flex into disciplines its secondary skills barely cover ignores the specific shortfall already spotted, and "the model is right" is not the same as "this instance is staffed correctly." (C) addresses the gap and may form part of the answer, but committing to cross-training before confirming which sprints truly need which depth risks investing scarce people's time in the wrong disciplines, and skills take longer to build than the two weeks before the heavy sprints hit. (D) is a legitimate lever and might be part of the adjustment, but reordering on the project manager's own initiative trades a staffing problem for a value-sequencing decision that belongs with the product owner and the backlog's priorities, and merely defers the same coverage gap rather than resolving whether the team can meet it. Only A first establishes the real gap against the real work, then opens the two valid remedies — roster change and resequencing — with the people who own those decisions.
