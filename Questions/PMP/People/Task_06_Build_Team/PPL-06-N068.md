# Question

A predictive infrastructure programme is in planning, and the project manager is building the delivery team against the WBS. The scope needs deep capability in three areas — geotechnical analysis, BIM coordination, and contract administration — but the available staff are strong only in the first two. The sponsor has limited budget for external hires and the start date is fixed. Which THREE actions are appropriate for the project manager to build a capable team within these constraints? (Choose three.)

## Options

- **A**: Conduct a skills assessment against the WBS to pinpoint exactly where capability is missing and how schedule-critical each gap is.
- **B**: Plan targeted development — mentoring or training — to grow contract-administration capability in an existing staff member where the timeline allows.
- **C**: Bring in a focused external hire or specialist contractor for the contract-administration work, sized to the genuine, schedule-critical gap only.
- **D**: Borrow contract-administration capacity part-time from another programme's resource pool and reflect the dependency in the resource plan.
- **E**: Cross-train a strong BIM coordinator into contract administration so a single person can flex between both as each phase demands.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Building a capable team starts with assessing skills against the actual work to locate and rank the gaps (A), then closing the critical one through a blend of developing existing people where time permits (B) and a focused external hire reserved for the gap the team genuinely cannot grow in time, sized so it does not blow a tight budget (C). The two distractors are deliberately plausible: (D) borrowing from another pool is a real acquisition route, but a part-time external dependency on a fixed-date critical capability imports schedule and priority risk the programme does not control, making it weaker than securing dedicated capacity through B and C; (E) cross-training sounds efficient, yet pulling a scarce, already-needed BIM coordinator into a second deep discipline risks leaving both areas under-covered when phases overlap, so it trades a known gap for a new bottleneck rather than building genuine capacity.
