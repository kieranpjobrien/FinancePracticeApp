# Question

A predictive programme to relocate a manufacturer's data centre has brought staff from infrastructure, networking, security, and facilities into one team. In the first fortnight, two tasks were done twice by different people, one sign-off sat for a week because three people each assumed another owned it, and a security checkpoint was missed because no one believed it was theirs. The team is skilled and adequately staffed. The project manager wants to stop the same failures recurring as the relocation ramps up. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Work with the team to build a responsibility assignment matrix that names, for each deliverable, who is responsible, accountable, consulted, and informed.
- **B**: Facilitate the team to co-create a team charter covering decision-making, escalation paths, and how the disciplines will work together.
- **C**: Establish a daily cross-discipline stand-up where the team confirms who is taking each upcoming task and surfaces anything unowned.
- **D**: Map the relocation's hand-off points into the schedule with predecessors and owners so dependencies between disciplines are explicit.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Every failure traces to undefined ownership of specific deliverables — duplicated tasks, a sign-off no one held, a checkpoint no one claimed — and the instrument built to fix exactly that is a responsibility assignment matrix, which names the responsible and accountable party for each item so nothing falls between disciplines (A). A team charter sets decision rules and escalation norms and is genuinely useful, but it works at the level of general ways of working; it would not by itself say who is accountable for each deliverable, so the specific gaps could recur (B). A daily stand-up coordinates verbally and would catch some unowned work, but it leaves no durable record of accountability and depends on who attends, so the ambiguity returns between meetings (C). Mapping hand-offs into the schedule clarifies sequence and dependency timing, yet a task can have a place in the network and still lack a named accountable owner — the precise problem here — so it addresses when, not who (D). The targeted first move for deliverable-level role ambiguity is the responsibility assignment matrix.
