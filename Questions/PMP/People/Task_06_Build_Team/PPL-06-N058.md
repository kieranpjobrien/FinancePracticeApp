# Question

A project manager has negotiated two named senior engineers for a predictive data-centre build. They are confirmed essential, no equivalent substitutes exist, and they can only join eight weeks after project start because they are finishing a prior commitment. Their activities currently sit on the critical path, so an eight-week-late start would push out the project finish date. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Update the resource calendar with the real availability, recalculate the schedule, and bring the resulting critical-path and finish-date impact to the sponsor with options.
- **B**: Resequence the network now to pull as much non-dependent work as possible into the first eight weeks, so the engineers' activities start the moment they arrive.
- **C**: Engage two qualified contractors for the eight-week gap to keep the critical activities moving until the named engineers take over.
- **D**: Ask the engineers' functional manager whether either could join even part-time during the eight weeks to make an early start on the critical work.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The honest first step is to model the known availability in the resource calendar, let the schedule reveal the true critical-path and finish-date impact, and surface that to the sponsor with options, because the sponsor owns the date-versus-resource trade-off and cannot decide on hidden information (A). (B), (C), and (D) are all sound mitigations the PM will likely pursue, but choosing any of them first commits to a response before the impact is quantified and before the sponsor has weighed in: (B) helps only if enough independent work exists and may not recover the slip; (C) places unfamiliar contractors on essential critical work and bets on a clean handover; (D) is worth asking but depends on a manager who has already committed the engineers elsewhere. The fine margin is integrate-and-expose the constraint before locking a specific recovery lever.
