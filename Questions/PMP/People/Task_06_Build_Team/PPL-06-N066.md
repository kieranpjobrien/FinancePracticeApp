# Question

Four weeks into a new agile programme, a freshly assembled cross-functional team is in visible difficulty. Members openly contest each other's technical approaches in standups, debates over who has the final say run long, the last two sprint goals were missed, and two people have privately told the project manager the team "isn't working" and asked to move back to their old squads. The next sprint planning is in two days. What is the most appropriate action for the project manager?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate a focused session for the team to agree its own ways of working — decision rules, technical standards, and how it settles competing approaches — and keep the membership intact.
- **B**: Replace the disputed informal decision-making by assigning clear technical-decision authority for each area to the most experienced member in it, so debates resolve quickly and the next goal is met.
- **C**: Meet the two members asking to leave individually to understand their concerns and decide whether a roster change is warranted, while signalling the team stays together for now.
- **D**: At the next retrospective, have the team name the friction itself and self-select two or three improvements to trial, then step back and let its own improvement cycle work.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The signals to read are that members are still actively contributing and the disputes are about technical approach and decision rights rather than personal animosity — this is a team thrashing over how to work and who decides, normal early formation that needs agreed ways of working, not a change of people; the project manager's strongest move is to help the team forge those norms quickly so the energy converges into delivery while membership stays stable (A). (B) does address the missing decision rules, but imposing per-area authority from outside hands the team a structure rather than letting it own one, and prematurely freezing who decides can suppress the very capability-sharing a cross-functional team is forming — it buys a faster next goal at the cost of the team's self-direction. (C) is humane and worth doing alongside, yet centring the response on the exit requests treats a symptom of unsettled formation and risks legitimising the break-up of a team that is developing normally. (D) trusts the team's own improvement mechanism, which is healthy, but a hands-off retro defers structure when goals are already slipping and members are asking to leave — the team needs active help reaching its norms now, not only a forum to discuss them.
