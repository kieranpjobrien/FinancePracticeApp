# Question

A predictive aerospace programme needs a stress-analysis engineer certified in a niche composite-fatigue method for a nine-month structural workstream that starts in two months and sits on the critical path. The internal resource pool has no one certified, and the one staff engineer who could train would need six months and would be unavailable for project work during that time. The market has experienced contractors available now at a premium, and the organisation's strategy is to build this composite capability in-house over the next two years. The programme cannot slip. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Engage a certified contractor for the full nine-month workstream so the critical-path work proceeds on schedule.
- **B**: Have the staff engineer begin the six-month certification now and re-plan the workstream to start once they qualify.
- **C**: Engage a certified contractor for the workstream while having the staff engineer certify in parallel and shadow the contractor where the schedule allows.
- **D**: Engage the contractor for the early critical-path months only, and switch the workstream to the staff engineer once their certification completes partway through.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Two valid objectives collide — the programme cannot slip, yet the organisation wants the capability in-house — and the action serving both is to buy the skill now to protect the critical path while simultaneously growing the internal capability and capturing knowledge through shadowing (C). Engaging a contractor for the whole nine months meets the schedule but does nothing toward the explicit strategic intent to build the capability, leaving the organisation dependent on the market again next time (A). Having the staff engineer certify first honours the strategy but breaks the hard constraint that the programme cannot slip, since waiting six months delays critical-path work (B). Handing the workstream to the staff engineer partway through is tempting for cost, but it forces a mid-stream changeover on safety-critical fatigue analysis from a contractor to a newly certified, still-inexperienced engineer, risking continuity and quality on the critical path for a saving the programme has not asked for — shadowing transfers the knowledge without that handover risk (D). Only C reconciles the immediate make-or-buy need with the longer-term capability goal without gambling the critical path.
