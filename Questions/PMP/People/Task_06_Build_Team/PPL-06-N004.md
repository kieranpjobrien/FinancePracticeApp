# Question

A museum-restoration project charter names a specific conservation chemist as a pre-assigned resource because of her rare experience with the pigments involved. As the project manager builds out the rest of the team and the schedule, the chemist mentions informally that her department may pull her onto an exhibition opening for several weeks during what looks like the project's critical conservation window. Nothing has changed formally. What should the project manager do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Continue planning around her committed availability as stated in the charter, since no formal change to her assignment has been made.
- **B**: Confirm her actual availability against the project's critical conservation window with her and her functional manager, and surface any conflict before baselining the schedule.
- **C**: Identify and line up a backup chemist with comparable pigment experience so the project is covered if she is pulled away.
- **D**: Raise the potential conflict with the sponsor, who owns the charter commitment, and ask them to protect her time on the project.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A pre-assigned resource is planned around as committed, but building the team responsibly means acting on a credible, specific availability signal before it hardens into a problem. Confirming her real availability against the critical window with both her and her resource owner is the grounded first step that tells the PM whether a conflict actually exists (B). (A) is technically defensible but treats "no formal change" as licence to ignore information the PM already has, baselining a schedule the PM has reason to doubt. (C) lining up a backup may follow, but provisioning a rare alternate before confirming there is even a conflict is premature and may be unnecessary effort. (D) escalating to the sponsor jumps a level before the PM has verified the facts or attempted resolution with the resource owner.
