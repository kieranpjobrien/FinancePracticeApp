# Question

During a two-day team chartering workshop, three team members have spoken for roughly 70% of the airtime while four barely contribute. The quieter members are visibly experienced and their input on sprint planning has been valuable in other settings. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Let the workshop continue as it is, on the basis that the most engaged voices typically produce the strongest team direction when workshops unfold naturally.
- **B**: Ask the three frequent speakers to sit out the remaining workshop sessions so the quieter members finally get an unimpeded chance to shape the charter.
- **C**: Directly name the participation imbalance in the room and insist that everyone now contribute equally for the rest of the chartering sessions without exception.
- **D**: Adjust facilitation: use silent writing, pair and share, round-robin, and digital input channels so all voices contribute and quieter members are heard.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

PMI expects facilitation to adapt to a range of participation styles so that decision quality reflects the whole team, not just the most vocal. (D) does that. (A) accepts a clear facilitation failure. (B) punishes participation rather than rebalancing it. (C) forces participation without creating the conditions for it and can shame quieter members.
