# Question

An organisation is splitting a large agile programme into three new feature squads from a pool of twenty-four engineers who already work together and know each other's strengths. Leadership wants the squads running within a week and has told the project manager that seniority must be roughly even across them so no squad is left weak. Some engineers have strong views on who they work with; a few pairings are known to clash. The work is genuinely separable. The project manager must decide how the squads get formed. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Set the constraints — squad sizes, the skills and seniority mix each must contain — then facilitate a self-selection session in which the engineers form the squads within those boundaries.
- **B**: Draft a balanced allocation himself using what he knows of each engineer's skills and the known clashes, then walk the team through it and adjust where they raise concerns.
- **C**: Run the self-selection session first to capture how the engineers want to group, then rebalance the result himself afterwards to meet the seniority requirement.
- **D**: Have the three incoming squad leads divide the twenty-four engineers between them against the seniority target, since the leads will manage the squads and know the people.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The engineers know their own strengths and working relationships better than anyone, and letting them form the squads inside firm constraints produces balanced, correctly skilled squads while generating the ownership self-selection creates — the constraints guarantee the seniority requirement is met without the project manager picking names (A). Drafting the allocation himself and then consulting reverts to manager-led assignment; the after-the-fact adjustment is a review, not genuine self-organisation, and it discards the buy-in and the team's superior insight (B). Running self-selection but then overriding the outcome to rebalance undercuts the very autonomy just offered and is likely to feel like a bait-and-switch, eroding the trust the approach depends on — building the constraints in from the start avoids the need to overrule (C). Handing the split to three squad leads is still top-down allocation by a small group and reintroduces the risk of horse-trading people, missing the wider team's knowledge and commitment (D). Constrained self-selection is the balance of structure and autonomy the situation calls for.
