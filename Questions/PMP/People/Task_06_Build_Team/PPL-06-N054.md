# Question

A hybrid project will be delivered by in-house staff plus a vendor's developers joining under a time-and-materials contract. Both groups are skilled; each has its own mature ceremonies and tooling and is used to working its own way. The two groups' work is tightly interdependent across most stories, not cleanly separable. Delivery starts in three weeks. As the project manager forms this blended team, which approach to ways of working should be set first?

## Options

- **A**: Convene both groups to agree the shared practices the interdependent work actually needs — a common board, definition of done, and joint ceremonies — while letting each keep internal habits that do not touch the other.
- **B**: Adopt the in-house ceremonies and tooling as the single team standard from day one and have the vendor's developers conform, so there is one consistent way of working before delivery starts.
- **C**: Let each group run its own established practices for the first few sprints, then converge on a shared approach once both understand the work and each other.
- **D**: Define clear interfaces and ownership between the two groups so each works in its own way and integrates at sprint boundaries.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Because the work is tightly interdependent, the groups must share the practices that govern the joint work — a common board, a single definition of done, joint ceremonies — yet there is no need to homogenise everything, so co-agreeing the shared surface while leaving private habits alone is both sufficient and fast enough for a three-week start (A). (B) gives one consistent standard immediately and the in-house practices may be perfectly good, but imposing them wholesale on capable vendor engineers discards practices that might be better and breeds the us-and-them friction that fractures interdependent work; consistency is bought at the cost of buy-in. (C) defers integration precisely where the work cannot be separated, letting two rhythms harden before they are reconciled. (D) suits separable scopes, but clean interfaces and boundary integration do not fit work that is interleaved across most stories, so it manages a seam that does not really exist while leaving the shared flow ungoverned. The fine margin is converging only the practices the joint work demands versus full imposition, deferral, or interface-based separation.
