# Question

A Scrum team that started cooperatively now argues openly three sprints into a new product: two senior engineers contest the technical direction in every planning session and estimation has become drawn-out debate. Stories are still completing and velocity has dipped only slightly. The Scrum Master has the next sprint to help the team settle. Which action will most durably reduce the recurring friction?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate the team in agreeing how it will make technical decisions when members disagree, so future debates resolve through an owned process rather than repeated arguments.
- **B**: Use the next retrospective to let members surface the underlying tensions openly, so the disagreements are named and worked through rather than left unspoken.
- **C**: Coach the two senior engineers one on one to reframe their clashes as a joint search for the best solution, lowering the personal heat in planning.
- **D**: Introduce a timeboxed estimation technique so planning debates cannot consume the meeting, keeping the team productive while the dynamics settle.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The friction is recurring because the team has no owned way to resolve technical disagreement, so each planning session re-litigates direction from scratch; agreeing a decision-making process the team itself owns removes the trigger and is the most durable fix (A). (B) is a sound complementary practice, but airing tensions without establishing how decisions will be made tends to recycle the same arguments next sprint. (C) addresses the two individuals' conduct rather than the structural gap in how the team decides, so the clashes can re-emerge with others. (D) caps the symptom by constraining the meeting and may suppress the very debate the team needs to work through, settling the surface while leaving the cause.
