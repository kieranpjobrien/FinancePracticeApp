# Question

A project manager standing up a team for a utilities billing replacement hits four situations in the same week. Each could be read more than one way, so the task is to match every situation to the instrument that addresses its real underlying cause rather than a similar-looking one. Match each situation on the left to the most appropriate instrument on the right.

## Items

- **1**: Three functional managers have each asked the project manager to "just tell me exactly what you need and when," but his staffing requests keep coming back as too vague to action because the roles, levels, and timing have never been pinned down.
- **2**: The people are in place, but in the first sprint members keep clashing over how decisions get made and what "done" means, and they have said they would rather agree these ways of working themselves than be handed a rulebook.
- **3**: Two strong engineers have privately told the project manager they are unsure their effort here will be noticed, and he wants to set out clearly, up front, what contributions will be acknowledged and how.
- **4**: Before he parcels out the work packages, the project manager suspects the assembled team may not cover everything the billing build demands and wants to see precisely where it falls short.

## Matches

- **A**: Facilitate the team to co-create a team charter.
- **B**: Develop the resource management plan's role requirements and timing.
- **C**: Run a skills assessment and gap analysis.
- **D**: Establish a recognition and rewards approach.

## Answer

1-B, 2-A, 3-D, 4-C

## Explanation

Each situation is matched to the instrument targeting its actual cause. Situation 1 fails because the role requirements, levels, and timing behind the staffing requests were never specified, which is what the resource management plan's requirements section sets out (1-B) — not a charter or a skills review. Situation 2 is the team wanting to define its own decision rules and norms, the defining purpose of a co-created team charter (2-A). Situation 3 is about motivation and making recognition expectations explicit, which a recognition and rewards approach addresses (3-D), and which a charter only touches obliquely. Situation 4 is locating capability shortfalls against the planned work, the exact output of a skills assessment and gap analysis (4-C). The traps are reading situation 3 as a charter matter or situation 1 as a skills question; matching on the underlying cause rather than the surface wording gives the correct set.
