# Question

A hybrid programme to overhaul a city transit authority's scheduling system has a key analyst role pre-assigned in the charter to Tomas, who was placed because he is between projects rather than for his fit. Three weeks in, Tomas is clearly struggling: he lacks the data-modelling depth the role needs and his deliverables are returning with significant gaps, putting an upcoming milestone at risk. He is engaged and willing. A genuinely strong analyst is available in the resource pool. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Assess the specific capability gap, then discuss the mismatch with Tomas and his manager and arrange the better-fit analyst for the role, redeploying or supporting Tomas appropriately.
- **B**: Keep Tomas in the role and provide intensive data-modelling training so he can grow into the analyst position over the coming weeks.
- **C**: Leave Tomas in place and quietly assign the stronger analyst to redo and check his work so the milestone is protected.
- **D**: Replace Tomas with the stronger analyst immediately without discussion, to remove the milestone risk as fast as possible.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Tomas was assigned for availability, not fit, and the role demands depth he does not have with a milestone now at risk, so the project manager should confirm the gap, then handle the change openly with Tomas and his manager while bringing in the better-fit analyst (A). This protects delivery and treats Tomas with respect. (B) commits to growing him into the role on a timescale the at-risk milestone does not allow; meaningful data-modelling depth will not develop fast enough. (C) leaves a known poor fit in place and sets up a shadow checker, which is wasteful, undermines Tomas, and hides the real problem. (D) reaches the right staffing outcome but does it abruptly and without conversation, damaging trust and Tomas's standing unnecessarily. Acting on the assessed gap while managing the person with transparency is the balanced response.
