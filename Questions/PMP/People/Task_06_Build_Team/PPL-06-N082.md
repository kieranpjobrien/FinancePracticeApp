# Question

In a weak matrix organisation, a project manager needs a named database expert, Priya, for a six-week migration on a predictive insurance-claims project. Her functional manager values the project but has competing demands and has not committed. The project manager knows Priya is the strongest fit, the migration is a month away with some schedule flexibility, and two adequate-but-less-expert database staff sit in the same function. The functional manager controls all three. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Meet the functional manager with the specific timing, duration, and flexibility, and negotiate Priya's release, exploring options such as a phased release or covering part of the window with one of the other two.
- **B**: Bring the choice to the functional manager but plan around the two other staff, asking only that he confirm whichever of the three he can spare so the migration is not held up.
- **C**: Use the month of lead time to schedule the migration into Priya's first clear window, then take that dated plan to the functional manager as a concrete request to approve.
- **D**: Raise the resourcing need at the programme's resource-planning forum so it is prioritised against the functional manager's other demands through the governance process.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

In a matrix the functional manager owns the resource, so the first move is a real negotiation that brings concrete needs and flexibility and actively surfaces creative options — phasing Priya's time or having one of the other two cover part of the window — which respects his authority and is most likely to secure the best-fit person (A). Asking him merely to confirm whoever he can spare concedes the strongest candidate before negotiating and frames the request around the weaker staff, settling for less than the project need when room to negotiate exists (B). Building a dated plan around Priya's availability first inverts the order: it commits the schedule to an assumption the functional manager has not agreed and presents him with a fait accompli rather than a negotiation, which in a weak matrix is likely to harden resistance (C). Taking it straight to the resource-planning forum escalates into the governance process before any direct conversation with the person who controls the resource, which is premature and bypasses the relationship — a fallback if direct negotiation fails, not the opening move (D). Negotiating first, armed with specifics and options, is the correct opening.
