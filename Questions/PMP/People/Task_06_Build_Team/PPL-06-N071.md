# Question

A project manager is starting a predictive highway-bridge refurbishment. The WBS is complete, but staffing decisions keep stalling: nobody can yet say what level a role needs, whether a trade will come from the internal pool or a subcontractor, when each crew should roll on and off, or what site-safety certification they must hold first. Three functional managers are waiting on concrete requests, and the project manager wants the staffing approach settled before she makes them. What should the project manager produce first?

## Options

- **A**: A resource management plan that sets out how the project's roles will be estimated, sourced, certified, scheduled on and off, and released.
- **B**: A responsibility assignment matrix that names, for each work package, who is responsible and accountable.
- **C**: A resource calendar built from each candidate's quoted availability once the functional managers nominate names.
- **D**: A staffing risk register entry for each role that is hard to fill, so the gaps are tracked and monitored.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The staffing decisions are stalling because there is no agreed approach to sourcing, levelling, certifying, scheduling, and releasing roles — the management approach that the resource management plan establishes (A); settling it is what lets the project manager make concrete requests. A responsibility assignment matrix allocates accountability for work but assumes the roles and sourcing approach are already decided, so it does not resolve what is blocking the requests (B). A resource calendar captures availability, but it can only be built after names are nominated, which itself depends on the requests the plan enables — it comes later, not first (C). A risk register entry tracks hard-to-fill roles but only records the problem; it neither defines how roles are sourced and levelled nor unblocks the requests (D). The plan is the upstream artifact the other three depend on.
