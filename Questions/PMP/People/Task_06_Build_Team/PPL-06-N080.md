# Question

Midway through a hybrid programme building a university's online-enrolment system, three new developers join an established team to lift capacity. After two weeks the newcomers are hesitant in planning, rework items because they misread conventions, and rarely speak in retrospectives, while the original members move fast on shared assumptions the new members do not have. Delivery targets are still being met, but the gap is widening. Which TWO actions should the project manager prioritise to integrate the new members? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Pair each new developer with an experienced member for the next sprints so conventions, context, and tacit norms transfer through working together.
- **B**: Make the team's working agreements, definition of done, and key conventions explicit and accessible, and walk the newcomers through them.
- **C**: Run a structured onboarding programme of documentation, system walkthroughs, and a clear ramp-up plan the newcomers work through before taking on sprint stories.
- **D**: Assign an existing member as a dedicated point of contact the newcomers route every question to, so the rest of the team keeps its pace undisturbed.
- **E**: Give the newcomers a self-contained component to own end to end, so they build context through real delivery and gain a clear area of responsibility.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

The newcomers lack both the tacit knowledge and the explicit conventions the established team takes for granted, so integration means transferring both at once: pairing moves context and unwritten norms through shared work (A), while making the working agreements, definition of done, and conventions explicit removes the guesswork that is driving the rework (B). Together they close the gap and bring the new members in as full participants. A structured onboarding pack helps, but routing them through documentation before they touch sprint work delays the integration and keeps them outside the team during the very ceremonies — planning, retrospectives — where they are already struggling (C). A single point of contact is better than leaving them stranded, yet it funnels all learning through one person, protects the established members' pace at the newcomers' expense, and limits the breadth of context they pick up (D). Giving them an isolated component builds some context through delivery but walls them off from the team's shared work and norms, entrenching a two-tier split rather than merging the group (E). The first-order moves are pairing and making the implicit explicit (A, B); the rest are partial or isolating.
