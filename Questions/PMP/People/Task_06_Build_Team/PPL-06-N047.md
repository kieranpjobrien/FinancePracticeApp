# Question

The charter for a major systems-integration project names a specific lead architect as a condition of approval, because the sponsor secured her involvement when justifying the investment. As the project manager begins building out the rest of the team, the resource requirements analysis suggests the architecture work would now be better served by a different specialist mix than the one the named architect represents. How should the project manager proceed?

## Options

- **A**: Build the staffing plan around the named architect as a fixed constraint, and design the supporting roles to complement her so the team still covers the capability the analysis identified.
- **B**: Take the analysis to the sponsor and recommend replacing the named architect with the mix the work now suggests, since staffing should follow the requirements.
- **C**: Keep the named architect in the charter but quietly assign the substantive architecture decisions to the better-fitting specialist, leaving her in a nominal lead role.
- **D**: Negotiate for the named architect through her functional manager on the same terms as every other team member, treating her assignment as still open.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A resource named in the charter is pre-assigned: the commitment predates and conditions the project, so the team is built around that fixed point, and the right response to a capability gap is to shape the surrounding roles to close it (A). (B) reopens a sponsor-level commitment that underpins the project's approval; raising it is heavy-handed unless the architect is genuinely unable to perform, which is not the case here. (C) undermines a charter commitment covertly and sets up a damaging mismatch between title and authority. (D) treats a settled assignment as still negotiable, contradicting the charter that already fixed it.
