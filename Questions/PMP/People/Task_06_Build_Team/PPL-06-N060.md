# Question

On a hybrid delivery team, one architect holds nearly all working knowledge of a brittle legacy integration layer that no one else can safely change. His five-week parental leave is confirmed eight weeks out and overlaps a critical integration phase. He is fully supportive and has the bandwidth now to help however the project manager directs. The project manager has eight weeks and the architect's goodwill to invest. Which TWO actions should the project manager prioritise? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Have the architect pair daily with two capable engineers on the live integration work over the coming weeks, so they build genuine hands-on competence with the layer before he leaves.
- **B**: Have the architect run working sessions to document the integration design and decision rationale, walking the team through it and leaving a reference they can use and extend.
- **C**: Engage a contractor experienced in the legacy technology for the leave window, with the architect briefing them thoroughly beforehand so the critical phase is covered by a known pair of hands.
- **D**: Re-sequence the schedule with the team so the heaviest, highest-risk integration work is completed before the leave begins, while the architect is present to guide it.
- **E**: Have the architect record a full set of walkthrough videos and an FAQ covering the layer, so the team has an on-demand reference to consult while he is away.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

The knowledge here is tacit and the layer is unforgiving, so the two actions that build the most durable capability are those that transfer real competence into the team: daily pairing on live work develops hands-on skill that telling alone cannot (A), and documenting the design with a guided walkthrough leaves a shared, extensible reference the team owns (B). Together these combine doing and understanding, which is how deep know-how actually transfers. (D) is a genuinely strong mitigation and a close call, but front-loading the risky work only de-risks this one leave; it moves the work earlier without making anyone else able to maintain the layer, so the single point of failure persists for every future absence. (C) covers the phase but the contractor's knowledge departs with the contract, recreating the dependency rather than dissolving it. (E) produces a passive artifact: videos and an FAQ help at the margin but, unlike paired practice or a walked-through design the team extends, they rarely convey enough of the tacit reasoning to let the team safely change a brittle layer. The fine margin is active competence-building in the team (A, B) over schedule mitigation (D) and passive or external coverage (C, E).
