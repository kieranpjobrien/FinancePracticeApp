# Question

A project manager is choosing the final member for a small agile team expected to stay together for at least two years. Candidate X is an outstanding database specialist whose depth would directly help with a demanding performance problem the team expects this quarter, but X is known to work heads-down alone and to resist pairing or sharing tasks. Candidate Y is a strong all-rounder, a little less deep on databases, who readily pairs, mentors, and takes on whatever the sprint needs. The performance problem is real but is one of many challenges the team will face over its life. Which decision is best?

## Options

- **A**: Hire Candidate Y, accepting slightly less database depth, because an all-rounder who pairs and shares lifts the whole team's flow and resilience across the two years that outweigh one quarter's problem.
- **B**: Hire Candidate X, because the looming performance problem is the team's nearest concrete delivery risk and X's depth is the surest way to retire it.
- **C**: Hire Candidate Y now and bring in Candidate X on a short engagement later only if the performance problem proves beyond the team's depth.
- **D**: Hire whichever candidate scored higher across the structured interview, keeping the decision objective and consistent with how the other members were selected.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

For a small team that must function together for years, the dominant factor is each candidate's effect on the team's flow, knowledge-sharing, and resilience; the collaborative all-rounder strengthens all three continuously, whereas one quarter's performance problem is a single bounded challenge — so hiring Y is the soundest fit even at a small cost in raw database depth (A). (B) optimises for the nearest visible risk but plants a member who works in isolation into a small team, deepening the silo-and-flow problems that hurt such a team every sprint, not just this one. (C) reaches a defensible outcome yet treats fit as a contingency bet and assumes X stays available, when the durable team effect of the permanent hire is the larger consideration. (D) defers the judgement to an interview score that need not capture collaboration and team fit — the very dimension that matters most for an enduring small team. The fine margin is choosing for the team's two-year health versus the quarter's technical risk, an interview metric, or a later contingency.
