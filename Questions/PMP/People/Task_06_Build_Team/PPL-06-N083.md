# Question

A product owner, eager to accelerate a travel-booking platform, has grown the agile team from seven to fourteen over two months by adding developers. Throughput has fallen instead of rising: stand-ups run long, coordination overhead has ballooned, integration conflicts are frequent, and members say they spend more time aligning than building. The product owner wants the gains the extra headcount was meant to bring and is reluctant to give anyone up. What should the project manager recommend?

## Options

- **A**: Split the fourteen into two smaller cross-functional teams, each owning a coherent slice of the product, with a defined way to coordinate the seam between them.
- **B**: Keep the team whole but break the work into independent sub-streams so the integration conflicts ease while the group stays together under one backlog.
- **C**: Keep the fourteen together and appoint two sub-leads to own coordination and integration, adding structure without losing the combined capacity.
- **D**: Return the team to around seven by releasing the added developers, since the original size delivered without the overhead now being seen.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Communication paths rise combinatorially with size, and at fourteen the coordination cost has swallowed the extra capacity; splitting into two right-sized cross-functional teams, each owning a coherent slice, cuts the intra-team paths sharply while keeping all fourteen people productive — it satisfies the product owner's wish to retain the capacity and restores throughput (A). Carving the work into sub-streams inside one fourteen-person team relieves some integration clashes but leaves a single oversized team with one backlog and one stand-up, so the coordination load that grows with headcount largely remains (B). Sub-leads add a coordination layer on top of the oversized team without reducing the number of communication paths that caused the problem, managing the symptom rather than the structure (C). Shrinking back to seven would relieve the overhead but discards capacity the product owner wants to keep, when restructuring can preserve it — a fallback only if the work genuinely cannot be split (D). Forming appropriately sized teams addresses the structural source of the slowdown while keeping the people.
