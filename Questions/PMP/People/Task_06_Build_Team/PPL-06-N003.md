# Question

Midway through a hybrid logistics-optimisation programme, a skills assessment shows the team is strong in routing algorithms but weak in the real-time streaming architecture the next release demands. Onboarding a contractor takes about eight weeks; the next release-planning event is in three weeks. Two internal developers are keen to learn but have no streaming experience. A peer team in the same programme already holds streaming capability but is fully committed to its own release. What is the BEST course of action?

## Options

- **A**: Arrange focused streaming training and pairing for the two developers immediately, accepting a lighter first release while their capability ramps.
- **B**: Ask the programme manager to lend a streaming engineer from the peer team for the first release in exchange for routing help in return.
- **C**: Engage a streaming contractor now and have them coach the two internal developers from the day they arrive, so the gap is bridged while internal capability grows.
- **D**: Bring the streaming-dependent stories forward only after the two developers have completed training, replanning the release sequence around their readiness.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Every option keeps capability-building in view, so the discriminator is which best meets the near-term release *and* grows the team durably. Engaging a contractor with an explicit coaching brief covers the immediate need and transfers skill into the team at the same time (C). (A) builds capability but bets the release on three weeks of ramp for a demanding architecture, risking the commitment. (B) is attractive but the peer team is fully committed; pulling its specialist trades one team's release for another's and is not the PM's to grant — it depends on a negotiation that may not hold. (D) protects the developers' learning but reshapes the release around internal readiness, effectively deferring committed value; the contractor-plus-coaching route delivers and develops without that trade.
