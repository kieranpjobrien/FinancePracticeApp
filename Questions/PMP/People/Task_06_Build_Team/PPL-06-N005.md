# Question

An insurer is forming its first three Scrum teams from specialists who have always worked in functional silos (UI, QA, data), each team meant to deliver a vertical slice of a claims product every sprint. There are just enough specialists that each of the three teams can be given the full UI, QA, and data skill set to complete a slice without outside hand-offs — that constraint can be met whichever way you allocate people. Beyond that, the data skill is the thinnest, a few specialists are openly anxious about broadening, and the three product areas will draw on the disciplines in different proportions. With end-to-end coverage already assured for every team, what should most shape who goes where?

## Options

- **A**: Concentrate the strongest data specialists on the one team whose product area is most data-heavy, accepting thinner data cover on the other two for now.
- **B**: Place each anxious specialist alongside a confident peer in the same discipline, so they have a safety net as they start working across disciplines.
- **C**: Distribute the scarce data specialists evenly across the three teams so none is left a single deep person away from a data bottleneck.
- **D**: Weight each team's makeup to the discipline mix its product area is forecast to demand, so capacity roughly matches the work each will pull.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Every team can already complete a slice, so completeness no longer decides it; the live risk in a brand-new set of teams is fragility around the thinnest skill. (C) is best: spreading the scarce data specialists means no team depends on a single person whose absence or overload would stall its slice, which protects flow across all three while the teams are still forming their norms. (A) concentrating data depth on the most data-heavy team optimises one team's throughput but leaves the other two a single deep person from a stall, and early demand forecasts for a new product are too uncertain to bet team resilience on. (D) matching capacity to forecast discipline mix is sound capacity planning once teams are stable, but it relies on the same unproven forecast and can still leave a thin team exposed on the scarce skill. (B) supporting anxious members is humane and worth doing, but pairing within a discipline organises composition around individual comfort rather than the team's resilience and delivery, and broadening comes through doing the work in a robust team. With coverage assured, protecting against single-point fragility on the scarcest skill should drive the allocation.
