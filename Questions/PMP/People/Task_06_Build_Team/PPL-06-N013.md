# Question

A project manager faces four distinct staffing situations while building the team for a hybrid utilities programme. Match each situation to the most appropriate acquisition approach.

## Items

- **1**: A lead engineer is committed in the signed contract before planning begins.
- **2**: A rare skill is needed for one short phase and never again afterwards.
- **3**: Internal staff are willing but lack a capability the programme will draw on release after release.
- **4**: A common, well-supplied skill is needed in volume for a fixed window, with several equally suitable internal candidates.

## Matches

- **A**: Engage an external contractor for the duration of the need only.
- **B**: Treat as a pre-assigned resource and plan around the commitment.
- **C**: Invest in training and pairing to grow the capability in-house.
- **D**: Negotiate with functional managers to acquire from the internal resource pool.

## Answer

1-B, 2-A, 3-C, 4-D

## Explanation

A resource committed in the contract before the project starts is pre-assigned, and the schedule plans around it (1-B). A short, non-recurring specialist need is met most economically by a contractor rather than hiring or training (2-A). A capability the programme will use repeatedly, with willing staff, justifies building it in-house so it is retained (3-C). A plentiful, common skill needed in volume with suitable internal candidates is best sourced by negotiating with functional managers (4-D). Swapping any pair fails: contracting the committed lead ignores the agreement, and training for a one-off short need wastes effort the programme never reuses.
