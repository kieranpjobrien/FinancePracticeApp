# Question

A predictive product-development team has grown from four to nine people over two months. Twice in the last fortnight, two members independently produced the same deliverable while another deliverable went unowned and was missed, causing rework. The work itself is well understood; the breakdowns happen at the edges, where it is unclear who carries a given deliverable to completion. The project manager wants a fix that keeps holding as the team continues to grow. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Work through the deliverables with the team and agree, for each, a single owner and how supporting roles relate to that owner, then keep the mapping visible and update it as the team grows.
- **B**: Restructure the nine into two or three small sub-teams, each owning a clear slice of the deliverables end to end, so ownership follows the team boundary.
- **C**: Expand the definition of done so every deliverable explicitly records its owner before work starts, embedding ownership in the team's existing workflow.
- **D**: Hold a brief daily ownership check at stand-up where the team confirms who is taking each new deliverable, catching gaps and overlaps as they arise.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The failures are unclear single-point ownership across many deliverables on a growing team; agreeing one accountable owner per deliverable with the team and keeping that mapping live scales directly with growth and closes both the duplication and the orphaned-work gaps at their source (A). (B) can work but reorganising into sub-teams is a heavier structural change that creates new hand-off seams between the slices and may fracture a team still forming, solving ownership by partitioning the team rather than clarifying it. (C) is a sensible reinforcement and complements A, but a definition-of-done field records ownership case by case without giving the whole-team view of who owns what, so cross-deliverable gaps can still slip through. (D) surfaces gaps quickly yet relies on a daily verbal catch that grows fragile as deliverables multiply, treating each recurrence rather than establishing durable ownership. The fine margin is a persistent, team-owned ownership map versus a structural split, a per-item workflow field, or a daily verbal check.
