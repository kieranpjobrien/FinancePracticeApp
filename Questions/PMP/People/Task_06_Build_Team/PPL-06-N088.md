# Question

A project manager has just been given the eight people allocated to a hybrid retail point-of-sale upgrade. She received their job titles and seniority bands from the resource manager but has not worked with most of them. Sprint planning and the first predictive work packages both start next week, and she must decide how to distribute the work. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Assess each person's actual competencies against what the planned work packages and backlog require, and identify where the team falls short.
- **B**: Allocate the work packages now using the seniority bands and titles she was given, since the schedule starts next week and the bands indicate capability.
- **C**: Facilitate the team to co-create a working agreement so they decide together how work will be picked up and shared.
- **D**: Hold one-on-ones with each person to understand their career goals and the kind of work they would like to take on.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

She is staffing against titles she cannot yet trust, so the first move is to establish what the team can actually do relative to the specific work and where the gaps are — a skills assessment and gap analysis (A) — which is what lets her distribute work soundly and target any development. Allocating straight from seniority bands treats a title as a proxy for capability, which is exactly the unknown she has, and risks assigning work to people who cannot do it (B). Co-creating a working agreement is valuable for how the team operates, but it sets norms rather than revealing whether the needed competencies are present, so it does not answer the distribution question she faces (C). Career-goal conversations inform motivation and growth but capture aspiration, not current capability against the work, so they cannot ground the allocation either (D). Knowing the team's real capability against the planned work has to come before the work is parcelled out.
