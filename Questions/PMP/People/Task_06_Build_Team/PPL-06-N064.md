# Question

A predictive rail-signalling upgrade relies on a specialist subcontractor whose two engineers work alongside the internal team for the entire delivery phase. The subcontract is signed, the engineers are technically excellent, and the interface specifications between their scope and the internal team's were baselined at the start. Integration defects are nonetheless rising specifically at the boundary between the two groups' work, the schedule has little float, and each group plans and tracks its work in its own cadence and tools, surfacing problems to the other only at scheduled integration points. The subcontractor's contract scopes deliverables and milestones, not day-to-day participation in the internal team's events. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Work with the subcontractor to fold its engineers into the internal team's shared planning, tracking, and team norms for the joint work, adjusting the engagement so the two groups plan and surface issues as one team.
- **B**: Establish a joint integration working session each iteration where both groups walk their interface contracts together and resolve emerging boundary issues before the next handoff.
- **C**: Add a dedicated, jointly owned integration test stage at the boundary so defects are caught and corrected before they propagate into downstream work.
- **D**: Reconfirm and tighten the baselined interface specifications, then hold each group to its side of the agreed contract through the existing milestone reviews.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The defects cluster at the boundary because two capable groups plan and surface problems on separate cadences and only meet at fixed integration points, so issues are discovered late by design; the durable fix is to make them plan and work as one team for the joint scope, which surfaces and prevents interface problems in the normal flow rather than at a wall (A). This is the strongest answer even though the contract does not mandate participation, because integration of the people is something the project manager arranges by agreement, not something the milestone-based contract prevents. (B) is a real, useful collaboration mechanism, but a periodic joint session still bolts coordination onto two separate teams and keeps problem discovery tied to a scheduled point rather than continuous shared planning. (C) catches more defects before they spread and is good engineering, yet inspection finds problems after they are created instead of removing the cause, and the boundary that generates them remains. (D) is sound predictive interface management, but the specifications are already baselined and defects are still rising, so the seam is not primarily an ambiguity problem — tighter specs and milestone enforcement manage the boundary rather than dissolving the divided way of working that produces it.
