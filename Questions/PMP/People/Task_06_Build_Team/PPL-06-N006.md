# Question

In a weak matrix organisation, the project manager has estimated that a pharmaceutical packaging-line validation needs two experienced validation engineers for four months. The functional manager offers two junior engineers available now, or one senior engineer free in six weeks. The validation path has limited float. What should the project manager do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Accept the two junior engineers now and begin protocol training immediately, since starting on time preserves the most float on the constrained path.
- **B**: Model each offer's effect on the validation path and the protocol skills the work requires, then negotiate with the functional manager from that comparison.
- **C**: Accept the single senior engineer in six weeks and replan the validation path to absorb the delay, since experience reduces protocol rework risk.
- **D**: Propose a blend to the functional manager — one junior now to begin preparatory work, with the senior engineer joining at six weeks for the protocol-critical steps.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Each of A, C, and D is a credible staffing decision, which is exactly why the PM should not commit to any of them before understanding their consequences. Modelling both offers against the constrained path and the required competencies, then negotiating from that analysis, is the disciplined first step (B). (A) optimises for an early start but may load critical protocol work onto under-experienced engineers, risking rework that costs more float than it saved. (C) buys experience at the price of a six-week delay the path may not tolerate. (D) is a thoughtful compromise and could well be the outcome, but proposing a specific blend before quantifying its schedule impact is deciding ahead of the analysis; the negotiation should be informed by the model, not precede it.
