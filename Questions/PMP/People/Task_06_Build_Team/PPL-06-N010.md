# Question

A cost engineer joins a predictive shipyard project three months in to fill a gap found during a skills review. The role is needed urgently, the team is established and busy, and the project manager wants the engineer contributing soon without destabilising the group. What should the project manager do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Orient the engineer to the project's objectives, processes, and tools and their specific role, and pair them with a team buddy for context.
- **B**: Schedule the engineer to shadow the existing cost lead on live tasks for the first fortnight so they learn the project's conventions by doing.
- **C**: Give the engineer a small, well-bounded current deliverable straight away, with the buddy on hand, so they contribute while learning the context around it.
- **D**: Walk the engineer through the cost baseline and the open cost issues first, since that is the work they were hired to take on.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Each option is a plausible piece of integration, but the *first* need when adding someone to an established team is to give them the shared context that makes everything else land: the project's goals, processes, tools, their role, and a person to turn to. That foundation is what onboarding provides (A). (B) is valuable but shadowing for two weeks delays contribution the urgent gap cannot afford, and it assumes orientation has already happened. (C) gets to value quickly, yet handing over even a small deliverable before the engineer understands the project's objectives and processes invites avoidable missteps; it is a strong second step once oriented. (D) jumps straight into the cost detail without situating the engineer in how the project runs, so they may apply the right technique in the wrong context.
