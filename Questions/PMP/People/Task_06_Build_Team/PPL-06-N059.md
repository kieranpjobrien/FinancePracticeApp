# Question

A mature, high-performing Scrum team adds a seventh developer next sprint to handle a growing backlog. The new developer is skilled but does not know the codebase or the team. The product owner is anxious that velocity not drop while the newcomer ramps up, and the next sprint's backlog is already full of high-priority stories. Sprint planning is tomorrow. As Scrum Master, what is the best way to bring the new member in?

## Options

- **A**: Put the newcomer's integration on tomorrow's agenda for the team to decide how to absorb them, and have the team adjust its own sprint commitment to fund whatever ramp approach it chooses.
- **B**: Pair the newcomer with the team's strongest developer for the first two sprints, accepting that the strongest developer's own output dips while context transfers fast.
- **C**: Have the newcomer take small, low-risk backlog items with a rotating reviewer, so they contribute from day one while learning the conventions through real work.
- **D**: Keep the sprint commitment as planned and let the newcomer absorb context informally across the team, revisiting the arrangement at the retrospective if velocity suffers.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

This team is mature and self-organising and is about to commit a full sprint, so the integration choice and its cost belong to the team at the planning event where the trade-off is actually made — letting it pick the ramp mechanism and adjust its own commitment to fund it keeps both ownership and a realistic plan intact (A). (B) is a strong, specific mechanism, but the Scrum Master pre-selecting it removes a decision this team is equipped to make and silently spends the top developer's capacity without the team weighing that against its commitment. (C) gets output sooner yet asynchronous review transfers tacit conventions slowly and loads reviewers invisibly, and again imposes one approach from outside. (D) protects the planned scope but defers integration to chance and only reacts after velocity has already dropped, which is what the product owner most fears. The fine margin is having the mature team own and resource its own integration at planning versus the Scrum Master imposing a single mechanism or letting the ramp happen by default.
