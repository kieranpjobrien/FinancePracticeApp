# Question

A product owner wants to staff a flagship agile team with fourteen developers to move fast on a crowded roadmap. The project manager, acting as Scrum Master, is concerned the team is too large to self-organise. Backlog refinement shows the work could be split into two areas, but the two share a core data model and would need to coordinate constantly. What should the project manager advise on team structure?

## Options

- **A**: Split into two appropriately sized teams along the two areas, and establish a lightweight mechanism for the cross-team coordination the shared data model requires.
- **B**: Keep one fourteen-person team for now because the heavy interdependence makes a split costly, but actively reduce the coupling in the data model so a clean split becomes possible later.
- **C**: Form the single fourteen-person team and appoint two sub-leads to coordinate the developers within it.
- **D**: Split into two teams and assign the shared data model to one of them as its owner, with the other consuming it as an internal service.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Two right-sized teams self-organise far better than one oversized group, and cross-team dependencies are managed with deliberate coordination rather than avoided by keeping everyone in one team (A). (B) correctly weighs the coupling but defaults to an unwieldy fourteen-person team while it waits for decoupling that may take many sprints, paying the self-organisation penalty throughout. (C) preserves the too-large team and bolts on a coordination layer, which is the very signal that the team cannot self-organise at that size. (D) is a plausible split, but unilaterally handing the shared model to one team can starve the other and create a queue at exactly the interface that needs the most fluid collaboration.
