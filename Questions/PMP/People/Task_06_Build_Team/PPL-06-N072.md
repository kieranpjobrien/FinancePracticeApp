# Question

A project manager is forming a hybrid team to deliver a hospital's new patient-flow system: an agile front-end build alongside a predictive integration with the legacy electronic health record. He has secured eight people. Two have deep EHR integration experience but have never worked in iterations; four are strong agile developers with no healthcare-domain knowledge; two are clinical analysts new to software delivery. The programme starts in three weeks and the team must deliver both halves. Which TWO actions should the project manager prioritise first? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Assess each person's competencies against what the backlog and integration work actually require, then plan targeted pairing and knowledge-sharing to close the specific gaps that surfaces.
- **B**: Work with the team to agree how the agile cadence and the predictive integration milestones will interface, so the two workstreams are reconciled before building starts.
- **C**: Stream the team by strength — EHR specialists on the integration, agile developers on the front end, analysts advising — and cross-skill later once both halves are delivering steadily.
- **D**: Front-load an intensive healthcare-domain induction for the four agile developers in the three weeks before launch so the whole team shares the clinical context from day one.
- **E**: Bring in an experienced hybrid delivery lead on secondment to run the agile-predictive coordination, freeing the project manager to focus on staffing the gaps.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

The team holds complementary strengths and real gaps, so building it starts with seeing the gaps precisely and designing how a mixed agile-and-predictive team will work as one. Assessing competencies against the actual work pinpoints exactly where pairing and knowledge transfer are needed rather than guessing (A), and agreeing how the iteration cadence meets the integration milestones is the interface a hybrid team must settle early to function as a unit (B). Streaming strictly by strength gets work moving but hard-codes silos and defers the cross-skilling that resilience needs; "cross-skill later" tends not to happen once delivery pressure builds, so it trades the team's integration for short-term comfort (C). A domain induction has value, yet cramming deep clinical knowledge into four developers in three weeks is unrealistic and would consume the runway better spent on a targeted assessment that shows how much context each role actually needs (D). A seconded coordination lead may help, but outsourcing the agile-predictive interface to a newcomer skips the team agreeing its own ways of working and adds an unfamiliar dependency right at launch (E). The first-order moves are locating the gaps and reconciling the workstreams (A, B); the others are plausible later steps or over-reach.
