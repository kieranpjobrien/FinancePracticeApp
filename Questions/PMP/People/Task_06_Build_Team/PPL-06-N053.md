# Question

A programme runs several short agile initiatives back to back, each needing a different blend of skills, drawing from a talent pool that two other programmes also depend on and cannot relinquish. Today each team is fully disbanded at initiative end and a fresh one assembled for the next, and the project manager observes that every new team burns its first two sprints re-establishing norms and re-learning the codebase before becoming productive. A standing claim on the whole pool is not available. What should the project manager recommend to cut the recurring ramp without breaking the shared-pool commitment?

## Options

- **A**: Hold a stable core of three or four members who carry the norms and codebase knowledge across every initiative, and flex the remaining seats from the pool as each initiative's skills demand.
- **B**: Keep one persistent, intact team that takes every initiative in turn, bringing each new piece of work to the same team rather than rebuilding for each.
- **C**: Standardise a thorough onboarding pack and shared engineering playbook so each newly assembled team reaches productivity faster.
- **D**: Pre-assemble the next initiative's team one sprint early so its norming and code familiarisation overlap the current initiative instead of delaying the next.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The shared pool cannot be locked to one team, so a single intact team for everything is not actually on offer; the realistic way to preserve the trust and domain knowledge that the two-sprint ramp keeps destroying is a stable core that persists across initiatives while the variable skills flex in from the pool (A). (B) would eliminate the ramp most completely, but it requires a standing claim on people the other programmes depend on, which the situation rules out — it solves the problem by ignoring the constraint. (C) speeds onboarding at the margin, yet a full re-forming cost is still paid every cycle no matter how good the pack, because norms and trust are not transferred by documents. (D) hides the ramp behind an overlap rather than removing it and pulls scarce pooled people early, straining the very programmes the constraint protects. The fine margin is preserving continuity through a persistent core within the shared-pool limit versus an option that needs the limit waived, a documentation fix, or a scheduling disguise.
