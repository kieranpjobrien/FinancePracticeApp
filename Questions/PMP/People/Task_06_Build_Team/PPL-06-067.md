# Question

In the last six months, your team has onboarded five new members. Retrospectives show that ramp-up time is long, newcomers feel thrown in, and onboarding artefacts are inconsistent. Two more people are joining next month. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Design a lightweight team-owned onboarding playbook covering buddy pairing, a ninety-day roadmap, required reading, and check-ins, and pilot it with the two new joiners next month.
- **B**: Continue the current ad-hoc onboarding for the two new joiners and plan a full onboarding redesign for later in the year when the project pressure has eased somewhat.
- **C**: Send new joiners exclusively to a generic corporate induction run by HR on the assumption that a consistent organisational programme will solve the team-specific ramp-up issue.
- **D**: Push back on the two new joiners by asking the sponsor to defer the hires for six months until the team has consolidated its existing members more fully.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

PMI responds to a retrospective signal with a concrete, team-owned improvement that is piloted with the next real case. (A) is exactly that. (B) defers a known issue with the fix conveniently dated after the next occurrence. (C) substitutes a general induction for the team-specific ramp-up that is actually slow. (D) blocks legitimate resourcing to avoid fixing the onboarding process.
