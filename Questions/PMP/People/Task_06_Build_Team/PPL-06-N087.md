# Question

A project manager has secured the eight people she requested for a predictive offshore-wind survey, but every one of them carries a different working pattern — part-time fractions, a booked holiday, an alternate-week share with another project, and a specialist released only for a fixed window. Her draft schedule keeps assigning tasks to people in weeks they cannot work, so durations slip the moment she levels the plan. She needs to fix the planning inputs the scheduling tool is using. What should the project manager develop?

## Options

- **A**: A resource calendar capturing each person's real working time and non-working periods across the project.
- **B**: A resource histogram of total staff assigned to the project across the timeline, to expose over- and under-loaded periods.
- **C**: Resource-levelled estimates that stretch task durations to fit the reduced part-time capacity available.
- **D**: An assumption log recording, for planning purposes, that the team is treated as fully available unless flagged otherwise.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The schedule is wrong because the tool is calculating against availability it does not have; the input that supplies each person's true working and non-working time is the resource calendar (A), and once it is in place the holidays, fractions, and fixed windows stop being violated. A resource histogram shows aggregate loading over time and helps spot peaks, but it works at the headcount level and never encodes the individual constraints — the holiday, the alternate weeks — that are actually breaking the plan (B). Resource levelling is a response applied after the constraints are known; without the calendar feeding it the real availability, levelling has nothing accurate to level against and would still slip (C). An assumption log that treats everyone as fully available enshrines the very error causing the problem rather than correcting it (D). The per-person calendar is the missing planning input the schedule depends on.
