# Question

Eight engineers pulled from four product squads have run as one Scrum team for three sprints on an e-commerce replatform. Velocity is stable, but the team's own retrospectives have repeatedly surfaced two gaps it agrees are real: members hold different definitions of done, and there is no agreed rule for who decides when the product owner is away. Tomorrow's sprint planning already has a full backlog of high-priority stories the business is expecting this sprint, and the product owner will be on leave for most of it. Two seniors have started routing work around each other. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: At tomorrow's planning, have the team agree its definition of done and a stand-in decision rule for the product owner's absence before it commits to sprint scope, trimming the committed stories to fit the time this takes.
- **B**: Let the team commit to the planned backlog as scheduled, and convene a separate working session within the sprint dedicated to settling the definition of done and the decision rule.
- **C**: Ask the product owner, before going on leave, to set the definition of done and name a delegate, then have the team adopt both at planning so the sprint can start fully loaded.
- **D**: Bring the two seniors together first to rebuild their working relationship, since their work-arounds are the immediate threat to this sprint's delivery, then let the team settle the broader gaps at the next retrospective.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The team has already diagnosed the gaps itself and is about to commit a full sprint, partly without its product owner, on unshared assumptions — so the root ambiguity should be closed at the very event where it would otherwise do the damage, even at the cost of committing fewer stories (A). The genuine tension is scope-now versus norms-now, and A is correct because committing more work on an undefined definition of done and no decision rule is what has been generating the rework and work-arounds. (B) protects this sprint's scope and still schedules the fix, but it lets the team commit yet again on the same unshared assumptions and during the absence that most needs a decision rule, so the gap shapes one more sprint before it closes. (C) gets the artefacts written quickly, but a self-organising team owns its own definition of done; a product-owner-authored version adopted wholesale reproduces the thin buy-in that caused the work-arounds, and a named delegate is not the team-agreed rule it actually lacks. (D) repairs the loudest symptom, yet the seniors are routing around each other precisely because the decision rule is missing — settle the rule and the symptom loses its cause, whereas a relationship patch leaves the whole team committing while still unaligned.
