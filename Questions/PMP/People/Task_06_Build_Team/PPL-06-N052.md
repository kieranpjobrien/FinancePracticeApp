# Question

In a weak matrix, an agile team's six developers each still report to functional managers and are allocated 60% to the project. In practice the managers keep pulling them onto operational tickets, so sprint commitments are routinely missed and the team never feels whole. The project manager has already taken this to the sponsor, who confirms the 60% split must stand: the operational systems cannot run without these people, and full-time release is not on offer. The functional managers are willing to discuss specifics. Working within the fixed 60%, what should the project manager do to build a team that can deliver predictably?

## Options

- **A**: Negotiate protected blocks of contiguous project time within each person's 60% and an agreed rule for what may interrupt them, so the team has stretches where it can work as a whole.
- **B**: Plan each sprint to the capacity that has actually been showing up rather than the nominal 60%, so commitments become reliable and the team rebuilds trust with stakeholders.
- **C**: Keep stories small and have the team swarm whoever is present each day, so flow continues and no item stalls when a member is pulled away.
- **D**: Ask the managers to name one developer who can be fully protected as a stable core, accepting reduced project time from the other five in exchange.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The sponsor has fixed the 60%, so the lever is no longer total exclusivity but how that time is shaped: securing protected, contiguous blocks plus an interruption rule gives the team uninterrupted stretches to actually function as a team while still honouring the operational need (A). (B) makes commitments honest and is a reasonable safeguard, but planning to whatever fragments show up institutionalises the fragmentation rather than reducing it, and a team scheduled around constant interruption still never gels. (C) is a sound coping tactic that keeps work flowing, yet swarming around churn manages the symptom and leaves the team permanently reactive instead of building rhythm. (D) buys one protected person at the cost of further diluting the other five, shrinking the team that can do whole-team work rather than improving how the six operate together. The fine margin is reshaping the available time into usable blocks versus accepting, smoothing, or concentrating the fragmentation.
