# Question

An agile release train has six teams and several inexperienced scrum masters. The RTE (release train engineer) wants to raise mentoring capability across the train without becoming the single coach everyone depends on. Which actions best support a scalable, sustainable mentoring approach? (Select three.)

## Options

- **A**: Establish a scrum master community of practice where experienced practitioners coach newer ones and share patterns that work.
- **B**: Pair each inexperienced scrum master with a seasoned one for a defined period of peer mentoring with agreed goals.
- **C**: Encourage scrum masters to set their own development goals and reflect on them in regular retrospectives of their own practice.
- **D**: Personally run a weekly clinic where every scrum master brings their hardest current problem to the RTE for individual coaching.
- **E**: Commission an intensive external training course for all the scrum masters and treat completion as the development milestone.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Scalable mentoring distributes capability and builds the practitioners' own judgement: a community of practice (A), structured peer pairing (B), and self-directed goals reinforced through reflection (C) grow mentoring across the train without one person at the centre. (D) is genuinely valuable and well-intentioned, but routing every hard problem through the RTE re-creates the bottleneck and dependence the question asks to avoid, so it is not a *scalable* answer. (E) can lift baseline knowledge, but a one-off course is an event, not an ongoing mentoring relationship, and treating completion as the milestone mistakes attendance for developed capability. The trap in D and E is that both help — they are simply the wrong fit for sustaining distributed mentoring.
