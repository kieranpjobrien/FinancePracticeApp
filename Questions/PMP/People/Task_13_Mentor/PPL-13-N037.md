# Question

A PM sees that a quiet but highly skilled senior engineer could help several junior engineers who are struggling with the codebase, but the senior engineer is reluctant, saying they "aren't a teacher" and would rather just do the work. The PM wants to draw the senior engineer into a mentoring role in a way that is likely to stick. What is the best approach?

## Options

- **A**: Make mentoring the junior engineers a formal objective in the senior engineer's goals so the expectation is clear and recognised.
- **B**: Explore what sits behind the reluctance, then start small — asking the senior engineer to talk one junior through a single problem they have already solved — and build from a low-pressure success.
- **C**: Set up regular pair-programming between the senior engineer and the juniors on real tasks, so knowledge transfers through the work without the senior engineer having to "teach".
- **D**: Ask the senior engineer to record short walkthroughs of the trickiest parts of the codebase that the juniors can work through, sharing expertise without a live teaching role.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The barrier the senior engineer named is a belief — "I'm not a teacher" — so the approach most likely to stick first understands that hesitation and then disconfirms it through a small, concrete success that reframes what mentoring feels like (B). (A) imposes an obligation on someone not yet convinced, which tends to produce grudging, low-quality mentoring and can harden the resistance. (C) pair-programming is a genuinely good transfer mechanism and lowers the "teaching" framing, but on its own it sidesteps rather than addresses the reluctance, and without any relational framing the development is left to chance. (D) shares expertise usefully and respects the engineer's preference, yet recordings are one-way and build none of the relationship or responsiveness that mentoring provides. The margin is fine because C and D both transfer knowledge; B is best because it converts the engineer into a willing mentor rather than working around them.
