# Question

A PM is mentoring a newly appointed scrum master at an insurance firm. After three iterations the scrum master runs tidy ceremonies that finish on time, but team members raise their real concerns to the PM in corridors rather than in the retrospective. The scrum master is well-prepared and the team likes them personally. The PM sits in on a retrospective and sees the scrum master move briskly through a fixed agenda, summarising each point and proposing the action themselves. What should the PM focus the next mentoring conversation on?

## Options

- **A**: How to use silence and open questions so the team surfaces and owns the actions, rather than the scrum master supplying them.
- **B**: How to enforce a working agreement that issues must be raised in the retrospective and not in side conversations.
- **C**: How to vary the retrospective format each iteration to keep the team engaged and willing to contribute.
- **D**: How to timebox each agenda item so discussion has room to breathe before moving on.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The symptom is that the team withholds in the room and the PM observed the scrum master answering their own prompts and owning the actions, which signals a facilitation stance that closes the space rather than opens it (A). (B) treats a symptom by mandate and is likely to drive concerns further underground rather than build trust. (C) assumes a novelty problem, but the team is engaged enough to raise issues elsewhere, so format is not the gap. (D) addresses pacing, yet the retrospectives already finish on time and the real issue is that the scrum master fills the space rather than that there is too little of it. Coaching the facilitation stance — silence, open questions, team-owned actions — is what shifts the dynamic the PM saw.
