# Question

A senior PM is mentoring a newly promoted PM on a hospital construction project. The mentee has begun forwarding routine clinical-equipment queries to the senior PM and asking, "What would you do here?" for decisions clearly within the mentee's authority and competence. The senior PM wants to keep helping without the mentee becoming reliant. What is the best response over the next few weeks?

## Options

- **A**: Continue answering promptly so the mentee feels supported, and raise the reliance pattern only if it persists past the project's busy opening phase.
- **B**: When the mentee asks "what would you do", turn it back with "what are you leaning toward and why" before adding anything, so the mentee reasons first and the PM responds to their thinking.
- **C**: Agree a rule that the mentee brings only decisions above an agreed threshold of cost or clinical risk, and handles everything below it independently.
- **D**: Tell the mentee directly that the queries being forwarded are within their authority and they should stop escalating routine ones.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The mentee is capable but defaulting to the mentor, so the aim is to shift them from seeking answers to owning their reasoning while support stays available. (B) does this in the moment of each request: requiring the mentee to state their lean and rationale first builds independent judgement and lets the PM coach the reasoning rather than supply the answer, gradually reducing the dependency. (C) sets a sensible boundary but a fixed threshold can feel like a brush-off and does not develop the mentee's confidence to decide — it just rations access. (D) is accurate and may be needed eventually, but a flat instruction can read as rejection and skips the chance to strengthen the mentee's reasoning. (A) preserves rapport yet lets the reliance harden during exactly the period it is forming, making it harder to unwind later.
