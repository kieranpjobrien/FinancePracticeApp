# Question

A senior project manager is mentoring a junior PM who runs their first project with executive stakeholders in two weeks. This steering committee is known to be demanding, the project is politically visible, and the junior PM has never presented to executives. The junior PM is confident and keen to lead the session. What should the senior PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Lead the first session jointly, presenting the high-stakes status items themselves and handing the junior PM the lower-risk parts, with a plan to step back at the next meeting.
- **B**: Work through the junior PM's intended messages and likely executive questions, have them rehearse, and let them run the whole meeting while the PM attends only as backup.
- **C**: Brief the junior PM thoroughly beforehand and let them run it solo without the senior PM in the room, so the executives deal with one clear owner and the junior PM owns the result.
- **D**: Have the junior PM lead, but agree in advance that the senior PM will step in and take over any item where the committee pushes back hard.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Effective mentoring prepares the mentee fully and then lets them perform, with the mentor present as a safety net rather than a participant who absorbs the hard parts. (A) protects the meeting but reserves exactly the high-stakes moments the junior PM most needs to attempt, diluting the experience. (C) builds ownership but removes the support a first executive exposure warrants when the forum is demanding and visible, raising the risk of an avoidable poor first impression. (D) looks supportive but the pre-agreed takeover undermines the junior PM's authority in the room the moment it is most tested, teaching the executives to look past them; backup should catch a failure, not pre-empt the stretch.
