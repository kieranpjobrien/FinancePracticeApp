# Question

On an agile team, a developer the PM is mentoring took an initiative the PM had encouraged — automating part of the release process — and it failed publicly, causing a deployment to roll back during a sprint review. The developer is dejected and says they should not have tried. The PM wants to protect the developer's willingness to take initiative. What is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: Reassure the developer that the failure was not their fault and that the PM, having encouraged the initiative, shares responsibility for how it turned out.
- **B**: Reframe the episode as a normal part of trying something new, and work through with the developer what can be learned and what they would do differently next time.
- **C**: Quickly help the developer plan a follow-up attempt that succeeds, so the setback is overtaken by a visible win before confidence erodes.
- **D**: Acknowledge the disappointment and advise the developer to pick lower-risk initiatives in future so the team's confidence in them is not dented.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The goal is to keep the developer willing to take initiative, which depends on framing failure as learning rather than something to avoid. (B) normalises the setback, removes the shame attached to it, and extracts the lesson, which both preserves the willingness to try and grows the developer's capability — the essence of mentoring through failure. (A) is kind and the shared-responsibility point is fair, but absolving the developer entirely can rob them of the agency and the learning that come from owning what happened. (C) rushes to a win and may rebuild confidence, yet papering over the failure without processing it teaches that setbacks must be hidden, not learned from. (D) protects against repeat embarrassment but counsels playing safe, directly undermining the initiative-taking the PM is trying to nurture. Treating failure as fuel for learning is what keeps the developer experimenting.
