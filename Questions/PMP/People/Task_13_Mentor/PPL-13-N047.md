# Question

A project manager finds that a recently appointed executive sponsor approves whatever the PM recommends, defers decisions, and rarely champions the project to peers. The PM judges the project would benefit if the sponsor were more active in the role. How should the PM approach this?

## Options

- **A**: Raise the gap with the sponsor's own superior or the PMO, asking them to clarify the sponsor's responsibilities, since shaping a sponsor is properly their remit rather than the PM's.
- **B**: Frame support around the project's needs, bringing the sponsor the specific decisions only they can make and the moments where their advocacy would unblock progress.
- **C**: Send the sponsor a concise guide to the sponsor role and offer to walk through it before the next steering committee.
- **D**: Quietly carry the decisions the sponsor defers and brief peers yourself, accepting that this sponsor may simply be hands-off and keeping the project moving.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Mentoring can flow upward when it is framed as helping a stakeholder fulfil their role in service of shared outcomes, which makes the support welcome and develops the sponsor through real decisions and advocacy. (A) is a defensible governance route but is slower, risks undermining the sponsor, and skips the direct, low-friction help the PM can offer first. (C) gives the sponsor something concrete and is a reasonable adjunct, yet a document rarely changes a busy executive's behaviour the way being engaged on live, role-specific decisions does. (D) keeps delivery moving but absorbs decisions and authority that are not the PM's, weakening governance instead of building the sponsor's capability.
