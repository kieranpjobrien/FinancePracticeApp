# Question

A PM mentors a product owner on a SaaS team who writes backlog items as feature descriptions without acceptance criteria, so developers pull work and then lose half a day mid-sprint clarifying it. The PM explained the value of acceptance criteria a fortnight ago and the product owner agreed, but the items still arrive thin. The product owner is willing and not short of examples of good criteria. What should the PM do next?

## Options

- **A**: Draft the next backlog item together at the product owner's desk, drawing out from them how the team would know it is done, then have them write the following items and review the first batch.
- **B**: Rewrite the next several items with full acceptance criteria as worked examples and ask the product owner to model their own on them.
- **C**: Ask the product owner to sit in on the next refinement session purely to watch where the developers get stuck on their items, then debrief what they saw.
- **D**: Agree a simple definition-of-ready checklist with the product owner that items must meet before they enter a sprint.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Telling the product owner about acceptance criteria already worked at the level of agreement but not behaviour, and they do not lack examples — so another exemplar or rule is unlikely to be what shifts them. The missing piece is doing it themselves with support, so co-authoring an item while drawing out their own "done" thinking, then having them continue, transfers the technique (A). (B) supplies more examples when examples are not the gap, and copying form without the reasoning is what produced thin criteria in the first place. (C) builds awareness of the problem the product owner already knows exists, so it diagnoses rather than develops the skill. (D) gives a useful artefact, but a checklist only helps once the product owner can actually write the criteria it asks for — it formalises the standard without building the capability to meet it. All four are reasonable; co-authoring is the one that closes the demonstrated gap.
