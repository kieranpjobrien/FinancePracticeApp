# Question

During a mentoring session, a Scrum Master tells the project manager they have decided to drop the team's retrospective for the next two sprints because the team finds it repetitive and a delivery deadline is tight. The decision sits squarely within the Scrum Master's accountability. What is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: Support the call and let it run, since the choice is theirs and owning the consequences is how the Scrum Master will learn what works.
- **B**: Tell the Scrum Master to keep the retrospectives, explaining that the inspect-and-adapt event exists precisely for teams that find delivery pressure mounting.
- **C**: Ask what dropping the retrospective is meant to solve and what the team might lose, so the Scrum Master weighs the trade-off before they commit.
- **D**: Suggest the Scrum Master replace the two retrospectives with a single lighter improvement check, as a compromise between the team's fatigue and the value of inspecting.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

A mentor challenges thinking without seizing the decision, surfacing consequences the mentee may have missed while leaving the call where it belongs. (A) respects autonomy but a mentor who stays silent on a foreseeable problem is withholding the very value they are there to add. (B) may protect this sprint but substitutes the mentor's judgement and a rule for the Scrum Master's reasoning, building nothing. (D) is a sensible-sounding fix, yet by supplying the answer the PM again skips developing the Scrum Master's own analysis of the trade-off, which is the lasting gain.
