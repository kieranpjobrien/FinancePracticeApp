# Question

A PM in a manufacturing programme is mentoring a process engineer who has just led their first design review. To finish inside the booked slot, the engineer waved past two unresolved supplier concerns rather than working them through. The engineer leaves the room visibly pleased it went smoothly. The PM has a short debrief with them now and a follow-up session booked next week. How should the PM open the debrief?

## Options

- **A**: Ask the engineer how they felt the review went and what, looking back, they might handle differently next time.
- **B**: Note specifically that the two supplier concerns were passed over to hit the time, and ask the engineer to walk through how that decision was made.
- **C**: Acknowledge what the engineer did well in their first review, then introduce the supplier concerns once that footing is set.
- **D**: Ask the engineer how they decided what to cover versus defer when the clock was running, so the trade-off they made surfaces in their own words.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

This turns on a fine margin: the gap is not general — it is a specific judgement the engineer made under time pressure, and they do not yet see it as a gap at all. (D) opens on exactly that decision but routes through the engineer's own reasoning, so they reconstruct the trade-off themselves rather than being told they got it wrong. (A) is a sound, widely-taught opening, but an unfocused "how did it go?" invites a capable, pleased engineer to recount the smooth parts and may never reach the deferred concerns in a short debrief. (B) targets the right issue but leads with the PM's verdict, which can put a first-time presenter on the defensive before they have looked at their own call. (C) "praise then concern" builds safety and is reasonable, yet here it risks burying a material point behind warm-up and softening a decision that needs examining while it is fresh. (D) is best because it is both specific to what actually happened and self-directed, getting to the real learning without correcting at the engineer.
