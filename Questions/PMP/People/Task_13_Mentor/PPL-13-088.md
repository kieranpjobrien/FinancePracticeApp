# Question

A seasoned PM on a manufacturing-automation programme is asked by a long-serving functional manager — a key stakeholder — to help them get comfortable with the team's new agile cadence, which the manager finds opaque and is quietly resisting in steering meetings. The manager is respected, busy, and slightly defensive about admitting unfamiliarity. How should the PM approach mentoring this stakeholder?

## Options

- **A**: Frame it as walking the manager through the cadence in their own context, inviting their operational expertise so the exchange feels two-way rather than remedial.
- **B**: Send the manager the team's agile playbook and offer to answer any questions they have after they have read it.
- **C**: Invite the manager to observe several ceremonies first so they can see the cadence in action before any discussion.
- **D**: Arrange for the scrum master to give the manager a formal training session on the agile framework the team uses.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The stakeholder is respected but defensive about unfamiliarity, so a mentoring approach that positions the exchange as two-way — drawing on their operational expertise while explaining the cadence in their context — protects their standing and lowers resistance (A). (B) offloads the learning to a document and risks the busy, slightly defensive manager never engaging, with no relationship built. (C) is useful but observation alone leaves the manager to interpret what they see unaided, and does not address the defensiveness. (D) labels the manager as a trainee in a formal session, which is most likely to wound their pride and harden the resistance the PM is trying to soften. Meeting the stakeholder as a peer and contextualising the material is the approach most likely to convert quiet resistance into understanding.
