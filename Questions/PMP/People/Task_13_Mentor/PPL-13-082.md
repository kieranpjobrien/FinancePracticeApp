# Question

A PM has informally mentored a junior business analyst on a logistics programme for several months. The analyst now asks the PM, in confidence, to advise them on how to handle a grievance they intend to raise about their own line manager, who is also a project stakeholder. The analyst trusts the PM and the mentoring relationship has been productive. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Listen, then make clear this falls outside the mentoring relationship and point the analyst to HR and the firm's grievance process, while remaining available for project-related mentoring.
- **B**: Advise the analyst on grievance tactics, since the relationship is built on trust and declining would damage the rapport they have worked to establish.
- **C**: Decline to discuss it and end the mentoring relationship to avoid any perception of taking sides in a personnel dispute on the project.
- **D**: Take the analyst's concerns to the line manager directly to try to resolve the underlying issue before a formal grievance becomes necessary.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Mentoring on project capability does not extend to coaching one stakeholder through a formal grievance against another, which is an HR matter with its own process and would compromise the PM's impartiality across the team (A). (B) over-extends the relationship into personnel-dispute tactics where the PM has no standing and a clear conflict, and "preserving rapport" is not a reason to act improperly. (C) overcorrects by terminating a productive relationship over a single out-of-scope request the PM can simply decline. (D) breaches the confidence and inserts the PM into a dispute as an intermediary the analyst never authorised. Holding the boundary while signposting the right channel protects both the analyst and the PM's neutrality.
