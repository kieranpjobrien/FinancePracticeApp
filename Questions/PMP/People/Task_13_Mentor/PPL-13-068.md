# Question

A PM on an agile software team is mentoring a junior developer who is technically strong but freezes when asked to explain trade-offs to non-technical stakeholders during sprint reviews. The PM wants to help the developer grow into confident stakeholder communication over the coming sprints. Which actions best support this development? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Before the next review, work with the developer to prepare and rehearse how they will explain one trade-off in plain language.
- **B**: Give the developer increasing exposure to stakeholder communication, starting with a small, low-stakes update and building from there.
- **C**: Have the developer observe how the PM frames a technical trade-off for stakeholders, then debrief what made it land.
- **D**: Arrange for the PM to handle all stakeholder-facing explanations on the developer's behalf until the developer feels more confident.
- **E**: Tell the developer that stakeholder communication is a skill some people simply do not have, and focus their growth on their technical strengths instead.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Developing a capability the mentee lacks works through preparation, graduated real exposure and observation of good practice. (A) builds the specific skill with rehearsal, turning a daunting moment into something achievable. (B) applies graduated stretch — starting small and increasing exposure — which builds confidence without overwhelming. (C) provides a model the developer can learn from and then dissect, reinforcing the skill. (D) removes all exposure and so prevents the very growth the mentoring is meant to produce, fostering dependence. (E) is a fixed-mindset write-off that abandons a developmental goal the mentee has the potential to reach and contradicts the purpose of mentoring.
