# Question

A PM has mentored a team member for a year. In a session the mentee confides that they are quietly interviewing elsewhere because they feel stalled, and asks the PM to keep it private. The mentee is the sole specialist on a critical workstream due to deliver in two months, and the PM is also the resource manager accountable for that delivery. How should the PM proceed?

## Options

- **A**: Keep the confidence as asked, and use the session to coach the mentee on what would make them feel less stalled, exploring whether anything could change their decision.
- **B**: Honour the confidence for now, while privately and discreetly assessing continuity options for the workstream as a prudent precaution.
- **C**: Tell the mentee the PM cannot fully hold this confidence given the delivery risk, and work with them on how and when to make their situation known so it can be planned for.
- **D**: Treat the disclosure as resource information and inform their joint line management so succession can be arranged, explaining to the mentee why this is necessary.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The PM holds two roles whose duties now collide — mentor (confidant) and resource manager (accountable for continuity) — and the honest move is to name that conflict rather than quietly resolve it one way. (C) is candid about the limits of the confidence the PM can offer and keeps the mentee as a partner in deciding how to surface it, preserving trust while protecting delivery; transparency about the dual role is what integrity requires here. (A) honours the mentee fully and is good coaching, but it ignores the PM's accountability and the real continuity risk on a critical workstream. (B) looks balanced yet acting on the information secretly while professing confidentiality is the very breach of trust the mentee would resent if discovered. (D) protects delivery but unilaterally overriding the confidence treats a mentoring disclosure as pure resource data and is likely to rupture the relationship and the mentee's trust in any mentor. Naming the conflict openly is both the most ethical and the most workable path.
