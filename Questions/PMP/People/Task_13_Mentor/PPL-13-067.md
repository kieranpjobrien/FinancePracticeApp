# Question

A PM has been asked to mentor a peer with roughly the same years of experience but in a different specialism. The PM feels uncertain they are qualified to mentor a peer and wonders if a more senior mentor would be appropriate instead. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Decline and suggest the peer seek a more senior mentor, since peer mentoring is not generally recognised as producing the same development value as traditional forms do.
- **B**: Accept with a clearly framed peer-mentoring arrangement — explicit purpose, mutual learning, and regular review — and proceed without pretending to be more senior.
- **C**: Accept the mentoring request and act as if the PM has the seniority of a traditional mentor, since the mentee has chosen them and the confidence will help.
- **D**: Accept the request but reframe it immediately as a reciprocal peer-coaching arrangement without any specific goals or structure beyond regular informal conversations.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

PMI-aligned mentoring can take a peer-mentoring form — what matters is an explicit frame, purpose and regular review rather than the seniority gap itself. (A) declines a genuinely useful development form. (C) adopts a posture of seniority that will feel false to both parties. (D) loses the purpose and structure that make peer mentoring worthwhile.
