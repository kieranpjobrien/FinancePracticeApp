# Question

A PM has mentored a coordinator for six months. The coordinator asks to lead the kickoff workshop for a new high-visibility phase. The PM judges the coordinator can handle most of it but not yet the parts where senior sponsors will push on scope and funding, and those exchanges set the tone for the whole phase. The coordinator is keen and the sponsors are influential. How should the PM respond?

## Options

- **A**: Have the coordinator lead the portions the PM judges them ready for while the PM takes the sponsor-facing scope and funding exchanges, then debrief the whole workshop together.
- **B**: Run the workshop jointly throughout, with the coordinator and PM visibly sharing the lead across every segment so the coordinator is never solely exposed on any part.
- **C**: Let the coordinator lead the whole workshop with the PM in the room ready to step in only if a sponsor exchange goes wrong.
- **D**: Have the coordinator co-design and lead a lower-stakes internal session first to build the sponsor-handling skill, and bring them into the kickoff in a supporting role this time.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Every option is a legitimate way to calibrate stretch to readiness; the margin is which one matches *this* gap and *this* setting. (A) gives the coordinator a real, owned lead on what they can do while the PM holds the specific high-stakes exchanges the coordinator is not ready for, and the shared debrief converts it into learning — the cleanest fit for a partial, identifiable readiness gap in a visible forum. (B) protects the coordinator but a permanently shared lead blurs ownership and gives them no segment that is genuinely theirs, so the stretch is weaker than it should be. (C) is the boldest development but "step in only if it goes wrong" stakes the phase's opening on a sponsor exchange the PM already expects to exceed the coordinator — recovering a faltering exchange in front of sponsors can damage credibility more than never holding it. (D) is a sound sequencing move and might suit a larger gap, but it defers the coordinator's lead on a real kickoff they are mostly ready for, under-using their demonstrated readiness. (A) is best because it grants real ownership where earned and a safety net exactly where the gap is.
