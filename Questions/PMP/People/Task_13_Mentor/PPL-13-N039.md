# Question

A PM mentors two graduates remotely: one eight hours ahead, one three behind. Live sessions that suit one fall outside the other's working day, and the PM has noticed the graduate who keeps taking the awkward slot is becoming harder to engage. The PM wants both relationships to stay strong and fair. Which two practices best serve that? (Select two.)

## Options

- **A**: Rotate the inconvenient time slot between the two graduates so neither consistently bears the cost of the time difference.
- **B**: Combine the two graduates into joint sessions at a single fixed time so the PM can run one consistent cadence for both.
- **C**: For the graduate on the awkward slot, blend shorter live calls with asynchronous video and written exchanges so engagement does not depend solely on a poorly timed meeting.
- **D**: Hold each graduate's session at the time most convenient for the PM, keeping the PM consistent and letting the graduates adjust around it.
- **E**: Reduce the awkwardly placed graduate's sessions to monthly to limit how often they meet outside their working hours.

## Answer

A, C

## Explanation

The problem is an unfair, fixed burden falling on one graduate and eroding their engagement. Rotating the inconvenient slot (A) shares the cost equitably, and blending live with asynchronous contact (C) keeps the disadvantaged graduate engaged without relying on a badly timed call — both directly address fairness and connection. (B) imposes one time on two people in different zones, which cannot be convenient for both and loses the individual relationship each graduate needs. (D) optimises around the mentor and entrenches exactly the one-sided burden that is causing the disengagement. (E) protects the graduate's hours but cutting cadence weakens the relationship and reads as deprioritising them — solving the symptom by reducing the mentoring. The trap is that B, D and E each look like reasonable scheduling fixes.
