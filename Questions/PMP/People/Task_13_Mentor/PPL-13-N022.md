# Question

A PM mentors a junior coordinator from another department under an informal arrangement. For the last two sessions the coordinator has spent most of the time describing, with rising frustration, how their own line manager overloads and undermines them. Today they ask the PM to raise it with that manager directly, saying the manager respects the PM and would listen. The coordinator is competent at the project work the mentoring was set up to develop. What should the PM do next?

## Options

- **A**: Speak to the line manager as asked, since the coordinator has judged it the most effective route and a mentor uses their standing to help where they realistically can.
- **B**: Keep the confidence in the room and work with the coordinator on how they could put the overload to their manager themselves, rehearsing the conversation with them.
- **C**: Offer to sit in alongside the coordinator if they raise it with the manager, so they lead the conversation but are not facing it alone.
- **D**: Name what the PM is noticing — that recent sessions have centred on the manager rather than the coordinator's own development — and ask the coordinator what they most want the mentoring to do for them.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

All four are defensible mentor responses, which is what makes the margin fine. (A) is genuine advocacy and the coordinator has reasoned it out, but a mentor stepping into another team's reporting line to act on the mentee's behalf creates dependence and trades the mentee's agency for a one-off intervention. (C) is a strong middle option — it keeps the coordinator leading while offering support — and would be a sound *next* step once they have decided to raise it; but committing the PM's presence now still pre-supposes the coordinator wants to act and skips building the capability to handle it. (D) is a legitimate boundary-and-contracting move and may be needed if the pattern continues, but jumping to reframe the relationship risks reading as a deflection of a real, specific request the coordinator has just made in good faith. (B) is best because it answers the actual ask — getting the overload addressed — while developing the coordinator's own ability to carry the conversation, which is what mentoring is for; the rehearsal makes it achievable rather than a brush-off.
