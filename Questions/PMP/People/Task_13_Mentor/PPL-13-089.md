# Question

A PM is four months into a six-month mentoring arrangement with a team member who wanted to grow into facilitating workshops. The mentee has run two workshops well and now tells the PM, sincerely, that they feel they have got what they came for and the sessions feel like they are running out of things to cover. The original goals were largely about workshop facilitation. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Revisit the original goals together, confirm what has been achieved, and ask whether to close the arrangement well now or reshape it around a fresh goal the mentee genuinely wants.
- **B**: Continue to the agreed six-month end date, as committing to the full term was part of the arrangement and stopping early sets a poor precedent.
- **C**: Introduce more advanced facilitation challenges to keep the mentee stretched and ensure the remaining sessions stay valuable.
- **D**: Conclude the arrangement immediately, since the mentee has met their goals and prolonging it would waste both parties' time.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The mentee's stated goals were about facilitation and they have been met, so the right move is to review progress against those goals together and let the mentee decide whether to close well or set a new, genuinely owned goal — mentoring is mentee-led and outcome-based, not duration-bound (A). (B) treats the calendar as the objective and keeps meeting after the purpose is served, which the mentee has already signalled is hollow. (C) imposes new content the mentee has not asked for, substituting the PM's idea of value for the mentee's own goals. (D) jumps to ending without the joint review or the chance to surface a worthwhile new direction, and a proper close (capturing learning, agreeing next steps) is itself valuable. Reviewing against the goals and co-deciding the next step honours both the achievement and the mentee's ownership.
