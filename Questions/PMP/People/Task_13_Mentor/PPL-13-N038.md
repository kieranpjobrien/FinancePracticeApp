# Question

A PM adapts how they support a developing team member depending on the situation. For each situation below, match the mentoring stance that best fits.

## Items

- **1**: The mentee has run this kind of analysis competently several times. They arrive with a worked answer but ask the PM to confirm it before they act, as they have done the last few times.
- **2**: The mentee is partway through a task they have done once before. They have hit a step they are unsure about, the team is waiting on the output today, and getting it wrong would mean redoing a day's work but nothing irreversible.
- **3**: The mentee is weighing whether to specialise in delivery or move toward a product role. They keep asking the PM which the PM would choose in their position.

## Matches

- **A**: Coach with questions — draw out the mentee's own reasoning and let them decide.
- **B**: Direct — give a clear, specific instruction so they can proceed now.
- **C**: Facilitate reflection — help them explore the options without steering them to an answer.

## Answer

1-A, 2-B, 3-C

## Explanation

The skill is reading readiness and stakes rather than reacting to surface cues. In (1) the mentee is fully capable and is seeking reassurance, not knowledge — coaching questions return the judgement to them and wean them off needing sign-off. In (2) they are genuinely stuck on an unfamiliar step under time pressure where a wrong move costs real rework; brief, specific direction is the responsible stance, even though the consequences are recoverable rather than catastrophic. In (3) the question is open-ended and personal, so the mentor facilitates the mentee's own exploration and resists the pull to answer "what would you do?" The difficulty is that (2) tempts you toward coaching because the stakes are not extreme, and (1) tempts you toward direction because the mentee is asking to be told — matching stance to the underlying need, not the request, is the point.
