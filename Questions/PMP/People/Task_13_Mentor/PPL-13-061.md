# Question

On a Scrum team building a logistics platform, the product owner repeatedly accepts mid-sprint scope additions from the operations director, then escalates to the PM when the sprint goal is missed. The PM has agreed to mentor the product owner on protecting the sprint. In their next session the product owner says, "I just can't say no to him — he's a director." How should the PM use this session?

## Options

- **A**: Offer to attend the next exchange with the operations director and demonstrate how to decline a mid-sprint addition, so the product owner sees it done before trying it.
- **B**: Suggest the product owner channel new requests into the product backlog for the next sprint planning, giving them a concrete process to point the director toward instead of refusing outright.
- **C**: Help the product owner work out how they want to handle the next such request, then role-play the conversation with the operations director so they can practise holding the sprint boundary in their own words.
- **D**: Explore why the product owner feels unable to push back on seniority, surfacing the belief driving the behaviour before working on any specific tactic.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The product owner has named both the skill gap (holding the boundary) and a real obstacle (the director's seniority), so the session should build the capability to act, not just diagnose it. (C) develops the durable skill on the actual problem and rehearsal turns a daunting refusal into something achievable, which is the core of mentoring toward independence. (D) is insightful and the belief about seniority is worth exploring, but stopping at the why leaves the product owner no better equipped for the next request that is already coming. (B) hands over a useful mechanism, yet a process without the confidence to invoke it will fold the same way under direct pressure from a director. (A) models the behaviour but doing it for the product owner keeps them dependent on the PM for the very exchange they need to own.
