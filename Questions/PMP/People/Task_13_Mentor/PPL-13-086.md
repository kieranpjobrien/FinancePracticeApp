# Question

A PM mentors a junior PM who runs a small website-replatforming project in parallel. Lately the junior PM forwards their own stakeholder emails to the mentor and asks "what should I reply?", and the mentor has been drafting suggested replies to keep things moving. The junior PM is capable and the replies they eventually send are sound. The mentor realises the forwarding has increased over the past month. What should the mentor do?

## Options

- **A**: Name the pattern with the junior PM and shift to asking how they would respond and why, reviewing their draft rather than supplying one.
- **B**: Keep drafting replies but cap it at the genuinely difficult stakeholder situations, declining the routine ones.
- **C**: Continue providing draft replies, since the junior PM is learning from well-crafted examples and the stakeholders are being well served.
- **D**: Tell the junior PM to stop forwarding emails and to escalate only if a stakeholder formally complains about a response.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The forwarding is increasing, which signals a growing dependency that the mentor's drafting is feeding, and the junior PM is capable enough to reason these out themselves (A). Mentoring should build the junior PM's own judgement, so naming the pattern and switching to "how would you handle this, and why?" with review transfers the skill while keeping a safety net. (B) still positions the mentor as the answer-provider for hard cases without building capability and leaves the dependency dynamic intact. (C) optimises for short-term output and worsens the very dependency the mentor has just noticed. (D) cuts off support abruptly and ties re-engagement to complaints, which is punitive and removes the developmental review that would help most. Surfacing the pattern and coaching the reasoning is the response that addresses the trend the mentor identified.
