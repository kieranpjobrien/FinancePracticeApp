# Question

Over three months of mentoring, a PM has watched a quiet team member grow from hesitant to confidently leading a small workstream and handling a difficult vendor conversation well, yet the team member still doubts they are progressing. The PM wants to help the team member recognise and build on their own growth. Which responses are appropriate? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Ask the team member to reflect on what they think they have improved at, then add the PM's own specific observations.
- **B**: Point to concrete moments of growth, such as how they handled the vendor conversation, so the progress is evidenced rather than asserted.
- **C**: Invite the team member to identify what they would like to develop next, building on the confidence they have gained.
- **D**: Reassure the team member in general terms that they are doing well, to lift their mood before the next challenge.
- **E**: Set a markedly harder stretch goal immediately to push past the self-doubt with a bigger challenge.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Overcoming evidenced self-doubt means helping the person see and own their progress, then channelling it forward. (A) builds self-assessment by eliciting their own reflection before reinforcing it with the mentor's specifics. (B) grounds the growth in concrete moments, which is far more convincing than general praise. (C) turns recognised progress into momentum by letting them choose the next focus, reinforcing ownership. (D) is well-meant but vague reassurance rarely shifts genuine self-doubt and can feel hollow. (E) risks deepening the doubt: piling on a much harder goal while the person already underrates themselves can overwhelm rather than encourage, and skips the recognition step that the situation calls for.
