# Question

A PM is mentoring a project coordinator located in another country whom they have never met in person; all sessions are by video call. After two months the coordinator's updates have become brief and guarded, and the PM senses the relationship is not deepening as it would in person. The PM wants to strengthen the mentoring across the distance. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Increase the frequency of sessions so more contact builds the relationship faster despite the distance.
- **B**: Add some informal, non-work conversation at the start of each call to build the personal rapport that distance tends to erode.
- **C**: Raise it openly in the next session, asking how the coordinator is finding the mentoring and what would make it more useful for them.
- **D**: Switch to a mix of channels, using occasional written check-ins between calls so the coordinator can engage in whatever way suits them.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The relationship is faltering and the PM is sensing rather than knowing why, so the first step is to understand the coordinator's experience directly before applying a fix. (C) surfaces what is actually happening — the coordinator may feel exposed, mismatched on goals, or uncomfortable with the format — and invites them to shape the relationship, which models the openness mentoring depends on and targets the real cause. (B) is a sound rapport-builder and may well help, but applying it before knowing the cause assumes the issue is rapport when it could be something else. (D) sensibly adapts channels to a virtual context, yet again presumes the problem is the medium without checking. (A) adds quantity without addressing why the existing sessions are not landing, and more of an unsatisfying format may worsen the guardedness. Understanding first makes any of the other moves better targeted.
