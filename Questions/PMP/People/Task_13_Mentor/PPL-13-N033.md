# Question

A capable team member the PM is mentoring keeps bringing the PM decisions that sit squarely within their own authority, asking the PM to choose. When the PM turns the question back, the team member produces a sound answer but explains they brought it because they were afraid of being blamed if it went wrong. The PM wants to build their confidence to decide alone. What is the most useful thing for the PM to do?

## Options

- **A**: Keep asking what they think and back the call they reach, while making explicit that the PM will stand behind decisions taken in good faith within their remit.
- **B**: Consistently turn each decision back with "what do you think you should do?" so the habit of deciding for themselves is reinforced every time.
- **C**: Confirm to the team member exactly which categories of decision are theirs to make without consulting the PM, removing the ambiguity about their authority.
- **D**: Agree that for the next few decisions the team member will decide and act first, then tell the PM afterward, so they experience owning the outcome.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The team member has revealed the real barrier: not weak judgement — their answers are sound — but fear of being blamed. Coaching questions alone treat a confidence problem the member does not have, so the move that fits the disclosed cause pairs the questions with explicit air cover, addressing the fear directly (A). (B) reinforces a habit they can already perform and ignores what they just told the PM is stopping them. (C) clarifying authority is genuinely useful and removes ambiguity, but the member already knows these are their decisions — the block is consequence, not boundary. (D) builds ownership through experience and would help, yet without addressing the blame fear it can heighten the very anxiety driving the escalation. The margin is fine because C and D are both reasonable; A is best because it answers the stated cause.
