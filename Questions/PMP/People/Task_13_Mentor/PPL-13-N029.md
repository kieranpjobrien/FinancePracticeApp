# Question

A PM informally mentors a team member from another department. Over several sessions the mentee discloses they are seriously considering leaving the organisation because of how their line manager treats them, and asks the PM to keep this strictly confidential. The PM believes the situation, if true, is one the organisation would want to know about and could act on. What is the most appropriate mentoring response?

## Options

- **A**: Honour the confidentiality and stay in the mentoring role, helping the mentee think through their situation and options and reach their own decision without reporting it onward.
- **B**: Honour the confidentiality for now, but help the mentee see the value of raising the treatment themselves through HR or a trusted channel, and support them to do so if they choose.
- **C**: Explain that a matter this serious cannot stay wholly confidential, and agree with the mentee what could be shared, with whom, before going further.
- **D**: Reassure the mentee and, without naming them, raise the general concern with HR so the organisation can look into the pattern of treatment.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Informal mentoring rests on confidentiality, and the mentor's role is to help the mentee think clearly and own their decision, not to act on or route the disclosure elsewhere — so honouring confidentiality while developing the mentee's own thinking (A) is the core mentoring stance. The difficulty is that B and C are both genuinely reasonable: (B) keeps the confidence yet builds the mentee's capacity to act, and is close to best, but it leans toward steering the mentee to a course (raising it) rather than first helping them decide whether they even want to — useful as a next step, not the primary response. (C) reflects real limits to confidentiality when serious harm may exist, but pre-emptively negotiating disclosure treats the mentee's account as something to escalate rather than as a confidence to hold, which most informal mentoring would not warrant here. (D) breaches the confidentiality that makes the relationship safe even with the name withheld, since the mentee is identifiable and did not consent. A is best because nothing disclosed meets a threshold that overrides the mentee's trust and agency.
