# Question

A PM agrees to mentor a delivery lead transitioning from a predictive shop to a hybrid one. At the first session the delivery lead says they are not sure what they need help with, only that they feel behind. The PM has deep hybrid experience and could readily list the gaps they expect to see. What is the best way for the PM to begin?

## Options

- **A**: Ask the delivery lead to describe a recent situation where they felt out of their depth, and use it to surface together what skills would have helped.
- **B**: Share a checklist of common predictive-to-hybrid capability gaps and ask the delivery lead to mark where they think they stand.
- **C**: Propose a structured curriculum covering the hybrid topics the PM judges most important and work through it session by session.
- **D**: Suggest they pause mentoring until the delivery lead can articulate specific goals, so the sessions have clear direction.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A mentee who cannot yet name their needs is best served by grounding the conversation in their own lived experience, which lets goals emerge from real situations rather than being imposed (A). (B) is a reasonable aid but starts from the PM's framework, and a self-assessment against gaps the mentee may not yet recognise tends to produce shallow answers. (C) hands the agenda entirely to the PM, which can fill knowledge but risks teaching what the mentee does not need while missing what they do. (D) withholds support precisely when the mentee has reached out feeling behind, and articulating goals is itself something mentoring should help with. Drawing goals out of a concrete experience respects the mentee-led nature of mentoring while still using the PM's expertise to interpret it.
