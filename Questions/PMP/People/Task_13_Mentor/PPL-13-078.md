# Question

A PM is rotating off a long-running infrastructure programme in three months. They have informally mentored a capable deputy who is the likely successor, but the mentoring has been ad hoc. The PM wants to make sure the deputy is genuinely ready to lead by handover. How should the PM use the remaining time?

## Options

- **A**: Document the programme's key decisions, relationships, and risks thoroughly so the deputy has a complete reference to draw on after the handover.
- **B**: Keep the deputy closely involved in everything the PM does over the period so they absorb the full breadth of the role by observation.
- **C**: Introduce the deputy to all the senior stakeholders and brief them that the deputy will be taking over, to smooth the relationship transition.
- **D**: Identify with the deputy the specific areas they have not yet led on, then deliberately hand those over with decreasing support across the three months.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Readiness to lead is demonstrated by leading, so the most effective use of the time is structured, fading delegation targeted at the gaps. (D) pinpoints what the deputy has not yet owned and transfers exactly those responsibilities with progressively less support, building and proving genuine capability before the PM leaves — the surest test and builder of readiness. (A) is valuable and should happen, but a reference document supports a successor; it does not develop or verify their ability to lead. (B) deepens exposure but observation without ownership leaves the deputy untested on the decisions they will soon have to make alone. (C) handles the relational handover well and matters, yet smoothing introductions is only one slice of readiness and says nothing about the deputy's capacity to run the programme. Graduated handover of real responsibility is what closes the readiness gap.
