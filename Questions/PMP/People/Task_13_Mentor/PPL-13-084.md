# Question

A PM has been mentoring a junior coordinator on a hospital construction project for two months. The coordinator has clearly improved at chairing supplier calls — they now prepare an agenda and follow up actions promptly. At the next session the coordinator seems unsure whether they are making progress at all. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Acknowledge the specific improvements the PM has observed in the coordinator's supplier calls before moving on to the next development area.
- **B**: Move straight to the next skill to keep the coordinator challenged and prevent complacency now that supplier calls are handled.
- **C**: Ask the coordinator to self-assess their progress across all areas so they learn to judge their own development.
- **D**: Arrange formal feedback from the suppliers so the coordinator hears about their progress from an objective external source.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The coordinator has genuinely improved but cannot see it, so specific, observed recognition is the immediate and proportionate response — it reinforces the behaviour and restores their sense of progress (A). (B) ignores a mentee who is quietly losing confidence and skips the cheap, important step of naming what is going well. (C) is a useful habit but is not the first move when the coordinator is already doubting themselves; self-assessment from that state often understates real gains. (D) is disproportionate effort for something the PM has directly witnessed and can simply affirm. Giving concrete recognition first is the foundational mentoring act here.
