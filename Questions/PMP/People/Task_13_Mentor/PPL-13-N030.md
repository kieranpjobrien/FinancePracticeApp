# Question

A seasoned predictive PM has moved to lead an agile team. A junior team member has deep, current fluency in the team's agile tooling and ceremonies that the PM lacks, while the PM has years of stakeholder and delivery experience the junior would benefit from. The junior is happy to help. The PM wants an arrangement that builds the junior's development as well as the PM's agile fluency. Which approach does that best?

## Options

- **A**: Set up a reciprocal arrangement: the junior coaches the PM on agile practice, and the PM mentors the junior on stakeholder and delivery judgement, with both treated as real development for each.
- **B**: Ask the junior to coach the PM on the agile tooling, openly acknowledging the junior's expertise and the PM's gap to the team.
- **C**: Have the junior run short "teach-back" sessions on each ceremony for the whole team, so the PM learns alongside everyone and the junior gains exposure leading.
- **D**: Spend several sprints pairing closely with the junior on live ceremonies so the PM absorbs the practice in context rather than through dedicated coaching.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The PM's stated aim has two halves — the PM's fluency *and* the junior's development — so the best fit deliberately runs the exchange both ways, framing each direction as genuine development (A). (B) captures the reverse-mentoring of the PM and the useful modelling of acknowledging a gap, but it is one-directional and does not develop the junior. (C) gives the junior valuable exposure to leading and helps the whole team, yet it dilutes the PM's own learning into a group setting and offers the junior nothing on the stakeholder side the PM could uniquely give. (D) is an effective way for the PM to learn but is essentially the PM absorbing from the junior with no reciprocal development built in. All four move the PM forward; only A develops both people by design.
