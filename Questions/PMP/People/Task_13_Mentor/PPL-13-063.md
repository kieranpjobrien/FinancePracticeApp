# Question

A PM mentoring a business analyst gives specific, evidence-based feedback that the analyst's requirement workshops are losing stakeholders because sessions run long and lack a clear agenda. The analyst responds that the stakeholders are simply disorganised and that the sessions are fine. The PM is confident the feedback is sound and the pattern is real. What should the PM do next in the mentoring relationship?

## Options

- **A**: Restate the observed evidence calmly and ask the analyst to walk through what happened in the last workshop from the stakeholders' point of view, so the analyst examines the pattern themselves.
- **B**: Drop the point for now to preserve the relationship and look for a moment when the analyst raises a workshop problem of their own to revisit it.
- **C**: Propose the analyst and PM co-facilitate the next workshop so the analyst can directly compare a structured session against their current approach.
- **D**: Provide a tighter agenda template and timeboxing technique for the analyst to apply, focusing on the fix rather than winning the argument about the cause.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The analyst is deflecting ownership, and lasting change depends on them seeing the pattern, not on the PM out-arguing them. (A) holds the evidence without escalating and uses a perspective shift to let the analyst arrive at the insight, which is what converts resisted feedback into accepted feedback and preserves the relationship. (D) is practical and may help, but handing over a fix while the analyst still attributes the problem to others means the tools will be applied half-heartedly or abandoned. (C) creates a strong comparison and could work, yet jumping to co-facilitation before the analyst accepts there is anything to compare risks them experiencing it as the PM proving a point. (B) protects rapport but lets a real, recurring problem continue unaddressed, and waiting for the analyst to raise it themselves may never come given their current stance.
