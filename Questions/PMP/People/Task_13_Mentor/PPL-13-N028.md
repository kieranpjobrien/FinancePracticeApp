# Question

A PM has mentored a graduate in another country entirely over video for three months. The sessions run on time and cover the graduate's tasks, but they have grown flat: the graduate answers questions briefly, raises nothing of their own, and the PM senses the relationship has become transactional. The PM wants to deepen it without adding meetings the graduate may not want. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Ask the graduate how the mentoring is working for them and what would make it more useful, and reshape the format together based on what they say.
- **B**: Spend the opening minutes of each session on non-task conversation to build the rapport that remote contact tends to starve.
- **C**: Shift some exchanges to a shared document and asynchronous notes so the graduate has time to compose fuller, more considered contributions.
- **D**: Ask the graduate to bring one topic of their own choosing to each session so they help set the agenda rather than only responding.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The relationship has gone flat and the PM is guessing at the cause; before applying any single remedy, the mentoring move is to make the relationship itself the subject and let the graduate say what would help (A). That both surfaces the real issue and models the two-way ownership a transactional relationship is missing. (B), (C) and (D) are all sound remote-mentoring techniques — warming up with rapport, using async for reflective contributors, and handing over agenda-setting — and any might be the right fix. But each presumes a specific diagnosis (no rapport, needs thinking time, too passive) the PM has not actually confirmed, so committing to one risks treating the wrong cause. Asking first is best precisely because the three alternatives are each plausible yet unverified.
