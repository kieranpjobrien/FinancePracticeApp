# Question

A project manager has agreed to mentor a junior analyst who wants to move into project coordination. The two have not worked together before. They have an hour for their first session. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Draw out what the analyst wants from the mentoring and what each of them will commit to, then use that to shape how they will work together.
- **B**: Walk the analyst through the coordination tasks a typical project needs, so the analyst gets an early, concrete picture of the role they are aiming for.
- **C**: Ask the analyst to talk through a coordination problem they are facing now, and work it through together so the first session delivers something useful immediately.
- **D**: Share the parts of the PM's own route into coordination that the analyst could realistically learn from, to give the relationship a candid, personal start.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A mentoring relationship works best when goals and mutual expectations are agreed up front, because that shared purpose is what later sessions are steered by. (B) gives useful orientation but presumes what the analyst needs before asking. (C) is genuinely valuable and tempting, but solving a problem before the relationship's direction is set risks an ad hoc series of sessions with no agreed aim. (D) builds rapport and is a reasonable early ingredient, yet leading with the mentor's story centres the wrong person for a first session whose job is to establish the mentee's goals.
