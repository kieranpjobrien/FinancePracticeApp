# Question

A PM is starting to mentor a junior analyst. The analyst's own development goal is to grow into stakeholder-facing work, but the PM can see the analyst has a pressing gap in the data skills their current role demands this quarter, and the talent programme that paired them lists a third set of competencies it expects the mentoring to cover. In the first session, how should the PM set the direction for the relationship?

## Options

- **A**: Surface all three — the analyst's aspiration, the immediate role gap, and the programme's competencies — and work with the analyst to agree goals they own that reconcile them.
- **B**: Start from the analyst's stated aspiration toward stakeholder work and shape the sessions around that, since mentee ownership is what makes mentoring effective.
- **C**: Prioritise the pressing data-skills gap first because it is the most urgent and concrete, then turn to the analyst's longer-term aspiration later.
- **D**: Use the talent programme's competency list as the backbone of the sessions, since it reflects what the organisation has invested in developing.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Mentee ownership matters, but ownership is not the same as letting a single stated preference override everything else the analyst genuinely needs and is accountable for. The opening move is to make the competing pulls explicit and help the analyst form goals that are theirs yet reconcile aspiration, the live role gap, and the programme's expectations (A). (B) honours ownership but ignores a real, time-bound gap and the programme's remit, which can leave the analyst exposed this quarter. (C) is reasonable and may well shape the early sessions, but deciding the priority for the analyst — rather than with them — undercuts the ownership that drives engagement. (D) imposes an external syllabus and is the least mentee-led. The distinction is fine because B and C are both defensible; A is best because it integrates rather than discards any of the three.
