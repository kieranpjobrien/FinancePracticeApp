# Question

A PM mentoring a mid-career project officer notices the officer has stopped acting on agreed development actions between sessions, arrives unprepared, and has become non-committal about goals they were enthusiastic about two months ago. The PM wants to get the relationship back on track. Which actions are appropriate responses? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Raise the change openly and without blame, asking the officer how they are finding the mentoring and whether their goals still feel right to them.
- **B**: Explore whether something outside the sessions — workload, a personal issue, or a shift in motivation — is behind the disengagement.
- **C**: Revisit and, if needed, renegotiate the development goals so they reflect what the officer actually wants now.
- **D**: Set firmer expectations with consequences for not completing agreed actions, to restore the officer's commitment to the process.
- **E**: Conclude the officer is no longer interested and wind the mentoring relationship down to free up the PM's time.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Disengagement is a signal to understand and re-align, not to police or abandon. (A) addresses it directly and without blame, opening space for the officer to say what has changed. (B) recognises that the cause often lies outside the sessions — workload, personal circumstances, or shifted motivation — and must be understood before responding. (C) follows naturally: goals set two months ago may no longer fit, and renegotiating them restores ownership and relevance. (D) treats a motivation or alignment problem as a compliance problem; imposing consequences on a voluntary developmental relationship is likely to deepen the withdrawal rather than rekindle commitment. (E) abandons the mentee at exactly the point where understanding could re-engage them, giving up on the relationship without first finding out why it stalled.
