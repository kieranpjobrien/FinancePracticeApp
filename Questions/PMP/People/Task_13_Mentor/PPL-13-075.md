# Question

A PM is both the line manager and the mentor of a team member whose formal performance review is due next month. In a mentoring session the team member opens up candidly about a serious skill weakness they are working hard to overcome. The PM values this honesty. As the review approaches, how should the PM handle the tension between the two roles?

## Options

- **A**: Be explicit with the team member about how the mentoring conversations and the formal review relate, so they understand what is and is not carried across before they decide how openly to share.
- **B**: Keep the mentoring disclosures entirely separate from the review and assess performance only on independently observed evidence, without referencing what was shared in confidence.
- **C**: Use the team member's openness about the weakness as a positive in the review, since their self-awareness and effort to improve are genuine strengths worth crediting.
- **D**: Pause the mentoring relationship until after the review is complete, to remove the conflict between confidant and evaluator during a sensitive period.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Holding mentor and evaluator roles creates a real conflict, and the team member can only calibrate their candour if they understand how the two interact — so transparency about the boundary comes first. (A) names the relationship between the conversations and the review up front, preserving trust and letting the team member make an informed choice about openness, which respects them and protects the integrity of both roles. (B) is a sound principle and close to right, but applying it silently leaves the team member guessing whether their disclosures count against them, so the trust issue is unresolved even if the PM's intent is fair. (C) means a confidential admission of weakness materially shapes the formal review, which — however positively framed — punishes candour and discourages future honesty. (D) avoids the conflict but suspending support during a stressful period abandons the mentee when they may most need it and treats the tension as unmanageable rather than addressable. Being explicit about the boundary is what makes the dual role workable.
