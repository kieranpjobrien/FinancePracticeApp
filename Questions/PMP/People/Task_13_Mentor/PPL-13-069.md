# Question

A veteran PM running a hybrid manufacturing project is mentoring a younger team member in project leadership. The team member is far more fluent than the PM in the collaboration and analytics tools the project now depends on, and quietly does most of the tooling work themselves rather than showing others. The PM wants the mentoring relationship to serve the project as well as the mentee. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Keep the mentoring focused on the mentee's leadership development, since mixing in tool-sharing duties could dilute the relationship's purpose.
- **B**: Establish a reciprocal arrangement where the PM mentors the team member on leadership and the team member helps upskill the PM and others on the tools.
- **C**: Ask the team member to document the tooling so the knowledge is captured, keeping the mentoring itself purely about leadership.
- **D**: Encourage the team member to keep owning the tooling, as their fluency makes them most efficient, and concentrate the sessions on their growth.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Good mentoring can flow both ways, and here a reciprocal arrangement serves the mentee's growth, the PM's own development and the project's resilience at once. (B) recognises the team member's expertise — which builds their confidence and standing — while spreading critical tool knowledge and modelling that mentoring is a two-way exchange, the strongest outcome for everyone. (C) captures knowledge but documentation alone rarely transfers a living skill, and it keeps the team member as sole practitioner. (D) is efficient short-term but entrenches a single point of failure and misses the chance to develop the mentee as someone who grows others. (A) protects a narrow definition of the relationship at the cost of an obvious mutual benefit, treating reciprocity as a distraction rather than the asset it is.
