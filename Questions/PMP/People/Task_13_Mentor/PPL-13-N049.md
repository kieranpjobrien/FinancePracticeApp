# Question

A project manager has mentored a Scrum Master for nine months. The Scrum Master is now competent but still messages the PM before making routine team decisions, seeking validation each time. The PM wants the mentoring to result in genuine independence. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: When the next message arrives, respond by asking what the Scrum Master themselves intends to do and why, affirming their reasoning rather than supplying a decision.
- **B**: Name the pattern openly, agree the Scrum Master will decide routine matters without checking, and refocus their sessions on reflecting after the fact rather than pre-approving decisions.
- **C**: Begin replying "what does your judgement say?" to the routine queries, steadily declining to validate them until the Scrum Master stops asking.
- **D**: Point out how capable the Scrum Master has become and express full confidence that they no longer need to check routine calls with the PM.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Lasting autonomy comes from naming the dependence with the mentee and deliberately renegotiating the relationship from approval-seeking to reflection, so the change is shared and understood. (A) is a good in-the-moment coaching reflex and better than giving an answer, but reacting query by query leaves the underlying pattern unaddressed. (C) gently withdraws validation and would erode the habit, yet doing so without naming it can read as the PM going cold and risks confusion rather than a clear new compact. (D) offers encouragement but reassurance alone rarely shifts an ingrained checking habit; an explicit agreement about how they will now work does.
