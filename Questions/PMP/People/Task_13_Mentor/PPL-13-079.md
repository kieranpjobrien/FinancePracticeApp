# Question

During a mentoring session focused on project leadership skills, a team member the PM mentors begins describing significant personal stress at home that is clearly affecting them, and which falls well outside the PM's expertise. The PM wants to respond supportively and appropriately. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Keep the focus of the session on the agreed leadership development topics, gently noting that personal matters fall outside the mentoring.
- **B**: Listen with empathy, acknowledge what the team member is going through, and point them toward appropriate support such as the employee assistance programme.
- **C**: Offer the PM's own advice on handling the personal situation, drawing on life experience to help the team member through it.
- **D**: Take on supporting the team member through the personal difficulty as part of caring for them as a mentor and colleague.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A mentor should respond with genuine empathy while staying within their competence and directing the person to proper help. (B) does both: it acknowledges the distress so the team member feels heard, and it signposts qualified support rather than overreaching, which is the responsible boundary for a mentoring relationship. (C) is well-intentioned but giving personal advice outside one's expertise can do harm and blurs the mentor's role into one they are not equipped for. (D) is compassionate yet taking on a personal-support role the PM is not trained for risks both the team member's wellbeing and the mentoring relationship. (A) maintains scope but doing so without first acknowledging the person's distress is cold and can damage trust; empathy and appropriate referral together are the supportive and professional response.
