# Question

A PM on a utilities programme has a sponsor who is new to the sponsor role and keeps making decisions the PM should own, bypassing the PM to instruct the team directly. The PM recognises the sponsor needs help understanding the boundary between sponsorship and project management. Given that the sponsor is senior to the PM, how should the PM approach mentoring them on the role?

## Options

- **A**: Send the sponsor a concise summary of the standard sponsor and project manager responsibilities so they can see the boundary for themselves.
- **B**: Request a short session framed as aligning on how they will work together, and use examples of recent decisions to clarify which sit with the sponsor and which with the PM.
- **C**: Wait until a direct instruction to the team causes a visible problem, then use that concrete consequence to open the conversation about roles.
- **D**: Ask the programme director to clarify the sponsor's remit to them, since role expectations carry more weight coming from the sponsor's own peer.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Mentoring upward requires tact, but the situation needs the boundary established now and through dialogue, not avoidance. (B) reframes a potentially awkward correction as a collaborative working-agreement conversation, and grounding it in real recent decisions makes the role distinction concrete and non-accusatory, which is the most effective and respectful way to develop a senior stakeholder's understanding. (A) is low-friction but a generic responsibilities document is easy to skim past and does not engage the sponsor with their own behaviour. (C) lets the problem worsen before acting, and tying the conversation to a failure makes it feel like blame rather than development. (D) may eventually be needed if dialogue fails, but escalating first bypasses the direct relationship and can damage trust with a sponsor the PM must keep working closely with.
