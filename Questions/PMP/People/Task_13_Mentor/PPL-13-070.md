# Question

A PM is mentoring a project lead who must choose between two suppliers for a critical component. The lead has done thorough analysis and is leaning toward Supplier X. The PM, from long experience, is fairly sure Supplier Y is the better choice and that the lead has underweighted a delivery-risk factor. The decision is the lead's to make. How should the PM handle the session?

## Options

- **A**: Share that the PM's experience points to Supplier Y and explain why, then make clear the choice remains the lead's to weigh.
- **B**: Hold back the PM's view entirely and support whichever supplier the lead chooses, since developing judgement means letting them decide unaided.
- **C**: Ask probing questions about each supplier's risks, including delivery, so the lead reconsiders the factor themselves, while leaving the decision with them.
- **D**: Tell the lead the delivery-risk factor they have underweighted is decisive and recommend they select Supplier Y on that basis.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The aim is to strengthen the lead's judgement while respecting their ownership, and the gap is a specific factor they have underweighted rather than flawed reasoning overall. (C) uses questioning to lead them back to the delivery risk so they re-evaluate it for themselves, which builds analytical judgement and keeps the decision genuinely theirs — the heart of developmental mentoring. (A) is honest and preserves the lead's authority, and is appropriate when experience is asked for, but volunteering the conclusion first can anchor the lead and short-circuit the reasoning the situation is ideal for developing. (D) hands over the answer and effectively makes the decision, removing the learning and the ownership. (B) over-corrects toward autonomy: withholding a known, material risk insight is not neutral mentoring but a failure to help, and could let an avoidable mistake stand. Probing first develops the lead while keeping the PM's experience available if the questioning does not surface the risk.
