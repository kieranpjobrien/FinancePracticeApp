# Question

A programme manager is letting four work packages, each with a different scope-certainty and risk profile. For each, match it to the contract type that best allocates risk to the party most able to manage it. Read each scenario carefully — the surface description does not always point to the obvious choice.

## Items

- **1**: Fabrication of standard steel components to fixed, fully detailed drawings, delivered over three years, where the dominant cost is raw steel whose market price is volatile and outside either party's control.
- **2**: Civil works with complete drawings, stable requirements, well-understood ground conditions, and a stable, predictable cost base, to be delivered within twelve months.
- **3**: Development work where the scope is broadly known and a target cost can be estimated, but the buyer wants the contractor to have a strong, objective reason to come in under that cost.
- **4**: A short, urgent diagnostic task where the work required will only become clear once a specialist begins investigating, with no reliable basis to estimate effort upfront.

## Matches

- **A**: Fixed-price with economic price adjustment (FP-EPA)
- **B**: Firm fixed-price (FFP)
- **C**: Fixed-price incentive fee (FPIF)
- **D**: Time-and-materials (T&M)

## Answer

1-A, 2-B, 3-C, 4-D

## Explanation

The trap is that items 1 and 2 both look like "defined scope → firm fixed-price." (1→A) Scope is fixed, but a long term plus a volatile, externally-driven commodity cost makes a *firm* fixed price unfair to whichever party guesses steel prices wrong; FP-EPA fixes the price while indexing the volatile input, allocating that uncontrollable risk to neither party unfairly. (2→B) Here scope *and* cost base are stable over a short term, so firm fixed-price cleanly transfers delivery risk to a seller who can confidently price it. (3→C) Known scope with an estimable target cost plus a desire for an objective cost incentive points to fixed-price incentive fee, where a share ratio rewards underruns against the target. (4→D) Genuinely unestimable, urgent, ill-defined effort suits time-and-materials — paying for actual effort as scope clarifies, ideally with a not-to-exceed cap. Matching the *risk driver* (not just scope clarity) to the contract structure is the negotiation skill being tested.
