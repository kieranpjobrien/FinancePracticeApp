# Question

On a hybrid pharmaceutical-logistics project, the fixed-price vendor contract assumes a stable scope, but the agile portion of the work is generating a steady flow of small, well-justified change requests. Each one currently triggers a slow formal change order, and the backlog of pending changes is starting to throttle the team's flow. The vendor is cooperative but will not work outside the contract. The project manager is negotiating a better mechanism with the vendor's commercial lead. Which approach best resolves the tension?

## Options

- **A**: Negotiate a pre-agreed change allowance within the contract — a pooled budget and a lightweight approval path for small changes up to defined limits, with formal change orders reserved for larger ones.
- **B**: Ask the vendor to absorb the small changes at no cost as a goodwill gesture, since each one is individually minor relative to the contract value.
- **C**: Keep raising a separate formal change order for every request as the contract requires, accepting the slower flow as the price of contractual discipline.
- **D**: Move the entire engagement onto a time-and-materials basis so that any change can be made instantly without contractual friction.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The fix is to negotiate a mechanism inside the contract that matches process weight to change size — a pooled allowance and fast track for small changes, formal orders only for large ones — which keeps the vendor contractually comfortable while restoring flow. (B) asks the vendor to bear cost it never priced for, which a cooperative-but-contractual vendor will reasonably refuse and which is unsustainable. (C) accepts a known bottleneck rather than negotiating it away, throttling exactly the agile flow the project needs. (D) discards the cost certainty of the fixed-price portion wholesale to solve a problem confined to small changes, an over-correction that re-prices the entire engagement.
