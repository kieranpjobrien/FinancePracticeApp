# Question

A government client wants a research deliverable where the technical approach is uncertain and several design iterations are expected, so total effort cannot be fixed up front. The client is risk-averse about cost overruns but specifically wants the supplier to share the upside of any cost savings rather than just being reimbursed. Allowable costs will be auditable to a clear standard. The project manager is advising on contract structure. Which best fits?

## Options

- **A**: Cost-plus-incentive-fee, with a target cost and a sharing formula so that under-runs against the target are split between client and supplier.
- **B**: Cost-plus-award-fee, where a fixed base fee is topped up by a discretionary award the client grants based on periodic judgements of the supplier's performance.
- **C**: Fixed-price incentive (firm target), so the parties set a target cost and ceiling now and the supplier earns more fee by coming in under target.
- **D**: Cost-plus-fixed-fee, so the supplier recovers audited costs plus a set fee and is fully insulated from the uncertainty in the scope.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The scope is genuinely undefined, which rules out any fixed-price form, and the client's stated aim is to share cost savings against a target — exactly the mechanism a cost-plus-incentive-fee provides, with auditable costs supplying the objective basis the sharing formula needs. (B) is also cost-reimbursable but ties the supplier's extra reward to the client's subjective assessment of performance rather than to measurable cost savings, so it does not deliver the cost-sharing the client specifically asked for. (C) requires committing to a target cost and a ceiling the parties cannot responsibly set when effort is unpredictable, pushing the supplier to load contingency or risk breaching the ceiling. (D) suits the uncertainty but offers no mechanism to reward the supplier for containing cost, missing the client's explicit savings-sharing goal.
