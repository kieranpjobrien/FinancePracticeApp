# Question

A defence systems project will engage a contractor for development work where the final design effort is genuinely uncertain and could vary widely. The buyer is prepared to carry the effort uncertainty and reimburse actual cost, and its own cost engineers can verify spending reliably. In negotiation the buyer's representatives stress that cost is only one of several things they care about: they also want the contractor to stay responsive to design direction, flag problems early, and collaborate across a long programme — and they are wary of fee arguments at settlement. Which contract approach should the project manager pursue?

## Options

- **A**: A cost-plus-incentive-fee contract with a target cost and a sharing ratio, so fee moves up or down with how actual cost lands against the target.
- **B**: A cost-plus-award-fee contract that reimburses cost and pays a fee the buyer determines periodically against stated performance areas, including cost management, responsiveness, and early problem disclosure.
- **C**: A fixed-price-incentive-fee contract with a target cost, a sharing ratio, and a ceiling above which the contractor bears all further cost.
- **D**: A cost-plus-fixed-fee contract that reimburses allowable cost plus a set fee, with the buyer steering the broader behaviours through close oversight rather than the fee.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The buyer will carry the effort uncertainty (so a ceiling is wrong) and cares about several dimensions a cost formula cannot capture — responsiveness, early disclosure, collaboration — alongside cost. Award-fee is built for exactly that: it reimburses cost and lets the buyer reward the rounded performance it actually values through periodic fee determinations, which is the better fit when the goal is broader than a single cost number. (A) cost-plus-incentive-fee rewards only the cost-versus-target axis through its formula; it is clean for cost but blind to the responsiveness and collaboration the buyer explicitly weighs, so it optimises the wrong, narrow thing. (C) fixed-price-incentive-fee puts a ceiling on cost the contractor cannot reasonably estimate, pushing risk onto the seller, who will load contingency or decline — inconsistent with the buyer's willingness to carry that uncertainty. (D) cost-plus-fixed-fee builds in no fee-based incentive at all, leaning entirely on oversight; the buyer wants the fee itself to pull behaviour. The fine margin is A versus B: a formula is more objective and avoids the settlement arguments the buyer dislikes, but it can only price cost, whereas the buyer's stated priorities are multi-dimensional — and award-fee, despite its judgement-based fee, is the structure that can reward all of them.
