# Question

An online retailer is engaging an external agency to build a new recommendation engine using an agile approach, and the backlog is expected to shift substantially as early experiments reveal what actually lifts conversion. The retailer's finance director insists on a hard ceiling on total spend. The agency is wary of an open-ended commitment to undefined work. The project manager is shaping the commercial terms with both. Which structure best reconciles the evolving backlog with the spending ceiling?

## Options

- **A**: A firm fixed price for the full recommendation engine as currently imagined, giving finance an exact figure and the agency a defined target to build to.
- **B**: A pure time-and-materials arrangement with no ceiling, leaving the backlog completely free to evolve and the agency fully protected against scope uncertainty.
- **C**: A capped time-and-materials arrangement: the agency bills for work done as the backlog evolves, but total spend cannot exceed an agreed ceiling, at which point the parties re-decide.
- **D**: A fixed price per agreed feature, with each new backlog item separately priced and approved before the agency begins it, however small the item.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

A capped T&M deal (C) gives finance the hard ceiling it demands while letting the agile backlog flex underneath it, with a defined re-decision point if the cap is reached — fitting both stated interests. (A) forces a firm fixed price onto work everyone expects to change, which will drive heavy contingency pricing or a stream of change disputes. (B) protects the agency but gives finance no ceiling at all, failing the one constraint finance will not move on. (D) imposes per-feature fixed pricing and sign-off on every item, which buries an experiment-driven backlog in transactional overhead and stalls the very iteration the approach depends on.
