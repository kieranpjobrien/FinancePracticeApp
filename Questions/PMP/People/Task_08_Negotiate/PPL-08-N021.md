# Question

Midway through sprint planning for a mobile banking team, the product owner wants two new high-value stories added to a sprint that is already at the team's measured velocity. The developers say the only way to fit both is to drop a partially built feature carried over from the last sprint, or to thin the testing on several stories. The product owner, the developers, and the carried-over feature's original requester are all in the room. As the scrum master, what is the best way to land the sprint agreement?

## Options

- **A**: Ask the product owner to re-order all candidate stories — including the carry-over — by value, then have the developers forecast how far down that order they can pull at a sustainable pace, and confirm the resulting sprint backlog with the room.
- **B**: Have the developers and product owner agree a one-sprint velocity uplift that absorbs the two new stories on top of the carry-over, on the basis that the team has been conservative and can stretch.
- **C**: Ask the product owner and the carry-over's requester to negotiate directly which of the three items is least valuable, and drop that one so the new stories fit.
- **D**: Propose the team take all the work but reduce the sprint to the highest-confidence stories only if it later runs short, treating the carry-over as a stretch goal beyond the forecast.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The sprint agreement holds when the two decisions stay with their owners and the forecast is honest: the product owner orders the whole candidate set (carry-over included) by value, the developers forecast capacity at a sustainable pace, and the team confirms what fits — no item is privileged by history and no quality is silently traded. (B) inflates the forecast on the hope the team was sandbagging; if the stretch fails, the agreement was never real. (C) narrows the decision to a head-to-head between two stakeholders and pre-commits to dropping one item before the developers have forecast what actually fits — it removes the carry-over from the same value ranking rather than placing it in it. (D) commits to all the work first and only protects quality reactively, which is how over-commitment is dressed up as a plan.
