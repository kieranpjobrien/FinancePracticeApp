# Question

During a supplier negotiation, the supplier offers an attractive multi-year deal, but it exceeds the spending authority delegated to the project manager and commits the firm well beyond the current project. The supplier presses for a decision in the room, hinting the terms may not last. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Tell the supplier the terms are genuinely attractive but a commitment of this size needs sponsor approval, and commit to bringing a decision back within a short, specific timeframe.
- **B**: Ask the supplier to put the offer in writing and hold it open for a defined short period, then take it to the sponsor for a decision before the window closes.
- **C**: Negotiate the deal down to the portion that falls within the PM's delegated authority and the current project, agreeing that now, and flag the multi-year extension for later.
- **D**: Get the sponsor on the phone immediately so the decision on the full multi-year commitment can be made while the supplier is still in the room.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A PM must negotiate within delegated authority, and the clean, standard move is to keep the attractive option alive while routing a commitment beyond that authority to the sponsor, with a concrete timeframe so the supplier sees the firm is acting in good faith. (B) is close and reasonable, but the real constraint is the PM's authority, not the offer expiring; asking for a written hold is a useful add-on, yet making it the answer treats the supplier's "may not last" pressure as the binding problem rather than the approval that must happen regardless. (C) needlessly fragments an attractive multi-year deal and may forfeit the volume pricing that made it good, settling for less before the sponsor has even seen it. (D) pressures the sponsor into an unprepared decision on a major multi-year commitment under supplier-applied time pressure, which is exactly the rushed, ill-considered approval that delegated limits exist to prevent.
