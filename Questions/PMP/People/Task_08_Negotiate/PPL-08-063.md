# Question

Midway through a fixed-price contract to build a visitor centre, the museum client asks the contractor to add a rooftop terrace that was never in the agreed scope. The site supervisor has already started informally pricing the extra steelwork with the client's facilities officer, who is keen to "just get it moving". The project manager learns of this when the supervisor mentions the figure in passing. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Ask the supervisor to pause the informal pricing and tell the facilities officer the terrace will be taken forward as a formal variation, so it is scoped, priced, and authorised by the right people before anyone commits.
- **B**: Let the supervisor and facilities officer settle the figure they have started on, then submit it as a change request for the client's and contractor's authorised representatives to ratify.
- **C**: Have the supervisor's estimate worked up properly into a priced variation straight away, so the change request that goes for authorisation reflects the real cost rather than a corridor figure.
- **D**: Notify the client's authorised contract representative that a scope change has been raised through an informal channel and ask them to confirm whether they want it pursued before the contractor expends any estimating effort.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A change to a contract's agreed terms must be negotiated and authorised through the contract's own change-control mechanism, and the immediate risk is that a back-channel figure between people without authority hardens into an apparent commitment. The first move is therefore to stop the informal pricing and signal that the request will run through the proper variation process — capping the exposure and putting the change on a defensible footing. The distractors are all plausible because they accept change control in principle, but each mis-sequences. (B) lets unauthorised parties fix the number first and only formalises afterward, so the negotiation has effectively happened in the wrong room. (C) rushes to produce a priced variation before the request is even validated and authority confirmed, investing effort and creating an anchor prematurely. (D) sensibly involves the client's authorised representative, but routing it as a notification-and-query leaves the live informal pricing running in the meantime; halting that comes first.
