# Question

A vendor delivering a fixed-price warehouse automation system says a requested conveyor reconfiguration is outside the agreed scope and submits a change order worth 18% of the contract value. The project manager thinks the requirement was implied by the original specification, but the contract wording is genuinely ambiguous on the point. Both parties want the project to continue, and the reconfiguration is on the critical path. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Sit down with the vendor to reach a shared interpretation of what the original specification and contract actually committed, settling the in-or-out-of-scope question before any price is discussed.
- **B**: Ask the vendor to provide a detailed cost and labour breakdown supporting the 18% figure, so the change order's value can be tested against what the work genuinely requires.
- **C**: Have the business sponsor re-confirm that the reconfiguration is truly needed for go-live, so the firm is not negotiating to pay for a change it could drop or defer.
- **D**: Authorise the reconfiguration to begin now under an interim time-and-materials arrangement to protect the critical path, and resolve the scope and price question in parallel.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The disagreement is fundamentally about what the existing fixed-price agreement already covers; until both sides reach a shared reading of the original scope, neither the validity of the change order nor its price can be settled, so establishing that common interpretation is the necessary first step. (B) is a strong instinct, but interrogating the price presumes the change is genuinely additional work — if a shared reading shows it was already in scope, the breakdown is moot and the firm should pay nothing extra. (C) is sensible diligence, yet the reconfiguration sits on the critical path and is wanted by both parties, so re-justifying the need is unlikely to be the binding question and risks looking like delay. (D) protects the schedule but, by starting work and opening a new commercial arrangement before the scope question is resolved, it concedes that the work is extra and weakens the firm's position in the very negotiation that has to happen.
