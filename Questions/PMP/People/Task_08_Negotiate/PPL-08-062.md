# Question

On a hybrid programme delivering a hospital scheduling system, the product owner keeps pulling the development team into mid-iteration meetings with clinical stakeholders, and velocity has dropped for two iterations running. The team has raised this in the retrospective. At the next iteration planning, the product owner argues these meetings are essential to keep clinicians engaged and insists they continue as needed. The project manager wants the engagement to continue without delivery sliding further. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Negotiate a working agreement that sets aside a fixed, bounded slot each iteration for clinician sessions and protects the remaining iteration time as uninterrupted delivery.
- **B**: Adjust each iteration's committed forecast downward to reflect the engagement time, so the team's commitments stay realistic while the meetings continue as the product owner wants.
- **C**: Propose the product owner send a delegate or run the clinician sessions herself, bringing developers in only when a specific technical question genuinely needs them.
- **D**: Have the team timebox their own participation to the part of each session that concerns them and return to delivery once that is covered, leaving the cadence to the product owner.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The conflict is between two legitimate interests — keeping clinicians engaged and protecting delivery time — and the most durable resolution is a jointly negotiated working agreement that bounds the engagement while ring-fencing the rest of the iteration, addressing the cause (uncontrolled interruption) rather than just its effect. The distractors each tackle a real facet but settle less. (B) keeps commitments honest yet permanently absorbs the cost of an unbounded demand without curbing the interruption itself. (C) is a sensible substitution the manager might even fold into the agreement, but imposed unilaterally it can read as cutting the developers off from clinicians and may erode the engagement the product owner values. (D) limits exposure session by session but leaves the cadence and the underlying tension unresolved, so the problem is likely to recur next iteration. Only A converts the standing dispute into a stable rule both parties have agreed to.
