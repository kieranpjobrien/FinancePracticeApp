# Question

A newly formed agile team drawn from two merged business units cannot agree how to handle code review: one group insists on two mandatory reviewers per change, the other on a single reviewer for speed, and the disagreement is now spilling into daily friction. As servant-leader, the project manager is helping the team settle it. What is the most effective way to reach a workable agreement?

## Options

- **A**: Choose the two-reviewer standard yourself, since it is the more thorough option and reduces the risk of defects reaching production.
- **B**: Let each former business unit keep its own review practice within the shared team, so neither group has to change how it works.
- **C**: Escalate the disagreement to both units' line managers and adopt whichever standard they jointly decide on.
- **D**: Facilitate the team to agree a review standard together — for example one reviewer by default and two for high-risk changes — and record it as a team ground rule they own.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

A self-organising team owns its working practices, so facilitating them to a shared, risk-tiered standard they record as a ground rule produces an agreement they will actually follow (D). (A) imposes a decision the team did not make, which undercuts ownership even if the option has merit. (B) entrenches two conflicting practices inside one team, perpetuating the friction and undermining the point of merging. (C) sends a team-level working agreement up to line managers who are removed from the work, weakening the team's accountability for its own process.
