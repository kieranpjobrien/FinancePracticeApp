# Question

An agile programme is bringing in an external development partner to supplement the team for an open-ended engagement. The partner wants revenue certainty and is wary of unpriced work, while the programme's detailed requirements are re-shaped from the backlog every sprint. The project manager must negotiate a contract that works for both. Which structure best fits?

## Options

- **A**: A capacity-based agreement buying a defined number of the partner's people for a block of sprints at an agreed rate, with the specific work pulled from the prioritised backlog each iteration.
- **B**: A fixed-price agreement for an initial scope, with a formal change request priced and approved each time the backlog shifts, so every change is covered by a controlled amendment.
- **C**: A fixed-price-by-feature agreement that lets the customer swap any not-yet-started feature for another of similar size at no charge, and exit early if priorities change.
- **D**: A time-and-materials agreement with a not-to-exceed ceiling, billing actual effort each sprint and reviewing burn against the cap at every sprint review.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The partner wants revenue certainty while scope changes every sprint, which is precisely what a capacity-based agreement reconciles: the partner is paid for guaranteed team capacity (certainty) while the programme pulls whatever the backlog prioritises into each sprint (flexibility), with no per-change repricing. (B) gives the partner price certainty but reintroduces a change request for every backlog shift, generating continuous administrative friction in an environment where change is the norm — the opposite of what agile contracting seeks. (C) flexes scope well, but a fixed price per feature requires stable, well-defined features to price, which the evolving backlog does not provide, and the early-exit clause works against the revenue certainty the partner is asking for. (D) tracks actual effort but, even with a ceiling, leaves the partner's revenue dependent on how much work is commissioned each sprint, so it does not deliver the predictable income the partner specifically wants.
