# Question

A project manager has three weeks before a key software vendor's renewal and wants to prepare thoroughly rather than improvise. Several preparation activities are part-done and none is finished, and in one afternoon the PM can realistically take only one of them through to completion. The renewal budget is set and the vendor's published list price is known. Which preparation activity should the PM complete first?

## Options

- **A**: Commission a costed assessment of the in-house build and the one rival product, so the PM knows the firm's true walk-away point before talks begin.
- **B**: Build a detailed model of the vendor's likely cost base and margin to estimate the lowest price they could accept.
- **C**: Draft the full set of service-level definitions and penalty clauses the firm would like to see in the renewed contract.
- **D**: Prepare a concession plan listing, in order, the terms the firm is willing to give away and what it would want in return for each.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four are legitimate preparation activities, so the question is which one most changes the PM's footing. The budget and list price are already in hand, but the firm still has no assessed alternative — meaning the PM does not yet know the firm's BATNA — and without a credible walk-away point, every other piece of preparation (concession plan, SLA wish-list, estimate of the vendor's floor) is calibrated against nothing. Establishing the BATNA first sets the ceiling that disciplines all subsequent moves. (B) estimating the vendor's reservation price is useful but speculative and far less reliable than knowing the firm's own alternative; it also presumes a ZOPA the PM cannot yet locate. (C) drafting SLAs is necessary detail work but defines what the firm wants, not what it can credibly hold out for. (D) a concession plan is valuable, yet sequencing give-aways is premature until the walk-away point that bounds those concessions is known.
