# Question

An agile delivery team needs a specialist UX vendor for the next three releases. Procurement's standard template is a fixed-price, fixed-scope contract requiring a detailed statement of work signed before any work starts. The product backlog will evolve release by release, so a frozen scope conflicts with how the team works. What is the project manager's best move when negotiating the agreement with procurement and the vendor?

## Options

- **A**: Accept the fixed-price, fixed-scope contract and have the team treat each release's requirements as final once the SOW is signed.
- **B**: Negotiate a contract structure that fixes price per sprint or per release while allowing the backlog content within each increment to be re-prioritised by the product owner.
- **C**: Engage the vendor on a pure time-and-materials basis with no ceiling, so the team retains full flexibility over what is built.
- **D**: Split the engagement into many tiny fixed-price SOWs, signing a fresh one for every user story before the vendor starts it.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The right agreement aligns the commercial structure with the delivery approach; fixing cost per increment while leaving scope flexible inside it gives procurement budget certainty and the team adaptive scope — a recognised agile contracting pattern. (A) forces a predictive contract onto adaptive work and surrenders the team's ability to re-prioritise. (C) gives flexibility but removes the cost predictability procurement legitimately needs, weakening the PM's negotiating position. (D) creates crippling administrative overhead and effectively reintroduces upfront fixing at story granularity.
