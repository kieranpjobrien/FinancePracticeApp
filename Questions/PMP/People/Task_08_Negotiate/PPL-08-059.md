# Question

A food-manufacturing company is bringing an external development vendor onto an agile programme to deliver a traceability system. Before work starts, the project manager wants to negotiate a few agreements that will prevent disputes over what "finished" means once delivery is under way. Which TWO agreements are most important to settle with the vendor up front? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: A shared definition of done that states the conditions every increment must meet before it counts as complete.
- **B**: An agreed acceptance process defining who reviews delivered increments, against what criteria, and within what timeframe.
- **C**: A fixed list of every user story to be built across the entire programme, locked before any sprint begins.
- **D**: A penalty schedule imposing financial deductions on the vendor for any story not delivered within its original sprint.
- **E**: A commitment that the vendor's developers will attend the company's all-hands meetings throughout the engagement.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

Disputes over "finished" are prevented by agreeing, before work starts, a shared definition of done (A) and an explicit acceptance process with criteria and timeframes (B); together they give both sides one standard for completeness. (C) locks the entire backlog up front, which contradicts the agile approach and guarantees friction as priorities evolve. (D) reaches for penalties before any collaborative footing exists, encouraging gaming and adversarial behaviour rather than shared quality. (E) concerns engagement logistics, not the meaning of done, and does nothing to prevent acceptance disputes.
