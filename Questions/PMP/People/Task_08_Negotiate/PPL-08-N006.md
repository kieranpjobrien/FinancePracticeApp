# Question

On a hybrid programme delivering a regulated banking API, the per-sprint automated security scan in the team's definition of done has grown to run for several hours, and the product owner says it is now the main reason fewer features ship each sprint. The scan is mandated by the firm's compliance function and cannot be dropped, but the product owner, the developers, and the security lead disagree on how to recover the lost throughput. The project manager is facilitating the conversation. What is the best way to reach a durable agreement?

## Options

- **A**: Have the group, including the security lead, agree a split: a fast incremental scan inside every sprint plus a full scan that gates each release, only if the security lead confirms the split meets the compliance obligation.
- **B**: Broker an agreement to run the full scan on a dedicated pipeline in parallel with development each sprint, so the gate stays intact and the team is no longer waiting on it to finish a story.
- **C**: Get the team and product owner to agree a slightly lower per-sprint story commitment that absorbs the scan time as a known cost, keeping the control exactly as compliance mandated it.
- **D**: Facilitate agreement to time-box the scan and triage only its highest-severity findings within the sprint, deferring lower-severity findings to a backlog cleared before release.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The control is mandatory, so the negotiation is not whether to keep it but how to recover throughput without weakening it, and the only option that both restructures the work and keeps the compliance owner as the arbiter of sufficiency is the incremental-plus-release-gate split conditioned on the security lead's sign-off — exactly who must own that judgement on a regulated product. (B) is a strong technical answer and may well be the right mechanism, but it assumes the scan can be fully parallelised without becoming a blocking release gate anyway, and it commits to a pipeline solution before the security lead has confirmed it satisfies the obligation. (C) keeps the control untouched and is the safe fallback, but it simply accepts the throughput loss rather than negotiating a way to recover it, which is what the parties are actually trying to solve. (D) changes what the gate enforces by letting lower-severity findings cross into a release-bound backlog, which alters the compliance posture the firm mandated and is the security lead's decision to make, not a facilitation default.
