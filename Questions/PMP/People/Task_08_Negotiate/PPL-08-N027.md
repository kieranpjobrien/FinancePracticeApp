# Question

A project manager is negotiating a multi-year managed-services agreement with a supplier who will host a critical customer-facing platform. The PM wants the agreement to protect the project's interests over the term without making the supplier price in so much risk that the deal becomes uncompetitive. All five provisions below are reasonable to include. Which TWO should the PM treat as the highest priority to secure in the agreement? (Choose two.)

## Options

- **A**: Service levels defined by measurable performance metrics (e.g. availability, response time) with proportionate, pre-agreed remedies if they are missed.
- **B**: A governance and escalation mechanism with named contacts and a defined review cadence for monitoring performance and resolving disputes as they arise.
- **C**: A benchmarking clause allowing the buyer to compare pricing against the market every two years and request a review.
- **D**: A right to audit the supplier's security controls annually against the agreed standards.
- **E**: An assignment clause restricting the supplier from transferring the contract to a third party without the buyer's consent.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

For a multi-year, critical, customer-facing service, the two provisions that do the heaviest lifting are an enforceable definition of *what good performance is* (A) and a *standing mechanism to manage and adapt the relationship* over the term (B) — together they let the buyer hold the supplier to account day-to-day and steer the deal as conditions change, which is exactly where ongoing value and risk live. (C) benchmarking, (D) audit rights, and (E) assignment control are all legitimate and worth negotiating, but each addresses a narrower, more occasional risk: price drift, periodic assurance, and a change of counterparty respectively. They protect against specific future events rather than governing the core performance the buyer depends on every day, so they rank below A and B when the PM must prioritise.
