# Question

A project manager for a regional rail upgrade is about to negotiate a critical signalling subcontract that will set both cost and the delivery window for an entire phase. The PM wants to walk in genuinely prepared rather than improvising. Which TWO actions best prepare the PM for a sound negotiation? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Identify the project's walk-away alternative if no deal is reached, and the point beyond which agreeing would be worse than walking away.
- **B**: Research the subcontractor's likely interests, constraints, and pressures so the PM can shape options that work for both sides.
- **C**: Decide in advance the single price the PM will accept and resolve to hold it regardless of what emerges in the discussion.
- **D**: Prepare a detailed opening demand pitched far beyond any realistic settlement so there is maximum room to be negotiated down.
- **E**: Arrange for the sponsor to attend so the subcontractor feels the weight of senior authority across the table from the outset.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

Real preparation means knowing your own walk-away and reservation point (A) and understanding the other party's interests and constraints so you can build agreements that hold (B); together these let the PM judge any offer and craft workable trades. (C) fixes a single number before learning anything, which forecloses value-creating options and turns the meeting into a standoff. (D) is an extreme anchor that risks signalling bad faith and damaging a relationship the project will depend on through delivery. (E) frames the meeting as a power display rather than a problem to solve and can entrench the other side rather than open it up.
