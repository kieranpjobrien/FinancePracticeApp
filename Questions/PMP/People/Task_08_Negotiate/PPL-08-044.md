# Question

Midway through a pharmaceutical packaging-line installation, the sponsor asks the project manager to add an additional validation suite that was never in scope, and wants it inside the existing budget and the original go-live date. The project manager has analysed it and is certain all three — the new scope, the fixed budget, and the unchanged date — cannot hold together. The sponsor is influential and is pressing for a yes. The project manager is meeting the sponsor to settle what will actually happen. What is the most effective approach?

## Options

- **A**: Agree to the new scope and date now to keep the sponsor's confidence, and raise the budget or schedule strain later once the work has visibly started to slip.
- **B**: Present the quantified trade-offs and negotiate which constraint the sponsor will relax — funding, date, or another scope item — so the sponsor chooses against real information.
- **C**: Decline the request on the basis that it is out of scope and the triple constraint cannot absorb it without a formal change being raised first.
- **D**: Accept the scope within the current budget and date, and protect the outcome by quietly trimming contingency and compressing the test window to make the numbers work.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The PM's job is to convert an impossible "all three" demand into an informed choice by laying out the quantified trade-offs and negotiating which constraint moves, so the sponsor decides with the facts visible. (A) buys short-term goodwill by concealing a certainty and guarantees a worse conversation when the slip surfaces. (C) is technically correct about scope but is a flat refusal that offers the sponsor no path forward and squanders the chance to find a workable deal. (D) silently consumes contingency and test rigour on a validated pharmaceutical line, trading away quality and risk buffer the sponsor never agreed to spend.
