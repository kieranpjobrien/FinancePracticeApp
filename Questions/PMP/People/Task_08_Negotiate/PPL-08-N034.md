# Question

A newly formed cross-functional agile team is establishing its working agreements at the start of an engagement. The scrum master wants the agreements to genuinely shape day-to-day behaviour rather than become a forgotten document. All five options below are reasonable. Which TWO most directly serve that aim? (Choose two.)

## Options

- **A**: Have the team co-create the agreements themselves, with the scrum master facilitating, rather than the scrum master drafting them for the team to ratify.
- **B**: Post the agreements prominently in the team's physical and virtual workspace so they stay visible during the working day.
- **C**: Revisit the agreements at retrospectives and adjust them as the team learns what actually helps and what it ignores.
- **D**: Have the scrum master gently call out in the moment when an agreement is being broken, so the agreements are consistently reinforced.
- **E**: Capture the agreements in a clearly written, accessible document so every member and any new joiner can refer to the exact wording.

## Answer

A, C

## Explanation

Agreements change behaviour when the team *owns* them and keeps them *alive*: co-creation (A) builds the genuine commitment that compliance-by-sign-off never produces, and revisiting them at retrospectives (C) keeps them matched to how the team actually works as it matures — ownership plus inspect-and-adapt are the two levers that make the difference. (B) visibility, (D) in-the-moment reinforcement, and (E) clear documentation are all helpful supporting practices, but each assumes the team already believes in the agreements; a posted, well-worded, consistently-policed set of rules the team did not author and cannot change will still be resented and worked around. They amplify ownership where it exists rather than create it.
