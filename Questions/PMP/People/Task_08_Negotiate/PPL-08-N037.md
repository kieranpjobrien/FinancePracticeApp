# Question

A contractor on a fixed-price infrastructure project submits a substantial claim for additional payment, arguing the buyer's late site access caused delay and extra cost. The project manager assesses the claim as partly valid but materially overstated. The same contractor must complete critical remaining works, and the contract includes a defined claims-and-disputes procedure with a documented escalation path. How should the PM approach this?

## Options

- **A**: Acknowledge the claim and follow the contract's defined claims procedure — formally requesting the contractor's substantiating records and issuing the buyer's assessment through the documented steps — before negotiating any figure.
- **B**: Open direct negotiation with the contractor to separate the genuinely attributable delay and cost from the overstated portion, working toward a fair settlement that preserves the relationship for the remaining works.
- **C**: Commission an independent quantum assessment of the delay and cost first, so both parties enter any negotiation from a neutral, evidence-based valuation of what is genuinely attributable.
- **D**: Propose mediation by a neutral third party, given the claim is contested, to reach a settlement without the cost and relationship damage of formal proceedings.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The claim has a valid core, is overstated, and the counterparty is still needed for delivery — a direct, good-faith negotiation that splits the attributable cost from the inflation resolves it fastest, protects the working relationship, and is the proportionate first move. (A) following the formal claims procedure is correct and may become necessary, but jumping straight to formal substantiation and assessments hardens the exchange and signals dispute before the parties have tried to simply settle; the procedure is the fallback if negotiation fails. (C) an independent quantum assessment is useful where the facts are genuinely unclear, but here the PM already has a view of what is attributable; commissioning one first adds cost and delay before a direct conversation has been attempted. (D) mediation is premature — it brings in a third party before the two sides have tried to resolve a partly-agreed claim between themselves, which is slower and signals an impasse that does not yet exist.
