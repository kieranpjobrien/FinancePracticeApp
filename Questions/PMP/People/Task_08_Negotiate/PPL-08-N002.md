# Question

A project manager is negotiating a support contract with the only vendor whose product currently integrates with the firm's platform. The vendor's quoted price is 35% above budget and has not moved after two rounds. A completed internal study shows the firm could integrate a competing product in-house within three months at a comparable annual cost, and the integration team has confirmed it has the capacity. The firm would prefer to stay with the incumbent if the price is reasonable. How should the project manager use this in the next session?

## Options

- **A**: State that the firm now has a viable in-house alternative and lay out its cost and timeline as the objective benchmark the support price needs to compete with, while signalling a continued preference for the incumbent at fair terms.
- **B**: Tell the vendor the firm will move to the in-house build unless the price is at budget by the end of the session, giving a hard deadline so the vendor knows the alternative is real and imminent.
- **C**: Keep the alternative undisclosed for now and instead probe the vendor's pricing — asking which cost components are driving the 35% — to look for a negotiated reduction before revealing any leverage.
- **D**: Begin the in-house integration in parallel now, mention it to the vendor as already under way, and continue the price talks from the stronger position that creates.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A BATNA is most powerful when used to set an objective standard the other side's offer must beat, framed in a way that keeps a deal achievable — here, presenting the credible in-house option as the benchmark while signalling preference for the incumbent invites the vendor to compete on price without forcing a rupture. (B) converts the same alternative into a deadline-backed ultimatum; it may work but risks hardening the vendor or collapsing a relationship the firm would rather keep, and concedes nothing to the firm's own stated preference. (C) is a legitimate tactic but voluntarily withholds the firm's strongest source of leverage and negotiates from apparent weakness when a disciplined disclosure would do more. (D) commits real money and effort to the alternative before the negotiation has run, spending the firm's resources to manufacture pressure it could exert simply by citing the option.
