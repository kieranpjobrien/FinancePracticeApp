# Question

A project manager needs a specialist embedded-systems engineer for ten weeks to hit a regulatory deadline. The only suitable internal engineer reports to a functional manager who, citing his own backlog, has offered just four weeks. The project manager has quietly confirmed that a contract engineer of equal calibre is available at short notice within budget, though onboarding would cost about a week and the internal engineer knows the legacy platform far better. Going into the meeting with the functional manager, how should the project manager use this knowledge?

## Options

- **A**: State plainly and early that an equally qualified contractor is lined up, so the functional manager negotiates knowing the project has a real alternative and can weigh that against losing his engineer's platform knowledge.
- **B**: Keep the contractor option in reserve and first explore whether a phased release of the internal engineer could meet the deadline, drawing on the alternative only if the talks stall.
- **C**: Treat the four weeks as settled and plan to bring the contractor in for the remaining six, proposing a handover so the two engineers overlap on the legacy platform.
- **D**: Lead with the regulatory deadline and the consequences of missing it, holding the contractor back so the functional manager engages with the project's need rather than reaching for an easy substitute.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A credible alternative to a negotiated agreement (a BATNA) is most valuable as quiet confidence that lets you push for the best joint outcome rather than as a card to be played immediately — so holding the contractor in reserve while first testing a phased internal release protects the relationship and may yield the genuinely better option, given the internal engineer's superior platform knowledge. The distractors are stronger than simple errors. (A) is legitimate transparency and sometimes the right call, but disclosing the alternative up front anchors the talk around substitution and can harden the functional manager's stance before any creative arrangement is explored, for little gain over keeping it as a fallback. (C) is a workable plan and the overlap mitigates the continuity risk, but accepting the split before testing whether a better arrangement exists forfeits the value the alternative was meant to protect. (D) keeps the alternative in reserve, which is right, but leaning on deadline pressure alone is a weaker route to agreement than constructively exploring a phased release.
