# Question

A project manager is negotiating a shared services agreement among three internal departments that must co-fund and co-staff a data platform. Their priorities genuinely differ: one cares most about data security, one about cost, one about delivery speed. The PM wants an agreement all three will actively support. Which THREE approaches best help reach a value-creating agreement? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Map what each department values most highly, since the differences in priority are exactly what create room for trades that leave every party better off.
- **B**: Look for terms where a concession that is low-cost for one department is high-value to another, expanding the total value on the table before it is divided.
- **C**: Capture the final agreement on funding, staffing, and service levels in writing, so each department's commitment is explicit and shared.
- **D**: Let the department contributing the largest share of the budget set the platform's priorities, on the basis that it carries the most financial risk.
- **E**: Settle each contested term by splitting the difference between the departments' stated positions, so no department feels it lost on any single point.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Multi-party value creation comes from mapping each party's distinct priorities (A), then trading concessions that are cheap to give but valuable to receive (B), and finally locking the result in an explicit written agreement so commitment is shared and unambiguous (C). (D) allocates control by spending power rather than interests, which suppresses the security- and speed-focused departments' buy-in and forfeits the very differences that make win-win trades possible. (E) reflexively compromises on every term, which ignores that the departments value different things — splitting each point down the middle leaves each party half-satisfied on what it cares about instead of fully satisfied on its priority in exchange for yielding on what it does not, the trade that actually creates value.
