# Question

In a negotiation over a software licensing renewal for a university, the vendor's representative becomes visibly frustrated and keeps restating the same demand for a multi-year commitment. The project manager senses there is a concern underneath that the vendor has not articulated. What is the most useful thing for the project manager to do?

## Options

- **A**: Restate the university's own position more firmly so the vendor understands the buyer will not be moved by repetition.
- **B**: Call a halt and reconvene another day, since the vendor's frustration means productive discussion is no longer possible.
- **C**: Ask open questions about why the multi-year commitment matters so much to the vendor, and listen for the concern driving the demand.
- **D**: Concede the multi-year commitment to defuse the vendor's frustration and keep the meeting moving towards a close.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

When a party keeps repeating a demand, the underlying interest is usually unspoken; asking why it matters and listening for the real concern is what opens the path to options that satisfy both sides (C). (A) meets repetition with repetition and hardens the deadlock rather than understanding it. (B) abandons a workable moment over ordinary frustration and simply defers the same blockage. (D) concedes a significant term to soothe an emotion, without ever learning whether a cheaper option would have met the vendor's actual need.
