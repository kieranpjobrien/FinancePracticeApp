# Question

Six months into a two-year vendor contract, the vendor requests a 15% price increase citing input-cost inflation. Switching vendors would delay the project by four months. What should the PM do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Reject the request outright and hold the vendor to the contracted price, since switching costs are not the vendor's problem to bear at all.
- **B**: Accept the 15% increase immediately to protect the schedule and route it through the change-control process as a cost variance item this month.
- **C**: Offer the vendor a smaller increase in exchange for extending the contract term and adding scope that both parties have already discussed informally.
- **D**: Examine the contract's price-variation clauses, benchmark the movement against objective indices, and meet the vendor to separate pass-through from margin protection.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

PMI's first move when an agreement is challenged is to return to the contract and objective data before negotiating, so the conversation is grounded in facts rather than leverage. (A) risks breach, damaged relationship and a retaliatory performance drop on a locked-in vendor. (B) concedes without testing the request and sets a precedent for future unilateral demands. (C) is a reasonable eventual move, but jumping to package deals before understanding the vendor's actual cost position is premature.
