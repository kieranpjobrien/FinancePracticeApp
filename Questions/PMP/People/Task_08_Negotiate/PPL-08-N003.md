# Question

During sprint planning for a retail mobile app, the product owner wants all nine high-value stories committed for the upcoming two-week sprint. The team's velocity over the last four sprints averages six stories of comparable size, and the developers judge nine to be beyond their capacity. The product owner is under board pressure to demonstrate progress and the stories are all genuinely valuable. The project manager is facilitating. What is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: Help the product owner and team use the velocity history to agree the highest-value subset that fits the sprint, and make explicit which stories move to the next sprint so the board sees a credible forecast.
- **B**: Ask the product owner to identify the two or three stories that matter most for the board, and have the team commit to those plus a stretch goal of the remainder if capacity allows.
- **C**: Let the team pull stories from the top of the priority order and stop at its own forecast, leaving the product owner to see how far down the list six stories reach.
- **D**: Suggest the team commit to the nine for this sprint as an experiment, then inspect the actual outcome at the review and recalibrate the forecast if they fall short.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Negotiating sprint scope is an interest-based discussion grounded in empirical data: the team owns capacity, the product owner owns priority, and velocity is the objective basis for jointly agreeing what fits — naming the deferred stories also gives the board the realistic progress picture the product owner actually needs. (B) is close and reasonable, but a vague "stretch goal of the remainder" reintroduces an over-commitment the velocity data already says is unrealistic and lets the original nine-story expectation persist rather than resolving it. (C) reaches a defensible number but by letting the team act unilaterally rather than reaching a shared agreement, leaving the product owner's priority interest unaddressed and the cut-off arbitrary. (D) deliberately commits to a forecast the team has reason to believe is unachievable, manufacturing a predictable shortfall and devaluing the commitment, which is poor practice even framed as an experiment.
