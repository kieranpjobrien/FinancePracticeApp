# Question

A project manager has an important negotiation tomorrow with a key supplier over delivery milestones and associated penalties. The PM has limited preparation time this evening. All of the options are useful. Which should the PM prioritise to be best positioned in the negotiation?

## Options

- **A**: Assemble the supporting data — historical delivery performance, the cost of each day of delay, and benchmark lead times — so any figure put forward can be substantiated.
- **B**: Draft a clear agenda and desired sequence for the meeting and circulate it to the supplier in advance so the discussion stays focused and efficient.
- **C**: Brief the PM's own sponsor on the negotiation range and secure pre-approval of the limits, so the PM can commit in the room without pausing to seek authority.
- **D**: Clarify the project's own priorities among the milestones, anticipate the supplier's likely interests and constraints, and decide the fallback to take if no acceptable agreement is reached.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The single most valuable preparation is knowing your own priorities, the other side's likely interests, and your walk-away alternative — this is the frame that lets the PM trade intelligently, recognise a good deal, and know when to stop, and every other preparation hangs off it. (A) supporting data is important, but data only persuades once the PM knows which milestones matter most and what the fallback is; arguing figures without that frame can win points on the wrong issues. (B) an agenda aids efficiency but does nothing to improve the substance of the deal the PM can reach. (C) sponsor pre-approval and a clear mandate are genuinely useful, yet the PM must first work out the priorities and fallback that *define* the range before it can be approved. D is the prerequisite the others depend on.
