# Question

A fixed-budget, fixed-deadline agile project is forecast to deliver about 80% of the originally hoped-for backlog. Velocity has been stable and realistic for six sprints. The business sponsor presses the project manager: "I need you to find a way to fit it all in." The product owner is available and has not yet been brought into the conversation. How should the PM negotiate expectations with the sponsor?

## Options

- **A**: Show the sponsor the velocity forecast and ask which of the budget or the deadline they would prefer to relax, presenting scope as fixed and the two other constraints as the levers available.
- **B**: Agree to re-plan the remaining sprints around the full backlog using a more optimistic velocity assumption justified by the team now being past its early ramp-up, and report the revised forecast back to the sponsor.
- **C**: Bring the product owner in with the sponsor, use the six-sprint velocity to show what the fixed constraints can hold, and agree to guarantee the highest-value items while treating the lowest-value 20% as the candidates to drop or defer.
- **D**: Commit to delivering the full backlog and manage the gap by descoping the acceptance criteria on lower-priority stories so they can still be marked done within the timebox.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

With budget and time genuinely fixed, scope is the lever, and the negotiation belongs with the person who owns scope value — so the PM brings the product owner in, grounds the discussion in empirical velocity, and converts "fit it all in" into "guarantee the most valuable 80%." (A) is not unreasonable in principle, but it pre-frames scope as untouchable and pushes the sponsor straight to relaxing budget or deadline before the cheaper option — re-prioritising scope with the product owner — has even been put on the table. (B) manufactures a velocity uplift from a hopeful assumption rather than the data; six stable sprints are the evidence, and overriding them re-creates the over-commitment. (D) hits the number by quietly lowering the bar for "done," trading real quality for apparent completion.
