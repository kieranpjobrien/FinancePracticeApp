# Question

In a scaled-agile programme, three product teams all need the same scarce performance-testing environment in the upcoming release window, and there is only enough capacity for two to use it fully. Each team's lead argues their feature is the most business-critical. The release-train engineer asks the project manager to broker an arrangement the three can accept rather than have it imposed. Which approach is most likely to produce a durable agreement?

## Options

- **A**: Allocate the environment equally in thirds across the window so every team gets the same share and no one can claim unfair treatment.
- **B**: Have each team lead submit a written case and decide the allocation yourself, announcing it as final so the dispute does not drag on.
- **C**: Give the environment to the two teams whose leads argue most forcefully for priority, on the basis that they evidently need it most.
- **D**: Facilitate the leads to rank the competing features against a shared business-value measure and agree a sequenced access plan, with the lowest-ranked team taking a defined alternative.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

A durable multi-party allocation comes from agreeing a shared value criterion, sequencing access against it, and giving the team that loses out a defined alternative, so the outcome is owned rather than imposed (D). (A) sounds fair but splits a resource that cannot deliver in thirds, likely leaving all three unable to complete their testing. (B) substitutes the PM's authority for the parties' agreement, producing a decision the losing teams have no stake in and will resist. (C) rewards the loudest advocacy rather than genuine business value, which is both arbitrary and corrosive to trust across the train.
