# Question

Three departments are co-funding a shared analytics platform. Each has submitted requirements that, taken together, exceed the agreed budget, and each department head believes their own requirements are the most important. The project manager must converge on a single agreed scope that all three will support. What is the most effective negotiation approach?

## Options

- **A**: Run a structured weighted-scoring exercise in which the three heads agree the scoring criteria, score every requirement, and accept the ranked output as the basis for what is funded.
- **B**: Facilitate a session where the heads negotiate trade-offs directly with each other, each conceding lower-value items in exchange for protecting their highest-value ones until the set fits the budget.
- **C**: Convene the three heads together to first agree the platform's shared business objectives, then jointly prioritise all requirements against that common value before fitting them to the budget.
- **D**: Work with each department head separately to sort their own requirements into must-have, should-have, and could-have, then combine the must-haves into the funded scope and treat the rest as a shared backlog.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Competing positions only become a jointly-owned decision once the parties share a *common frame of value*; agreeing the platform's overall objectives first gives the subsequent prioritisation a basis everyone has bought into, which is what makes the agreed scope durable rather than a tally of who pushed hardest. (A) weighted scoring is a strong technique, but applied before shared objectives are agreed it just formalises three competing agendas — the criteria and weights become the new battleground. (D) per-department MoSCoW optimises within each silo and can fund three sets of local must-haves that miss the platform's cross-cutting value, the very thing a shared platform exists to capture. (B) direct horse-trading can reach a deal, but without an agreed value frame the trades reflect negotiating strength, not organisational benefit, and the losing department leaves resentful. C establishes the shared value that A, B, or D would then operationalise.
