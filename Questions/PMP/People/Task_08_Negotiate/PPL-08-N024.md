# Question

A project manager is about to enter final pricing talks with the only vendor that bid on a niche telemetry component. Internally, an engineer has proposed a credible workaround that would let the team build a reduced in-house version — slower and missing some features, but viable — avoiding the vendor entirely. The PM has confirmed the workaround is technically feasible but has not yet costed it precisely. The vendor does not know the option exists. How should the PM prepare to use this in the negotiation?

## Options

- **A**: Treat the existence of a feasible workaround as sufficient leverage, open with an aggressive target price, and signal to the vendor that the team has a viable alternative without disclosing what it is.
- **B**: Cost the in-house workaround to a firm number first, set the reservation price from it, and negotiate toward the best vendor price while holding the alternative in reserve unless the vendor's offer exceeds that walk-away point.
- **C**: Cost the workaround, then open the conversation by sharing the in-house figure transparently and inviting the vendor to come in below it, using openness to build a long-term supply relationship.
- **D**: Use the workaround's feasibility to justify deferring the negotiation, restart a market search for a second vendor, and only return to this vendor once a competing bid exists.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A credible alternative is only usable as leverage once it is quantified: costing the workaround converts "feasible" into a concrete reservation price, which is what tells the PM when to walk away and anchors a firm position even against a sole bidder — and holding it in reserve preserves its value. (A) negotiates aggressively before the walk-away point is actually known, so the PM cannot tell whether a given offer is good enough; "we have an alternative" without a number behind it is a bluff that collapses under probing. (C) gives away the figure prematurely; the vendor may simply match it rather than beat it, and a feature-reduced internal build is not a relationship-building benchmark to hand over. (D) discards real present leverage in favour of a speculative second bid that costs time the integration may not have, when a strong deal is achievable now.
