# Question

A project manager negotiating a bespoke fabrication contract for an offshore wind project has reached an impasse: the supplier will not move below a price that sits just above the project's reservation point, and is pressing for an immediate yes. The PM has one credible alternative supplier who could deliver to spec at a slightly lower price, though with a less proven delivery record. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Accept the supplier's price immediately, since it falls within the reservation point and the supplier is demanding a decision now.
- **B**: Bluff that the project will walk away entirely unless the supplier drops the price, regardless of whether the alternative is truly preferable.
- **C**: Weigh the supplier's offer against the alternative — price, delivery confidence, and risk — and proceed only if this deal genuinely beats walking away to the other option.
- **D**: Reject the offer on principle because the supplier refused to move, and switch to the alternative supplier to avoid rewarding inflexibility.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

At an impasse, the right test is whether the offer on the table beats the best alternative once price, delivery confidence and risk are all weighed — that comparison, not the pressure to decide, drives the choice (C). (A) accepts under time pressure without making the comparison the situation calls for, even though a better alternative may exist. (B) threatens a walk-away the PM may not actually prefer, risking a bluff being called and the deal collapsing on a tactic rather than a judgement. (D) lets pique decide and switches suppliers on principle, ignoring that the incumbent's stronger delivery record might still make it the better deal.
