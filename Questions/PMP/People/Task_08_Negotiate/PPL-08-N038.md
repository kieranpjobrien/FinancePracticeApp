# Question

A project manager will work with the same specialist supplier across several future projects whose scope is not yet defined. In the current single-project negotiation the PM holds strong leverage: the supplier badly needs this win and would accept a price near its own cost floor. The PM is accountable for securing genuinely good commercial terms on this project, and equally for the cooperation and quality the project depends on from this supplier later. Both parties say they value the ongoing relationship. What stance best serves the PM's accountabilities?

## Options

- **A**: Settle this project at a price that gives the supplier a workable margin while still beating the PM's own benchmark, and keep the wider relationship out of the commercials so each future project is negotiated on its own merits.
- **B**: Convert the leverage into a standing arrangement now — lock in the supplier's current keen rates as the baseline for the future work in exchange for a modest concession on this project — so the relationship is secured on terms set while the PM is strong.
- **C**: Take the low price the supplier has offered on this project, and separately give the supplier a documented commitment to preferred-supplier status on the future projects to offset the thin margin and keep the relationship healthy.
- **D**: Hold a firm but not floor price on this project and use the room created to settle non-price terms — payment timing, change handling, escalation contacts — that make the supplier dependable across the future work.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Two facts decide this: the future scope is *undefined*, and a one-project squeeze damages the cooperation the PM is equally accountable for. (A) takes the available value on this project (beating the benchmark) without paying for it in degraded goodwill or in commitments made blind — leaving the supplier a workable margin protects the relationship, and refusing to bind the undefined future work keeps each later deal negotiable on its own facts. (B) locks the future rate while the PM holds the upper hand, but "keen rates" set against work whose scope is unknown become a misfit the moment that scope firms up, and a baseline extracted under pressure is the kind of term that frays; it spends future flexibility to bank a present advantage. (C) trades a real, documented future commitment (preferred-supplier status) to justify accepting a price so low it strains the supplier now — it solves the margin problem by mortgaging future competitive tension the PM may need. (D) is a sound and relationship-aware deal, but conceding the project's *own* terms — payment timing, change handling — to buy future dependability shifts cost or risk onto this project to benefit work that has no defined scope yet; the future relationship should not be funded out of this project's interests. The fine margin is between A and D: both protect the relationship, but only A does so without trading away something this project needs.
