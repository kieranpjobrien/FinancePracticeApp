# Question

A project manager is negotiating a two-year framework with a specialist supplier the firm expects to depend on for several future projects. Independent benchmarking shows the supplier's opening rate is about 12% above the market median. The firm has real budget pressure this year. How should the PM approach the price discussion?

## Options

- **A**: Present the benchmarking data as the objective basis for a rate at or near the market median, and pair it with a two-year volume commitment so the supplier gains predictable revenue in return for the lower price.
- **B**: Use the firm's status as a long-term, repeat client to ask the supplier for their best possible rate as a goodwill gesture, accepting whatever figure they offer to keep the relationship warm.
- **C**: Run a competitive process with two other specialists and use the lowest quote obtained to drive the supplier's rate down as far as it will go for this framework.
- **D**: Accept the supplier's opening rate to avoid friction at the start of a multi-year relationship, planning to negotiate harder at the first annual review once trust is established.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A durable agreement with a supplier the firm will rely on repeatedly should be both fair and grounded in objective criteria, and the strongest move trades something the supplier values — guaranteed multi-year volume — for the rate reduction the benchmarking justifies, creating value on both sides rather than simply extracting it. (B) leans on the relationship but gives the supplier no concrete reason to move and surrenders the firm's objective leverage, likely settling above market. (C) may win a low price this cycle but treats a strategic long-term partner as a commodity, risking the cooperation and priority the firm will need on future projects. (D) protects the relationship at the cost of locking in a 12% premium across the framework and merely defers a harder negotiation to a moment when the firm has even less leverage.
