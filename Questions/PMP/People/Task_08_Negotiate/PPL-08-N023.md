# Question

In a weak matrix organisation, a project manager needs a senior database engineer for six weeks during a critical integration phase. The functional manager who owns the engineer says she is fully committed to operational support and cannot be released. The integration genuinely cannot proceed at the required quality without that expertise, and the phase starts in two weeks. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the dated integration plan and the resource request to the functional manager and ask her to formally confirm in writing that the resource cannot be released, so the constraint is documented before next steps.
- **B**: Ask the sponsor to arbitrate between the project's need and operational support, presenting both the integration impact and the functional manager's stated commitments for a decision.
- **C**: Meet the functional manager to understand exactly what the operational commitments are and when they peak, then jointly explore options — part-time allocation, a timing shift, or a backfill — that could free enough of the engineer's time.
- **D**: Propose pairing a mid-level engineer who is available with a few hours of the senior engineer's review time each week, reducing the senior engineer's required allocation to something the functional manager might accept.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Negotiating an internal resource agreement starts by understanding the other party's actual constraints, not just the stated "no" — only once the PM knows when the operational load peaks can any option (partial release, re-timing, backfill, or a pairing arrangement) be shaped to satisfy both needs, so the exploratory conversation must come first. (A) documents the refusal before any negotiation has happened, which hardens the position and signals the PM is building an escalation file rather than solving the problem. (B) escalates to the sponsor before the two peers have genuinely tried to find a deal — premature, and it spends political capital. (D) is a sound *proposal*, but advancing a specific solution before understanding the functional manager's real constraints risks solving the wrong problem; it is a move to make *during* the negotiation C initiates, not before it.
