# Question

A project manager is negotiating the project charter with several senior stakeholders who hold differing views on the project's objectives and success measures. The PM wants a charter that is durable rather than a fragile compromise. Which TWO actions best support reaching a sound, shared agreement? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Surface the underlying interest behind each stakeholder's stated objective, so the charter can be shaped to satisfy real needs rather than only the headline demands.
- **B**: Drive the group to a single agreed set of objectives and success measures and record them explicitly, so every stakeholder is committing to the same understanding.
- **C**: Obtain each stakeholder's formal sign-off on the draft charter early, before the conflicting objectives are reconciled, to secure approval while goodwill is high.
- **D**: Ask the sponsor to arbitrate each disputed success measure and set the final figures, so the stakeholders do not have to reach agreement among themselves.
- **E**: Record each stakeholder's objectives as separate parallel goals in the charter, so none has to be reconciled against the others and all feel represented.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

A durable charter comes from understanding the interests beneath each stated objective (A) and then converging the group on one explicit, shared set of objectives and measures they all commit to (B); genuine alignment plus an unambiguous record is what makes the agreement hold. (C) treats approval as a procedural milestone and locks in signatures over still-conflicting objectives, producing a charter that looks agreed but unravels in execution. (D) settles disputes by authority rather than negotiation, leaving the stakeholders uncommitted to numbers imposed on them. (E) avoids the hard reconciliation work entirely; parallel, unreconciled goals embed the conflict into the charter and surface it later at a worse moment.
