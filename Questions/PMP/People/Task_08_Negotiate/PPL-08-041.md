# Question

During a contract renewal for a hospital's catering service, the supplier opens by demanding a 9% price rise and the procurement lead counters with a flat freeze. Both sit firm on their numbers and the meeting stalls. The project manager, who needs the supplier to stay engaged for a further three years, is asked to help the two sides move forward. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Ask each side to explain what is driving its number — the supplier's rising input costs and the buyer's fixed budget envelope — before any figure is discussed further.
- **B**: Propose splitting the difference at roughly a 4.5% rise, since a midpoint is the fastest way to unblock two parties who are each holding firm.
- **C**: Suggest both sides take the figures away and return next week, giving tempers time to cool before the commercial discussion resumes.
- **D**: Recommend the buyer accept the 9% this year in exchange for a written commitment to a freeze in the following two years, securing the longer relationship.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The parties are locked on positions (9% versus 0%), and the first move in interest-based negotiation is to surface what each position is protecting so the discussion can shift from figures to the needs behind them. (B) splits the difference before anyone understands the drivers, which trades value blindly and can land on a number that suits neither budget reality nor cost reality. (C) just defers the same deadlock without changing anything that caused it. (D) jumps to a specific package before the underlying interests are even on the table, so it may solve the wrong problem.
