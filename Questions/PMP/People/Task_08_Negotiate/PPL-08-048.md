# Question

A defence prime is letting a subcontract for a sensor sub-assembly where the design is largely settled but a novel coating process carries real cost uncertainty. The prime wants the subcontractor motivated to control cost, but a pure fixed price would force a heavy risk premium and a pure cost-plus would remove any cost discipline. The project manager is negotiating the commercial form. Which structure best aligns the subcontractor's incentive with the prime's interest?

## Options

- **A**: A cost-plus-incentive-fee arrangement with an agreed target cost and a share ratio, so cost savings and overruns are split between the parties to defined limits.
- **B**: A firm fixed price set high enough to cover the coating uncertainty, transferring all cost risk to the subcontractor in exchange for the premium.
- **C**: A cost-plus-fixed-fee arrangement, so the subcontractor recovers all allowable costs plus a set fee and is fully protected against the coating uncertainty.
- **D**: A time-and-materials arrangement billed at agreed rates, keeping the commercial form simple while the coating process is proven out in practice.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

An incentive structure with a target cost and share ratio gives the subcontractor a direct financial stake in controlling cost while keeping it protected against an uncertainty it cannot fully price — exactly the alignment the prime wants. (B) loads a large premium because the subcontractor must price the coating unknown, and once it is fixed the subcontractor's only incentive is to do the minimum, not to share savings. (C) reimburses all costs plus a fixed fee, removing any reward for efficiency and leaving cost discipline entirely to goodwill. (D) bills hours at rate with no target and no share, so there is no incentive to contain the very cost driver that worries the prime.
