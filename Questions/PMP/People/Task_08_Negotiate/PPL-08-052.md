# Question

A project manager negotiating a three-year maintenance agreement for a fleet of mining haul trucks has the leverage to push the supplier to a price that would barely cover the supplier's cost. The supplier will be the project's sole source of spares and field engineers for the whole period, and responsiveness in a breakdown will matter enormously. The project manager wants the best commercial outcome the situation actually supports. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Settle on terms that beat the PM's benchmark while leaving the supplier a workable margin, so the supplier stays motivated to respond well throughout the agreement.
- **B**: Push the price down to the supplier's cost floor, since securing the lowest possible figure is the clearest measure of a successful negotiation.
- **C**: Offer the supplier a generous, above-market price to guarantee goodwill and the fastest possible breakdown response over the three years.
- **D**: Accept the supplier's opening price without negotiation to start the long relationship on the most cooperative footing possible.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Where the counterparty is a sole source whose responsiveness will matter for years, the best outcome captures real value (beating the benchmark) without stripping the supplier's margin so far that motivation and service quality suffer. (B) treats lowest price as the only metric and ignores that a squeezed sole-source supplier becomes a slow, grudging one when it matters most. (C) over-pays for goodwill that a fair, workable margin would secure anyway, wasting the project's money. (D) gives away the PM's leverage entirely and accepts an unexamined opening figure, which is not cooperation but a failure to negotiate.
