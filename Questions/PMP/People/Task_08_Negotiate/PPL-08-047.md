# Question

A project manager is negotiating a managed-services agreement for an airline's crew-scheduling system. During the meeting the vendor's commercial lead uses several tactics in turn. For each tactic the vendor uses, match the project manager's most appropriate response.

## Items

- **1**: The vendor suddenly claims a "policy" forbids the indemnity clause the PM needs, offering no detail
- **2**: The vendor makes a large early concession, then implies the PM now owes a matching concession in return
- **3**: The vendor says a rival provider would accept these exact terms today, pressing the PM to commit now

## Matches

- **A**: Test the claim against the project's own walk-away — if the alternative is weaker, hold position; if stronger, the pressure is real and worth weighing
- **B**: Ask to see the policy and the reasoning behind it, and keep the clause on the table until a substantive justification is given
- **C**: Acknowledge the concession but evaluate any reciprocal move on its own merits, declining to trade reflexively just because something was offered first

## Answer

1-B, 2-C, 3-A

## Explanation

Each tactic calls for a measured, interest-based response rather than reaction. The vague "policy" block (1) is tested by asking for the actual rule and rationale and refusing to drop a needed clause without substance (B) — unexamined "policy" is often negotiable. The early concession followed by an implied debt (2) is met by judging any return move on its merits, since reciprocity pressure is a tactic, not an obligation (C). The "a competitor would take this now" push (3) is assessed against the project's own BATNA: if the PM's alternative is stronger the threat is hollow, but if it is genuinely weaker the time pressure carries real weight (A). The discrimination is matching the right counter to each manoeuvre, not naming the manoeuvre.
