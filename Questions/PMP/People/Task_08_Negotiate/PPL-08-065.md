# Question

Two agile teams in the same organisation share a dependency: Team A needs an authentication component that Team B owns. Team B's product owner has deprioritised the component in favour of her own roadmap, and Team A is now blocked. The scrum master for Team A has the two product owners and an architect available for a joint conversation tomorrow. How should the scrum master approach that conversation?

## Options

- **A**: Open by having both product owners set out the business value and timing behind their priorities, then work toward a sequencing the two backlogs can agree serves the organisation best.
- **B**: Frame the conversation around Team A's blocked state and ask Team B's product owner to slot the component into her next sprint, on the basis that an active blocker should take precedence over roadmap work not yet started.
- **C**: Bring the architect's view that Team A could be unblocked sooner with a thin interim stub that Team B's full component later replaces, and negotiate who builds and owns it.
- **D**: Ask the two product owners to agree the decision rests above them and refer the priority clash to the portfolio level, since it is a contest between two funded roadmaps.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

With the right people in the room and competing-but-legitimate priorities on the table, the approach most likely to yield a durable agreement is an integrative one: surface the business value and timing behind each backlog and negotiate a sequencing optimised for the organisation rather than either local roadmap. The distractors are all defensible moves a capable scrum master might consider, which is what makes the discrimination real. (B) is a reasonable opening claim and blockers often do warrant priority, but asserting precedence before the value of each side is understood is positional and may simply be resisted. (C) is a sound creative option and may well form part of the deal, yet leading with a technical workaround can entrench the duplication and sidestep the priority conversation that actually needs settling. (D) is the correct fallback if the parties cannot agree, but escalating before genuinely attempting resolution among people fully able to resolve it is premature. A both seeks the best joint outcome and keeps the other options live within it.
