# Question

A client commissioning an agile delivery wants two things from the contract: enough cost certainty to plan its own budget release by release, and the freedom to walk away after any completed release once it judges the value delivered to be sufficient, without paying for releases it never receives. The team delivers in two-week iterations grouped into quarterly releases, and the overall backlog is expected to evolve as the product takes shape. The project manager is negotiating the commercial structure with the client and the supplier's commercial lead. Which structure best fits both of the client's stated interests?

## Options

- **A**: A fixed total price for the full anticipated scope, paid in instalments tied to each release, with a clause letting the client terminate after any release on payment of a defined percentage of the remaining contract value.
- **B**: A fixed price agreed per release against that release's scope envelope, with the client deciding at the end of each release whether to commission the next, and no charge for any release not commissioned.
- **C**: A fixed price covering a committed block of releases that the client funds upfront, with any budget unspent if the client stops early converted into a pool the client may later draw on for additional change.
- **D**: An incremental-funding arrangement in which each release is scoped and individually fixed-priced only at the moment the client chooses to commission it, so the client funds and prices one release at a time as the backlog firms up.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The client wants cost certainty it can plan against *and* a clean, cost-free exit at a release boundary — pricing each release as a fixed unit agreed in advance gives the forward certainty, and a per-release commissioning decision with no charge for uncommissioned work gives the clean stop, so B is the only structure that fully serves both interests at once. (A) is a recognised agile fixed-price termination pattern and genuinely viable, but pricing the whole anticipated scope upfront sits awkwardly with an evolving backlog, and the early-exit payment on remaining value means the client *does* pay something for releases it never receives — a negotiated settlement rather than the no-penalty walk-away it asked for. (C) delivers cost certainty but requires committing and funding a block of releases in advance and ties unspent money into a change pool, which both blunts the certainty (the client is exposed to the whole block) and muddies the clean exit. (D) gives an excellent cost-free exit, but pricing each release only at the point of commissioning means the client never has price visibility beyond the current release, sacrificing the forward budget certainty it explicitly wants. The fine margin is that B fixes price *ahead* per release while leaving the commit decision open; A, C, and D each trade away one of the two interests to protect the other.
