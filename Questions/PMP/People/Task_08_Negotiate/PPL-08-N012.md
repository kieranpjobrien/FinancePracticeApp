# Question

In a contract negotiation, a supplier opens with a price far above any reasonable market figure and presents it as a firm starting point. The project manager has solid, defensible benchmarking data on the true market range. The PM wants both a good price and a constructive working relationship for the contract term. How should the PM respond to this opening?

## Options

- **A**: Decline to treat the inflated figure as the reference point and table a counter-offer grounded in the benchmarking data, explaining the objective basis so the discussion resets on the market range.
- **B**: Ask the supplier to walk through how they built the opening figure, using their answer to expose where it departs from the market before the PM puts any number on the table.
- **C**: Respond with an equally aggressive low counter-offer below the benchmark range, so the midpoint between the two openings lands near the price the firm actually wants.
- **D**: Name the benchmarked market range, state the firm will not negotiate from the opening number, and ask the supplier to come back with a revised quote within that range.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

An extreme opening is an anchoring tactic, and the most effective response both refuses the anchor and immediately re-anchors on objective criteria by countering with a benchmark-based offer and its rationale — this resets the frame while keeping the conversation constructive and moving toward a number. (B) is a useful diagnostic, but probing the supplier's reasoning without yet offering a figure leaves their inflated anchor sitting on the table as the only number in play, ceding the framing for longer than necessary. (C) fights an anchor with a counter-anchor, which can work but, by going aggressively below a range the PM can actually defend, risks looking unreasonable and souring a relationship the firm wants to keep. (D) is firm and cites the benchmark, but issuing an instruction to requote rather than tabling the firm's own offer is more confrontational and slower, handing the next move back to the supplier instead of advancing toward a deal.
