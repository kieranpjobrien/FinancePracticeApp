# Question

A civil-engineering subcontractor on a port expansion submits a substantial claim for additional payment, citing ground conditions worse than the geotechnical baseline in the contract. The relationship is otherwise good and the subcontractor is still needed for the remaining works. The project manager must negotiate a resolution that is fair and keeps the partnership intact. Which TWO actions best position the project manager to negotiate this well? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Establish what the contract's baseline and changed-conditions provisions actually entitle the subcontractor to, so any settlement rests on the agreed terms.
- **B**: Examine the evidence for the claimed conditions and quantify the genuinely attributable cost and time impact before discussing any figure.
- **C**: Offer a generous round-number payment quickly to settle the claim and avoid any strain on a relationship the project still depends on.
- **D**: Reject the claim outright on the basis that the subcontractor accepted the ground risk by signing the contract.
- **E**: Defer the claim to the end of the project so it does not disrupt the current working relationship while works continue.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

A sound claim negotiation rests on knowing the contractual entitlement (A) and on testing the evidence and quantifying the real impact (B), so the settlement is grounded in both the terms and the facts. (C) buys peace with an unexamined payment that may over-compensate, set a bad precedent, and is hard to justify to the project's own stakeholders. (D) dismisses the claim without testing whether the changed-conditions provisions actually apply, inviting formal dispute and damaging the relationship. (E) lets an unresolved claim fester through the remaining works, where it sours collaboration far more than addressing it would.
