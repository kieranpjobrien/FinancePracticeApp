# Question

Two teams in the same company share a delivery dependency: Team A's API must be ready for Team B's release. They report to different functional leads, hold different priorities, and have started blaming each other for slippage. The programme manager wants a working agreement that actually holds between them. What should the programme manager facilitate first?

## Options

- **A**: A joint session focused first on surfacing why the slippage is really happening — competing priorities, unclear requirements, or capacity — before defining any commitments, so the agreement targets the actual cause.
- **B**: A joint working session where both teams negotiate and document an interface agreement — the API contract, the date it will be ready, the test data Team B owes, and what each side can expect from the other.
- **C**: A meeting with the two functional leads to align Team A's and Team B's relative priorities, since the teams cannot commit to each other while their managers' direction conflicts.
- **D**: A revised programme schedule that builds explicit buffer around the A-to-B handoff and sequences the release to reduce how tightly the two teams are coupled.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The teams are blaming each other because no shared, explicit definition of the obligation exists; a negotiated interface agreement — API contract, date, and what each side owes — replaces blame with a concrete commitment both have signed up to, and it is the artefact the dependency actually needs. (A) is attractive, but a root-cause session without producing a mutual commitment tends to relitigate the blame; the agreement B creates is itself the forum where competing priorities and unclear requirements surface and get resolved into dates. (C) may be needed if the agreement stalls on conflicting direction, but going to the managers first skips the teams' own negotiation and can impose a priority the teams never bought into. (D) re-engineers the schedule to mask the coordination failure rather than fixing the broken hand-off, and buffer without an agreed contract just relocates the slippage.
