# Question

A hybrid project team has eight people who work two days a week in a shared office and five who are fully remote in other cities. Over a quarter, the PM observes that the in-office members are given the more visible, career-enhancing tasks slightly more often, are praised more readily in updates, and are tacitly treated as the "core" group — none of it deliberate. The remote five have not complained, and delivery is on track. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Leave it; nothing is malicious, no one has raised a grievance and the project is delivering, so intervening risks creating a problem where the team feels none.
- **B**: Deliberately rebalance opportunity and recognition — track how high-profile work and visible praise are distributed, and correct the drift so location stops predicting who gets seen.
- **C**: Encourage the remote five to be more assertive about putting themselves forward for visible work and to make their contributions louder in shared channels.
- **D**: Require the in-office members to also work fully remotely on some days so everyone experiences the same conditions and the in-group advantage dissolves.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

This is proximity bias: physical presence is quietly converting into more opportunity and recognition, and because it is unintentional it will persist unless the PM actively manages distribution (B) — measuring and correcting how visible work and praise are allocated removes location as the hidden variable. (A) mistakes the absence of complaints and on-track delivery for the absence of harm; the damage to the remote five's growth and engagement accrues silently and is exactly what a leader should pre-empt. (C) shifts the burden of fixing a systemic bias onto the disadvantaged group, asking them to out-shout a structural tilt rather than levelling it. (D) attacks the symptom by degrading everyone's conditions instead of managing fairness, and imposing remote days on people for whom the office works is a blunt, resented instrument.
