# Question

On a virtual team spanning offices in Tokyo and São Paulo, the Brazilian members find their Japanese colleagues slow to commit in meetings and hard to read, while the Japanese members feel the Brazilian members interrupt and push for premature decisions. Tension is building and collaboration is suffering. What is the most effective first action for the project manager?

## Options

- **A**: Establish meeting protocols that work across both styles — circulating topics in advance, using a round-robin to ensure all voices, and confirming decisions in writing afterwards.
- **B**: Coach each group toward the other's expectations — helping the reserved members surface their position earlier and the vocal members leave more space — so both adapt their style to meet in the middle.
- **C**: Facilitate a session where the two groups explain how they typically work and what the behaviours they are misreading actually mean, then agree shared norms together.
- **D**: Appoint a liaison in each office to translate intent between the groups and head off misunderstandings before they escalate.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The friction is not behaviour that needs correcting but interpretation that is wrong — each group is reading the other's culturally rooted style through its own assumptions, so silence looks evasive and directness looks pushy. Surfacing what those behaviours actually mean and agreeing norms from that shared understanding (C) treats the real cause and is what makes any later protocol hold rather than feel arbitrary. (A) prescribes a sensible structure and is the closest competitor, but installed before the groups understand each other it is a fix applied to people who still misread the intent behind each other's behaviour, so it manages the symptom while the misreading festers. (B) is a legitimate adaptation move and genuinely useful once trust exists, but asking each side to change how it communicates before either understands why the other behaves as it does tends to read as "you are doing it wrong" and hardens the divide. (D) can keep things civil, but a standing intermediary routes around the misunderstanding instead of dissolving it and never lets the two groups work together directly. The discriminator is whether the first move builds the mutual understanding the conflict is rooted in or jumps to managing behaviour before that understanding exists.
