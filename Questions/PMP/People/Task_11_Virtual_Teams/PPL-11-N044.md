# Question

A distributed agile team agreed long ago that a wiki is the single source of truth for decisions and current state, with chat for quick discussion. Decisions get made well in the live overlap and the right people take part. Yet members report that the wiki is regularly out of date: the substance of recent decisions sits in chat threads, and to learn the real current state people end up scrolling chat history rather than trusting the wiki. The project manager wants the wiki to be reliably current again. Which change should the project manager recommend?

## Options

- **A**: Add a rule that whoever raised a decision copies it from chat to the wiki when the thread concludes, keeping the source of truth current.
- **B**: Have a rotating member compile an end-of-day digest of the day's decisions into the wiki, so the record is brought up to date reliably once daily.
- **C**: Configure the chat tool to archive decision threads into a searchable space automatically, so the full discussion is always retrievable even when the wiki lags.
- **D**: Treat a decision as not yet complete until its outcome and rationale are in the wiki, so capturing the record is part of reaching the decision rather than a step that happens afterwards.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The wiki drifts because writing the record is a distinct act that happens after the decision is already settled, so under delivery pressure it is the step that slips. Folding the record into the definition of a completed decision (D) makes the wiki a by-product of deciding rather than a follow-up someone has to remember, which closes the gap at its source rather than adding another reminder. (A) is the intuitive fix and is genuinely reasonable, but it is precisely the after-the-fact manual step that is already being missed — naming an owner does not stop it being skipped when the thread ends and everyone moves on. (B) guarantees the record catches up, but only once daily and as a separate person's chore, so the source of truth is routinely stale within the working day and still depends on a discrete task being done. (C) makes the discussion searchable and is a real improvement to retrieval, but an archived raw thread is not a curated decision record — readers are still scrolling history, which is the exact experience the wiki was meant to replace. The discriminator is whether the record is intrinsic to deciding or a step bolted on after it.
