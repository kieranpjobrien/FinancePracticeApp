# Question

A Scrum team is split across Sydney, Bangalore and Berlin, giving roughly two hours of working-day overlap. The team currently holds the daily stand-up, sprint planning, review and retrospective as live video calls, and Berlin members routinely join planning at 7 pm their time. Velocity is stable but the Berlin members have started declining optional ceremonies and seem disengaged. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Rotate the meeting times each sprint so that no single location always carries the inconvenient hours, sharing the burden evenly across the three sites.
- **B**: Work with the team to decide which ceremonies genuinely need everyone live and convert the rest to asynchronous formats, reserving the overlap window for true collaboration.
- **C**: Record every ceremony so Berlin members can watch later and post their input in the team channel rather than attending outside working hours.
- **D**: Raise the staffing imbalance with the resource manager and request additional Berlin-hours members so ceremonies can run within their normal day.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

With only two hours of overlap, the scarce resource is synchronous time, so the PM should protect it for work that truly needs real-time interaction (planning, retro) and move the rest to async, which directly tackles the cause of Berlin's disengagement. (A) spreads the pain but still forces everyone into live calls when several do not need to be live. (C) makes Berlin passive observers and loses their voice in decisions, worsening engagement. (D) is a long-lead structural change that does not help the current team and assumes a resourcing problem rather than a working-agreement problem.
