# Question

A high-performing engineer on a fully remote team has, over the past two months, stopped contributing to team chat, keeps her camera off, and gives only minimal answers in calls, though her individual output remains strong. In a one-to-one she says she feels "like a cog who never sees anyone" and is quietly considering leaving. The project manager wants to retain her. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Introduce regular team social events such as virtual coffee chats and online games so she and everyone else have more informal contact.
- **B**: Praise her strong output more visibly in team channels so she feels recognised and valued by the group.
- **C**: Reassure her that remote isolation is a common phase and check in with her again in a few weeks to see whether the feeling has passed.
- **D**: Work with her to understand what connection she is missing and adjust how she works with the team — pairing, a role with more visible collaboration, or involvement she values — rather than treating it as a morale problem to be jollied along.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

She has named a specific, structural lack — meaningful human connection in her work — and is a genuine flight risk, so the response must engage with her actual experience and change how she is connected to the team. Exploring what she is missing and reshaping her collaboration accordingly addresses the cause directly and signals she is seen as a person, not just an output (D). Blanket social events (A) may help the team at large but are a generic intervention that may not touch what she specifically needs, and forced fun can feel hollow to someone already disengaged. More public praise for her output (B) reinforces exactly the "cog who delivers" framing she is recoiling from — recognition of throughput is not the connection she asked for. Reassuring and waiting (C) minimises a stated intention to leave and risks losing her while the PM hopes the feeling passes. The discriminator is whether the action grapples with her specific, voiced need or substitutes a generic morale gesture.
