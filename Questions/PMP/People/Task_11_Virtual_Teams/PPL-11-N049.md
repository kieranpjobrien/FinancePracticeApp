# Question

A distributed agile team maintains a live digital board with work in progress, a burndown and an impediments list, yet "what's the status?" questions keep coming from two directions. Team members, who use the board daily, say the cards are too terse to read real state at a glance and they end up asking each other. Stakeholders ask because several do not know the board exists or how to interpret it. The board itself is kept current. The PM wants to stop the recurring status questions. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Enrich the cards and label the chart so the board is fully self-explanatory, and trust that once it reads clearly the status questions fall away.
- **B**: Give stakeholders access and walk them through the board in a short session, so teams and stakeholders are reading from the same single source going forward.
- **C**: Post a short written status summary at the top of the board each day in plain language, so anyone arriving sees the current picture without interpreting the cards.
- **D**: Improve the board for each audience in the way it needs — fuller, clearer card states and a labelled chart for the team, plus giving stakeholders access with a short guide and an outcome-level summary view they can read unaided.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The two streams of questions have different roots: the team can see the board but cannot read it quickly, while stakeholders cannot see it at all. Because the causes differ, a single fix aimed at one audience leaves the other still asking — so the response has to address both the legibility gap for the team and the access-and-interpretation gap for stakeholders. (D) does exactly that on both fronts. (A) makes the board self-explanatory and would genuinely quiet the team's questions, but stakeholders who do not know it exists keep asking, so half the problem remains. (B) closes the stakeholder access gap and is genuinely useful, but it does nothing about the terse cards the team is struggling with, and a one-off walkthrough is not a standing view stakeholders can read unaided thereafter. (C) is the tidiest-looking option and a daily narrative does help anyone glancing in, but it papers a summary over cards that are still hard to read and still does not put the board in front of stakeholders who do not know to look. The discriminator is recognising two distinct causes and treating each, rather than applying one fix to both.
