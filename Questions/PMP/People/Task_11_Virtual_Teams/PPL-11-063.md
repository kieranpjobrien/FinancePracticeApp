# Question

A distributed agile team's velocity has been stable for months, demos are well received and the burndown looks healthy. Yet in the last two retrospectives, participation has thinned: cameras stay off, the same one or two people speak, and written feedback is sparse and bland. One senior engineer privately mentions to the PM that "retros don't change anything, so people have stopped bothering." Metrics give no warning sign. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Treat the healthy metrics as the reliable signal and keep the cadence steady; engagement in ceremonies naturally ebbs and flows and will recover on its own.
- **B**: Make the retrospective visibly consequential — surface the unaddressed improvement actions, commit to closing a concrete few before the next sprint, and show the team that their input changes the work.
- **C**: Refresh the retrospective format with new techniques and icebreakers to re-energise the sessions and draw quieter members back into the conversation.
- **D**: Replace the live retrospective with an anonymous async survey so disengaged members can give candid feedback without the pressure of speaking on camera.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The engineer's remark names the real cause: the team has learned that retro input goes nowhere, so the fix is to make the ceremony consequential by closing real actions and demonstrating that their voice moves the work (B) — engagement follows evidence of impact. (A) misreads stable delivery metrics as proof of a healthy team; the leading indicator here is the falling engagement, and complacency lets quiet disengagement harden. (C) treats a credibility problem as a novelty problem — new games on top of a ceremony people believe is futile will wear off fast. (D) may raise the volume of feedback but does nothing about the grievance that feedback is ignored, and removing the synchronous space can further erode a team already drifting into silence.
