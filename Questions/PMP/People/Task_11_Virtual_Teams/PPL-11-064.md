# Question

A new developer is joining a fully remote agile team that has been running smoothly for a year. The PM wants the onboarding to make the newcomer productive and connected quickly, despite no in-person contact. Which actions would most directly help the new remote member become an effective, engaged part of the team? (Select three.)

## Options

- **A**: Assign an onboarding buddy on the team who is explicitly available for informal questions the newcomer would otherwise hesitate to raise.
- **B**: Give the newcomer a guided tour of where the team's information lives — boards, repos, decision log, the single source of truth — and how the team actually uses each.
- **C**: Have the newcomer observe the team's first few ceremonies and a small starter task, so they learn the team's rhythms and norms before taking on substantial work.
- **D**: Hand over a comprehensive written onboarding document and let the newcomer work through it independently so they do not consume the team's time.
- **E**: Hold all introductions and questions until the newcomer has read every relevant document, so meetings are efficient and nothing is asked that the docs already answer.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Effective remote onboarding builds both capability and connection: a named buddy lowers the cost of the small questions that go unasked when no one is sitting nearby (A); a guided tour of where information lives and how it is used turns scattered tools into a usable map (B); and observing real ceremonies plus a starter task lets the newcomer absorb the team's norms before being thrown into deep work (C). (D) is the isolation trap — a document dropped on a remote newcomer with no human contact leaves them alone with their confusion and disconnected from the team. (E) inverts the order of trust-building: making a newcomer read everything before they may ask or meet anyone delays connection and treats reasonable questions as failures rather than a normal part of joining.
