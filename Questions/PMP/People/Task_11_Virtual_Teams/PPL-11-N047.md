# Question

After a return-to-office policy, a project team is hybrid: five members sit together in a conference room with a microphone and a screen showing the remote participants, and four join from home. The remote four say decisions effectively form among the in-room group through glances, nods and quick asides they cannot pick up, and they only realise a decision was reached when it is announced. The PM cannot change the office policy. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Upgrade the room with a wide camera and table microphones so remote members can see faces and catch the side comments and reactions they are currently missing.
- **B**: Add a facilitator role to each meeting who deliberately brings in the remote members, voices what the room is leaning toward, and pauses to confirm decisions explicitly before they are treated as made.
- **C**: Run working meetings "one person, one screen" so that everyone, including the five at the office, joins from their own device and there is no in-room channel the remote members are excluded from.
- **D**: Keep substantive decisions out of the meeting itself, recording each as a written proposal afterwards with a short window for all members to weigh in before it is final.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The exclusion is a channel asymmetry rather than a simple equipment gap: people sharing a physical room exchange low-latency, non-verbal cues — eye contact, a nod, a muttered aside — and decisions coalesce in that channel before they are ever spoken for the remote feed. Putting everyone on their own screen (C) dissolves the privileged in-room channel and makes participation structurally symmetrical, which attacks the asymmetry itself. (A) is the natural first reach and better hardware does help at the margin, but a camera and table mics chase cues that co-located people transmit faster and more richly than any feed can relay, so the in-room group still shapes decisions first — it narrows the gap without closing it. (B) genuinely helps and may be the most realistic in practice, but it relies on a facilitator continuously compensating for an unequal format by force of skill rather than removing the inequality. (D) equalises by moving decisions out of the meeting, but that re-plumbs the team's whole decision rhythm to route around the room instead of fixing what happens in it. The discriminator is whether the fix removes the unequal channel or merely compensates for it.
