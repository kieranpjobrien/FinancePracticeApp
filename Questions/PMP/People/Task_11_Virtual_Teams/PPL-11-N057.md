# Question

A team is split between a large group at headquarters and three members working from home. The team has a clear, well-used decision log and shared boards, and significant decisions are genuinely recorded there. Even so, the remote three say that by the time a decision reaches the log it is effectively settled, and they have had no real chance to influence it; two are starting to disengage. The PM cannot require anyone to relocate. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Schedule regular structured working sessions for the whole team where the shaping of upcoming decisions happens live with everyone present, reducing what gets settled informally at HQ.
- **B**: Strengthen the norm that any HQ side conversation moving a decision forward is written up promptly, so the remote three can see the reasoning and respond before it is finalised.
- **C**: Shift the early, formative discussion itself — not just the final decision — into accessible channels, so options are floated and debated where the remote three can take part, and treat undocumented in-person shaping as not yet decided.
- **D**: Pair each remote member with an HQ counterpart who loops them in on emerging thinking in real time, so they hear and can influence discussions as they form.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The log captures decisions faithfully, so the exclusion is not a recording gap — it is that decisions are effectively shaped in informal HQ conversation before anything reaches the log, leaving the remote three to react to near-final outcomes. The structural fix is to move the formative discussion into channels they can join and to deny informal in-person shaping the standing of a settled decision (C), which relocates where decisions are made rather than just where they are written down. (B) genuinely improves the trail and the remote three would at least see the reasoning, but reporting a side conversation after it has shaped a decision still leaves them responding to a fait accompli rather than helping form it. (A) is a strong complementary practice and worth doing, but scheduled sessions cannot absorb all the ad-hoc shaping that happens in the gaps between them, so informal HQ influence carries on around them. (D) gives each remote member a line into emerging thinking, but it depends on each counterpart's diligence and hard-wires a second-hand relay instead of opening the shaping itself to everyone. The discriminator is whether the fix moves the place decisions are shaped or merely improves visibility of shaping that still happens out of reach.
