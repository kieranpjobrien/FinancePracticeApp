# Question

A distributed agile team agreed to make most decisions asynchronously on a shared thread, with members commenting in their own working hours. In practice, decisions now drift for days because there is no point at which a proposal is considered settled: late objections reopen things others thought were closed, and members hesitate to act for fear of being overruled. The team still wants to decide asynchronously. What should the project manager introduce?

## Options

- **A**: A clear async decision protocol: a proposal stands unless a substantive objection is raised within a defined window, after which it is considered agreed and acted upon.
- **B**: A weekly synchronous decision meeting where any proposals raised async during the week are confirmed live so there is a definite point of closure.
- **C**: A rule that decisions require explicit approval from every team member before they take effect, so no one is bypassed.
- **D**: Designation of the project manager as the decider who reviews each async thread and calls the outcome once enough input has been gathered.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The async process lacks a closing rule, so proposals never reach a defensible "decided" state and anyone can reopen them, which paralyses action. A protocol with a defined objection window and a default-to-agreed outcome creates that missing point of closure while keeping the decision asynchronous and giving everyone a fair chance to object in their own hours (A). A weekly synchronous confirmation (B) reintroduces a meeting to do what the async process should handle itself and delays every decision to a fixed cadence, undercutting the speed async is meant to provide. Requiring unanimous explicit approval (C) makes closure even harder — a single silent or absent member blocks everything — worsening the drift. Making the PM the sole decider (D) abandons the team's wish to decide collaboratively and creates a bottleneck and single point of failure across time zones. The discriminator is whether the mechanism gives async decisions a fair, time-boxed point of finality or merely shifts where and how slowly they are made.
