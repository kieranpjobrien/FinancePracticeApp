# Question

A virtual team works well across European and Asian zones with a healthy daily overlap. A single critical specialist sits in a Pacific time zone with no usable overlap with the rest — when she is working, everyone else is asleep. She is fully capable but increasingly out of the loop: she inherits decisions made without her, and her questions take a full day to round-trip. Relocating her or shifting her hours significantly is not an option. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Restructure the team's working approach around genuine asynchronicity for the decisions and dependencies that involve her — documented context, clear async decision points, and work partitioned so a full-day round-trip is rarely on the critical path.
- **B**: Ask the specialist to shift a few of her hours each day to create a short overlap with the rest of the team for the most important discussions.
- **C**: Assign a team member in an overlapping zone as her liaison to brief her on decisions and carry her input back to the group.
- **D**: Schedule a small number of essential synchronous meetings at a time she can join, accepting they fall outside everyone else's normal hours.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

With no overlap and relocation off the table, leaning on synchronous contact will always disadvantage her, so the team's way of working — not the individual — has to change. Re-engineering the decisions and dependencies that touch her to be genuinely asynchronous (documented context, explicit async decision points, work partitioned to keep a day-long round-trip off the critical path) lets her contribute fully on her own clock (A). Asking her to shift hours (B) is ruled out by the premise that her hours cannot move significantly, and even a small daily shift quietly loads the cost onto the one person already most disadvantaged. A liaison (C) makes her a second-hand participant dependent on one colleague's relaying and availability, reproducing the out-of-the-loop problem in a new form. Occasional meetings at her time (D) help for a few set-pieces but cannot carry the ongoing flow of decisions and dependencies, and they push everyone else outside their hours; the deeper fix is to stop those decisions requiring everyone to be awake together. The discriminator is whether the team redesigns its workflow for true asynchronicity or keeps relying on synchronous contact that her time zone makes structurally unfair.
