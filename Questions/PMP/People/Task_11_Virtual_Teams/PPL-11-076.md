# Question

A PM notices that a distributed agile team rarely surfaces bad news: blockers are revealed late, retrospectives are bland, and people seem to wait until problems are undeniable before raising them. The team is polite and delivery looks fine on the surface. The PM wants to build the psychological safety that would let problems surface early, in a setting where there is no in-person reassurance to fall back on. Which actions would most directly help? (Select three.)

## Options

- **A**: Visibly respond to early warnings and surfaced problems with curiosity and support rather than blame, so raising a concern is repeatedly shown to be safe.
- **B**: Model fallibility as the leader — openly naming your own mistakes and uncertainties — so the team sees that admitting problems is normal and expected here.
- **C**: Provide low-exposure routes to raise concerns, such as anonymous retro inputs or a private channel to the PM, alongside the open ones, lowering the cost of speaking up.
- **D**: Tell the team directly and repeatedly that this is a psychologically safe environment and that they should feel free to raise anything without fear.
- **E**: Track who raises issues and who stays silent, and follow up privately with the consistently quiet members to require more candour from them.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Psychological safety is built by evidence, not assertion: consistently meeting bad news with curiosity rather than blame teaches the team that candour is safe (A); a leader who openly owns their own mistakes makes admitting problems normal (B); and offering lower-exposure channels reduces the personal cost of speaking up while trust is still forming (C). (D) is the classic trap — declaring an environment "safe" does nothing on its own and can even ring hollow against members' lived experience; safety is demonstrated through reactions over time, not announced. (E) is actively corrosive: surveilling who speaks and pressing the quiet ones for candour creates fear and self-protection, the precise opposite of safety, and is especially threatening on a remote team where people already feel exposed.
