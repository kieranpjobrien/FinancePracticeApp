# Question

One member of a distributed delivery team consistently has poor video and audio on live calls; their home broadband in a remote region drops and stutters throughout. They miss parts of live discussions and decisions routinely have to be repeated for them, which is wearing on the team and on them. The project manager has talked it through with the member to understand their situation and options. The PM wants to remove the barrier to their full participation. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Shift the team's key decision points to an asynchronous flow — proposals and discussion written up with a window for input — and have this member join the remaining synchronous parts by phone audio, so their participation no longer hinges on video bandwidth.
- **B**: Arrange a connectivity stipend so the member can upgrade to a more reliable home internet package and take part in video calls fully like everyone else.
- **C**: Fund a desk at a nearby co-working space with reliable enterprise internet, so the member has a dependable connection for the working day.
- **D**: Keep meetings live but record them and assign a notetaker to capture decisions, so the member can review what they missed and respond afterwards.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The barrier is specifically reliable video bandwidth at this member's location, and the durable response routes their participation through channels that do not depend on it — async for the decisions that matter most, low-bandwidth phone audio for the live remainder — so they take part in real time rather than catching up afterwards. This works whether or not a better connection can be bought, which is why it is the most robust fix. (B) is the reflexive move and is worth trying, but it bets the member's full participation on an upgrade that may simply not be purchasable in a region where the service is poor; if no better line exists, the barrier remains. (C) can deliver a reliable connection, but it solves the team's design problem by imposing a daily commute and a fixed location on one person, which may not fit their circumstances and is a heavy answer to a problem the team can absorb itself. (D) lets the member recover what they missed, but recording and notes still leave them outside the live discussion where decisions are actually shaped, so they remain a step behind. The discriminator is whether the accommodation removes the dependence on the failing channel or merely compensates for missing it.
