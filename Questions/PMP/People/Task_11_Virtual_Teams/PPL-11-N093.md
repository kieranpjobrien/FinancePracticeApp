# Question

Members of a remote team report exhaustion from back-to-back video calls that fill most of the working day, leaving little uninterrupted time for actual delivery. Several say they end the day drained but feel they have accomplished little. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Make camera use optional on internal calls so members can rest their eyes and reduce the intensity of being on screen.
- **B**: Shorten the default length of every meeting from sixty to forty-five minutes to claw back time across the day.
- **C**: Review the team's meeting load with them, cut or shorten calls that do not need to be synchronous, and protect blocks of meeting-free focus time.
- **D**: Encourage members to block personal focus time in their own calendars and decline meetings that clash with it.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The core problem is too many synchronous calls crowding out delivery time, so the first move should reduce the meeting load itself and deliberately protect focus time, done with the team so the cuts are sensible (C). Optional cameras (A) ease screen fatigue but leave the same wall-to-wall schedule, so people are still in calls all day. Trimming every meeting to forty-five minutes (B) recovers some minutes but keeps the same number of interruptions fragmenting the day and does not question whether each call needs to be live at all. Asking individuals to defend their own focus time (D) pushes the burden onto each person and invites conflict when they decline meetings others expect them at; the fix belongs at the team level. Addressing the overall meeting load and ring-fencing focus time tackles the cause rather than softening one symptom.
