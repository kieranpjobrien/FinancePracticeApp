# Question

A hybrid product team runs its workshops from a conference room where five members sit together while four join by video. The remote four report that side comments, whiteboard sketches and quick hallway-style asides among the in-room group pass them by, and that decisions feel half-made before the call even starts. The room is well equipped with a good camera and microphone. The team works in two-week iterations. What should the project manager change?

## Options

- **A**: Upgrade the room's audio-visual setup further — a wider camera, better microphones and a large screen showing the remote participants — so the remote four are more present in the room.
- **B**: Run the workshops with every participant, including those in the building, joining individually from their own device on equal footing, so there is no privileged in-room channel.
- **C**: Appoint an in-room facilitator to repeat side comments aloud and narrate the whiteboard for the benefit of the remote participants.
- **D**: Alternate workshop locations so that on some iterations the majority are remote, balancing out who experiences the in-room advantage over time.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The problem is structural, not technical: a co-located subgroup generates a stream of informal interaction the remote members cannot access, creating two tiers regardless of how good the equipment is. Having everyone join individually from their own device removes the privileged in-room channel entirely and puts all participants on one equal surface — the most reliable fix for hybrid two-tier dynamics (B). Better hardware (A) sharpens the picture but does nothing about the asides, sketches and pre-decisions that happen off-camera in the room. A facilitator narrating the room (C) relies on one person catching and relaying everything in real time, which is partial and exhausting, and still leaves the remote four a beat behind. Rotating the majority (D) merely moves the disadvantage between groups instead of eliminating it, so someone is always second-class. The discriminator is whether the change dissolves the in-room channel or just tries to widen a window onto it.
