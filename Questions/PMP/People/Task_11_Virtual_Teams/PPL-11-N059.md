# Question

A remote Scrum team's retrospectives have gone flat — cameras off, members multitasking, the same two people talking. Looking back over recent sprints, the project manager notices that most improvement actions the retros produced were never delivered: they were raised, agreed, and then quietly dropped as the next sprint's work took over. The Scrum Master asks the PM which change to make first. What should the PM suggest?

## Options

- **A**: End every retro with a small number of owned, dated improvement actions, and open the next retro by reviewing what happened to each, so follow-through becomes visible and the ceremony is seen to change things.
- **B**: Vary the format each sprint and run input on an interactive online board everyone posts to at once, so the meeting feels fresh and participation no longer hinges on who speaks up.
- **C**: Rotate facilitation so a different member runs each retro, bringing new techniques and spreading ownership of the ceremony beyond the Scrum Master.
- **D**: Carry only one improvement action per sprint, chosen by the team, so they can fully focus on completing it rather than spreading effort thin and finishing none.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The flat behaviour is best read as a consequence: when actions are raised every sprint and then never delivered, the team learns the meeting changes nothing and stops investing in it, so cameras go off and engagement drains. The first move should therefore restore visible follow-through — owned, dated actions plus a standing review of them at the next retro closes the loop and rebuilds belief that the ceremony matters, after which participation tends to recover on its own. (D) is the strongest competitor and genuinely sound: limiting actions in progress to one makes completion more likely. But on its own it neither tracks nor surfaces whether that action actually got done, so the team still cannot see change — pairing it with the review loop in (A) is what makes it land. (B) makes the meeting livelier and would help if the problem were a dull format, but a fresher meeting that still produces undelivered actions will go flat again. (C) spreads ownership and brings fresh technique, yet does nothing about whether actions are completed, so the underlying futility persists. The discriminator is whether the change makes follow-through real and visible, not just the meeting more engaging.
