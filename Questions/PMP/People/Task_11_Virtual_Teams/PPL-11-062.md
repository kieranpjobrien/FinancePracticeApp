# Question

On a fully remote insurance-claims modernisation project, the PM notices that the same three vocal people dominate every video meeting while six others rarely speak. When directly asked, the quiet members say everything is fine and they have nothing to add. The work of the quiet members is sound, but the PM suspects useful perspectives are being lost. What is the PM's best response?

## Options

- **A**: Redesign how the meetings run — circulate questions in advance, use round-robin and written brainstorming tools — so contribution does not depend on competing for the floor.
- **B**: Privately ask the three dominant members to hold back and leave deliberate space, trusting that quieter colleagues will step into the silence once it opens up.
- **C**: Accept the members' own assessment at face value; they have said they are fine and their deliverables are good, so there is no real problem to solve.
- **D**: Call on the quieter members by name in meetings to put them on the spot, forcing them to contribute so their perspectives are heard.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Uneven participation on a virtual team is usually a structural problem with the meeting format, not a deficiency in the quiet people, so the leverage is in changing the format to make contributing easy (A) — advance questions, round-robin and written input remove the need to win airtime. (B) helps a little by opening space, but space alone does not overcome the dynamics that silenced people in the first place, and it leaves participation reliant on the dominant few choosing to yield. (C) takes a polite "I'm fine" as proof of engagement when the PM already has evidence that perspectives are being lost. (D) can humiliate people who are not ready and damages psychological safety; surfacing voices should be enabled, not coerced.
