# Question

In a one-to-one, a previously outgoing developer on a fully remote team tells the project manager they have felt isolated for the past month. Their days have become a run of solo tickets, and the video calls in between leave them more drained than connected. Their delivery is still on time. They ask the PM not to make it a team-wide discussion. What should the PM do next?

## Options

- **A**: Set up a standing informal video catch-up between the developer and a couple of teammates so they have dependable, friendly contact on the calendar each week.
- **B**: Restructure some of the developer's upcoming work so it is done collaboratively — pairing or a small sub-team on shared problems — giving regular working contact with teammates through the work itself.
- **C**: Reduce the developer's meeting load by making them optional for sessions where they are not essential, freeing time and easing the video fatigue they described.
- **D**: Connect the developer with a peer mentor outside their immediate team for regular one-to-one sessions, giving them a supportive relationship and a wider sense of belonging in the organisation.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Two threads run through what the developer described: isolation from a string of solo work, and calls that tire rather than connect them. The strongest reading is that the isolation is the core problem and that more scheduled video is unlikely to cure it, since their existing calls are already a drain. Routing connection through the work itself (B) puts the developer back in regular contact with teammates inside their normal hours without adding to the call load that is already wearing them down. (A) is a fair instinct and would add contact, but it does so through exactly the medium — more scheduled video — that the developer experiences as draining, so it risks treating the loneliness while deepening the fatigue. (C) eases the fatigue thread but leaves the isolation untouched, so the bigger problem persists. (D) offers a genuine supportive relationship and can help belonging over time, but it is outside their own team and one-to-one, so their day-to-day stays solo and disconnected from the people they actually deliver with. The discriminator is which intervention reconnects them to their team through the work rather than adding or subtracting meetings.
