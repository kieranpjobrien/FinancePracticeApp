# Question

A distributed analytics team spans Sydney, London and San Francisco. The one weekly synchronous ceremony everyone must attend has always been held at a time that is convenient mid-morning in London. The Sydney members take it late at night and the San Francisco members very early; both groups have quietly accepted this for months, but engagement from those two locations on the call has visibly dropped. The London members are unaware there is a problem. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Rotate the fixed ceremony's time on a set schedule so each location bears the inconvenient slot in turn, and make the rotation visible so everyone sees the burden is shared.
- **B**: Keep the established time but record the ceremony so the Sydney and San Francisco members can catch up if attending live is too hard.
- **C**: Poll the whole team for the single time slot that is least bad on average across all three locations and move the ceremony there permanently.
- **D**: Reduce the team's reliance on the synchronous ceremony by shifting most of its content into asynchronous updates, keeping only a brief live touchpoint.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The harm is not just inconvenience but its unfairness: one location has enjoyed a comfortable slot indefinitely while two others absorb the cost, and that asymmetry is what corrodes engagement and goodwill. Rotating the slot so each location takes its turn distributes the burden equitably and signals that no group's comfort is privileged (A). Recording it (B) helps people who miss the call but concedes that two locations are effectively excluded from the live interaction, entrenching rather than fixing the inequity. The "least bad on average" slot (C) optimises a single fixed time and will almost always keep landing hardest on the same edge zone, reproducing the very imbalance in a new guise. Shifting to async (D) may be worth doing but is a larger redesign that dodges the immediate fairness issue and could still leave the remaining live touchpoint unfairly timed. The discriminator is whether the fix shares the unavoidable pain or merely relocates it onto the same people.
