# Question

Members of a fully remote team work flexible hours across several time zones. People keep messaging colleagues with urgent-sounding requests only to find the recipient is offline or off shift, and then feel ignored when there is no quick reply. What is the most effective thing the project manager can put in place?

## Options

- **A**: Mandate that every team member be reachable for at least the standard business day in the company's headquarters time zone.
- **B**: Ask members to reply to all direct messages within thirty minutes during their own working hours.
- **C**: Establish a shared, visible way for members to signal their working hours and current availability, and a norm of checking it before expecting a fast reply.
- **D**: Require that anything genuinely urgent be raised in the daily synchronous stand-up rather than by message.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The friction comes from invisible availability: people cannot tell when a colleague is working, so they misjudge whether a reply should be quick. A shared, visible signal of working hours and presence, plus the habit of consulting it, directly fixes that by setting accurate expectations (C). Forcing everyone onto headquarters hours (A) defeats the purpose of a flexible distributed team and pushes the burden onto edge time zones. A blanket thirty-minute reply rule (B) chains people to notifications and still does not tell a sender whether the recipient is even on shift. Routing all urgent matters through a single daily stand-up (D) cannot serve things that arise between stand-ups and ignores that members are online at different times. Making availability visible is the lightweight change that resolves the actual misunderstanding.
