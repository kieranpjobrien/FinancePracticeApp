# Question

A PM is launching a new virtual team whose members are in four countries and have never collaborated before. The PM wants to reduce the coordination friction that distributed teams typically hit in their first weeks. What is the most useful thing to do at the outset?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate the team in agreeing a team charter or working agreement covering overlap hours, channels and how decisions are made.
- **B**: Wait until the team has worked together for a few sprints, then capture the norms that have organically emerged into a written agreement.
- **C**: Send each member a copy of the organisation's standard remote-working policy and ask them to read and follow it.
- **D**: Schedule frequent check-in calls in the first weeks so the PM can resolve coordination problems individually as they arise.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Distributed teams hit predictable early friction over hours, channels and decision rights, and agreeing these explicitly up front — ideally as a team-owned working agreement — heads most of it off (A). (B) lets the friction happen first and only documents the norms after the painful weeks the PM is trying to avoid, and unaddressed norms can settle badly. (C) substitutes a generic corporate policy for the team's own agreed ways of working and generates little ownership. (D) makes the PM a permanent bottleneck for coordination the team should be able to handle itself, and treats recurring friction one incident at a time instead of preventing it.
