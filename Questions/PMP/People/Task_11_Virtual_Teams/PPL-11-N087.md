# Question

On a distributed software team, most written communication happens in English, which is a second language for four of the eight members. In retrospectives those four say they often miss nuance in long, idiom-heavy written threads and hesitate to respond for fear of misreading, which is slowing decisions. The team works in two-week sprints. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Move the team's important discussions onto synchronous video calls so the four members can ask for clarification in real time.
- **B**: Coach the team on clear, plain-language writing norms — short sentences, no idiom, explicit summaries of any decision or ask — and make those norms part of the working agreement.
- **C**: Arrange business-English training for the four second-language members so they can keep pace with the existing written style.
- **D**: Ask the native English speakers to write less and lean on a shared glossary of project terms to reduce misunderstandings.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The barrier sits in how the team writes — long, idiomatic threads — not in the four members' competence, so the fix should change the shared norm rather than place the burden on those disadvantaged by it. Plain-language conventions with explicit decision summaries make async communication accessible to everyone and bake the improvement into how the team operates (B). Shifting to synchronous calls (A) helps in the moment but sacrifices the async working that suits a distributed team and does not improve the written record people must rely on between calls. Sending the four to English training (C) frames them as the problem and asks them to adapt to an exclusionary style instead of fixing it; it is also slow relative to the sprint cadence. A glossary (D) helps with terminology but does nothing about idiom and nuance in long threads, which is the stated difficulty. The best action reshapes the team's writing for inclusivity rather than remediating the people the current style excludes.
