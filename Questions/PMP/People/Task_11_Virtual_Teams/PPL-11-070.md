# Question

During a hybrid project's planning meetings, six people sit together in a conference room while four join by video. The remote four report that side conversations happen in the room that they cannot hear, decisions seem half-made before the formal discussion, and they struggle to break into the flow. The in-room group is unaware of the effect. What is the PM's most effective change?

## Options

- **A**: Run the meeting as if everyone were remote — each person on their own device and audio, even those physically in the building — so there is one shared conversation.
- **B**: Position a better wide-angle camera and microphone in the conference room so the remote four can see and hear the in-room group more clearly.
- **C**: Ask the in-room participants to avoid side conversations and to repeat aloud anything important that is said off-microphone for the benefit of remote attendees.
- **D**: Appoint a facilitator in the room whose job is to watch the video feed and bring the remote four in whenever they have something to say.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The remote four are disadvantaged because two unequal meeting experiences are running in parallel; the most effective fix is to collapse them into one by having everyone join individually, which removes the in-room side channel entirely (A) — "one person, one screen" is the established equaliser for hybrid meetings. (B) improves the remote view of the room but leaves the structural asymmetry — there is still a room with its own dynamics that the remote four watch from outside. (C) depends on the in-room group's discipline and self-awareness, which they have already shown they lack, and asking people to remember to repeat themselves is fragile. (D) is a helpful supplement but relies on one person's vigilance and still treats the remote four as guests to be invited into a meeting centred on the room.
