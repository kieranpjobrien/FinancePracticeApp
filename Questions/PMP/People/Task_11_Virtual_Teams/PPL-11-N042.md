# Question

A project manager is forming a new distributed team whose members have never worked together and are spread across four countries. There is no budget to fly anyone in, so the kick-off must be virtual. The sponsor wants delivery to start within days and is sceptical of spending much time before work begins. The team will pick up real work the moment the session ends. The PM has time for one virtual kick-off. How should the PM spend it?

## Options

- **A**: Work through the project goals, scope and schedule thoroughly, and let the team's ways of working and rapport develop naturally as they start delivering together.
- **B**: Cover the goals at a working level, then spend the bulk of the session having the team co-create how they will operate remotely — overlap window, channels, decision-making — and meet one another in the process.
- **C**: Front-load connection — introductions and informal time so people form bonds first — on the basis that a team that trusts each other will sort out goals and working norms quickly once delivery is underway.
- **D**: Keep the kick-off tight to respect the sponsor's urgency: confirm the goal, hand the team a clear working agreement the PM has prepared, and let them get straight into the work.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The binding fact is that the team starts delivering immediately with no shared history, so the single session has to leave them able to actually work together across four zones — which means a usable shared goal plus agreed remote operating norms they own, with first contact happening as a by-product. (B) spends the scarce session on exactly the things that will not "just happen" once delivery starts. (A) is defensible — alignment on the what is real value — but it bets that norms and trust emerge on their own, and on a brand-new cross-zone team they tend not to; the team hits coordination friction in week one with nothing agreed. (C) is also a real school of thought (trust first), but pure bonding leaves the team with no operating model as work begins, and goals/norms are harder to retrofit under delivery pressure than to seed now. (D) respects the sponsor and is fast, but a working agreement the team merely receives carries far less commitment than one it co-creates, and the trimmed session skips the first-contact a never-met team needs; speed here is bought at the cost of the ownership that makes norms stick.
