# Question

A fully remote team complains it can never get deep work done, and the project manager audits the calendar with them. Total meeting hours per person are moderate, and the team confirms the standing ceremonies and recurring syncs are valued and that the coordination they provide is genuinely useful. But the meetings sit scattered through every day — a call here, a sync there — so the longest uninterrupted stretch anyone has is rarely more than 45 minutes. The PM wants to give the team real focused time. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Default every meeting to 25 or 50 minutes and make attendance optional for anyone not essential, so people recover slices of time across the day.
- **B**: Agree team-wide focus blocks — a protected window each day, and a no-meeting day or half-day each week — and consolidate the scattered syncs into the remaining time so deep work has uninterrupted runways.
- **C**: Require each recurring meeting to have an owner, agenda and stated purpose, dropping any that cannot justify one, so only meetings that earn their place remain.
- **D**: Shift as many of the recurring syncs as possible to asynchronous updates, replacing the live coordination with written status so the calendar empties out.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The audit points to arrangement, not volume: the hours are moderate and the team itself says the coordination is worth keeping, while the symptom — no stretch longer than 45 minutes — is about how the meetings are spread, not how many there are. Protected focus blocks plus pulling the scattered syncs into the remaining time (B) builds the uninterrupted runways the team lacks without sacrificing coordination they value. (A) shortens meetings and trims attendees, recovering slivers of time, but those slivers stay sprinkled across the day, so the fragmentation that actually blocks deep work survives. (C) is good governance and tempting whenever a calendar feels heavy, but the team has already vouched for these meetings, so culling is aimed at a problem that is not present — and it still leaves whatever remains scattered. (D) does defragment by deletion, yet it does so by converting coordination the team called genuinely useful into async, trading away a real benefit to solve a layout problem. The discriminator is recognising the load is needed but badly arranged, so the lever is clustering rather than cutting.
