# Question

Members of a distributed agile team complain of video-call exhaustion: between the daily stand-up, refinement, planning, review and retrospective — all synchronous and spanning awkward hours for some — they feel they are always in meetings and never doing focused work. The PM does not want to lose the value of the ceremonies. What is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: Examine each ceremony and convert the parts that do not need real-time interaction — status sharing, backlog grooming inputs — into async formats, keeping live time for genuine collaboration and decisions.
- **B**: Shorten every ceremony by strictly time-boxing it and enforcing the limit, so the total time spent in synchronous meetings each week falls.
- **C**: Reduce the cadence of the ceremonies — for example move from a daily to a twice-weekly stand-up — so members spend fewer hours in calls overall.
- **D**: Rotate the timing of the ceremonies so that the burden of inconvenient hours is shared evenly across the time zones rather than always falling on the same members.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The complaint is too much synchronous time, and the leverage is recognising that not every ceremony needs to be a live call — moving the async-suitable parts (status, grooming prep) out of real time reclaims focus while preserving live slots for true collaboration (A). (B) trims the edges but keeps everything synchronous, so the all-day-in-meetings feeling and the awkward-hours problem persist. (C) cuts cadence bluntly and risks losing the coordination value that frequent touchpoints provide, especially the daily alignment a distributed team relies on. (D) addresses only the unfairness of the timing, not the underlying volume of synchronous meetings that is causing the fatigue.
