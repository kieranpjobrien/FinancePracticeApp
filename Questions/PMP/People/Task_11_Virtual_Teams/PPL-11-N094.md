# Question

On a remote agile team, knowledge is concentrating in two senior developers who pick up the hardest stories and resolve them alone over screen-share only when stuck. Junior members rarely touch complex work, and the team is increasingly exposed if either senior is unavailable. The seniors are willing to help but say teaching slows them down. What should the project manager encourage the team to adopt?

## Options

- **A**: A requirement that the two seniors write detailed documentation of how they solve the hard problems, for the juniors to study.
- **B**: Regular remote pairing or mob sessions on complex stories, so a senior and one or more juniors work through the problem together as the work is done.
- **C**: A policy of assigning complex stories to junior members, with a senior available to answer questions when the junior gets stuck.
- **D**: Recorded technical walkthroughs by the seniors after they finish each hard story, shared with the team for later viewing.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The exposure comes from knowledge being created and held solo; the durable remedy is to spread it at the moment the work happens. Remote pairing or mobbing on the hard stories transfers reasoning and technique in real time and naturally distributes the complex work, addressing both the bus-factor risk and the juniors' lack of exposure (B). Documentation (A) captures conclusions but rarely the live judgement and problem-solving, ages quickly, and still leaves juniors learning passively rather than doing. Throwing complex stories to juniors with a senior on call (C) risks stalls and rework on genuinely hard work and gives the junior thin, after-the-fact help instead of guided practice — close to how the seniors already operate when "stuck." Recorded walkthroughs (D) are one-way and retrospective, conveying the polished path without the dead-ends, and depend on people watching them. Pairing and mobbing transfer the tacit skill as it is exercised, which neither documents nor recordings reliably do.
