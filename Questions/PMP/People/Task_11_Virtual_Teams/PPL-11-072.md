# Question

A PM has inherited a fully remote data-migration team whose members are technically strong but interact only when a task forces them to. There is no rapport, people are reluctant to ask one another for help, and a couple seem to be quietly disengaging. The PM wants to build trust and cohesion without adding pointless overhead. Which actions would most directly help? (Select two.)

## Options

- **A**: Create low-pressure informal interaction — a short non-work segment in syncs, an optional social channel — so people connect as people, not just as task owners.
- **B**: Make helping behaviour visible and safe by modelling asking for help, pairing members on tasks, and recognising those who support teammates.
- **C**: Mandate that cameras stay on for the full duration of every meeting so members can see each other and build familiarity faster.
- **D**: Introduce a public individual-productivity leaderboard so members can see how they compare, sharpening engagement through visible performance.
- **E**: Schedule a recurring all-hands social call at a fixed time and require attendance so that team bonding becomes a non-negotiable habit.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

Trust on an isolated remote team grows from informal human contact and from making mutual help visibly safe and normal, so creating low-pressure social interaction (A) and modelling and rewarding helping behaviour (B) attack the cohesion gap directly. (C) forces always-on camera presence, which causes fatigue and resentment and pressures people rather than inviting connection — voluntary, contextual camera use builds familiarity without coercion. (D) introduces individual competition, which corrodes the very willingness to help one another that the PM is trying to build, and can deepen the disengagement of those already pulling back. (E) is well-intentioned but mandatory, fixed-time socialising across a distributed team imposes bad hours and turns connection into an obligation, which rarely produces genuine rapport.
