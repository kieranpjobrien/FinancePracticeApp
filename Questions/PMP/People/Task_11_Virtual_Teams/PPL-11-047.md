# Question

A virtual product team spans Sydney, London and San Francisco. The weekly all-hands has always sat at 8am Sydney, which is midnight for the San Francisco engineers, and they have started skipping it. The Sydney members prefer keeping the slot. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Keep the current slot and record the session, asking the San Francisco engineers to submit questions ahead via the project channel.
- **B**: Rotate the meeting time across a three-week cycle so each region carries the inconvenient slot roughly a third of the time.
- **C**: Split the all-hands into two regional sessions the PM runs separately, ensuring every member attends one at a civilised hour.
- **D**: Move the meeting permanently to a time that favours San Francisco, since they are the members currently disengaged from the cadence.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

PMI treats virtual-team fairness as a shared burden — a rotating cadence distributes inconvenience equitably and keeps the whole team in a single conversation. (A) preserves the inequity and makes recordings a substitute for participation. (C) fragments the team and doubles the PM's load without addressing the fairness issue. (D) just moves the pain from one group to another rather than sharing it.
