# Question

A distributed agile team of nine is spread one-third each across three continents, with almost no overlapping working hours between any two regions. Work constantly blocks: a story picked up in one region needs an input that only another region can give, and the answer arrives a day later. The team is delivering, but slowly, and morale is sagging under the constant waiting. The product is a single tightly coupled codebase. What should the PM facilitate first?

## Options

- **A**: Help the team restructure how work is sliced so that each region can own coherent, loosely coupled areas and complete most stories without waiting on another zone.
- **B**: Introduce a strict, detailed async hand-off protocol with templates and SLAs so that the unavoidable cross-zone dependencies are at least handled cleanly and predictably.
- **C**: Establish a small daily synchronous window that all three regions attend, even though it falls outside normal hours for two of them, to unblock dependencies in real time.
- **D**: Reassign the most interdependent work to a single region so that the tightest dependencies are resolved within one time zone, accepting an uneven workload distribution.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The root cause is that tightly coupled work is split across non-overlapping zones, so every story spawns a cross-zone dependency; the highest-leverage fix is to re-slice the work so regions own loosely coupled areas and can finish independently (A) — change the dependency structure and the daily waiting largely disappears. (B) makes the dependencies tidier but leaves them in place, so the day-long round trips and the morale drain continue. (C) buys real-time unblocking at the cost of imposing unsociable hours on two-thirds of the team indefinitely, which trades one morale problem for another. (D) concentrates the coupled work in one zone but creates a lopsided workload and leaves the other regions under-utilised; it relieves the symptom without addressing the architecture-and-slicing cause.
