# Question

Two sub-teams on a distributed logistics platform — one in Manila, one in Berlin — have started blaming each other for missed integration deadlines. In calls the tone is now cool and defensive, and each side privately tells the project manager the other "doesn't really pull its weight." Both sub-teams have only ever met online. What is the BEST way for the project manager to begin addressing this?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the two sub-teams together in a facilitated working session focused on jointly mapping the integration workflow and where it actually breaks down.
- **B**: Hold separate calls with each sub-team to hear their grievances in full before deciding whether the two groups need to be brought together.
- **C**: Propose an in-person workshop bringing both sub-teams to one location to build the personal trust that has never had a chance to form.
- **D**: Restate each sub-team's responsibilities and the shared integration deadlines so accountability for the misses is unambiguous on both sides.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Distributed sub-teams that have never met easily slide from "the work is failing" into "the other group is failing"; a facilitated joint session on the shared workflow rebuilds a common picture, surfaces the real process breakdown and turns two opposing camps back into one team working a problem. (B) can entrench positions by letting each side rehearse grievances, and delays the contact that actually resolves attribution conflict. (C) may help eventually but is slow and costly, and trust here is being lost over work, which the joint session tackles directly and now. (D) reasserts accountability without addressing the broken collaboration, and is likely to harden the blame rather than dissolve it.
