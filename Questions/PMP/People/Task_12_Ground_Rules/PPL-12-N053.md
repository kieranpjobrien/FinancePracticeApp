# Question

You are facilitating a brand-new cross-functional team in co-creating its ground rules at project start, and you want the agreements to actually hold up once the work gets busy. All five practices below are reasonable, but three do the most to make the agreements durable and self-enforcing. Which THREE? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Have the team draft the rules in its own words rather than reacting to a pre-written list you supply.
- **B**: Keep the set small and specific to behaviours the team genuinely cares about, parking anything speculative.
- **C**: Make the agreements visible and put a standing item on the retrospective to revisit them.
- **D**: Capture each agreement together with the reason the team wanted it, so the intent survives when membership changes.
- **E**: Run a quick anonymous poll at the end so any member uneasy about a rule can flag it before it is adopted.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

(A), (B) and (C) are the highest-leverage drivers of adherence: drafting in the team's own words builds ownership, a short list of rules the team values stays memorable and used, and visibility plus a retrospective hook keeps the agreements alive and adapting. (D) is genuinely good practice — recording rationale aids onboarding and prevents cargo-cult rules — but it strengthens longevity of meaning rather than the immediate likelihood the team self-enforces the agreements, so it ranks just below the core three. (E) is a sound inclusion check and surfacing quiet dissent is valuable, yet an end-of-session poll is a refinement on top of good co-creation; it does not by itself make rules stick the way ownership, focus, and living review do. The question turns on selecting the strongest three, not on rejecting two bad options.
