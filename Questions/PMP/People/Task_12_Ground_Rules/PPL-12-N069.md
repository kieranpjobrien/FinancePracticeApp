# Question

An agile team created its working agreement nine months ago. It is pinned in the team's virtual workspace but rarely opened, and several rules no longer match how the team works. The team holds retrospectives every sprint, yet they are spent almost entirely on the most recent sprint's incidents and the agreement is never reopened. The project manager wants the agreement to be revisited reliably rather than left to drift again. What should she do?

## Options

- **A**: Propose the team add a standing item to review the working agreement at one retrospective each release, so it is examined deliberately rather than crowded out by sprint incidents.
- **B**: Ask the team to keep the agreement open during every retrospective and check the recent sprint's friction against it as they go.
- **C**: Schedule a separate, dedicated working-agreement review session outside the retrospective so it gets undivided attention.
- **D**: Encourage members to flag any rule that no longer fits the moment they notice it, and amend the agreement as those points arise.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The retrospective is the right event for inspecting and adapting ways of working, but this team's retrospectives are consumed by recent incidents, so simply having retrospectives is not enough — the review needs a protected, recurring slot at a sustainable cadence. (A) embeds a deliberate review into the existing inspect-and-adapt event at a release rhythm, frequent enough to stay current without competing with every sprint's firefighting. (B) keeps the agreement visible, which helps, but folding it into every already-overloaded retrospective is exactly the pressure that has been crowding it out; visibility alone has not prompted revision. (C) gets undivided attention but creates a separate meeting that is easy to skip and detaches the review from the team's natural inspect-and-adapt forum, making it more likely to lapse, not less. (D) is a useful continuous habit but relies on individuals noticing in the moment — the same passive reliance that let the agreement go stale for nine months — and provides no guaranteed point of review. The discrimination is among four real cadences for keeping the agreement live, not between obviously right and wrong Scrum events.
