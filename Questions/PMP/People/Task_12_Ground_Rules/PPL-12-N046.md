# Question

A high-performing Scrum team follows a mature set of working agreements almost automatically — they are rarely spoken about because everyone already knows them. Two new developers join mid-project and, unaware these unwritten norms exist, interrupt during the daily scrum and skip the team's pull-request review etiquette. The existing members are starting to bristle. As the project manager, what is the best way to handle this?

## Options

- **A**: Ask the Scrum Master to brief the two newcomers on the established agreements so they can get up to speed quickly without taking team time.
- **B**: Pair each newcomer with an experienced team member who models the norms in the flow of work, letting them absorb the agreements through collaboration.
- **C**: Raise it at the retrospective as a team topic, so the group decides together how to bring the newcomers up to the agreed standard.
- **D**: Have the team surface its working agreements with the new members in the next session, and use the moment to check whether any norm should be written down or adjusted now the team has grown.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The newcomers cannot follow agreements they have never seen, and the trigger is that the norms were tacit. (D) is strongest because it both makes the agreements explicit to the new members and treats the team's change in composition as the natural prompt to review and document norms that had survived only as oral tradition — keeping the agreements living and owned by the whole, now larger, team. (A) fixes the information gap efficiently but a one-way briefing from the Scrum Master misses the team's role in welcoming people and the chance to refresh agreements that clearly were not robust enough to onboard anyone. (B) is a sound onboarding practice, yet relying on osmosis to transmit norms that even the existing team never articulated is slow and inconsistent, and friction is already occurring. (C) sensibly involves the team but waiting for the retrospective lets the daily-scrum and review-etiquette friction continue for the rest of the sprint when a near-term conversation would resolve it sooner.
