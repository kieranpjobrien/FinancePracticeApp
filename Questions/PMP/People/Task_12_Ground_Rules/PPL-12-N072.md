# Question

A predictive maritime engineering team agreed a ground rule that all design decisions are logged in the decision register within one working day. Three months on, the register is half-empty: decisions are made verbally and forgotten, and two recent reworks traced back to undocumented choices. The team still considers the rule valid and important. Which THREE actions best help the rule actually take hold? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Have the team agree, at a retrospective, on a lightweight trigger — such as logging the decision before the meeting ends — so the rule fits how they actually work.
- **B**: Make recent register entries briefly visible at the start of relevant meetings so the team sees the rule being used and gaps become obvious.
- **C**: Reaffirm together why the rule matters, linking it explicitly to the two reworks the missing entries caused.
- **D**: Assign the project manager to log all decisions on the team's behalf to guarantee completeness.
- **E**: Add a penalty whereby any member who fails to log a decision must present a remediation plan to the team.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

The rule is sound but does not fit the team's flow and has lost its felt purpose, so the fixes target friction, visibility, and meaning: (A) lowers the effort by embedding logging into the moment of decision, (B) makes adherence visible so the rule is reinforced and gaps surface, and (C) reconnects the rule to the concrete cost of ignoring it. (D) appears to guarantee completeness but transfers ownership away from the team to one person, so the team never internalises the habit and the rule stops being theirs. (E) introduces a punitive mechanism that typically breeds resentment and defensive minimal compliance rather than genuine adoption, and it treats a workflow-fit problem as a discipline problem. The question turns on choosing the three that build durable ownership over the two that bypass or coerce it.
