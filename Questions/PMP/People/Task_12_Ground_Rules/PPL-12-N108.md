# Question

A project manager has inherited a struggling hybrid construction-technology project. A team charter exists but is changing nothing: members routinely ignore it. She wants to pin down the structural reasons it carries no force. Which THREE characteristics of the charter would most plausibly explain why it fails to influence the team's behaviour? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: It was written by the previous project manager alone and handed to the team as a finished document.
- **B**: It is held in a shared repository that every member can open, though few ever do.
- **C**: It is never invoked — not in meetings, onboarding, or retrospectives — so it has slipped out of the team's awareness.
- **D**: It runs to about thirty detailed rules, many covering situations this team has never faced.
- **E**: It states near the top that the agreements may be revisited and revised whenever the team sees fit.

## Answer

A, C, D

## Explanation

A charter loses force when the team never owned it (A — sole authorship by the prior PM kills buy-in), when it is absent from daily practice (C — agreements no one references are forgotten), and when it is so bloated that the few rules that matter are lost among many that do not (D). Those three directly explain weak influence. (B) is the more tempting trap: that members rarely open it looks damning, but accessibility is a neutral-to-positive property — the defect is that the charter is never referenced in the team's actual rituals (C), not where the file sits; storage location is not why it fails. (E) reads like permissiveness that might invite members to ignore the rules, but declaring an agreement adaptable is recognised good practice that keeps it alive, so it cannot be a structural cause of failure. The question turns on separating genuine weaknesses from properties that are benign or beneficial.
