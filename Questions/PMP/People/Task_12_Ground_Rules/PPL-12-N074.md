# Question

Midway through a hybrid programme, the project manager notices that in-room members dominate discussions while remote members struggle to break in, and the existing working agreement says nothing about this. She wants to revise the agreement with the team to level participation between co-located and remote members. Several reasonable agreements are proposed. Which THREE would most directly close the gap between in-room and remote voices? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: A facilitator monitors the remote channel and actively invites remote members into the discussion at decision points.
- **B**: When any member is remote, everyone joins the call from their own device so all participants share the same medium.
- **C**: Decisions taken in a meeting are summarised in writing and remote members have a defined window to raise objections before they are final.
- **D**: Minutes and an action log are published after every meeting so remote members can see what was decided.
- **E**: A rotating chair runs each meeting so facilitation does not always sit with the same person.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

The imbalance stems from co-located members having a richer, faster channel during the meeting than remote members, so the most direct agreements equalise the live medium and guarantee remote influence in the moment: (A) actively draws remote input in as decisions are made, (B) removes the in-room advantage by putting everyone on the same footing, and (C) protects remote members' ability to shape decisions even after the discussion. (D) is good practice and improves transparency, but publishing minutes after the fact informs remote members of decisions already taken — it does not give them a voice while those decisions are being made, so it does not close the participation gap. (E) distributes the facilitation role and is healthy generally, but rotating who chairs does nothing in itself to bring remote voices into a room that structurally favours the co-located; an in-room chair can dominate remote members just as easily. The question turns on selecting agreements that equalise live participation over ones that are generally sound but leave the in-room/remote channel gap intact.
