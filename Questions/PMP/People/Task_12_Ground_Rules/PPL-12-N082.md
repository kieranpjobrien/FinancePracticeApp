# Question

A self-managing Scrum team co-created a working agreement that says "we hold each other accountable to our agreements." For three iterations a developer has merged code without the agreed pair review, each time triggering a defect that the whole team then scrambles to fix. Other members grumble privately but no one raises it with him, and they have begun looking to you, the project manager, to discipline him. What is the most appropriate action?

## Options

- **A**: Have a direct private conversation with the developer to correct the behaviour, since the team has clearly delegated the matter to you.
- **B**: Coach the team to surface the breach with the developer themselves, as their agreement makes peer accountability the team's own responsibility.
- **C**: Add an automated branch-protection rule that blocks merges without a recorded review, removing the need for anyone to confront him.
- **D**: Raise the pattern anonymously in the next retrospective so the issue is aired without singling the developer out.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The team explicitly owns "holding each other accountable," so the durable fix is to help the team exercise that accountability rather than relieve them of it. (B) coaches the members to do what their own agreement commits them to, preserving the self-managing dynamic. (A) takes the problem off the team's shoulders, which is what they want but precisely what undermines the agreement and trains them to escalate every lapse to you. (C) is a useful safeguard but treats a behavioural and trust problem as purely technical, leaving the avoidance of difficult conversations untouched. (D) keeps the breach anonymous, which avoids the specific, direct feedback the situation calls for and lets the developer dodge ownership of a repeated, named behaviour.
