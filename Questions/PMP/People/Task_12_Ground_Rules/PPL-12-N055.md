# Question

A team member has twice broken an agreed ground rule about not committing code without a peer review, each time causing a build failure, and the team is visibly frustrated. All five responses below are things a project manager might reasonably consider. Which TWO best uphold the agreement while preserving team ownership and the individual's dignity? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Add a branch-protection rule that technically blocks any merge until a peer review is recorded.
- **B**: Speak with the individual privately to understand what is driving the lapses and reaffirm the shared expectation.
- **C**: Restate the peer-review rule to the whole team in writing and ask everyone to recommit to it.
- **D**: Bring the recurring build failures to the team's next retrospective as a pattern to examine, without singling out the individual.
- **E**: Have a respected peer who pairs with the member raise the impact directly, since peer accountability often lands better than manager intervention.

## Answer

B, D

## Explanation

(B) and (D) together cover the two dimensions a ground-rule breach needs: a private, respectful conversation that seeks the cause and re-asserts the norm to the individual, and a team-level look at the pattern in the team's own improvement forum, which reinforces shared ownership without shaming anyone. (A) — branch protection — is a sensible engineering safeguard and many teams would add it, but enforcing the agreement purely through tooling sidesteps the behavioural conversation and the team's role in owning the norm; it is a complement, not the primary response. (E) peer accountability can be effective, yet directing a colleague to deliver the message risks putting that peer in an awkward spot and can feel orchestrated; the PM addressing it directly first is more appropriate when failures are already affecting the team. (C) a written recommitment is harmless but weak on its own — it neither uncovers why this member is lapsing nor engages the team in examining the pattern.
