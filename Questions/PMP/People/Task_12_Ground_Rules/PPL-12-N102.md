# Question

An agile team building a mobile banking feature wrote a working agreement in its first sprint. Six sprints on, two original members have rotated off and three developers have joined. In a retrospective a new member says she did not know an agreement existed and has been breaking one of its norms; the two other joiners nod that they had not seen it either. The remaining original members still regard the agreement as serving them well. What should the Scrum Master do?

## Options

- **A**: Walk the whole team through the existing agreement now to close the awareness gap, and put a review of it on the agenda for an upcoming retrospective.
- **B**: Facilitate the team in revisiting and refreshing the agreement together in this session, so its current membership re-owns it.
- **C**: Have the new joiners read the agreement and raise anything they would change, then bring only those points to the team to decide.
- **D**: Add agreement walkthroughs to onboarding so future joiners are covered, and leave the current agreement as the originals still endorse it.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The trigger is a significant change in who the team is: nearly half the members had no part in the agreement and one is already unknowingly breaching it, so the agreement no longer reflects the team that must live by it. Re-ratifying it with the current group both closes the awareness gap and restores ownership, which is why (B) is strongest. (A) is reasonable and informs everyone, but a walkthrough is one-way — the newcomers still had no voice in norms they are now held to, and deferring the review leaves that gap open. (C) gives joiners some input but frames the agreement as the originals' to amend at the margins rather than the whole current team's to own, and it splits the team into authors and reviewers. (D) fixes the future but not the present and treats the originals' endorsement as sufficient when a third of today's team never consented, so it under-responds to the actual problem.
