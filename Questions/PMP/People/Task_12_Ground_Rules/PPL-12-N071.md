# Question

You are facilitating a new team as it drafts its working agreement. The team is enthusiastic and proposes more than thirty rules covering everything from meeting times to tooling preferences to dress code on video. You want the final set to be effective and actually used. Which TWO principles should most guide what makes it into the agreement? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Keep the agreement focused on behaviours the team genuinely needs to coordinate, rather than cataloguing every preference.
- **B**: Ensure each rule is one the team can observe and hold itself to, not an aspiration no one will track.
- **C**: Include as many rules as the team proposes, since broader coverage reduces future ambiguity.
- **D**: Have the project manager pre-select which proposed rules are sensible before the team finalises them.
- **E**: Phrase the rules to mirror the organisation's standard policies so the agreement aligns with corporate norms.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

A working agreement sticks when it is short, relevant, and self-enforceable: (A) keeps it to the behaviours that actually need coordinating, and (B) keeps every rule observable so the team can hold itself accountable. Together they produce a usable agreement. (C) is the opposite of what works — a sprawling rule set becomes noise no one references, which is the very risk in this scenario. (D) strips ownership from the team by inserting the project manager as gatekeeper, undermining buy-in. (E) confuses the team's behavioural agreement with organisational policy; aligning to corporate norms is not the test of whether a ground rule helps this team coordinate, and importing policy wording rarely reflects how the team actually wants to work.
