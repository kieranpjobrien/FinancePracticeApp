# Question

A project manager is forming a new cross-functional team of eleven people drawn from five departments. Several members report that their home departments have very different working norms around meetings, chat etiquette and deadlines. The PM wants to set team ground rules. What is the BEST approach?

## Options

- **A**: Circulate a draft set of ground rules drawn from the PMO's template and ask members to flag any specific objections before adoption.
- **B**: Facilitate a kickoff session where the team discusses past friction points and co-creates their own ground rules around those real tensions.
- **C**: Observe how the team actually works for the first two sprints, then propose ground rules that address the specific issues that have surfaced.
- **D**: Adopt the ground rules of the largest contributing department as a sensible default, on the grounds that most members are already familiar with them.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

PMI's team-building and servant-leadership principles say ground rules should be co-created against real tensions — ownership comes from the people who will be held to them. (A) invites compliance rather than commitment and skirts the actual departmental friction. (C) waits for avoidable conflict before acting. (D) privileges one group's norms and imposes them on the rest.
