# Question

A Scrum team has self-authored working agreements it has followed well for two sprints, including a 9:00 daily scrum the team chose. A functional manager who line-manages three of the developers tells you a new recurring departmental meeting clashes with the stand-up and asks you to move the team's stand-up to 9:30. The clash is real and the departmental meeting is not going away. As the project manager, what is the most appropriate way to handle this?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the manager's constraint to the team and have it decide whether and how to adjust its own stand-up time, since the team owns its working agreements.
- **B**: Move the stand-up to 9:30 yourself to resolve the clash immediately, then tell the team at the next stand-up and invite them to flag any problem with the new time.
- **C**: Propose 9:30 to the team as your recommended resolution and ask them to confirm it, so the clash is settled quickly with the team's agreement on record.
- **D**: Ask the manager whether the departmental meeting could move instead, and only take the stand-up clash to the team if it cannot.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A working agreement is owned by the team it governs, so a genuine external constraint should be brought to the team as input for the team to resolve — it will almost certainly accommodate a reasonable need it understands, and may find a better answer than the one proposed. (A) does exactly this and preserves the self-organisation that makes the agreement hold. (B) reaches a workable outcome but changes the team's own agreement first and consults after, which inverts the ownership; "tell them and let them object" is materially weaker than letting them decide. (C) keeps the team in the loop and is close, but pre-loading a single PM-preferred time and asking for confirmation narrows the team's decision to a yes/no on your answer rather than genuinely handing them the problem — subtle erosion of ownership. (D) is a sensible thing to explore and may even be the kinder fix, but it addresses the clash one level up while sidestepping the team's right to weigh in on its own ceremony; checking whether the other meeting can move is a parallel courtesy, not a substitute for taking a constraint that affects the team's agreement to the team.
