# Question

While the team drafts its ground rules, a member proposes "we assume positive intent when reading each other's written messages." Another counters that such a vague rule cannot be observed or enforced and will just be ignored, unlike concrete rules about meeting times. The team looks split and turns to you as facilitator. What is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: Invite the team to keep the norm if it values it, and work it into something observable — for example, "if a written message reads as harsh, ask the sender what they meant before reacting."
- **B**: Affirm that interpersonal norms belong in ground rules and add the rule as proposed, since how members treat one another is exactly what ground rules are for.
- **C**: Suggest the team set the norm aside for now and reconsider it only if an incident of misread messages actually occurs.
- **D**: Recommend the team capture the sentiment as a shared value in the charter's purpose section rather than as an operating ground rule.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Both members have a point: relational norms genuinely belong in ground rules, and a norm too vague to act on will be ignored. (A) honours the proposer's intent while answering the sceptic's valid concern, turning an aspiration into a behaviour the team can actually practise — and it keeps the decision with the team. (B) is correct that interpersonal norms belong, but adding the rule exactly as worded ignores the legitimate enforceability objection and risks creating the dead-letter rule the sceptic predicted. (C) defers a norm the team is actively discussing until something goes wrong, which forfeits the chance to prevent the very misreadings the rule targets. (D) relocating it to a values statement is not unreasonable, but it quietly downgrades a behavioural norm the team could operationalise into an aspiration with no practice attached, weakening rather than strengthening it.
