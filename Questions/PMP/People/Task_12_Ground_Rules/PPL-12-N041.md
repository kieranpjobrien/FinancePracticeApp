# Question

A newly formed Scrum team is co-creating its working agreements in a facilitated session. The team quickly fills a board with proposed norms, but a senior developer pushes back: "We're guessing at problems we haven't hit yet. Half of these are theoretical." The Scrum Master looks to you. The team genuinely does not yet know how it will work together. What is the most effective way to proceed?

## Options

- **A**: Have the team agree only the few norms it feels strongly about now, and explicitly schedule the retrospective as the point to add or revise agreements once real friction surfaces.
- **B**: Keep each proposed agreement that a member can tie to a real problem from a previous team, and drop the purely hypothetical ones, so the set is fuller but every rule is grounded in lived experience.
- **C**: Ask each member to privately rank the proposed norms and adopt the ones that score highest across the team.
- **D**: Park the working agreements entirely and let the first sprint run, so every rule the team later sets is grounded in something it actually experienced.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The senior developer's point is partly valid — a bloated speculative list will not stick — but starting with no agreements at all leaves the team without basic operating norms. (A) keeps a small set the team genuinely owns now and builds in the retrospective as the mechanism to evolve them, which is exactly how living working agreements are meant to grow, and it directly answers the developer's objection. (B) is a thoughtful response to the same objection — grounding each rule in a member's real prior experience filters out pure speculation — but agreements imported from people's former teams are not yet this team's own, and a fuller starting set still risks adopting norms that pre-empt friction this particular team may never hit; it is close to (A) but anchors to others' history rather than this team's emerging reality. (C) is a reasonable prioritisation technique but a silent ranking skips the discussion where shared understanding and buy-in actually form, and still risks adopting norms no one feels strongly about. (D) over-corrects to having no agreements at all, so the team's first sprint runs without even minimal shared expectations and early bad habits can set before the retrospective.
