# Question

During ground-rule co-design, ten of eleven team members agree to a rule that all sprint commitments are team commitments rather than individual ones. The eleventh strongly prefers individual ownership and will not agree. Time pressure is real. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Explore the holdout's concern in depth, understand the underlying worry, and either adjust the rule to address it or genuinely convince them of the team-ownership intent.
- **B**: Adopt the rule anyway on the strength of the clear majority since waiting for unanimity on every single rule would stall the team indefinitely.
- **C**: Drop the proposed rule because a single team member's continued objection means the agreement will never feel genuinely shared or psychologically safe.
- **D**: Keep the rule as drafted and grant the holdout a private personal exemption so they can continue to be held to individual accountability on their work.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

PMI's collaborative problem-solving approach says to surface and address the underlying concern — this often reshapes the rule in a way the holdout can commit to, without damaging the majority's intent. (B) overrides a legitimate voice without trying to hear it first. (C) abandons a widely-supported rule too quickly. (D) creates a two-tier team where one member works under different terms.
