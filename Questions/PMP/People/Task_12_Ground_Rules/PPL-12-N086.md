# Question

A predictive rail-signalling project's team charter states that technical disagreements the team cannot resolve within one working day are escalated to the lead engineer for a binding decision. Lately the lead engineer complains he is being pulled into trivial disputes the team should settle itself; the team, pointing to the same rule, says it is simply doing what the charter tells it to. Neither side is breaking the rule as they read it, yet the friction is mounting. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Direct the team to stop escalating minor disputes, since the charter clearly intends escalation only as a last resort.
- **B**: Remove the escalation rule entirely so the team is forced to reach its own decisions and stops relying on the lead engineer.
- **C**: Facilitate the team and lead engineer to refine the rule together, clarifying what qualifies for escalation and what the team is expected to resolve on its own.
- **D**: Ask the lead engineer to simply decline disputes he considers trivial, leaving the rule unchanged.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The rule is being applied in good faith by both sides, which signals that it is genuinely ambiguous rather than being flouted — the gap is in the agreement's definition, not in anyone's behaviour. (C) brings the affected parties together to clarify the escalation threshold, which both restores a shared understanding and preserves their ownership of the agreement. (A) imposes one party's reading by authority and leaves the wording — the actual source of the dispute — unchanged, so the conflict will recur. (B) discards a legitimate mechanism the team relies on for genuine deadlocks, overcorrecting a calibration problem. (D) gives the lead engineer an inconsistent, unilateral filter with no agreed criteria, which will feel arbitrary to the team and breed further friction.
