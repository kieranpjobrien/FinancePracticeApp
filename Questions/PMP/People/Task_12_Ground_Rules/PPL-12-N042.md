# Question

On a predictive infrastructure project, the signed team charter states that design reviews start on time and latecomers catch up on their own. You have already spoken privately with a normally reliable senior engineer who has arrived late to the last three reviews; she explained that a parallel assignment her functional manager gave her overruns into the review slot, and she expects it to continue for several more weeks. Meanwhile other members, unaware of the cause, have started recapping for her each time and are privately resentful. What should you do next?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the recurring lateness to the team as a shared problem to solve together, so the group decides how to adapt the review while the clash persists.
- **B**: Reschedule the design reviews to a slot that avoids the engineer's conflicting assignment, removing the clash for everyone.
- **C**: Work with the engineer and her functional manager to resolve the scheduling clash so she can attend the full review, and let the team see the conflict is being addressed.
- **D**: Hold the charter rule firm and ask the team to stop recapping, so the agreed "latecomers catch up unaided" norm is actually applied.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The lateness has a genuine external root — a clash created by another manager — so the highest-value move is to fix that cause at its source while keeping the team informed enough to defuse the resentment. (C) does both: resolving the clash restores the engineer's attendance and signals to the team that the breach is being handled, without exposing or blaming her. (A) is a legitimate collaborative instinct and the team may well help, but taking one person's manager-imposed scheduling conflict to the whole team risks singling her out and asks the group to work around a problem you can actually remove; it treats the symptom. (B) could work, but unilaterally moving a recurring review for the whole team to accommodate one person's temporary external assignment is disproportionate and may simply create fresh clashes for others — it should follow, not precede, an attempt to resolve the conflict directly. (D) enforces the letter of the charter but ignores that the rule was written for ordinary tardiness, not a sanctioned competing commitment; rigidly applying it while the cause goes unaddressed leaves the resentment and the lost review content intact.
