# Question

A predictive infrastructure team's charter states that technical disagreements unresolved within the team are escalated to the lead architect for a decision. Lately the team has been escalating almost every minor design choice, and the architect is becoming a bottleneck while the team's own decision-making atrophies. The charter rule is being followed to the letter. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate the team to refine the escalation rule, defining what genuinely warrants escalation versus what the team should resolve itself.
- **B**: Ask the lead architect to push trivial decisions back to the team each time, so they learn where the threshold lies.
- **C**: Remind the team that escalation is a last resort and encourage them to decide more matters themselves.
- **D**: Add a rule that the team must attempt at least two rounds of discussion before any escalation.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The rule is being applied correctly but its scope was never defined, so it is being overused; the right response is to revise the agreement with the team to draw a clear line between team-resolvable choices and genuine escalations. (A) does this collaboratively and addresses the root cause. (B) pushes the problem onto the architect to police case by case without giving the team a shared, agreed threshold, so the bottleneck and inconsistency persist. (C) is a verbal nudge with no agreed criterion behind it, so behaviour is unlikely to change durably. (D) adds a mechanical hurdle that may simply delay the same escalations rather than helping the team judge which decisions are theirs to make; it treats the symptom of frequency, not the missing definition.
