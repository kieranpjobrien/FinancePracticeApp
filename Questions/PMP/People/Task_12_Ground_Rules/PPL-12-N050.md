# Question

Your agile team agreed a ground rule that anyone may call a "pause" when a conversation drifts off-topic, and that the team self-polices its own agreements. Lately, only you ever invoke the pause; members visibly notice the drift but stay silent and wait for you to act. As the project manager, what is the best way to restore shared ownership of the rule?

## Options

- **A**: Stop calling the pause yourself for a few sessions, so the resulting drift creates space and need for others to step in.
- **B**: Put the rule on the next retrospective agenda as a team health item, letting the group decide collectively how to revive peer enforcement.
- **C**: Raise the pattern with the team, ask what is making them hesitant to invoke the pause, and adjust the agreement together so peers feel safe using it.
- **D**: Make a point of warmly thanking anyone other than you who invokes the pause, reinforcing the behaviour you want to see spread.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The real issue is that peer-enforcement has quietly defaulted to you, and you do not yet know why members stay silent — it could be deference, psychological safety, or an unclear rule. (C) diagnoses that cause directly and repairs the agreement with the team, addressing both the behaviour and its root. (A) might prompt others to step up, but deliberately letting meetings drift to force the issue wastes the team's time and risks looking like a test rather than a fix, and if no one steps in the rule simply dies. (B) is a sound, ownership-preserving venue, yet deferring to the next retrospective postpones a conversation you could have now, and the retro may surface the issue without uncovering why people hesitate. (D) positive reinforcement is a fine supporting tactic and worth doing, but on its own it treats the symptom and may not reach members whose silence stems from not feeling safe to act in the first place.
