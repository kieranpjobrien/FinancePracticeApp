# Question

A hybrid aerospace programme's team charter states "decisions made in a meeting are final unless new information emerges." A senior engineer keeps reopening settled design decisions in side conversations after meetings, which is slowing progress. Two other members have separately mentioned the same frustration to the project manager. The charter says nothing about how breaches of its norms are handled, and the next decision-heavy design review is in two days. How should the project manager proceed?

## Options

- **A**: Speak with the engineer one to one before the review, name the pattern against the agreed norm, and ask what is driving it.
- **B**: Put the charter's enforcement gap on the next retrospective so the team agrees together how reopened decisions will be handled going forward.
- **C**: Open the upcoming review by restating the norm to everyone and asking the team to recommit to it, without singling anyone out.
- **D**: Add a standing "parking lot" step to the meeting format so post-meeting reopenings are captured and dispositioned in the room instead.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

This turns on what to do first when one identifiable person is repeatedly breaching a known norm and the friction is live. The lowest-exposure, most direct move is to understand from the engineer what is driving it before changing any structure or going public, and it may surface a legitimate concern — that the reviews are closing decisions the engineer believes are still unresolved. (A) does this. (B) is a sound way to fix the charter's missing enforcement mechanism, but reforming the rule is a slower, second-order response to behaviour happening now and it sidesteps the individual whose conduct is the actual problem. (C) keeps things blameless and reinforces shared ownership, but a general recommitment lets the specific person hear a group reminder without ever being engaged, so the targeted behaviour is unlikely to shift. (D) is a genuinely useful process fix that would help any team and may well follow, but adjusting the meeting mechanics treats a personal pattern as a workflow defect and still leaves the engineer's underlying reason unexamined. Each option is a defensible PMI move; the discriminator is that addressing the individual directly comes before reshaping the team's rules or rituals.
