# Question

On a predictive pharmaceutical validation project, the team charter — co-authored and signed by everyone three months ago — requires peer sign-off before any test protocol is finalised. A respected senior analyst, under deadline pressure, has begun finalising protocols alone and the work has so far been flawless. A junior member quietly tells the project manager he is uneasy but does not want to seem to be criticising a senior colleague. What is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: Remind the whole team at the next meeting that the peer sign-off rule applies to everyone, without referring to the specific instances.
- **B**: Ask the team to revisit whether the peer sign-off rule still fits, given delivery pressure, and adjust or reaffirm it together.
- **C**: Speak privately with the senior analyst, restating the agreed rule and the reason the team adopted it, and agree how sign-off will happen under the deadline.
- **D**: Let the matter rest while the analyst's work remains error-free, and revisit only if a defect appears.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The charter is a team agreement that one member is now bypassing, so the project manager addresses it directly and privately with that person, anchoring the conversation in the shared rationale (independent review catches errors a single author misses) rather than blame. (C) restores the agreement and problem-solves the deadline pressure at once. (A) is too oblique: a generic reminder lets the analyst infer it is not really aimed at her and leaves the junior member's concern unresolved. (B) treats a compliance lapse as if the rule itself were in question — the team has not asked to change it, and reopening it now would reward circumvention; revisiting agreements is for when they genuinely no longer fit, not when someone simply skipped one. (D) ignores that the value of peer sign-off in a regulated environment is precisely to prevent the defect before it occurs; waiting for one defeats the control's purpose.
