# Question

A predictive water-treatment plant upgrade is starting. The team is drawn from four contractor firms whose members have not worked together and whose firms each have their own conduct policies. The schedule is tight, and the sponsor has stressed that the team must reach productive output quickly. As the project manager weighs how to put the team's ground rules in place, which approach should she take?

## Options

- **A**: Adopt the conduct policy of the lead contractor as the interim baseline now, and let the team adjust specific rules later once it has worked together for a few sprints.
- **B**: Run a facilitated session at kickoff in which the team drafts and agrees its own behavioural norms before detailed planning begins.
- **C**: Circulate a concise draft she has prepared from comparable past projects, invite written comments, and finalise it from the responses.
- **D**: Agree only a minimal set of safety- and access-related rules now, and let the broader behavioural norms form through working together and be captured once patterns emerge.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The defining feature here is a brand-new team with no shared norms and several competing firm cultures; ground rules carry weight only when this particular team authors them, so the schedule pressure is a reason to build that shared footing early, not to skip it. (B) invests a short facilitated block up front and is the strongest. (A) is defensible — borrowing a baseline is faster — but it imports one firm's culture onto a multi-firm team and the "adjust later" rarely happens once work is underway, so ownership never forms. (C) is a real consultative method and quicker than a workshop, but the team is reacting to her draft rather than co-creating, which yields thinner commitment when no prior norms exist to anchor the comments. (D) suits a stable team that can let norms emerge, but a freshly assembled multi-firm team under time pressure is exactly where unmanaged early friction costs the most, so deferring the behavioural norms is the weakest fit.
