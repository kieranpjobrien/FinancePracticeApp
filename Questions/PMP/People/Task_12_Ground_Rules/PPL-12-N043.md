# Question

You lead a distributed product team split across three continents that has been working for a month without explicit working agreements, and friction is mounting. You have decided to run a ground-rules workshop. Because the time-zone spread means the whole team is rarely awake at once, you can get everyone live for only ninety minutes. How should you run the session to produce agreements the team will actually own?

## Options

- **A**: Before the live session, collect the team's pain points and draft proposals asynchronously, then use the ninety minutes to debate and converge on the contentious agreements together.
- **B**: Spend the full ninety minutes generating agreements from a blank page as a group, so the whole team witnesses every rule being created.
- **C**: Draft the complete set of agreements yourself from the complaints you have heard and use the live time to walk the team through them for sign-off.
- **D**: Skip the synchronous session and run the entire process asynchronously over a week so no region is disadvantaged by meeting time.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

With scarce synchronous time across three continents, the skill is to spend that time where it adds the most value — resolving disagreement — and push divergent idea-gathering to async, where everyone can contribute on their own schedule. (A) does exactly this and preserves team ownership because the proposals come from the team's own surfaced pain points. (B) burns the precious live window on blank-page generation that async handles well, leaving little time to actually resolve the hard trade-offs. (C) is efficient but converts a team agreement into a PM-authored document the team merely ratifies, which weakens ownership and adherence. (D) protects fairness but a fully async process on genuinely contentious norms tends to stall or paper over real disagreement that a short live debate would settle; some synchronous convergence is worth the cost.
