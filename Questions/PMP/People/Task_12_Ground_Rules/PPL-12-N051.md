# Question

On a predictive construction project, the project team has solid internal ground rules, but recurring steering-committee meetings are chaotic: senior stakeholders arrive unprepared, talk over one another, and reopen decisions that were already settled. The sponsor, who chairs the committee, asks you to bring order. What is the most effective first step?

## Options

- **A**: Introduce a structured agenda with timeboxes and a decision log that records each decision as closed, and run the next meeting to that structure.
- **B**: Propose a short set of meeting ground rules to the committee — covering pre-reads, one-speaker-at-a-time, and not reopening closed decisions without new information — and have the group agree them at the start of the next session.
- **C**: Brief the sponsor privately on facilitation techniques to use from the chair, since a committee follows the tone its chair sets.
- **D**: Circulate decision-quality materials and a clear pre-read pack ahead of each meeting so stakeholders arrive prepared and have less reason to relitigate.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Ground rules apply to stakeholder forums just as they do to the team, and the durable fix is a small set of norms the committee itself agrees to and then holds itself to. (B) does this and, because the group adopts the rules together, it gives the sponsor-chair something legitimate to enforce. (A) attacks the same symptoms with structure and a decision log, which genuinely helps, but structure imposed without the committee's shared agreement is easier for senior stakeholders to override — the norms underpinning it are what make it hold. (C) is worth doing since the chair sets the tone, yet coaching the sponsor alone leaves the committee with no shared expectations of its own and puts the whole burden on one person. (D) tackles the unpreparedness and some of the relitigating, but better pre-reads do not by themselves stop people talking over each other or reopening settled matters; it is one supporting measure rather than the foundational step.
