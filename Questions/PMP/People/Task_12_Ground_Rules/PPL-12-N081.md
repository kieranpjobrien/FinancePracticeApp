# Question

On a predictive water-utilities upgrade, the newly appointed project manager inherits a team charter written by her predecessor and the sponsor before the delivery team was assembled. In the first month the team repeatedly works around the charter's ground rules — joining meetings late, skipping the agreed decision log — and reacts with indifference when reminded. The project manager has a fortnight before the next stage gate and limited time to intervene. What should she do first?

## Options

- **A**: Reissue the charter with the sponsor's signature visible so the team understands the rules carry executive authority.
- **B**: Convene the team to review the existing ground rules and revise them together so the team genuinely owns the agreements.
- **C**: Add a compliance check to the status report so adherence to each rule is tracked and visible every week.
- **D**: Tighten the wording of the most-breached rules so there is no ambiguity that could excuse the lapses.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Ground rules hold only when the people bound by them have authored or at least consciously adopted them; here the rules were written for the team rather than by it, so there is no ownership and indifference is the predictable result. (B) restores that ownership by having the actual team review and revise the agreements, which is the root fix. (A) leans on imposed authority, which can secure grudging compliance but not the commitment that makes ground rules self-enforcing. (C) measures the symptom without addressing why the team feels no allegiance to rules it never made. (D) assumes the wording is at fault, but a team that ignores rules and shrugs off reminders is not confused about what they mean — sharper wording cannot manufacture the ownership the rules never had.
