# Question

An agile team in a pharmaceutical company has the working agreement "we disagree openly and respectfully." In practice one influential member dominates discussions and the others have largely stopped offering dissent; no one is ever disrespectful, so the rule is met on its face while its purpose is not. The team has not raised this in any retrospective, and the Scrum Master sees the quieter members defer almost reflexively. What is the best action?

## Options

- **A**: Coach the dominant member privately to draw others in and leave more space, addressing the strongest single driver of the silence.
- **B**: In a retrospective, describe the deferral pattern neutrally and invite the team to judge whether its agreement is doing what it was meant to.
- **C**: Bring in a structured method such as round-robin or silent writing so every member is given the floor regardless of who dominates.
- **D**: Reframe the agreement to set an explicit expectation that dissent is not just permitted but actively expected from everyone.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The norm is honoured in letter but defeated in spirit, and the underlying cause is a team dynamic the team has never named; the highest-leverage move is to make that visible and let the team examine and adjust its own agreement, which addresses the root while keeping ownership where it belongs. (B) does this. (A) targets a real driver and may genuinely help, but treating a team-level safety problem as one person's coaching need leaves the rest of the team unaware and unchanged. (C) is a strong technique and might well be what the team chooses, but imposing it before the team has recognised the problem fixes a symptom the team has not yet examined and can feel like a workaround. (D) sharpens the wording, yet mandating dissent by rule tends to produce hollow, compliant contributions and still skips the team's own reflection on why people went quiet in the first place.
