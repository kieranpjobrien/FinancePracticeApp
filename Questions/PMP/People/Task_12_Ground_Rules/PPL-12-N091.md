# Question

On a predictive hospital-construction project, the team agreed a ground rule that decision-making meetings begin promptly and latecomers accept whatever was decided in their absence. The project sponsor, who attends the fortnightly steering meeting, has now arrived late three times and each time reopened settled decisions, frustrating the team. What is the most appropriate response from the project manager?

## Options

- **A**: Enforce the team's ground rule on the sponsor in the next meeting by declining to revisit any decision made before their arrival.
- **B**: Accept that the rule cannot apply to the sponsor and instruct the team to re-present decisions whenever the sponsor arrives late.
- **C**: Speak with the sponsor privately, explain the impact of reopening decided items, and agree how the punctuality norm can work for the steering meeting.
- **D**: Remove the punctuality rule from the team charter, since it cannot be applied consistently to senior stakeholders.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The team's ground rule was authored by and for the team; a senior stakeholder who never agreed to it cannot simply be held to it, yet their behaviour is undermining the team's decisions, so the issue needs to be addressed directly and diplomatically. (C) opens a private, respectful conversation about impact and seeks a workable arrangement, which respects the sponsor's position while protecting the team's outcomes. (A) attempts to impose an internal team rule on someone outside its scope, likely damaging the relationship and overstepping. (B) capitulates entirely, rewarding the disruptive pattern and signalling that the team's decisions are provisional. (D) discards a rule that works well within the team because of one external party, throwing out a sound norm rather than managing the stakeholder.
