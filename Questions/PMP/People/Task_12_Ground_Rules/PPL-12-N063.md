# Question

At the kick-off of a new construction project, the project manager has set aside time to establish team ground rules covering communication, meeting conduct, and how disagreements are handled. Several members are working together for the first time, the kick-off agenda is tight, and a few participants have sat through ground-rules exercises before that produced a list no one ever used again. How should the project manager run this?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate the team to surface and agree the rules in the session, prompting with topics but letting the wording and choices come from the members.
- **B**: Bring a draft set of rules based on her experience, walk the team through them, and invite edits and additions before adopting.
- **C**: Share a ground-rules template from a recent successful project of similar size as a starting point and have the team amend it to fit.
- **D**: Keep the kick-off moving and instead ask members to propose rules asynchronously over the first week, then consolidate the agreed set.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Ground rules become self-policing when the people bound by them author the wording and the choices, which builds the ownership that the cynical participants have not seen before; doing this live, while the whole team is together, also captures the shared understanding that a first-time team most needs. (A) maximises authorship at the one moment everyone is present. (B) is genuinely collaborative but anchors the team to the project manager's framing — members react to her draft rather than forming their own, which dilutes ownership for a team that already doubts the exercise. (C) is faster but a template carries another team's context and values, so amendments tend to be cosmetic and adoption shallow. (D) preserves agenda time and can gather input, but splitting authorship across asynchronous proposals loses the joint sense-making and negotiation that make a first-time team's rules stick, and risks the consolidation becoming the project manager's list again. The discrimination is among four real co-creation methods, weighing depth of team authorship against speed and structure.
