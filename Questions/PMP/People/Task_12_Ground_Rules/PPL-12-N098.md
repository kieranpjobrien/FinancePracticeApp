# Question

During a retrospective, an agile gaming-studio team raises that a ground rule it set at kickoff — "no asynchronous messages outside core hours, to protect focus time" — is now hurting it, because two members recently relocated to distant time zones and can rarely reach the others within core hours. The rule was useful when everyone was co-located. What is the most appropriate response?

## Options

- **A**: Keep the rule unchanged to preserve the focus-time benefit, and ask the relocated members to shift their hours to overlap with core hours.
- **B**: Facilitate the team to revise the rule in the retrospective so it still protects focus time while accommodating the new time-zone spread.
- **C**: Drop the rule entirely, since it no longer fits the team's distribution and any restriction now causes more harm than good.
- **D**: Make an informal exception for the two relocated members while leaving the written rule as it stands for everyone else.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Ground rules are living agreements meant to be inspected and adapted as the team's context changes, and the retrospective is precisely where that adaptation belongs; the rule's intent (protecting focus) is still valid, only its current form no longer fits. (B) revises the rule collaboratively so it preserves the underlying benefit while working for a now-distributed team. (A) rigidly defends the old form and pushes the burden of accommodation onto two members by demanding they upend their working hours, which is neither fair nor sustainable. (C) discards a still-valuable intent rather than adjusting the rule, an overcorrection. (D) creates an unwritten exception that diverges from the documented agreement, breeding confusion and a two-tier set of norms.
