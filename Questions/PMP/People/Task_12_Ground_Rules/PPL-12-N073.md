# Question

A fully distributed agile team across four time zones is about to draft ground rules for how it communicates and collaborates. Because the team rarely meets live all together and almost everything moves through written channels, the project manager wants the agreement to address the distributed reality directly. Several sensible topics are proposed and all are reasonable, but the team wants to keep the agreement short. Which THREE topics will most directly keep work flowing for a team that is rarely online together? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Expected response times for asynchronous messages, distinguishing urgent from routine.
- **B**: Which decisions can be made asynchronously and how agreement is confirmed when not everyone is online.
- **C**: Core overlap hours when members commit to being available for synchronous collaboration.
- **D**: Which channel is used for which purpose — chat for quick coordination, the tracker for decisions, video for design discussions.
- **E**: A norm that meetings start and end on time and have a circulated agenda.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Every option here is a defensible ground rule, so the discrimination is about which three most directly address the binding constraint: people are rarely online together and depend on written channels. (A) stops asynchronous threads from stalling by setting response expectations; (B) makes decisions possible without everyone present, the single hardest thing for a time-zone-spread team; and (C) protects a guaranteed window for the synchronous work that genuinely needs it. (D) is a good practice and helpful on any team, but channel-routing manages where information goes rather than whether distributed work can progress when members are offline — it is secondary to having response norms, async decision rules, and overlap hours in place. (E) is sound meeting hygiene that applies to co-located teams equally; it does little to solve the distinctly distributed problem of coordinating across time zones. The question turns on prioritising the agreements that specifically unblock asynchronous, time-zone-spread collaboration over generally-good norms that are not particular to this team's context.
