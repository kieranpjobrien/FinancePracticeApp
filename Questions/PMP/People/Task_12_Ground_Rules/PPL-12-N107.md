# Question

You are facilitating a newly formed agile team at a telecommunications company as it drafts its first working agreement. Ideas come fast and the group is keen to capture everything. To produce an agreement the team will genuinely use and own, which TWO actions should you take during the session? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Press each proposed item until it names an observable behaviour the team can act on, rather than accepting a broad aspiration.
- **B**: Capture every proposal now to honour the team's energy, and let unused or vague items fall away naturally over the coming sprints.
- **C**: Before adopting each item, confirm that every member can live with it, rather than carrying it on a simple majority.
- **D**: Have the team nominate one member as keeper of the agreement to track adherence and flag lapses in retrospectives.
- **E**: Anchor the agreement to a respected reference team's published working agreement and adapt from there to save time.

## Answer

A, C

## Explanation

An agreement the team will use and own is built from concrete, observable behaviours (A) and is consented to by everyone rather than imposed by a bare majority (C); these two most directly determine whether it is actionable and genuinely owned. (B) is tempting because preserving momentum matters, but adopting vague or untested items wholesale produces a bloated list the team never internalises, and "they'll fall away naturally" rarely happens — curating during the session is the better discipline. (D) sounds like healthy accountability, yet a self-organising team owns and upholds its agreement collectively; appointing a single keeper drifts toward one person policing the rest. (E) can be a useful prompt, but starting from another team's text frames the agreement as adapted-from-elsewhere rather than authored here, which thins the ownership the session exists to build.
