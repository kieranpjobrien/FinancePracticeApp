# Question

During working-agreement creation, a brand-new agile team splits sharply over one proposed rule: half want a strict "no meetings after 3 p.m." norm to protect focus time, the other half argue this is unworkable given an offshore stakeholder who is only available later. The discussion is becoming heated and personal. As facilitator, what is the best way to move the team forward on this point?

## Options

- **A**: Park this specific rule, adopt the rest of the agreement now, and return to the contested item at the next session once tempers have cooled.
- **B**: Refocus the team on the shared interest each side is protecting — focus time and stakeholder access — and ask them to design a rule that serves both.
- **C**: Put the proposed rule to a majority vote so the team can settle it and move on.
- **D**: Adopt the rule on a two-week trial as written, then review whether the offshore access problem actually materialises.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The heat comes from two positions colliding, but each position defends a legitimate underlying interest; surfacing those interests lets the team craft an agreement that satisfies both (for example, protected focus mornings with a defined late slot reserved for offshore stakeholder calls). (B) is the collaborative, interest-based move that produces a durable rule and defuses the personal edge. (A) avoids the conflict rather than resolving it and risks the same clash recurring, while leaving a real scheduling constraint unaddressed. (C) lets a majority impose a rule on a sizable minority who have a genuine operational objection, undermining ownership and likely breeding non-compliance. (D) trials a rule that one half already knows is unworkable for a fixed external constraint, so it predictably fails and wastes two weeks rather than engaging the underlying needs.
