# Question

A fully remote agile team building a video-conferencing product is drafting ground rules aimed specifically at three failure modes of its distributed meetings: people talking over each other, quieter members being squeezed out, and decisions made in a call evaporating because no one captured them. The team also wants its meetings to run better generally, but for this exercise it is keeping only the rules that fix one of those three named problems. Which proposed ground rules should it keep? (Choose three.)

## Options

- **A**: "We use a visible hand-raise queue and the facilitator calls on people in order, so no one is talked over."
- **B**: "Each meeting has a named scribe who records decisions and actions in the shared notes before the call ends."
- **C**: "The facilitator explicitly invites input from anyone who has not spoken before a decision is finalised."
- **D**: "We circulate an agenda the day before so everyone arrives prepared and the meeting stays on time."
- **E**: "We keep cameras on so people can read each other's reactions during the discussion."

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

The team named three specific problems — interruptions, quieter members being crowded out, and decisions not being captured — so the rules to keep are those that each target one of them. (A) tackles people talking over each other with a turn-taking queue; (C) ensures quieter members are drawn in before decisions close; (B) prevents decisions from evaporating by assigning a scribe to record them. (D) and (E) are both genuine, sensible meeting practices — a shared agenda improves preparation and timekeeping, and cameras-on aids general engagement — but neither directly fixes interruptions, exclusion, or lost decisions, which is the test the team set. Cameras-on in particular does nothing to stop people talking over each other or to capture what was decided. Only A, B, and C map onto the three problems named.
