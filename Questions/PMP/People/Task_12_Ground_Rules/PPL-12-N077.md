# Question

A project manager is forming a new virtual team whose members have never worked together and sit in four different countries. The work starts in a few days. She knows remote teams suffer misunderstandings when shared expectations are missing, and she also knows the members have no rapport yet and bring different national working styles. In her first session with the team, what is the most effective way to spend the limited time on ground rules?

## Options

- **A**: Have members briefly share how they each prefer to communicate and work, then use that to agree the team's communication and meeting norms together in the same session.
- **B**: Bring a concise set of remote-working norms — channels, response times, meeting conduct — and have the team confirm or adjust them so an agreement is in place before work begins.
- **C**: Run the session as introductions and relationship-building only, and defer drafting any ground rules until the team has worked together for a sprint and knows what it actually needs.
- **D**: Send out a short proposed agreement in advance for written comment, then ratify it in the session so live time is spent on the work rather than on rule-drafting.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A brand-new, multicultural virtual team has two needs that reinforce each other: surfacing each member's working preferences and converting them into agreed norms. Doing both in the first session uses the members' own differences as the raw material for rules they own, which both pre-empts remote misunderstanding and starts building rapport. (A) integrates the two and produces a team-authored agreement. (B) gets an agreement in place quickly, but imposing the project manager's norms on people who have not yet voiced how they work skips the sense-making that makes norms fit a cross-cultural team and lowers ownership. (C) values relationship-building but defers the very expectations that prevent early remote friction; a team rarely online together cannot afford a sprint of avoidable misunderstanding before it agrees how to communicate. (D) is efficient and asynchronous input is fine, but ratifying a pre-written draft turns the team into editors of the project manager's agreement and forgoes the live negotiation where a new team aligns and connects. The discrimination is among four reasonable early approaches, weighing speed and structure against team authorship and rapport for a team with no shared history.
