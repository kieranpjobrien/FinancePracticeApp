# Question

While drafting the team charter for a predictive railway signalling project, the team brainstorms and lands on roughly a dozen behavioural rules spanning email response times, laptop use in meetings, and on-site dress code. Several were proposed to cover situations this team has not actually encountered. The list is adopted by a show of hands and the project manager is asked to publish it. Before doing so, how should the project manager proceed?

## Options

- **A**: Publish the full list as adopted; the team voted for it, and a fuller charter gives more situations explicit coverage on a safety-critical project.
- **B**: Take the list back to the team to pare it to the handful of agreements it most cares about and will actually use, then publish that.
- **C**: Publish all the rules but flag a subset as "core," so members know at a glance which ones the team treats as most important.
- **D**: Publish the rules that map to behaviours the team has actually exhibited, and drop the speculative ones without reconvening, to keep the list lean.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Behavioural ground rules change conduct only when they are few enough to be remembered and centred on what the team genuinely values; a dozen rules, several aimed at problems that have never arisen, will not be internalised, and the team — not a quick vote — owns whether each one earns its place. (B) returns the refinement to the team and is strongest. (A) treats the show of hands as settling quality, but it confuses behavioural norms with safety procedures and keeps speculative rules that dilute the ones that matter. (C) correctly senses that not all rules carry equal weight, yet it leaves the full load in place and only labels it, so the clutter and the cognitive cost remain. (D) reaches a sensible end state — a lean list — but the project manager edits shared agreements unilaterally, removing the team's ownership of its own charter, which is the very thing that makes ground rules stick.
