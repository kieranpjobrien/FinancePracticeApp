# Question

A team co-authored its ground rules a month ago, including one that meetings start on time. Lately two members have drifted into arriving five to ten minutes late, and the meetings now routinely start late while everyone waits. No one has said anything, and the team is otherwise collaborative and high-performing. The project manager wants the rule to hold without taking on the role of timekeeper herself. What is the best thing for her to do?

## Options

- **A**: Raise at the next retrospective that the start-on-time rule is slipping, and ask the team how it wants to hold itself to the agreements it made.
- **B**: Speak privately with the two members who have been arriving late, reminding them of the rule and asking them to be on time.
- **C**: Begin every meeting precisely at the scheduled time whether or not everyone is present, letting latecomers join an in-progress discussion.
- **D**: Add a light accountability mechanism the team agrees to, such as a rotating timekeeper who flags the start time each meeting.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Because the team authored the rule, the durable fix is to put the slippage back to the team and let it decide how to uphold its own agreement — this preserves the collective ownership that makes co-created rules self-policing and keeps the project manager as facilitator rather than enforcer. (A) does exactly that. (B) quietly handles the two individuals, which can resolve the immediate lateness but makes the project manager the one policing the rule and treats a team-agreement issue as a private performance matter, bypassing the team's own accountability. (C) enforces the norm through a consequence she imposes unilaterally; it may work but it is her mechanism, not the team's, and it can feel punitive to a team that has not agreed to it. (D) is a reasonable accountability device and may well be what the team chooses — but proposing the specific mechanism herself pre-empts the team's decision; the better move is to surface the issue first (A) and let a mechanism like this emerge from the team if it wants one. The discrimination is among genuinely viable accountability responses, turning on keeping ownership and the choice of remedy with the team.
