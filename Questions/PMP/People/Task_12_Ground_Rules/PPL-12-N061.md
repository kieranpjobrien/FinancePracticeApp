# Question

A newly formed agile team has spent its first two iterations without any explicit working agreement. Delivery has been adequate, and at each retrospective the team is upbeat and proposes no changes. The project manager, however, has watched the same patterns build across both iterations: people talk over each other in planning, two members have gone quiet, and decisions keep being reopened days after they seemed settled. None of it has surfaced in the team's own reflection. At the next retrospective, what should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Open the retrospective by describing the recurring patterns she has seen across both iterations and ask the team whether any are worth turning into working agreements.
- **B**: Ask the team open questions about how planning and decisions have felt lately, letting them name any friction before she adds what she has observed.
- **C**: Keep her observations back for now and let the team continue running its own retrospectives, raising the patterns only if delivery or morale starts to suffer.
- **D**: Speak with the two quiet members before the retrospective to understand their disengagement, then decide what to bring to the wider team.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The team's self-run reflection has twice failed to surface patterns that are already compounding, so a facilitator legitimately puts her own observations on the table — naming what she sees while still leaving the team to decide which patterns become agreements. (A) does this directly and turns the retrospective toward the real issues without dictating the remedy. (B) is a sound facilitation instinct, but the team has now shown twice that open prompting alone does not draw these patterns out; relying on it a third time risks the same silence and lets the friction keep building. (C) waits for a threshold the situation is already approaching — disengagement and reopened decisions are themselves the early signs — so it defers a problem that is visible now. (D) treats a systemic ways-of-working issue as if it were mainly about two individuals; a private pre-meeting can shade into a personal performance conversation and still leaves the team-level patterns (interrupting, reopening decisions) unaddressed. The choice turns on how directly to inject observed data into a team that is not surfacing it, not on whether the team should own its agreements.
