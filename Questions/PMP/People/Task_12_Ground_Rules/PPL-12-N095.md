# Question

A predictive bridge-refurbishment team's charter contains a clear ground rule that all site-inspection findings are logged in the shared register by end of day so that nothing is lost between shifts. Most of the team follows it, but one engineer has slipped twice, recording his findings only verbally to a colleague. The lapses have not yet caused a problem. What is the most appropriate action for the project manager?

## Options

- **A**: Wait until a lapse actually causes a problem before raising it, to avoid making an issue of something that has so far been harmless.
- **B**: Send the whole team a reminder of the logging rule without naming the engineer, to avoid singling anyone out.
- **C**: Have a brief, direct conversation with the engineer, reminding him of the agreed rule and why it matters before a lapse causes harm.
- **D**: Revise the rule to allow verbal handover as an acceptable alternative, since the engineer evidently finds logging burdensome.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

A ground rule is upheld by addressing lapses promptly and directly, before they become habits or cause harm; the issue is specific to one engineer, so a quiet, direct conversation is the proportionate response. (C) reinforces the agreement and the reason behind it while keeping the matter low-key. (A) waits for damage that the rule exists to prevent, which defeats its purpose. (B) broadcasts a general reminder to a team that is largely compliant, diluting the message and leaving the actual individual unaddressed. (D) weakens a sound rule to accommodate one person's reluctance, sacrificing the cross-shift continuity the rule was designed to protect.
