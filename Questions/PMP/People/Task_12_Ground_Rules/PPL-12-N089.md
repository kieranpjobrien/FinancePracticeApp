# Question

A predictive defence-electronics project pairs an onshore team that voices disagreement freely with an offshore team whose members regard openly contradicting a senior colleague in a group setting as disrespectful. In review meetings the offshore members stay silent, and avoidable design flaws surface only later. The project manager wants to add a ground rule that will get concerns about a design onto the table before it is signed off. Which ground rule should the project manager propose?

## Options

- **A**: "Concerns may be raised through any channel — written, one-to-one, or in the meeting — and a designated reviewer compiles them for the group, so dissent need not be voiced face-to-face."
- **B**: "Every member must verbally state at least one concern in each design review to ensure all viewpoints are heard."
- **C**: "Disagreements are to be raised only with the onshore lead, who will represent them on the offshore team's behalf."
- **D**: "Meeting minutes record who agreed and who objected to each decision, so silence is taken as agreement."

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The aim is to make dissent safe and routine while accommodating a culture in which public contradiction is uncomfortable; the rule must therefore open multiple low-exposure channels rather than dictate one behaviour. (A) does this by letting concerns flow through written or private routes and having a reviewer surface them collectively, capturing the input without demanding face-to-face confrontation. (B) compels public verbal dissent, which directly violates the cultural norm and will produce hollow, performative concerns. (C) funnels all disagreement through a single onshore gatekeeper, distorting and filtering the offshore voice rather than empowering it. (D) treats silence as consent — the precise failure mode causing flaws to be missed — and so entrenches the problem instead of solving it.
