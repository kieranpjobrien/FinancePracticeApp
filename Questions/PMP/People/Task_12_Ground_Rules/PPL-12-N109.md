# Question

An agile team at an electric-vehicle manufacturer is sorting several statements it scribbled down during a workshop, placing each into the artefact where it will be owned and enforced correctly. Each statement sounds like a "rule the team agreed," but each belongs primarily in a different artefact. Match each statement (left) to the artefact it most properly belongs in (right).

## Items

- **1**: "No story is shippable until it has passed peer code review and the new automated tests are green."
- **2**: "Cameras stay on during stand-up and planning, and we don't multitask in ceremonies."
- **3**: "We pull the next-highest item from the top of the backlog rather than cherry-picking work we prefer."

## Matches

- **A**: Team working agreement / ground rules
- **B**: Definition of Done
- **C**: Sprint backlog selection / pull policy (way of working for taking on work)

## Answer

1-B, 2-A, 3-C

## Explanation

Item 1 sets the completeness bar every increment must clear before it is releasable — a product quality criterion, which is the Definition of Done (B), despite reading like a general rule. Item 2 governs how members behave in meetings — a behavioural norm that lives in the working agreement (A). Item 3 looks behavioural but actually defines how the team selects and pulls work, a way-of-working policy about taking on items (C), not a quality bar or a conduct norm. The discrimination matters because the Definition of Done, behavioural agreements, and the team's work-selection policy are each reviewed and enforced through different mechanisms even though the team consents to all three; misfiling Item 1 as a ground rule, for instance, would wrongly remove it from the gate that controls what counts as "done."
