# Question

At the kickoff of a six-month predictive project, the team is drafting the ground-rules section of its charter. One member proposes adding "the go-live date is 30 November and the approved budget is 1.2 million" so the constraints are written down where the team will see them daily. Others nod, but you sense the section is starting to fill with items that are not really behavioural norms. As facilitator, how should you respond?

## Options

- **A**: Note that the date and budget are baselines tracked elsewhere, and steer the section back to how the team will work together — for example, how it will surface disagreements and make decisions.
- **B**: Add the date and budget to the ground rules as requested, since keeping the team's key constraints constantly visible supports accountability to them.
- **C**: Record the date and budget in the charter's project-description section and leave the ground-rules section for behavioural norms, explaining the distinction to the team.
- **D**: Capture the date and budget on a separate information radiator the team sees daily, so visibility is met without crowding the ground rules.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Ground rules govern how members behave toward one another — communication, decision-making, conflict handling, accountability between people — so the facilitator's job is to keep that section focused on norms and redirect items that belong elsewhere, while keeping the team engaged in defining its own behaviours. (A) does both: it corrects the misplacement and actively re-engages the team on the norms the section is for, which is the most useful facilitation outcome. (C) is technically correct about where the baseline belongs and is a close second, but it stops at filing the item in the right place and misses the chance to turn the moment back into productive norm-setting, so it is less effective than (A). (D) sensibly satisfies the member's underlying wish for visibility, yet on its own it solves the radiator question without refocusing the ground-rules work, leaving the core task half-addressed. (B) keeps the constraints visible but loads the ground-rules section with a schedule-and-cost baseline that is not a behavioural norm and is owned and maintained as part of the project plan, diluting the very section the team is there to build.
