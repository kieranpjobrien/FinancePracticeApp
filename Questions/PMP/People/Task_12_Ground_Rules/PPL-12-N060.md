# Question

Two previously separate Scrum teams are merging into one larger team to deliver a unified platform. Each arrives with its own well-established but conflicting working agreements — different stand-up times, different code-review thresholds, different definitions of "blocked." The merge is effective next sprint. As the project manager, what is the best way to establish ground rules for the merged team?

## Options

- **A**: Have each former team list its agreements, then facilitate the merged team to keep the items both already shared and decide the genuinely conflicting ones together.
- **B**: Run the first sprint with a minimal interim agreement on only the few rules that must align immediately, then co-create the full set at the first retrospective once the team has worked together.
- **C**: Have the two former Scrum Masters reconcile the conflicting agreements and bring a merged proposal for the team to review and adjust.
- **D**: Facilitate the merged team to build one new set of agreements together, deliberately drawing on what worked well in each former team.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

A merged team is effectively a new team and its agreements stick only when the whole group jointly owns them; harvesting the best of both former sets resolves the conflicts while honouring each team's experience. (D) does this most completely and builds buy-in across the combined team. (A) is a sound, efficient variant — banking the shared items and focusing effort on the genuine conflicts — but by treating the existing agreements as the starting frame it can anchor the team to old norms and quietly skip rethinking practices that neither team articulated; it is close to (D) but slightly less likely to produce true joint ownership of the whole. (B) sensibly avoids over-planning before the team has worked together, yet deferring most agreements means the first sprint runs with known conflicts (e.g. clashing "blocked" definitions) unresolved, risking exactly the friction the merge should prevent. (C) is faster and uses the Scrum Masters' knowledge, but having two leaders pre-reconcile the norms makes the team a reviewer of someone else's compromise rather than an author of its own, weakening ownership.
