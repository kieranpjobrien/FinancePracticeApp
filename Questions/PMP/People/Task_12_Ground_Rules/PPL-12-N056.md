# Question

A hybrid team — some co-located, some remote — finds that in-office members make ad hoc decisions in hallway conversations that remote members only learn about later, breeding resentment. You are running a session to update the team's ground rules. Every option below is a plausible agreement, but three most directly close the gap between where decisions happen and who gets a say. Which THREE? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Decisions that affect the wider team are logged in the shared decision record regardless of where they originated.
- **B**: A "no significant decision in side conversations" norm: such topics are taken to a forum all members can join.
- **C**: A lightweight async review window before a near-final decision is locked, so remote members can weigh in.
- **D**: A norm that any in-office discussion that turns into a decision is paused and reconvened with remote members included.
- **E**: A rule that the team holds a daily synchronous decision review so nothing is finalised without everyone present.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

(A), (B) and (D) attack the problem at its source — the moment a hallway chat becomes a decision: logging team-affecting decisions makes them visible to all, the no-side-conversation norm routes such topics to inclusive forums, and pausing-and-reconvening handles the realistic case where an informal chat unexpectedly turns consequential. (C) is genuinely helpful and gives remote members a real say, but an async review window operates after a near-final decision exists; it mitigates exclusion rather than preventing the out-of-band decision from forming, so it is slightly less direct than the three that act at the point of decision. (E) a daily synchronous decision review could surface decisions, but forcing every decision to wait for a daily ceremony with everyone present is heavy, slows the team, and is impractical across differing schedules — a worse fit than the lighter structural norms. The task is to pick the three most directly on-target.
