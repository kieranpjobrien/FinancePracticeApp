# Question

On a predictive hospital fit-out project, an integration vendor has begun quietly buffering its own milestone dates because it does not believe the PM's published schedule is realistic, having been burned on a previous engagement. The PM only learns of this when a junior vendor engineer mentions it offhand. The relationship is otherwise cooperative and delivery is currently on track. What is the PM's best response?

## Options

- **A**: Re-baseline the integrated schedule with larger explicit buffers so the published dates align with what the vendor privately believes is achievable, removing the need for hidden ones.
- **B**: Ask the vendor's lead, in the next regular check-in, whether they have any concerns about the current milestone dates, and let them raise the buffering in their own time.
- **C**: Invite the vendor's lead to jointly walk through the integrated schedule assumptions, openly inviting challenge to the dates and agreeing a shared view of where the real risk sits.
- **D**: Note the buffering quietly and increase the PM's own monitoring of the vendor's milestones, intervening only if a date actually starts to slip.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The root issue is a trust gap from prior experience; collaboration is rebuilt by jointly examining assumptions and inviting the vendor to challenge them openly, converting hidden buffers into a shared, transparent risk view. (C) addresses the relationship and the schedule realism together. (A) pads the dates to match the vendor's belief but never surfaces the disbelief itself, so the mistrust persists and may reappear elsewhere. (B) creates a soft opening but, given the vendor is already concealing the buffers, an open invitation is likely to draw a reassuring non-answer rather than the real conversation. (D) leaves the trust gap entirely unaddressed and waits for a problem to materialise, which is reactive and forfeits the chance to rebuild the relationship while delivery is healthy.
