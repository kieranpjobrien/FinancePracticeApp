# Question

On a hybrid logistics-optimisation project, the business analysts write user stories that the development team repeatedly says are "not buildable as written", while the developers produce technical solutions the business says "don't match what we asked for". Each side blames the other and refinement sessions have become tense. As the agile project lead, what is the most effective intervention?

## Options

- **A**: Run joint story-refinement where business and developers shape each story together and attach concrete acceptance criteria and worked examples both sides confirm before it is ready.
- **B**: Introduce a definition of ready that every story must meet — clear outcome, acceptance criteria and dependencies — and hold stories out of the sprint until they satisfy it.
- **C**: Pair a business analyst and a developer to co-author each story before refinement, so the room reviews a draft both disciplines have already shaped.
- **D**: Have the team build a thin throwaway prototype for contested stories so the business reacts to something concrete and the developers learn the real intent.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The breakdown is a missing shared understanding caused by business and developers working on stories separately, so the most effective fix is to bring both sides together to co-create stories with agreed acceptance criteria and examples — that joint act is what builds the common definition both currently lack, and it scales to the whole backlog (A). (B) a definition of ready is a useful standard, but it sets a bar a story must clear without itself creating the conversation that gets it there; stories can fail the gate repeatedly while the underlying disconnect persists. (C) analyst-developer pairing genuinely helps and is close to right, but a single pair co-authoring narrows the shared understanding to two people, leaving the wider business and team still to reconcile their views in the room. (D) prototyping is powerful for resolving specific contested items, but spinning up throwaway builds for disputes is heavier and slower than refining stories together and does not, by itself, change how the two groups collaborate on the rest of the backlog.
