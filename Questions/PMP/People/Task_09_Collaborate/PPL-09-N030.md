# Question

Two sprints from a contracted release, a key customer representative emails a developer directly asking for a "small" reporting tweak. The developer, wanting to be helpful, has already built and merged it before mentioning it to the project manager; it works and the customer is delighted. This is the third time this representative has gone straight to a developer. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Keep the delivered tweak, brief the product owner so it is reflected in the backlog and release scope, and agree with the customer and team a single intake channel for future requests.
- **B**: Have the product owner assess the merged tweak now and back it out if it does not fit the release scope, to protect the integrity of what was committed.
- **C**: Raise the recurring direct-to-developer pattern with the customer's sponsor so the behaviour is addressed at the relationship level before the release.
- **D**: Let this tweak stand given the customer is pleased, and ask the team to flag any future direct requests to the product owner before acting on them.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The work is already merged, so the priority is to restore proper prioritisation visibility and prevent recurrence without destroying customer goodwill. (A) brings the change under the product owner's backlog control retrospectively and fixes the channel for next time, addressing both the artefact and the pattern. (B) may be warranted if the tweak truly breaks scope, but reflexively backing out delivered, working, customer-valued functionality is a poor opening move before the product owner has even assessed value. (C) jumps to the customer's sponsor over a pattern the PM has not yet tried to resolve directly with the representative and team, which is premature and risks the relationship. (D) addresses future requests but leaves this change invisible to prioritisation and the product owner unaware of what is now in the release.
