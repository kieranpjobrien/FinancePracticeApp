# Question

Midway through a hybrid project upgrading a city's bus ticketing system, a newly appointed transport director takes over as the most senior client-side stakeholder. They have inherited the project and know little about its history, commitments, or current state. The project manager wants to build a productive working relationship from the outset. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Send the new director the full set of existing status reports and governance papers so they can get up to speed in their own time.
- **B**: Meet the new director to understand their priorities and concerns and to walk them through where the project stands and what has been committed.
- **C**: Carry on with the existing engagement rhythm unchanged so the new director sees a stable, well-run project rather than disruption.
- **D**: Ask the outgoing director to brief their successor on the project before the project manager makes any direct approach.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A new senior stakeholder is best engaged through a direct, two-way conversation that surfaces their priorities and orients them to the project. (B) builds the relationship and shares context in both directions, which is the foundation for collaboration going forward. (A) hands over a document pile with no relationship and risks the director forming views from reports alone. (C) keeps a rhythm that was set for the predecessor and never checks whether it fits the new stakeholder. (D) outsources the first contact to someone leaving and delays the PM building their own relationship with the person who now holds the authority.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- The easy traps substitute documents, inertia, or a third party for the PM's own direct engagement with the new stakeholder.
