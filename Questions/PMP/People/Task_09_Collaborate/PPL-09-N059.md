# Question

On an agile telematics product, a key business stakeholder demands exhaustive written assurances before each release and openly second-guesses the team's status reports, even though the current team has been straightforward with them throughout. In a one-to-one they mention that on a previous project, serious problems were kept from them until it was too late to act, and they are determined not to be caught that way again. How should the project manager best rebuild this collaboration?

## Options

- **A**: Give the stakeholder open, real-time visibility into the team's actual working state — board, metrics and issues as they emerge — and invite them to watch problems being worked, not just outcomes.
- **B**: Provide the detailed pre-release assurances they are asking for, complete and on time, to meet them where they are and show the team has nothing to hide.
- **C**: Invite the stakeholder to the team's daily stand-up and a standing weekly risk-and-issue review, so they hear the team discussing problems openly as they surface.
- **D**: Establish a regular one-to-one where the project manager walks the stakeholder through current risks, issues and bad news candidly before each release.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The distrust is rooted in problems once being concealed, so confidence returns through sustained, unmediated openness about the real state of the work. (A) gives the stakeholder direct visibility of the team's genuine working state, including problems as they arise — the most credible answer to a fear of concealment, because it removes any filter between them and the truth. (B) delivering thorough assurances is reasonable and shows good faith, but it feeds the document-driven, second-guessing cycle and keeps the relationship at arm's length. (C) inviting them into stand-ups and a risk review is strong and genuinely transparent — close to the right idea — but it is periodic and team-meeting-bound, where (A) gives continuous, self-serve line of sight to the live state. (D) a candid PM-led one-to-one is honest, yet it routes the truth through the PM as narrator, exactly the mediation a concealment-wary stakeholder distrusts. The margin is direct, continuous visibility of real work versus thorough documents, scheduled openness, or PM-narrated candour.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- No strawman: complete pre-release assurances, joining stand-ups and risk reviews, and a candid PM one-to-one are all real trust-building moves. Each is honest but mediated, periodic, or document-bound; the discrimination is unmediated, continuous transparency.
