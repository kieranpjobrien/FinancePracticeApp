# Question

In a scaled agile games-platform programme, three feature teams each have their own product owner, and each product owner sequences their own backlog independently. As a result, cross-team dependencies are mis-sequenced — Team A builds in sprint 4 what Team B does not consume until sprint 9, and the reverse also happens. The product owners cooperate willingly when asked but rarely coordinate on their own, and the release-train engineer role that would normally orchestrate this is vacant. What is the best way to improve collaboration between the three product owners?

## Options

- **A**: Establish a regular cross-team backlog-alignment cadence where the three product owners jointly sequence shared dependencies and agree the order across their backlogs.
- **B**: Stand up and maintain a programme-level dependency board yourself, reviewing the three backlogs each sprint and convening the product owners only when a conflict needs resolving.
- **C**: Ask the three product owners to route any backlog change that touches a shared dependency through you for a consistency check before they commit it.
- **D**: Propose that one of the three product owners take an acting cross-team coordinator role alongside their own, sequencing shared dependencies on the others' behalf until the release-train engineer is hired.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The teams have genuine interdependencies but no shared forum to sequence them, so the fix is a joint cadence the product owners themselves own and sustain. (A) builds that coordination habit directly between them. (B) a PM-run dependency board with conflict-triggered meetings would catch many issues and is a reasonable stopgap, but it centres coordination on the PM and convenes the product owners only reactively, so they never build the habit. (C) a PM consistency-check on shared-dependency changes is defensible governance, yet it inserts the PM as a gate and still leaves the product owners sequencing in isolation between checks. (D) deputising one product owner as acting coordinator fills the vacant role but concentrates cross-team sequencing in one person rather than building collaboration among the three, and can strain peer relationships. The margin is the product owners coordinating directly and routinely versus coordination routed through the PM or delegated to one of them.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- No strawman: a PM-run dependency board, a consistency-check gate, and an acting coordinator are all real ways to manage cross-team dependencies. The discrimination is building direct, routine collaboration among the product owners versus centralising or delegating it.
