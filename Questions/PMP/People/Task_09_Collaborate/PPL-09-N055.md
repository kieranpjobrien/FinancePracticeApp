# Question

On a hybrid airport-baggage-system project, the IT director and the airport operations director disagree over cut-over timing and have stopped attending the same meetings, each sending deputies and contacting you separately. The IT director cites system-stability risks; the operations director cites operational-readiness risks. The cut-over date needs both of them to agree, and the slot under discussion is six weeks away. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Meet each director separately to understand the concerns behind their positions, then bring them together in a facilitated conversation centred on the shared goal of a safe cut-over.
- **B**: Develop a recommended cut-over date that weighs both directors' stated risks, and take it to them jointly as a concrete proposal to react to and agree.
- **C**: Work through the two deputies to settle the technical and operational detail, then ask the directors to ratify the position their deputies converge on.
- **D**: Ask the airport CEO to bring the two directors together and have them resolve the cut-over timing before the slot is lost.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The decision needs both directors, but they have disengaged from each other, so the PM must first rebuild a working channel between them grounded in their real concerns. (A) surfaces each director's underlying drivers privately, then convenes them on common ground — the route most likely to restore a decision-making relationship they will need again. (B) a PM-authored recommendation is a legitimate way to force movement and may well be the next step, but presenting a solution before the two have re-engaged invites both to reject it and skips the relationship repair the situation needs. (C) settling via deputies can resolve the detail, yet the accountable directors stay disengaged from a decision only they can own, and deputies cannot commit them. (D) a CEO intervention may produce a date but deepens the rift and signals the PM could not facilitate it. The margin is the PM reconnecting the two decision-makers around their concerns first versus proposing a solution, working through deputies, or escalating.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- No strawman: a PM-authored compromise, a deputy-led settlement, and a CEO escalation are all defensible. The discrimination is rebuilding the directors' direct engagement first versus substituting a proposal, a proxy, or an escalation for it.
