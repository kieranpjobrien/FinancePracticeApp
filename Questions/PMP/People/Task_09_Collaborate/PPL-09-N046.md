# Question

An agile team is building a logistics tracking portal for an external client. The client's operational users feed back enthusiastically each sprint and the portal has evolved well beyond the original fixed feature list in the statement of work in response to their input. The client's procurement sponsor — who controls the contract, holds the budget, and makes the next funding decision alone — has not attended since kick-off and tracks progress only against that original feature list. The funding decision is six weeks out. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Arrange a walkthrough with the procurement sponsor that maps the evolved portal back to the business outcomes in the statement of work, showing how the changes serve the original intent.
- **B**: Prepare a written progress report for the procurement sponsor that reconciles every delivered change against the SOW feature list and the value it adds, ahead of the funding decision.
- **C**: Have the operational users' senior manager brief the procurement sponsor on the portal's value, since the message lands harder coming from inside the client.
- **D**: Capture the evolved scope as formal change requests against the SOW so the sponsor sees the deviations through the contract mechanism they already use.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The exposure is a budget-holder deciding against an outdated baseline while the real value sits in changes they have never seen; closing that gap is the PM's responsibility and is best done by direct engagement on terms the sponsor recognises. (A) re-establishes the relationship and ties the evolved product to the outcomes behind the original scope, in a two-way format where the sponsor can question and absorb it. (B) is the strongest distractor — a rigorous written reconciliation is professional and gives the sponsor exactly the comparison they use — but a document sent to a disengaged stakeholder is easily skimmed or ignored and does nothing to rebuild the relationship before a sole-decision-maker funding call. (C) routing the message through the client's own manager may add weight but hands a funding-critical, PM-owned conversation to someone who may frame it inconsistently. (D) change requests are the right hygiene but, used as the engagement vehicle, foreground deviation and contract variance rather than value and intent. The margin between A and B is direct two-way engagement versus a one-way document.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- No strawman: a written value reconciliation, an internal-champion briefing, and formal change requests are all defensible. The discrimination is the PM directly engaging the decision-maker versus a document, a proxy, or a contract mechanism.
