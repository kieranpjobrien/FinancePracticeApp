# Question

A predictive aerospace-components project depends on three internal departments — manufacturing, quality assurance, and supply chain — each with its own manager, priorities, and reporting line. Hand-offs keep failing because each department optimises its own schedule without visibility of the others, and the managers have started trading blame in writing after each missed hand-off. All three have a genuine workload case for their current sequencing. As project manager, which actions would most directly build collaboration *between the three departments*? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate a joint planning session where the three managers map their interdependencies together and agree shared hand-off milestones and the acceptance criteria each will hold the next to.
- **B**: Establish a standing cross-department forum the managers themselves run on rotation, where they review upcoming dependencies and resolve sequencing conflicts before they bite.
- **C**: Build an integrated master schedule yourself from each department's plan and publish it, so all three can see how their work connects without having to coordinate directly.
- **D**: Set up a shared dependency-and-hand-off board the three departments jointly own and keep current, so status is visible to all without passing through you.
- **E**: Agree escalation thresholds with the three managers so that any hand-off slipping beyond an agreed tolerance is brought to the sponsor for a ruling.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

Collaboration *between* the departments grows when they plan, coordinate and maintain visibility together: mapping interdependencies jointly and agreeing mutual acceptance criteria (A), a forum the managers themselves run to resolve conflicts directly (B), and a shared board they co-own (D) all create direct working relationships and joint ownership among the three parties. (C) is a reasonable and common PM action, but the PM integrating the schedule and publishing it keeps coordination flowing through the PM rather than building it between the departments — useful, yet not collaboration among them. (E) agreeing escalation thresholds is sound governance, but it routes unresolved conflict upward to the sponsor for a ruling, which is the opposite of the departments collaborating to resolve it themselves; it is a backstop, not a collaboration mechanism.
