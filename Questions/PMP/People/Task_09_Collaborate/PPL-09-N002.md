# Question

On a predictive rail-signalling upgrade, an integration vendor has quietly fallen four weeks behind but is still reporting green status. Your own test team has spotted the slippage through the shared build pipeline. The vendor's account manager is a long-standing contact who has asked you, off the record, not to raise it formally yet so they can "recover quietly" before the next governance review. What is the best course of action?

## Options

- **A**: Agree with the account manager to record the work as amber rather than red for this one cycle, noting a recovery plan is in progress, so the relationship is preserved while the true position still appears in the report.
- **B**: Report the slippage to your sponsor immediately and recommend issuing a formal performance notice to the vendor under the contract terms.
- **C**: Tell the account manager you value the relationship but will reflect the true status in the next report, and offer to present a joint recovery plan alongside the variance.
- **D**: Ask your test team to re-verify the four-week figure against the vendor's own data first, so the status you report is jointly agreed rather than disputed on the day.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Honest, transparent reporting is non-negotiable, but collaboration means giving the vendor a fair route to participate in the response — so stating plainly that the variance will be reported while offering to present a joint recovery plan alongside it (C) upholds integrity without needlessly turning a partner into an adversary. (A) softening red to amber to protect the relationship dresses up concealment as nuance — a four-week slip reported as "amber, recovering" still misleads governance about the real status and is an ethics failure however it is framed. (B) jumping straight to a formal performance notice is disproportionate before any recovery conversation and treats a partner as an adversary prematurely. (D) reconciling the figure against the vendor's data sounds collaborative, but the slippage is already visible in the shared pipeline and is not genuinely in doubt; making "jointly agreed numbers" a precondition for honest reporting is a delay tactic that risks the information surfacing through others first.
