# Question

A key customer representative on an agile insurance-claims platform keeps approaching individual developers directly during the sprint, asking them to "just slot in" small extra tweaks. The developers, wanting to keep the customer happy, are quietly accommodating these, and the sprint goal is starting to drift. The representative is a genuinely valuable source of domain detail and the product owner is unaware any of this is happening. What is the best action for the project manager?

## Options

- **A**: Speak with the customer representative and the product owner together to reaffirm that scope and ordering flow through the product owner, and agree a fast triage route so the representative's genuine quick needs still get handled responsively.
- **B**: Brief the product owner on what has been happening and ask her to set the boundary with the customer representative directly, since protecting the backlog from unmanaged change is her accountability.
- **C**: Coach the development team to redirect any in-sprint request from the representative to the product owner, so the channel is corrected at the point the requests actually land.
- **D**: Raise the pattern at the next sprint retrospective so the whole team, the product owner and the representative agree together how mid-sprint requests should be handled going forward.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The problem has two parts — a broken channel and a valuable stakeholder who needs a legitimate outlet — and it is live now, drifting the current sprint, so the best move brings the representative and the product owner together at once to restore the proper channel while giving the representative a responsive triage route, fixing cause and relationship together (A). (B) correctly identifies the product owner's accountability, but having her set the boundary alone, without the PM convening the conversation, risks the representative feeling shut down and does nothing to give him the responsive route that stops the side-channel recurring. (C) redirecting at the developer end treats the symptom where it lands but leaves the representative with no sanctioned way to raise real quick needs, so the pressure simply finds another gap. (D) the retrospective is the right place to harden the agreement, but deferring to it leaves the current sprint drifting and is a slow response to a problem already in motion.
