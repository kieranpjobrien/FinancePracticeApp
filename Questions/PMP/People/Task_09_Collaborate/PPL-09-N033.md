# Question

On a predictive ERP implementation, the PM has just confirmed that a critical integration will miss the agreed phase gate by three weeks. An influential business unit VP, who championed the project to the board, has told the PM informally that "there had better not be any surprises before the board meeting." The phase-gate review is two days before that board meeting. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Present the slippage at the phase-gate review as scheduled, letting the formal governance process surface it to all stakeholders together and on the record.
- **B**: Report the integration as "amber with a manageable variance" until a firm recovery plan is confirmed, then give the VP the full picture once there is a solution to accompany the problem.
- **C**: Brief the VP promptly and directly on the slippage, its cause, the recovery options, and the impact, so the VP can decide how to position it with the board.
- **D**: Tell the project sponsor first and ask them to decide whether and when the VP should be informed before the board meeting.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Collaboration and integrity require giving the accountable stakeholder timely, honest information so they can act, even when they have signalled they would rather not hear it. (C) respects the VP by letting them manage the board narrative with full facts while there is still time. (A) lets a key ally learn critical news in a public forum alongside everyone else, effectively ambushing them. (B) understates a confirmed problem and delays the truth, which is misleading and erodes trust once the real position emerges. (D) defers a disclosure the PM is responsible for making and routes it through the sponsor, slowing honest communication to the very stakeholder who most needs it and who has a direct relationship with the PM.
