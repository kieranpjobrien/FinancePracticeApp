# Question

On an agile project delivering a grid-monitoring platform for a utility, a senior operations stakeholder whose input the team needs says they "don't do meetings" and will only read a short written status once a week. The team values this stakeholder's operational knowledge and has open events they are welcome to join. The project manager wants their genuine involvement, not just acknowledgement. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Accept the weekly written status as the channel and make it as clear and complete as possible so the stakeholder stays well informed.
- **B**: Talk to the stakeholder to understand what makes meetings unworkable for them and find a lightweight way they can give input that fits how they work.
- **C**: Add the stakeholder to the invitee list for sprint reviews and demos, trusting that seeing the product will eventually draw them in.
- **D**: Ask the stakeholder's manager to require their attendance at key events given how important their operational knowledge is to the team.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Collaboration starts with understanding the stakeholder's constraints and tailoring engagement to them rather than to a default format. (B) seeks to understand why meetings fail for them and then meets them where they are, which is the route to genuine input. (A) settles for one-way reporting and abandons any attempt to draw out their knowledge. (C) invites them to events they have already declined and hopes for the best, ignoring what they told the PM. (D) compels attendance, which produces presence without real collaboration and can damage the relationship the team depends on.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- The stated preference is a constraint to work with, not a reason to give up on input or to force compliance.
