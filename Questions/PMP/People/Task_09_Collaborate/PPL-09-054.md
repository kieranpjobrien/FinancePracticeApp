# Question

The PM realises that two influential stakeholders are quietly opposing the project for internal political reasons unrelated to the project's merits. Neither has raised a formal objection but both are slowing decisions in their area. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Map the political interests, engage each stakeholder one-to-one to understand what would make the project net-positive for them, and route legitimate asks through the sponsor.
- **B**: Raise the political obstruction with the steering committee and ask the committee to formally direct both stakeholders to support the project decisions going forward.
- **C**: Document the slow decisions in the issues log and let the schedule impact naturally escalate the situation to the sponsor without any direct intervention by the PM.
- **D**: Work around both stakeholders by routing decisions to their deputies and escalating only when the deputies themselves lack sufficient authority to make the calls.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

PMI recognises political dynamics as legitimate stakeholder engagement territory, not obstacles to pretend away; the PM's move is to understand interests, find aligned asks, and route through proper governance. (B) skips the conversation and invites retaliation from stakeholders who will outlast the project. (C) uses schedule pain as a communication tool, which damages trust and delivery. (D) bypasses stakeholders the project will still need and stores up a political problem for later.
