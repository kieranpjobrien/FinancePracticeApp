# Question

A hybrid rail-signalling upgrade is delivered jointly with an external systems integrator. Comparing notes informally, your engineers find the integrator's lead has been reporting testing as "on track" in joint reports to your steering group while several test cases are failing. Asked about it, the lead says they expected to close the issues out quickly and did not want to alarm the steering group prematurely. Your steering group reads these joint reports as assured status and the next gate decision relies on them. How should the project manager handle the collaboration going forward?

## Options

- **A**: Agree with the integrator's lead a shared, evidence-based status definition and a joint dashboard fed directly from the test-management tool, so reported status traces to data both sides can see.
- **B**: Keep joint authorship but add a rule that any "on track" rating must cite the open-defect count and severity, and that the PM signs off the joint report before it reaches the steering group.
- **C**: Report the discrepancy to the integrator's account director and ask them to ensure their lead reports failing tests accurately in future.
- **D**: Tell the steering group that reported status has been optimistic, and ask them to treat the integrator's ratings cautiously until confidence is re-established.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The root problem is that "status" means different things to the two partners and the steering group cannot see the evidence behind it; the durable fix builds shared, traceable transparency rather than adding a checkpoint or assigning blame. (A) ties reported status directly to the test tool so neither side can drift from the data, and strengthens the relationship through visibility. (B) is the strongest distractor — citing defect data and adding PM sign-off improves the report and is defensible — but it inserts the PM as a gatekeeper over the integrator's reporting, signalling distrust and creating a bottleneck, without giving the steering group direct line of sight to the underlying data. (C) escalating to the account director treats a fixable practice as a discipline matter and damages a working direct relationship. (D) warning the steering group protects them short-term but undercuts the integrator publicly and still leaves status unverifiable. The margin between A and B is shared visibility of the source data versus a verification step layered on top of opaque reporting.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- No strawman: a data-citing report with PM sign-off, escalation, and informing the steering group are all real options. The discrimination is building shared traceable transparency versus inserting control, escalation, or a caution.
