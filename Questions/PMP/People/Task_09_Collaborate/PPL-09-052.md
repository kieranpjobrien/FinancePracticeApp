# Question

A senior operations manager has made clear, in one-to-ones, that she does not support the new system and will "comply but not advocate." Her quiet resistance is slowing adoption in her department compared to peers. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Brief the sponsor and recommend that the operations manager's objections be overridden from above so that adoption in her department can be visibly accelerated now.
- **B**: Work around the manager by engaging her direct reports through super-user channels and training sessions, building bottom-up support in her area deliberately over time.
- **C**: Meet the operations manager to surface the specific concerns behind her stance, co-design adjustments where possible, and rebuild engagement from awareness to ownership.
- **D**: Accept her position as a reasonable one, adjust the project's adoption expectations for her department downward, and report the divergence in the status updates.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

PMI's change-adoption model (consistent with ADKAR) treats resistance as information; surfacing the "why" is the only path from compliance to advocacy. (A) uses authority to paper over adoption and damages the longer-term relationship. (B) undermines a peer stakeholder, which is a trust violation she will eventually detect. (D) is learned helplessness — lowering expectations rather than engaging the root cause.
