# Question

A predictive submarine-cable project depends on a marine-survey subcontractor whose survey findings are essential inputs to your engineering team's design. The subcontractor delivers polished final reports on schedule but declines to share interim findings or its working assumptions, citing commercial confidentiality. Your engineers say earlier visibility of the assumptions would let them de-risk the design weeks sooner. The contract requires only the final reports. What is the best action for the project manager?

## Options

- **A**: Meet the subcontractor to agree a staged information-sharing arrangement — interim findings and key assumptions released under suitable confidentiality terms — that gives the engineers the early visibility they need.
- **B**: Ask the subcontractor to share just the working assumptions underpinning each survey, leaving its raw interim data confidential, since the assumptions are what the engineers actually need to de-risk.
- **C**: Propose a joint technical review partway through each survey where the subcontractor's specialists and your engineers discuss emerging findings verbally, without interim documents changing hands.
- **D**: Offer to extend the contract to add interim deliverables as a priced milestone, so the subcontractor is paid for the additional sharing and the obligation is formalised.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Confidentiality concerns are usually negotiable, and the engineers' need is for early visibility generally — not one specific artefact — so the strongest move is to sit down with the subcontractor and agree a staged sharing arrangement under appropriate terms, which both solves the de-risking problem and lets the subcontractor shape how its commercial interests are protected (A). (B) requesting only the assumptions is a sensible *opening position to bring into* that conversation, but committing to it pre-judges that assumptions alone will suffice and forecloses the broader arrangement the engineers may actually need. (C) verbal-only reviews are a reasonable confidentiality-preserving option, yet fixing on "no documents change hands" up front concedes ground before negotiating and may leave the engineers without the written detail they can design against. (D) pricing interim deliverables as a contract change is a heavier, slower route that treats a relationship problem as a procurement one; it may be the fallback if collaboration fails, but reaching for the contract first signals distrust on a partner you depend on.
