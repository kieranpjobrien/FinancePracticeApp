# Question

A predictive project to extend an open-cut mine has an influential local environmental group that opposes it and is lobbying the regulator against approval. The group has declined two invitations to the project's public information sessions, saying the sessions are "presentations, not consultation". The sponsor wants the project manager to keep them at arm's length so they cannot delay approval. The project manager judges their opposition is partly driven by concerns the design could actually accommodate. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Respect the sponsor's position and continue formal public information sessions, recording the group's non-attendance so the approval file shows consultation was offered.
- **B**: Approach the group directly to understand their specific concerns and explore which could be reflected in the design, then feed genuine options back to the sponsor.
- **C**: Commission an independent environmental review and share its findings with the group, letting the evidence address their objections without direct negotiation.
- **D**: Ask the regulator to facilitate a joint session so the group's concerns are aired in a neutral, formally minuted setting that protects the project's position.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Collaborating means engaging the opposing stakeholder's actual interests, and the PM has assessed that some concerns are genuinely accommodatable, so a direct, two-way approach can convert opposition into workable design change. (B) seeks to understand and then shapes real options, which is how influential opponents are brought toward the project rather than entrenched. (A) satisfies a paper trail but abandons any chance of resolving substantive concerns the design could meet. (C) substitutes a report for a relationship; the group has already signalled they reject one-way "presentation" and want to be heard. (D) outsources the relationship to the regulator and frames engagement as positioning, which is unlikely to lower genuine opposition.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- The sponsor's arm's-length steer is a deliberate distractor; the PM still owns the stakeholder relationship and must surface real options.
