# Question

The product owner for an agile telematics product is called away on sudden extended medical leave; the duration is unknown but expected to be several weeks. A senior marketing stakeholder and a senior engineering stakeholder have each begun directing the team's priorities in the product owner's absence, and the team is getting contradictory steers each sprint. There is a well-maintained, prioritised backlog reflecting the product owner's most recent decisions, and the next sprint planning is in two days. As PM, what should you do first?

## Options

- **A**: Have the team plan the next sprint from the existing prioritised backlog as it stands, and in parallel work with the sponsor to appoint a single empowered proxy product owner for the absence.
- **B**: Work with the sponsor to appoint a single empowered proxy product owner before the next sprint planning, so the sprint is planned under proper restored authority rather than the existing backlog alone.
- **C**: Convene the marketing and engineering stakeholders with the team to agree a shared priority order for the next sprint, since both hold legitimate stakes and a proxy may take time to confirm.
- **D**: Ask the sponsor to confirm who holds product decision authority during the absence and to communicate it to the two stakeholders, then proceed once that is settled.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Two competing pressures collide: the team needs to plan in two days, and contradictory unofficial steering must be stopped by restoring single legitimate authority. The existing backlog already encodes the real product owner's latest priorities, so it is the safest basis for the immediate sprint while proper authority is re-established. (A) protects the imminent sprint from both the gap and the contradictory steers, and fixes the structural problem in parallel, rather than trading one against the other. (B) is the right destination but gates sprint planning on confirming a proxy in two days; if that slips, the team is blocked or falls back on contradictory steers anyway, so making the appointment a precondition is riskier than running it in parallel. (C) institutionalises shared, conflicting authority for the sprint, which is the exact dynamic producing the contradictions, and lets two stakeholders override the product owner's standing backlog. (D) clarifying authority is necessary, but on its own it stops at naming who decides without yet producing a workable plan for a sprint two days out; pairing the existing backlog with the appointment is more complete.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- No throwaway "PM becomes the product owner" or "freeze everything" options; all four are defensible ways to handle a product owner vacuum.
- Not crackable by the "single empowered proxy" heuristic alone: A and B both appoint a proxy and differ only on sequencing against an imminent sprint, so the candidate must weigh the two-day constraint and the standing backlog rather than just spotting the empowerment buzzword.
