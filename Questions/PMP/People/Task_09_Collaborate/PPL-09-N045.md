# Question

A predictive offshore-wind construction project depends on an environmental-survey team that reports to a different programme. That team has started fitting your survey requests around their own programme's deadlines, and your installation window — fixed by seasonal weather and weeks away — needs the survey complete beforehand. You have no authority over the team, and when you ask their manager for firm dates you get acknowledgement but no commitment. What should you do next?

## Options

- **A**: Arrange a joint session with the survey team's manager to lay out your fixed weather window against their competing demands and agree together a sequencing both programmes can sustain.
- **B**: Engage an external survey contractor now as a parallel path, so your installation window is protected regardless of how the internal scheduling resolves.
- **C**: Escalate to your sponsor to have the survey work formally reprioritised above the other programme's deadlines for the period you need it.
- **D**: Send the survey manager a written request restating your dates, the dependency, and the impact on the installation window if the survey slips.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

You share a real scheduling problem with a counterpart who has not refused, so the first move is to collaborate on a sequence that accounts for both programmes' constraints before spending money or spending political capital. (A) brings the manager into joint problem-solving with full visibility of the immovable window and is the most likely route to a workable commitment while preserving a relationship you will keep needing. (B) is the strongest distractor — with a fixed window and a failing dependency, a parallel contractor is a legitimate, even prudent, contingency — but committing the cost and effort before attempting to align with the willing internal team is a heavyweight first response. (C) escalating to reprioritise may win this round but sets the programmes against each other before collaboration has been tried. (D) a written restatement with impact reads as pressure from someone without authority and tends to harden a non-committal stance. The margin between A and B is collaborating to secure the commitment first versus buying an independent path before trying.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- No strawman: a parallel contractor, sponsor escalation, and a formal written request are all defensible. The discrimination is attempting collaborative sequencing first versus jumping to a costly contingency, escalation, or pressure.
