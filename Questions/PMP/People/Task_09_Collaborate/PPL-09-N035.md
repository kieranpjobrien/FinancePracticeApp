# Question

A hybrid public-health data project is delivered jointly by a government agency and a university research partner. Each side has its own governance, and decisions needing both parties keep stalling because the representatives in the room cannot commit without checking internally, causing repeated delays at iteration boundaries. The relationship is constructive but slow. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Ask each organisation to send a representative who is pre-authorised to commit on shared matters, so the people in the room can actually decide.
- **B**: Agree with both parties a defined turnaround time for shared decisions, so internal checking still happens but within a bound that protects the iteration cadence.
- **C**: Work with both parties to define a joint decision-making mechanism that names who decides on shared matters, the authority they hold, and the timeframe for decisions, owned equally by both sides.
- **D**: Introduce a standing joint forum at each iteration boundary where both parties' representatives meet to work through the pending shared decisions together.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The drag has two roots, no clear shared decision authority and no agreed timeliness, so the fix must establish both, co-owned by the partners. (C) names the decision-makers, their authority, and the turnaround in a single jointly owned mechanism, addressing the whole problem. (A) tackles authority but not timeliness, and "pre-authorised" representatives still lack an agreed scope or deadline for what they may commit. (B) addresses timeliness but not who actually holds authority, so decisions can still stall on internal escalation. (D) creates a venue to meet but, without named decision rights, the same inability to commit simply recurs in the new forum. Only the combined mechanism resolves both causes.
