# Question

Two retail banks are merging, and a predictive project to consolidate their loan-origination systems now reports into two project management offices that use different templates, gate criteria, and reporting rhythms. The two PMO leads are cordial but each insists their own way is the standard the project should follow, and the team is wasting effort producing everything twice. The integration steering group has not yet ruled on which governance applies. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Convene the two PMO leads together to agree a single shared set of templates, gates, and reporting for the project, escalating only the points they cannot resolve.
- **B**: Follow whichever PMO the project's budget is funded through, since that office has the strongest claim to set the governance the project obeys.
- **C**: Continue producing both sets of artefacts until the integration steering group formally decides which governance framework will apply across the merged organisation.
- **D**: Adopt the lighter of the two PMOs' frameworks unilaterally to cut the duplicated effort, and inform both leads of the efficiency the project has gained.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The duplication stems from two stakeholders who have not yet collaborated on a shared approach, so the PM's job is to bring them together to converge where they can and escalate the genuine deadlocks. (A) builds a working agreement between the two PMOs and reserves escalation for true sticking points, which resolves the waste without overreach. (B) picks a winner on funding grounds and ignores the relationship with the other PMO, which still has standing. (C) accepts ongoing double effort indefinitely while waiting for a decision that may be slow. (D) imposes a unilateral choice that bypasses both stakeholders and risks rework if the steering group later rules differently.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- B, C, and D each avoid the collaboration; the margin is facilitating agreement between the two stakeholders versus picking, waiting, or imposing.
