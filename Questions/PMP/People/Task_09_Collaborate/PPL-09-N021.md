# Question

During sprint review for a logistics scheduling platform, the warehouse operations director praises the new picking workflow, while the finance director states the same workflow makes month-end stock valuation harder and wants it changed next sprint. The product owner has already heard both arguments in detail, understands the trade-off, and tells the project manager privately that they intend to keep the picking improvements ahead of the valuation change for now. The finance director is pressing the project manager to intervene. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Support the product owner's prioritisation and help them explain the value-based reasoning to the finance director so the decision is understood, while logging the valuation need in the backlog.
- **B**: Bring both directors and the product owner into a joint session to re-examine the two needs together before the next sprint is planned.
- **C**: Suggest the product owner sequence a portion of the valuation change into the next sprint as a goodwill gesture so the finance director feels heard.
- **D**: Ask the product owner to take the prioritisation question to the sponsoring executives for a decision, given that two directors are in disagreement.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The product owner owns backlog priority and has already weighed both needs and made an informed call; the PM's collaborative role is to back that legitimate decision and help it land with the dissatisfied stakeholder, not to reopen or override it. (A) respects the product owner's authority while ensuring the finance director's concern is acknowledged and tracked. (B) re-litigates a decision the product owner has already made deliberately, undermining their role and implying the call is the PM's to reconsider. (C) trades scope to soothe a stakeholder rather than on value, pressuring the product owner to compromise a sound decision. (D) escalates a routine prioritisation the product owner is empowered to own, eroding that authority unnecessarily.
