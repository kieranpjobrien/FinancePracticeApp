# Question

On a hybrid energy-trading platform project, a shared data-engineering team is owned by a functional manager who also supplies two other projects. The PM needs three engineers for an upcoming integration iteration; the functional manager has verbally agreed but keeps pulling them onto incidents from the other projects at short notice, and the iteration goal is now at risk. The relationship has been good until now. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Ask the sponsor to have the functional manager ring-fence the three engineers for the duration of the iteration, given the iteration goal is now at risk.
- **B**: Meet the functional manager to jointly agree a protected allocation for the iteration and an explicit rule for how genuine incident conflicts are triaged and traded off between the competing projects.
- **C**: Request that the functional manager name backfill engineers who can step in whenever the primary three are pulled to incidents.
- **D**: Replan the iteration around the engineering availability actually being seen in practice, so the goal becomes achievable without depending on a firmer commitment.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

This is a collaborative resourcing negotiation with a manager whose relationship the PM wants to preserve; the durable fix is a jointly agreed allocation plus an explicit, mutually understood rule for handling real incident conflicts. (B) secures the immediate need and resolves the recurring pattern by agreement. (A) reaches for sponsor authority over a relationship the PM can still negotiate directly, risking the goodwill and the manager's cooperation on the other two projects. (C) treats engineers as interchangeable and adds onboarding churn each time someone is pulled, without securing reliable capacity. (D) absorbs the problem by lowering ambition and rewards the broken commitment, leaving the underlying conflict to recur next iteration.
