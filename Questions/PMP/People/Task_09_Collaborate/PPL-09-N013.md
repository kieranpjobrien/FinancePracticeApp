# Question

On a scaled agile programme building a connected-car platform, three feature teams share a common product owner. Two of the teams believe the product owner consistently favours the third team's features in backlog ordering, and cross-team collaboration is deteriorating — teams have quietly stopped flagging shared dependencies to each other. You have reviewed the recent ordering and the sequencing does in fact follow the agreed value criteria, but the two teams have never been shown that reasoning. As the programme's project manager, what is the best first action?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate a session where the product owner makes the prioritisation rationale and value criteria transparent to all three teams and invites them to test the ordering openly against those criteria.
- **B**: Bring the two aggrieved teams and the product owner together to re-establish the cross-team dependency-flagging the teams have abandoned, since that breakdown is the concrete risk to delivery.
- **C**: Have the product owner publish the value criteria and the score behind each upcoming backlog item, so every team can see for itself why the ordering falls as it does.
- **D**: Raise the perception of favouritism privately with the product owner first and agree how she will demonstrate even-handedness to the two teams going forward.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The corrosive element is a perception of unfairness fed purely by opacity — the ordering is actually sound — so the most effective first move is a facilitated session that makes the rationale transparent and lets the teams test it directly with the product owner present, which addresses the false belief and rebuilds the cross-team trust in one step (A). (B) restoring dependency-flagging targets a real symptom, but the teams stopped collaborating *because* they feel the game is rigged; fix the perceived unfairness and the flagging returns, so leading with the mechanics treats the effect before the cause. (C) publishing criteria and scores supplies the same information but as a one-way document, without the live dialogue that lets aggrieved teams challenge and be answered — transparency lands better when it can be questioned in the room. (D) a private word with the product owner first is reasonable, but the grievance and the eroding trust sit with the two teams, so resolving it out of their sight does little to change what they believe.
