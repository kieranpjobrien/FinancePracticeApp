# Question

On a predictive airport-terminal expansion, your client's facilities team wants to maximise design quality with little regard for the contractor's margin, while the contractor wants to lock the design early to protect profitability. Both behave cooperatively in meetings but each is privately working to constrain the other through the change process. As project manager, what is the best way to foster genuine collaboration?

## Options

- **A**: Meet each party separately to understand their real interests, then broker compromises between them without bringing the conflicting incentives into the open.
- **B**: Tighten the change-control process so neither party can constrain the other informally and all design changes must pass through a strict, auditable gate.
- **C**: Establish a shared decision framework that makes the cost, quality and schedule trade-offs of design choices explicit to both parties, so decisions are made on common data rather than positional manoeuvring.
- **D**: Escalate the misaligned incentives to both organisations' executives and ask them to instruct their teams to prioritise the project's overall success.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

When stakeholders have legitimately different incentives, collaboration is built by surfacing the trade-offs on shared data so both can see the consequences of choices and decide together (C) — moving them from hidden positional play to joint, transparent decision-making. (A) shuttle diplomacy that keeps the conflicting incentives hidden may broker short-term deals but never establishes the open, trusting basis that genuine collaboration needs. (B) tightening change control treats the symptom (informal constraining) with process and can make relations more adversarial, not less. (D) executive instruction to "prioritise the project" is an exhortation that does not change the underlying incentives driving the behaviour.
