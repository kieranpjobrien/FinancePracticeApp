# Question

In a weak-matrix manufacturing organisation, your predictive ERP project relies on specialists who report to functional managers. One functional manager agreed in principle to release a key analyst, but keeps pulling them onto operational line issues whenever one flares up, and your critical-path analysis has stalled twice as a result. When you raise it, the manager points to the production demands their function is under. You have no line authority over the analyst or the manager. What is the best approach?

## Options

- **A**: Work with the functional manager to agree a realistic, protected commitment for the analyst — specific time blocks tied to your critical-path needs — that fits around their operational pressures.
- **B**: Re-plan the critical path around the analyst's intermittent availability, so your schedule no longer assumes uninterrupted access to them.
- **C**: Build a cost-of-delay analysis showing the impact of each redeployment and present it to the functional manager to make the case for protecting the analyst's time.
- **D**: Escalate to the steering committee to have the analyst formally ring-fenced to the project for the critical-path period.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The manager has not refused — they are caught between your need and real operational demands — so collaboration means finding a commitment that works within their reality rather than overriding or pressuring them. (A) negotiates protected, specific time tied to your critical-path needs while respecting the line pressures, which is realistic and preserves a relationship you depend on in a weak matrix. (B) re-planning around intermittent access is a sensible and often necessary contingency — the strongest distractor — but adopting it first accepts ongoing critical-path risk and gives up on securing the commitment before trying. (C) a cost-of-delay case is a reasonable influence tool, yet it treats a manager who already agreed in principle as if they need persuading, applying pressure where a workable arrangement is what is missing. (D) escalating to ring-fence the analyst may force access but undermines a cooperative manager and can leave operations exposed, breeding later resistance. The margin is collaborating on a workable commitment first versus re-planning around the gap, building a pressure case, or escalating to compel access.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- No strawman: re-planning around availability, a cost-of-delay case, and a steering-committee escalation are all defensible PM moves. The discrimination is securing a realistic commitment collaboratively before resorting to workaround, persuasion-pressure, or compulsion.
