# Question

A predictive rail-signalling upgrade has a steering committee split: the operations members want a phased cutover to minimise service disruption, while the engineering members want a single cutover to avoid running two signalling regimes in parallel. Both positions are technically defensible. The committee has deadlocked across two meetings, with each side re-arguing its preferred option rather than engaging with the other's concerns. The chair asks the PM to help break the impasse. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Present the committee with a recommended option backed by a disruption, cost, and risk analysis, so there is a clear proposal to accept and the deadlock has something concrete to resolve around.
- **B**: Help the committee first agree the decision criteria and their relative weighting against the project's objectives, then assess both cutover options against those agreed criteria together.
- **C**: Facilitate a session in which each side presents the other's option as fairly as it can, to break the entrenched re-arguing, then call for a vote.
- **D**: Propose a hybrid that phases the busiest lines and single-cuts the rest, giving each camp part of what it wants so the committee can move forward.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The committee is deadlocked because it is arguing options without a shared basis for choosing, so the PM's collaborative contribution is to establish agreed criteria first and then evaluate against them jointly. (B) gives the committee a common yardstick tied to objectives, which is what converts two entrenched preferences into a decidable question they own. (A) supplies analysis but attaches the PM's recommendation, nudging a decision that is the committee's to make and risking it becoming the PM's call. (C) is a useful empathy technique and may soften positions, but jumping to a vote without agreed criteria can still produce an arbitrary, unprincipled outcome. (D) manufactures a compromise that may inherit the risks of both regimes and is offered to relieve tension rather than because it best meets the objectives.
