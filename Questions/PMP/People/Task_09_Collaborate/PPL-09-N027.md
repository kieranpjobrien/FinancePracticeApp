# Question

Midway through a municipal water-metering rollout, the council replaces its community-relations officer; the new officer is now the key interface for all resident communications, a sensitive area where the previous officer had quietly negotiated several local objections through informal commitments to residents that were never written down. A round of resident notifications, drafted and approved under the previous officer, is due to go out in three days. The new officer tells the PM they are still getting up to speed and are not yet comfortable with the area. What should the PM do next?

## Options

- **A**: Sit with the new officer to walk through the imminent notifications and check, before they go out, whether anything in them cuts across the informal commitments the previous officer made to residents.
- **B**: Reassure the new officer that the notifications were already approved through the proper process, and offer to brief them fully on the resident sensitivities once this round has gone out.
- **C**: Ask the council to second the departing officer for a short structured handover to the new officer, since only the departing officer holds the undocumented commitments, and hold the notifications until that handover is done.
- **D**: Pause the imminent notifications and work with the new officer to map the undocumented commitments by reviewing resident correspondence together before anything further is sent to residents.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The specific, time-bound risk is that an already-approved notification may contradict undocumented promises made to residents, and it goes out in three days; the proportionate move is to check this particular batch against those commitments with the new officer now, before it ships. (A) targets exactly the live exposure within the available time and begins building the working relationship. (B) leans on prior approval, but that approval predates anyone realising the commitments were undocumented, so "already approved" does not make the batch safe; it ships the risk. (C) a structured handover from the departing officer is genuinely valuable, but making it a precondition for an approved, scheduled communication, and depending on a departing employee's availability, is heavier and slower than checking this one batch first; the broader handover can follow. (D) mapping all undocumented commitments is sound in principle but is a large exercise to gate three-days-out notifications on; the immediate need is to vet this batch, not to reconstruct the entire history before any contact continues.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- No throwaway options: all four are reasonable PM actions; the margin turns on proportionality and timing against a specific live exposure.
- Not crackable by heuristic: both C and D are thorough "do it properly" options that a pattern-matcher might favour, but each over-scopes the immediate three-day decision; B is a real (if weaker) governance-based position, not a strawman.
