# Question

Business stakeholders attend sprint reviews for a warehouse-management product but rarely give substantive feedback in the room, then raise material concerns by email a week after the increment ships. When the PM asks a few of them why, a consistent reason emerges: the changes touch several depots, and each stakeholder will not commit a view in the review until they have checked the impact with their own depot managers, which takes them a few days. The reviews are already hands-on and well run. What is the best next step?

## Options

- **A**: Give stakeholders access to the increment a few days before each review so they can consult their depot managers first and arrive at the review able to give a committed view.
- **B**: Add a structured round at the end of each review where every stakeholder is asked in turn for their reaction, so feedback is captured in the session rather than by email later.
- **C**: Accept that considered feedback will arrive a few days after the review and build a short post-review feedback window into the cadence, triaging what comes in before the increment is acted on downstream.
- **D**: Ask each stakeholder to bring their relevant depot manager to the review, so the people with the operational detail are in the room when the increment is shown.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The cause is known and specific: stakeholders need to consult depot managers before they can responsibly commit a view, and that consultation takes a few days. The best move respects that real constraint and still gets feedback in time to matter. (A) shifts the consultation to before the review by giving early access, so stakeholders arrive ready to commit, keeping decision-making inside the review without rushing anyone. (C) accommodates the constraint but bakes a permanent lag into the loop, meaning the review itself stays low-value and decisions are perpetually deferred; useful as a fallback, not the best fix. (D) brings depot managers in, which can work, but it scales the meeting up considerably, assumes every depot manager can attend each review, and pushes the consultation into the room rather than letting it happen properly beforehand. (B) forces an in-room answer, which directly contradicts the discovered cause; pressed for a view they have not yet validated, stakeholders will either withhold or give an uncommitted one and still email later.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- Cause is given, so this is not a "diagnose first" question; the margin is which remedy fits the stated constraint (right move for this context).
- All four options are legitimate responses to slow feedback; none is a strawman. A and C both honour the consultation need but differ on whether feedback stays timely; D solves it a costlier way; B is the plausible-but-wrong "just ask everyone in the room" move.
