# Question

A scrum team building a clinician-facing diagnostics tool has a sponsor who attends every sprint review and consistently asks for one more feature before agreeing the increment is shippable, even though the product owner has marked it releasable. The product owner concedes each time to avoid friction with the sponsor, so the team's definition of "done" is effectively being overridden at the review, and morale is dropping. As project manager working alongside the scrum master, what should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Raise with the sponsor at the next review that the increment meets the agreed definition of done and should be accepted as releasable, defending the team's standard in the moment.
- **B**: Coach the product owner to hold the releasability decision themselves and, with them, agree with the sponsor up front how genuinely new scope enters the backlog rather than gating the increment at the review.
- **C**: Facilitate a session for the sponsor, product owner, and team to jointly rewrite the definition of done so it reflects the additional quality bar the sponsor keeps asking for.
- **D**: Propose that the sponsor's desired extras be pulled into the current sprint when raised, so increments still ship and the sponsor's expectations are met.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The structural problem is that the product owner is ceding the releasability call to the sponsor, so the durable fix is to restore the product owner's ownership and agree a proper channel for new scope. (B) addresses the root by coaching the product owner to hold their authority and aligning the sponsor on backlog intake, which is the PM's collaborative role. (A) defends the team once but leaves the product owner still folding next sprint, treating the symptom in a single instance. (C) misreads the issue as a too-low quality bar and rewrites the definition of done around a moving target, institutionalising scope gating rather than ending it. (D) accommodates the sponsor's additions every sprint, which is exactly the dynamic crushing morale and overriding the product owner.
