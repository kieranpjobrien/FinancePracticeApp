# Question

On a predictive hospital electronic-records implementation, the clinical group and the medical-records and billing function have submitted conflicting requirements for the patient-encounter screens: clinicians want the fewest possible fields for fast capture, while billing wants additional mandatory fields for accurate coding. Both mandates are legitimate, each group has been lobbying the sponsor separately, and the two leads have a history of talking past each other. Design work on those screens is scheduled to begin next week. What is the best next step for the project manager?

## Options

- **A**: Convene both groups in a facilitated session to expose the underlying drivers behind each requirement and jointly search for a screen design that serves both mandates.
- **B**: Have the business analyst work with each group separately to translate their requirements into the underlying data and workflow needs, then bring a reconciled options set to a joint review.
- **C**: Take the conflict to the sponsor with a clear statement of the trade-off and the cost of each option, since both groups are already escalating and the sponsor owns the scope decision.
- **D**: Let design proceed on the fields both groups agree on and timebox the disputed fields for resolution before that part of the screen is built.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The two requirements conflict on the surface but rest on different underlying needs that are not necessarily incompatible, and the two leads talking past each other is itself part of the problem — so getting them into direct, facilitated contact both tests for an integrative solution and rebuilds the cross-group relationship. (A) does this and keeps both groups owning the outcome. (B) is a credible analyst-led path and produces good options, but it keeps the two leads apart and reconciles their needs by proxy, leaving the relationship unaddressed and risking both rejecting a solution they did not build. (C) sponsor escalation may be needed if facilitation fails, but going there first converts a solvable design problem into a political win/lose and disengages the losing group. (D) sequencing around the dispute keeps the schedule moving but defers the hardest decision and the relationship work without attempting resolution. The margin between A and B is whether the PM brings the parties together themselves or reconciles their needs through an intermediary.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- No strawman: analyst-led reconciliation, sponsor decision, and timeboxing the disputed fields are all defensible. The discrimination is direct joint problem-solving versus by-proxy reconciliation, escalation, or deferral.
