# Question

The business stakeholders for an e-commerce replatforming project attend sprint reviews but stay quiet, then raise substantive objections to the same features weeks later at release sign-off, forcing rework. When you ask why, they tell you the reviews move too fast through technical detail for them to judge whether a feature actually meets their business need, so they hold comment until they can study it properly. The team is demoralised by the late churn. As the agile project lead, what is the most effective response?

## Options

- **A**: Reframe each review around the business outcomes the increment is meant to serve and demonstrate features against those outcomes, so stakeholders can assess fitness during the session.
- **B**: Send a recorded walkthrough and a written summary after each review so stakeholders can study the detail at their own pace and return considered feedback within two days.
- **C**: Add a short business-acceptance checkpoint a few days after each review, giving stakeholders a scheduled point to lodge considered feedback before the increment is built on.
- **D**: Agree with the stakeholders that feedback raised after a review carries less weight than feedback given in it, so there is a clear incentive to engage live.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The stakeholders are not disengaged — they attend — but the event is pitched so they cannot judge business fitness in the moment, so the fix is to change what the review shows them: demonstrate against business outcomes so they can assess and react live (A), which removes the actual cause of the delay. (B) recorded walkthroughs and a two-day turnaround still move the feedback out of the live session and institutionalise a lag, leaving the late-churn dynamic largely intact. (C) a post-review acceptance checkpoint formalises feedback arriving after the review rather than enabling it during the review, and adds a gate the team must wait on. (D) penalising late feedback punishes stakeholders for a problem the event design created and will breed resentment without making them any better able to judge a feature in the room.
