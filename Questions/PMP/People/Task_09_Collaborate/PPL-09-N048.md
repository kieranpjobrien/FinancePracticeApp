# Question

A predictive water-treatment upgrade has run for four months when the client appoints a new operations director who becomes the primary business stakeholder for the remainder of the project. They have asked for "a quick catch-up at some point" and have no detail on the project's history, decisions or status. A scope decision that needs the primary business stakeholder's input is due in two weeks. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Arrange a structured onboarding session covering objectives, key decisions, current status and the points where the new director's input will matter, including the decision two weeks away.
- **B**: Take the new director straight through the upcoming scope decision and its options, giving them the background each option depends on as it comes up.
- **C**: Send the new director a concise briefing pack of objectives, decision log and status to read first, then meet to answer their questions.
- **D**: Introduce the new director to the project's workstream leads so they can build their own picture from the people doing the work, then convene once they are up to speed.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A new primary stakeholder facing a decision in two weeks needs a deliberate, PM-led orientation that gives them the whole picture and connects it to where their input is required. (A) does this in one guided pass and arms them for the imminent decision. (B) is the strongest distractor — anchoring onboarding on the live decision is efficient and decision-relevant — but feeding background only as each option arises leaves the director without the surrounding context that informs a sound scope call, and skips the relationship-building a primary stakeholder needs. (C) a pre-read then Q&A is reasonable but puts the work of making sense of an unfamiliar project onto someone with no context and little time before they must act. (D) self-assembly via the workstream leads is slow, risks an inconsistent picture, and may not converge before the decision. The margin is a guided, decision-ready orientation versus a decision-only briefing, a self-serve pre-read, or self-assembly.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- No strawman: a decision-anchored briefing, a pre-read-then-meet, and introductions to workstream leads are all real onboarding approaches. The discrimination is a guided whole-picture orientation versus narrower or self-directed alternatives.
