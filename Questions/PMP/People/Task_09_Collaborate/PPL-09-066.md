# Question

A hybrid programme is rolling out an electronic medication-administration system across a hospital network. The programme manager faces four distinct stakeholder collaboration situations in the same week. Each could be handled several ways, but one mechanism fits each best given who the stakeholder is and what the situation needs. Match each situation to its most appropriate collaboration mechanism.

## Items

- **1**: Ward nurses keep finding that the new workflow does not match how they actually give medicines, and the design needs their hands-on input every iteration to stay usable.
- **2**: A consultant physician with strong influence is privately sceptical of the system but has never voiced specifics; their backing is needed for clinical adoption.
- **3**: The pharmacy, IT, and clinical-governance leads each control a dependency for go-live and must stay jointly aligned on cross-cutting decisions throughout delivery.
- **4**: Frontline staff across twelve wards need to absorb a steady stream of small interface changes and feed back quick reactions without attending formal sessions.

## Matches

- **A**: Hold a brief one-to-one to draw out their specific concerns and understand what would earn their support.
- **B**: Embed their representatives in iteration reviews so their practice directly shapes each build.
- **C**: Run a standing cross-functional working group that meets regularly to keep the interdependent leads aligned.
- **D**: Use a lightweight always-on channel for pushing changes and gathering rapid feedback at scale.

## Answer

1-B, 2-A, 3-C, 4-D

## Explanation

The skill is matching the collaboration mechanism to the stakeholder and the need, not to surface words. The nurses need their practice to shape every iteration, so embedding their representatives in iteration reviews fits (1-B). The influential but silent sceptic needs a private conversation to surface specifics and win backing, which a group setting would not achieve (2-A). The three dependency-owning leads must stay continuously aligned on cross-cutting calls, which is exactly what a standing working group provides (3-C). Twelve wards absorbing many small changes need a scalable, low-friction channel rather than meetings (4-D). The tempting traps are swapping 1 and 4 — using a broadcast channel for the deep design input the nurses owe, or trying to embed twelve wards in iteration reviews they cannot all attend.
