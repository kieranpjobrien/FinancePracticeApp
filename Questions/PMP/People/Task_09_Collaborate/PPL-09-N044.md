# Question

On an agile retail-loyalty app, the head of marketing attends sprint reviews but rarely speaks, then later blocks decisions in other forums on the grounds that the direction is off-brand. The reviews are run as technical walkthroughs of the increment. The head of marketing is influential, time-pressured, and accustomed to working through their own team. As project manager, which actions would most directly improve this stakeholder's collaboration? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Reshape the review so increments are shown from the customer's point of view, with explicit moments where brand and experience feedback is invited.
- **B**: Meet the head of marketing briefly before reviews to surface the brand considerations that matter most, so they are already on the table in the room.
- **C**: Invite the head of marketing to co-present the customer-facing parts of the increment, building their brand perspective into the demonstration itself.
- **D**: Agree with the head of marketing a standing brand-acceptance checkpoint their nominated team member attends each sprint, so brand input is captured without relying on the head of marketing's own time.
- **E**: After each review, send the head of marketing a concise summary highlighting the customer-facing changes and ask for any brand concerns by a set date.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

The stakeholder stays silent in reviews and then blocks elsewhere because the format engages their expertise too late and too indirectly; the actions that work pull that expertise into the room while decisions are live. Reframing the demo around the customer with explicit brand moments (A), a pre-review conversation to surface what matters (B), and co-presenting so their perspective is embedded (C) all create direct, timely engagement. (D) is the strongest distractor — delegating to a nominated team member respects the head of marketing's time and is a legitimate engagement design — but it keeps the influential decision-maker themselves out of the room, so the after-the-fact blocking is likely to continue. (E) a written summary moves their input outside the room and preserves the post-hoc pattern. The discrimination is between drawing the actual decision-maker into live collaboration (A, B, C) and routing their input through a delegate or a document (D, E).

## Anti-patterns avoided

- No strawman: a delegated brand checkpoint and a structured written follow-up are both real, defensible engagement designs. Both fall short because they keep the influential stakeholder out of the live conversation rather than bringing them in.
