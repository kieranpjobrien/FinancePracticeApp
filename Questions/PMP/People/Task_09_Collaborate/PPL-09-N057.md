# Question

On a predictive enterprise-finance-system rollout, the sponsor and the finance department head — the two most senior stakeholders — describe the first release differently in their separate conversations with you. The sponsor frames it as replacing the legacy ledger; the department head frames it as replacing the ledger and retiring three spreadsheet-based workarounds. Each speaks as though the scope is settled, and they have not discussed it directly with each other. The baseline is due to be locked this week. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the sponsor and the department head together to reconcile their understandings of the first release before the baseline is locked.
- **B**: Write up both descriptions side by side with the scope, cost and schedule implications of each, and circulate it to the two stakeholders for them to resolve in writing.
- **C**: Baseline the ledger replacement now to hold the schedule, and raise the spreadsheet retirements as a scope change once their cost and effort are assessed.
- **D**: Baseline both the ledger replacement and the spreadsheet retirements, since both senior stakeholders' expectations are then covered and neither is disappointed.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Two key stakeholders hold divergent definitions of the release and have not realised it; locking the baseline now would cement a hidden disagreement that surfaces painfully later, and reconciling it is the PM's responsibility. (A) surfaces and resolves the difference between them before the baseline fixes it, protecting both the plan and the relationship. (B) a written comparison is a useful input and more rigorous than a chat, but asking two senior stakeholders to reconcile a scope divergence by correspondence is slow, risks hardening positions, and keeps them from the direct conversation that settles it. (C) baselining the ledger and deferring the spreadsheets to change control is clean process, yet it commits to one stakeholder's view of "done" and buries the other's expectation as a later variance. (D) baselining both avoids disappointing anyone but commits scope, cost and schedule without the two agreeing it, and conceals from each that they ever differed. The margin is reconciling the two stakeholders directly before committing versus resolving it on paper, or baselining one view or both without agreement.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- No strawman: a written options comparison, baseline-then-change-request, and covering both expectations are all defensible baseline moves. The discrimination is the PM reconciling the two stakeholders' understandings before locking the baseline versus committing first.
