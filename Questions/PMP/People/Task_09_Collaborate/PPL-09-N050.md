# Question

On an agile e-commerce replatforming, three business units — fulfilment, merchandising and customer service — each nominate work for the backlog and each maintains that their items are the most important. The product owner is new and has been setting the sprint order through one-on-one negotiations with whichever unit pushes hardest. Two units have told you they no longer see how the order is decided, and goodwill between them is fraying. As project manager, what is the best step to take?

## Options

- **A**: Help the product owner establish a shared prioritisation basis — value and effort criteria agreed with all three units — so the order is visible and defensible to everyone.
- **B**: Help the product owner set a fixed share of each sprint's capacity per business unit, so all three see predictable, even access to delivery.
- **C**: Advise the product owner to order strictly by which items deliver revenue soonest, giving a single objective rule everyone can check against.
- **D**: Advise the product owner to rotate which unit's items lead the backlog each sprint, so influence is spread evenly and no unit is permanently behind.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The friction comes from an order that looks personal and opaque, so the fix is a shared basis the units help define and can see applied to every item. (A) makes prioritisation transparent and jointly owned, improving the decisions and rebuilding trust across the units. (B) fixed capacity shares are predictable and feel fair, but they detach sequencing from relative value and can push low-value work ahead of high-value work within each share. (C) revenue-soonest is genuinely objective and checkable — its weakness is that as the sole rule it sidelines fulfilment and customer-service work that protects revenue and experience, so it is one input, not the basis. (D) rotation removes the lobbying advantage but substitutes a different arbitrary rule and still divorces order from value. All four give the units a clearer rule than ad-hoc lobbying; the margin is a transparent value-and-effort basis the units co-own versus imposing a single fixed heuristic.

## Anti-patterns avoided

- No strawman: capacity-sharing, revenue-first, and rotation are all real prioritisation heuristics with genuine appeal. The discrimination is establishing a shared, jointly-owned basis versus adopting another single fixed rule.
