# Question

On a water-utility upgrade, a long-serving operations manager has been cooperative in writing but cold and clipped in every meeting since the project was announced. Their sign-off is needed for the next phase. A colleague mentions the manager spent fifteen years building the system this project will replace. The project manager wants the relationship to function. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Send a detailed, evidence-based briefing showing why the upgrade is necessary, so the manager's cooperation rests on the business case rather than sentiment.
- **B**: Open a private conversation acknowledging the manager's deep history with the current system and ask how they see the transition, before pressing for the sign-off.
- **C**: Route the sign-off request through the manager's director, who is a project sponsor, to keep the schedule moving while the relationship warms over time.
- **D**: Invite the manager onto the project steering group so their experience is formally recognised and they have a stake in the outcome.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Social awareness means reading the unstated emotion — here, likely grief or threatened identity over a system the manager built — and responding to it before transacting. (B) surfaces and honours that before asking for anything, which is what unlocks genuine cooperation. (A) answers a feelings problem with a logic argument and will not thaw the coldness. (C) bypasses the person and is likely to deepen the resentment that is causing the friction. (D) may be a good later step, but offering a role before understanding the manager's stance risks looking transactional; the empathetic conversation comes first.
