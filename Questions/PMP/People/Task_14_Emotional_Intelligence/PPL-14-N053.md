# Question

During a high-pressure sprint review, a developer challenges the project manager's call in front of the team in a pointed, edged tone, and the PM feels a hot flash of irritation and a strong pull to fire back. The PM stays outwardly composed. Reviewing the moment afterwards, the PM wants to know which of their possible responses genuinely reflected self-regulation, managing their own impulse in the moment, rather than merely looking calm while the emotion still drove the response. Which two responses best reflect self-regulation here? (Choose two.)

## Options

- **A**: Notice the irritation, let it crest without acting on it, and respond to the substance of the developer's point as if the tone had not been there.
- **B**: Take a beat to weigh how a sharp reply versus a measured one would land, then deliberately choose the measured reply.
- **C**: Answer in an even, controlled voice but pointedly remind the developer, in front of the team, that the decision was theirs to make and the tone was uncalled for.
- **D**: Keep a level expression, say nothing in the moment, and raise the developer's behaviour with them firmly in private straight after the review while the irritation is still fresh.
- **E**: Hold composure outwardly, give a brief non-committal answer to close the exchange, and privately resolve to be less accommodating to this developer going forward.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

All five look calm on the surface, which is the trap: self-regulation is managing the impulse itself, not masking it while it still steers the response. (A) lets the emotion peak and pass and then engages the substance, which is impulse control in its purest form. (B) inserts a deliberate pause to weigh consequences and then chooses the response on purpose rather than on reflex, the textbook self-regulation move. (C) sounds controlled but is a measured counter-attack: the irritation is still driving a public rebuke, so the emotion won, just quietly. (D) defers the reaction rather than regulating it, then acts on it "while the irritation is still fresh," meaning the same impulse is simply discharged a few minutes later in private. (E) suppresses outwardly but lets the resentment harden into a lasting grudge against the developer, which is avoidance and rumination, not regulation. The fine margin is that A and B actually master the impulse, whereas C, D, and E only postpone or disguise it.
