# Question

A project manager is leading the third attempt at a troubled ERP rollout that two predecessors abandoned. Six weeks in, a key integration test fails badly and a sponsor remarks, half-joking, that "this thing is cursed." The PM notices their own optimism flagging and the team beginning to mirror their mood in the daily check-ins. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Raise with the sponsor that the repeated failures suggest the underlying approach may be flawed and that a feasibility reassessment may be warranted.
- **B**: Project confidence to the team that this rollout will succeed where the others failed, to keep morale from sliding further.
- **C**: Acknowledge to themselves that the setback stings, then deliberately refocus the team on the specific, solvable cause of the test failure and the next concrete step.
- **D**: Give the team a day off to reset, since fatigue and the weight of the project's history are clearly affecting everyone's outlook.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Self-motivation in emotional intelligence is the capacity to register disappointment honestly yet keep oneself and others oriented toward action — and the PM's own regulated mood is what the team reads. (C) names the feeling without being ruled by it and channels energy into a tractable next step, modelling resilience. (B) papers over real difficulty with forced positivity the team will see through. (A) may eventually be valid, but reaching for a feasibility reassessment after a single test failure is premature and would amplify the defeatist mood rather than manage it. (D) treats a motivational problem as a fatigue problem and risks confirming the team's sense that the project is foundering.
