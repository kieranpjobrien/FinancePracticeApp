# Question

A PM leads a hybrid programme modernising a hospital's patient-records system. The clinical director, an influential stakeholder, has grown cool towards the programme after an earlier phase overran; she now responds tersely, declines optional working sessions, and her ward's adoption of the new workflow is lagging behind every other ward. The PM has solid data showing the new phase is on track and that the overrun was caused by a vendor defect outside the programme's control. The PM wants to restore a productive working relationship with the clinical director. Which approach is most likely to succeed?

## Options

- **A**: Meet her one-to-one to understand how the earlier overrun affected her ward, acknowledge that impact genuinely, and then explore what would make this phase work for her staff.
- **B**: Walk her through the performance data and the root-cause analysis showing the overrun was a vendor defect, so she can see the programme was not at fault.
- **C**: Ask the programme sponsor to reinforce with her the expectation that all wards adopt the new workflow on the agreed timeline.
- **D**: Invite her to co-chair the next phase's steering group so she has visible ownership of its success.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Relationship management under emotional intelligence rebuilds a damaged relationship by first attending to the other person's emotional experience, not by winning the argument on facts — so (A), which leads with empathy and acknowledgement before problem-solving, is most likely to thaw the relationship and is the only option that addresses why she is cool. (B) treats a relational and emotional rupture as a factual misunderstanding; being shown she is wrong about blame, however true, tends to harden a defensive stakeholder rather than re-engage her. (C) escalates around her and applies positional pressure, which may force surface compliance but deepens resentment and damages the relationship further. (D) is a plausible influence move and might form part of a later step, but offering co-ownership before acknowledging the past can feel like a transaction to someone who feels unheard, and it presumes she wants more involvement. The fine margin is that B, C, and D all skip the empathy step that the cool relationship actually requires.
