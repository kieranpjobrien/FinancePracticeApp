# Question

A project manager has realised that during high-pressure sprints their own stress is leaking into how they lead, and they want to strengthen self-awareness specifically, the ability to recognise their emotions and impact as they happen. They have limited time and can commit to only two new practices this quarter. Which two practices most directly develop self-awareness, as distinct from the other emotional-intelligence competencies? (Choose two.)

## Options

- **A**: Keep a brief end-of-day note after tense interactions, recording what they felt, what triggered it, and how they responded.
- **B**: Ask three trusted colleagues each quarter for candid feedback on how the PM's demeanour affects the team under pressure.
- **C**: Practise a short pause-and-breathe routine to interrupt sharp reactions before responding in ceremonies.
- **D**: Run regular one-on-ones to read each team member's mood and surface concerns they are not voicing.
- **E**: Hold a mediated session to repair a strained relationship between two team members and realign them on a shared goal.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

Every option is a legitimate emotional-intelligence practice, so the discriminator is which ones build self-awareness rather than a neighbouring competency. (A) develops insight into one's own emotional patterns and triggers over time, the core of self-awareness. (B) gathers outside perspective on one's impact, which matters because impact is partly a blind spot only others can see, and is still squarely about knowing oneself. (C) is a sound practice but it is self-regulation, managing the response once the emotion is already recognised. (D) is social awareness or empathy, perceiving others' states, not one's own. (E) is relationship management, acting on a relationship between other people. The trap is that all five are good things to do; only A and B target self-awareness itself.
