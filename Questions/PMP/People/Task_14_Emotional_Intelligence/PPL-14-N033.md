# Question

At an agile retrospective, a senior developer attacks a tracking process the project manager introduced, calling it "box-ticking that treats us like children," in a sharp tone that visibly makes two newer team members uneasy. The PM concedes privately that the developer has a fair point about the overhead, but the delivery is corrosive and the room has gone quiet. The PM feels the sting and is aware everyone is watching how they handle it. What is the most emotionally intelligent response?

## Options

- **A**: Take a breath, thank the developer for raising it, and ask them to say more about what specifically is not working with the process.
- **B**: Acknowledge the substantive point that the overhead is real, name that the retro works best when concerns are aired in a way that keeps it safe for everyone, and invite the team to shape a lighter approach.
- **C**: Stay composed and steer the discussion to the wider team, asking whether others find the process burdensome, so the decision rests on the group rather than one strong voice.
- **D**: Validate the frustration, agree to trial dropping the process next sprint, and move on so the team sees their feedback produces action.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The test is regulating the sting while managing two things the senior developer's outburst has put in play at once: a legitimate process concern and a delivery that has chilled the room for newer members. (B) does both — it affirms the valid point so the developer feels heard, gently resets the tone to protect psychological safety without publicly rebuking anyone, and opens a constructive path. (A) regulates well and invites substance but addresses only the content, leaving the corrosive delivery and the uneasy newcomers unattended. (C) is composed and consultative but sidesteps both the merit already conceded and the relational damage, deferring to a poll instead of leading the moment. (D) jumps to a decision at the peak of the emotion to look responsive, rewarding the destructive delivery and skipping the understanding and tone-setting that should come first. The margin is fine because (A) and (C) are both poised, defensible facilitation moves; (B) is best because it is the only one that handles the feeling, the substance, and the room together.
