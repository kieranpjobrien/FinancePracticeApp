# Question

A project manager is coaching a new team lead who is technically strong and well-liked, reads the team's mood well, and handles conflict between others skilfully. The one recurring problem: whenever a sprint commitment is questioned in front of the team, the lead snaps back sarcastically in the moment and regrets it within the hour, and the team has started to stay quiet to avoid setting them off. What is the most useful focus for the PM's coaching?

## Options

- **A**: Help the lead build a deliberate pause between feeling the sting of a challenge and responding, so they can choose their reaction rather than fire back.
- **B**: Coach the lead to read the emotional cues of the person challenging them, so they respond to the concern behind the challenge rather than the words.
- **C**: Work with the lead on rebuilding trust with team members who have grown wary, so the team feels safe speaking up again.
- **D**: Help the lead communicate the reasoning behind sprint commitments more clearly, so their decisions are questioned less often in the first place.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The lead's profile is given precisely: strong empathy, sound relationship management, and the lone failure is an automatic, impulsive reaction they cannot contain in the moment, which is a self-regulation gap. Coaching is most useful when it targets the actual missing competency. (A) builds impulse control, the stimulus-to-response pause, which addresses the root behaviour directly. (B) develops empathy, which the lead already demonstrates, so it misdiagnoses the problem. (C) repairs the damage the outbursts caused but does not stop the next one; without (A), trust will erode again. (D) reduces how often the lead is challenged but leaves them just as likely to snap when they are, and people will always question commitments. The trap is that B, C, and D are each reasonable development areas, but only A fixes the specific competency the lead lacks.
