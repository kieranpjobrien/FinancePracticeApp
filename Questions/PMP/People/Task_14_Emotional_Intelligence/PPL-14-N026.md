# Question

After a difficult release, an agile team's retrospective produces an anonymous comment: "Decisions feel like they're made before we're asked." The project manager prides themselves on consulting the team and is genuinely stung, recalling several times they sought input. Their first instinct is to clarify in the next stand-up how often they do ask. What is the most emotionally intelligent course?

## Options

- **A**: Explain to the team, with examples, the recent decisions where their input genuinely shaped the outcome, so the record is balanced.
- **B**: Sit with the discomfort, treat the comment as data about how their behaviour is experienced, and look for the specific moments where consultation may have felt like ratification.
- **C**: Ask the team directly in the next retrospective who feels this way and what prompted it, to get to the root of the perception quickly.
- **D**: Accept the feedback at face value and commit to bringing every upcoming decision to the team before forming any view of their own.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Self-awareness is the willingness to hold a gap between intention and impact without rushing to defend the intention. (B) tolerates the sting and treats perception as real data, then investigates honestly — the necessary first move. (A) is defensiveness dressed as balance and will likely confirm the very perception it tries to dispel. (C) puts team members on the spot over an anonymous comment and can feel like a hunt for the author, suppressing future candour. (D) overcorrects out of guilt, abdicating the PM's legitimate judgement and creating decision paralysis; reflection must precede any change of approach.
