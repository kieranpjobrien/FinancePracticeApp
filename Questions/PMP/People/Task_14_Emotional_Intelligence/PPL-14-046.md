# Question

A PM receives anonymous team feedback that their meetings feel tense and that people are careful not to disagree with them. The PM feels blindsided — they believe they explicitly invite disagreement and are open to challenge. What should the PM do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Sit with the feedback before reacting, reflect on recent moments, and seek out one or two trusted colleagues to test their own read of the pattern.
- **B**: Address the feedback in the next team meeting by explicitly asking the group what they meant and inviting any examples they feel able to share openly.
- **C**: Dismiss the feedback on the basis that the PM's own experience of being open to challenge is directly contradicted by the anonymous claim in the survey.
- **D**: Book an external executive coach to work through the feedback rigorously, taking the issue out of the team context until the PM has made personal progress.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

PMI's self-awareness principle starts with pause and reflection — the gap between self-perception and team perception is itself the development signal. (B) asks the team to do the work of explaining a pattern the PM needs to sit with first. (C) rejects the feedback because it is uncomfortable. (D) outsources the reflection and delays any change the team will experience.
