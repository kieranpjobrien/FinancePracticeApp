# Question

On a predictive civil-engineering project, a highly capable structural engineer has begun doing only the minimum: meeting deadlines but no longer proposing the design improvements that once distinguished their work. In a casual conversation they remark, "It doesn't really matter what I suggest — it all gets value-engineered out anyway." The PM values this engineer's contribution and wants to re-engage them. Which action best reflects an emotionally intelligent response to what is driving the disengagement?

## Options

- **A**: Explore with the engineer which of their ideas could realistically influence outcomes, and create a visible route for those suggestions to reach decisions.
- **B**: Praise the engineer's past contributions and tell them how much the team relies on their expertise.
- **C**: Set the engineer a stretch goal on the next design package to rekindle their interest.
- **D**: Explain the commercial constraints that force value engineering so the engineer understands why ideas get cut.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The remark reveals that the engineer's disengagement is driven by a sense that their input has no impact — a loss of the intrinsic motivators of autonomy and meaning — so the emotionally intelligent response addresses that specific driver, which (A) does by restoring a real path from their ideas to decisions. (B) offers recognition but recognition does not cure the feeling of futility; being told you are valued while your ideas keep vanishing can ring hollow. (C) adds challenge, but a stretch goal on top of an unchanged "nothing I propose survives" dynamic does not touch the root and may add pressure. (D) is rational and may even be accurate, but justifying why their ideas get cut confirms rather than relieves the powerlessness the engineer is describing. The fine margin is that B, C, and D respond to the surface symptom or to logic, whereas (A) targets the unmet motivational need the engineer actually voiced.
