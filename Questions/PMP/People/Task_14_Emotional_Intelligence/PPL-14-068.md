# Question

A PM is presenting a recovery plan for a delayed aerospace components project to the team that will execute it. The plan is technically sound and clearly explained. As the PM speaks, several engineers nod and say it sounds fine, but they sit with arms folded, avoid eye contact, and raise no questions despite being invited to. One engineer offers a flat "sounds fine." Ten minutes of agenda remain. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Pause the presentation, name the hesitation the PM is picking up, and invite the team to voice any concerns or reservations.
- **B**: Continue and close out the plan as planned; the team has been invited to object and has verbally agreed, so the PM should take them at their word.
- **C**: Use the remaining time to confirm ownership and assign the recovery actions while the team is in agreement, locking in the timeline.
- **D**: Close the session on schedule and follow up one-to-one with the engineers who seemed least convinced to draw out their concerns.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Each option is a defensible facilitation choice, so the question turns on weighing the non-verbal signal against the verbal one rather than on spotting "read the room." Social awareness includes registering body language and tone that contradict the words; folded arms, avoided eye contact, and flat agreement signal unvoiced doubt, and (A) surfaces it in the moment, treating commitment — not mere compliance — as the goal, while there is still time and the whole team present. (B) honours the team's stated agreement and avoids reading too much into posture, but taking "sounds fine" at face value risks a plan no one has truly committed to. (C) is reasonable momentum, yet confirming ownership on top of hollow agreement hardens the appearance of buy-in while real concerns go underground and resurface as execution failures. (D) genuinely pursues the hidden concerns and is a sound follow-up, but deferring to private chats forgoes the chance to build trust openly now and lets the group's unspoken doubt set before it is addressed. The fine margin is that (A) engages the cues live and together, whereas D, though well-intentioned, delays and fragments the conversation the room is inviting.
