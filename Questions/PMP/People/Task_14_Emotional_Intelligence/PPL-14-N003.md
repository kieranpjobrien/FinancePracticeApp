# Question

At an agile retrospective for a logistics software team, the conversation stays superficial: people cite generic process tweaks but nobody raises the real reason the last two sprints slipped, which the PM suspects is friction between two team members. Everyone seems reluctant to go first. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Share an observation about their own contribution to a recent miss, modelling candour, then invite the team to explore what is genuinely getting in the way.
- **B**: Directly name the friction between the two members so the issue is on the table and the team can address it openly.
- **C**: Accept the surface-level items, capture them as actions, and follow up with the two members individually after the retrospective.
- **D**: Introduce an anonymous input method for the next retrospective so people can raise difficult issues without attribution.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Self-awareness and vulnerability from the leader is what unlocks psychological safety in a retrospective. (A) lowers the perceived risk of speaking up by going first and admitting fault, which is the most reliable way to deepen the discussion in the moment. (B) may be accurate but ambushing two people in front of the group can damage safety and put them on the defensive. (C) abandons the live opportunity and pushes a team issue into private channels. (D) is a reasonable future mechanism but does nothing for the current session and treats a trust problem as a tooling problem.
