# Question

A capable analyst on a market-research project has twice asked the project manager, in an upbeat tone, to "just take" a complex modelling task off them, saying they would rather focus on the survey work they enjoy. The PM senses the cheerful framing is masking that the analyst feels out of their depth and is quietly avoiding the stretch. The analyst is waiting for an answer. What is the most emotionally intelligent response?

## Options

- **A**: Take the task back as requested, since a motivated analyst doing work they are confident in will produce better results than a reluctant one.
- **B**: Agree to keep the analyst on the task but pair them with an experienced colleague, so the stretch remains without leaving them exposed.
- **C**: Name gently that the modelling looks daunting, ask what specifically feels out of reach, and shape support around the real gap before settling who owns the task.
- **D**: Encourage the analyst to keep the task and reassure them they are more than capable, pointing to the strong survey work they have just done.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The cue under test is the gap between the analyst's cheerful framing and the avoidance it is masking; empathy means responding to the unspoken feeling before transacting on the request. (C) reads past the upbeat tone, surfaces the real difficulty, and only then decides ownership and support — which is the move that both respects the person and protects their development. (D) is plausible and supportive but reassures without first understanding what is actually blocking them, so it can land as a brush-off that leaves the fear intact. (B) is a sensible support arrangement but commits to a solution before the PM knows what kind of help is needed, and may not fit the actual gap. (A) accepts the surface request at face value, removing the stretch and rewarding avoidance — the least attuned reading of the situation. The margin is fine because (B) and (D) are both genuine supportive responses; what makes (C) best is that it diagnoses before it acts.
