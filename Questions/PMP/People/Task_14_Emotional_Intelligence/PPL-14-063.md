# Question

On an agile analytics team, a normally energetic member has grown withdrawn over the last two sprints: shorter answers in stand-up, camera off, picking up fewer stories, no longer joking in the team channel. Their work is still correct but slower. The PM has heard of no change in the person's circumstances and is aware the person is fairly private. The PM wants to support them while keeping delivery on track. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Reach out privately, describe the change the PM has observed in neutral terms, and ask the person how they are doing.
- **B**: Give the person room for now and signal availability — a low-key "I'm around if you ever want to chat" — rather than initiating a conversation about it.
- **C**: Quietly reduce the person's story load this sprint to ease any pressure while their pace recovers.
- **D**: Ask a peer the person is close to whether they know what is going on, before approaching the person directly.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Each option reflects a value a thoughtful PM might weigh — directness, respect for privacy, practical relief, or discretion — so the choice turns on which best fits social awareness rather than on spotting "empathy." (A) names the observed behavioural shift without diagnosing its cause and opens a direct, private channel, which both gathers real information and signals the person is seen; that the person is private is a reason to approach gently and one-to-one, not a reason to avoid it. (B) respects privacy but, faced with a two-sprint decline and slipping engagement, passivity can read as indifference and leaves the person to struggle alone. (C) acts on an unverified assumption that workload is the issue and may land as a demotion, worsening how the person feels. (D) routes through a third party, risking the person's confidence and making the situation feel discussed behind their back. The fine margin is that (A) obtains the very information the other options presume, while demonstrating direct, respectful care.
