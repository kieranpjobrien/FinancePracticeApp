# Question

During sprint planning for a fintech payments platform, a senior engineer interrupts twice within five minutes to dispute the story-point estimates, cutting across other team members as they speak. The project manager notices their own jaw tightening. The rest of the team has gone quiet and is watching how the PM reacts. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Recognise internally that the reaction is to the interruptions rather than the engineer's argument, then invite the engineer to set out their reasoning so the team can weigh it together.
- **B**: Reaffirm that estimates are the team's collective output and ask the engineer to bring detailed objections to the product owner after the session.
- **C**: Defer to the engineer's seniority, accept the points raised, and revise the estimates so the session can proceed without further friction.
- **D**: Call a short break to let the tension in the room settle before resuming the planning conversation.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The emotionally intelligent move pairs self-awareness (separating the trigger, the interruptions, from the content of the argument) with self-regulation, then redirects to the substance so estimating stays a team activity. (A) does both and keeps the engineer engaged rather than shut out. (B) is a reasonable boundary on the interruptions but pushes legitimate estimating input to a later forum and can read as avoidance of the friction in the room. (C) is conflict-avoidant appeasement that lets seniority override team consensus. (D) manages the room but sidesteps the actual disagreement and can feel like the PM is buying time rather than addressing it. The fine margin is between protecting the team's process by deferring the engineer (B) and channelling the disruption back into shared evaluation now (A); the latter both regulates the PM's reaction and keeps the estimate a team decision.
