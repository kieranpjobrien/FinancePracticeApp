# Question

A project manager has been visibly enthusiastic about a new agile way of working they championed. Three sprints in, the team is grumbling, metrics have dipped during the learning curve, and a peer manager pointedly asks whether the change was "worth the disruption." The PM privately feels their confidence wobble. What is the most emotionally intelligent response?

## Options

- **A**: Reaffirm strongly to the team and the peer that the new approach is the right one and that the dip is a normal part of any transition.
- **B**: Pause the rollout and revert to the previous way of working until the team is more comfortable, then reintroduce the change later.
- **C**: Survey the team on whether to continue with the new approach and let the majority view decide the path forward.
- **D**: Separate the temporary dip from the change's underlying merit, openly acknowledge the team's pain points, and recommit to the adoption while adjusting where it is genuinely struggling.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Self-motivation and resilience mean sustaining commitment through the predictable trough of a change without ignoring real difficulty. (D) holds the conviction while staying honest about the pain and willing to adapt — managing both the PM's own wobble and the team's experience. (A) defends with forced certainty and dismisses the grumbling, which erodes trust. (B) abandons the change at the first sign of the expected dip, the classic failure that prevents any improvement from bedding in. (C) outsources a leadership judgement to a vote taken at the lowest point of morale, when the team is least placed to assess it fairly.
