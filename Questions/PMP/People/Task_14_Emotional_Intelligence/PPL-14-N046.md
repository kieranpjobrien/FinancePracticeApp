# Question

On an agile product team, two senior developers who used to drive technical discussions have gone quiet and now do only assigned work. Velocity is stable, but proactive problem-solving has faded. The change roughly coincides with a run of sprints in which several of the team's technical recommendations went the other way, and the product owner and a tight deadline were both in play. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Recognise the developers' past contributions at the next review to rebuild their sense of value and re-energise them.
- **B**: Explore with the developers what has changed for them, listening for the root cause behind the withdrawal before acting.
- **C**: Raise with the product owner that overriding the team's technical recommendations may be demotivating the engineers, and ask them to explain their rationale in future.
- **D**: Introduce a technical spike in the next sprint to give the senior developers space to shape direction again.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Emotionally intelligent leadership diagnoses before it prescribes, and the scenario offers several plausible drivers, decisions going against the team, the product owner, deadline pressure, without confirming which one is biting. (B) goes to the affected people to surface the actual cause before acting, which is what makes any subsequent fix land on the real problem. (C) is a reasonable move if the product owner is in fact the cause, but acting on the most salient guess risks confronting the wrong factor and misframing the conversation. (A) and (D) are plausible remedies, yet applying a fix before understanding the cause treats a symptom and may miss the point entirely. The fine margin is that all three alternatives are sound responses to a cause not yet established, whereas B establishes it first.
