# Question

In a heated change-control meeting, a functional manager accuses the project manager of incompetence in front of peers. The PM feels the accusation is unfair and their pulse spikes. The decision on the table still needs to be made in this meeting. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Calmly acknowledge the manager's evident frustration, decline to engage the personal attack, and steer the group back to the change decision that must be made.
- **B**: Respond firmly to the accusation point by point so peers do not take the manager's claim at face value.
- **C**: Let the remark pass without any response and simply proceed to the next agenda item.
- **D**: Suggest the meeting be adjourned so tempers can cool before the change decision is taken.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Self-regulation here means neither retaliating nor freezing, but de-escalating and protecting the meeting's purpose. (A) keeps composure, names the emotion behind the attack without being drawn into it, and refocuses on the decision, which preserves the PM's standing and the meeting's productivity. (B) takes the bait and risks a public quarrel that overshadows the change decision. (C) ignores a charged dynamic that may fester and can read as the PM having no answer. (D) cedes control of a decision that the scenario says must be made now and rewards the disruptive behaviour.
