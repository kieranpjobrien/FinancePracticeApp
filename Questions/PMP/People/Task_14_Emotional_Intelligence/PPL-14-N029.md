# Question

Midway through a tense sprint review with the client present, a Scrum team's lead engineer makes a barbed remark implying the project manager hid a slipped commitment from the client. The PM knows the slip was openly flagged in the last status report and feels a sharp jab of being unjustly blamed in front of the customer. The client is watching the exchange closely, and the engineer is clearly stressed about the missed item. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Calmly correct the record on the spot, noting the slip was reported in writing last week, so the client is not left with a false impression of concealment.
- **B**: Let the remark pass in the room, steady themselves, and raise it privately with the engineer afterwards to understand the stress behind it and reset expectations.
- **C**: Acknowledge to the client that communication on the slip could have been clearer and move the review forward, absorbing the comment to protect the team's image.
- **D**: Briefly and evenly note that the item was flagged in last week's report, redirect the review to the recovery plan, and follow up with the engineer one-on-one once the session ends.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

This tests holding self-regulation, social awareness, and the PM's accountability in front of a client all at once, when several emotionally intelligent-looking moves compete. (D) regulates the jab, protects the factual record with a brief evidence-based note (not a defensive rebuttal), keeps the meeting productive, and reserves the relationship repair with the stressed engineer for private follow-up — the right ordering of all three concerns. (A) gets the facts right but litigating it in the room, however calmly, turns the review into a public dispute and corners the engineer. (B) protects the relationship but lets an unfair implication of concealment stand uncorrected with the client present, which the PM's accountability does not allow. (C) over-absorbs by half-accepting blame for something done correctly, which misleads the client and quietly endorses the accusation; the slip was reported, so conceding a communication failure is dishonest.
