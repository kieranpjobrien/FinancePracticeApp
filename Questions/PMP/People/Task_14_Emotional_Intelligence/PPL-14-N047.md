# Question

A project manager opens an email from a key vendor stating that a critical component will slip by six weeks, jeopardising a regulatory deadline. The deadline is externally fixed, the sponsor expects to be told promptly of anything that threatens it, and the PM will depend on this same vendor to execute any recovery. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Reply to the vendor straight away to make clear the regulatory deadline is fixed and to ask for a recovery plan, so the clock on recovery options starts now.
- **B**: Take a short pause to steady the reaction, confirm the actual facts and likely impact, then decide how and to whom to communicate.
- **C**: Brief the sponsor immediately with the email, since they expect prompt warning of threats to the deadline and may have levers the PM does not.
- **D**: Convene the project team without delay to scope recovery options while the situation is fresh and people can react fast.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

All four are plausible, and three of them are reasonable arguments for acting fast, the deadline is fixed, the sponsor wants early warning, recovery options shrink with time. The discriminator is that each fast move is better made on verified facts than on a first read of one email. (B) creates a small gap so judgement rather than adrenaline drives the next step, then grounds the communication in confirmed impact, which makes whichever follow-up action is right land better. (A) may be necessary soon, but a reply fired off before the facts are checked can strain the very vendor the PM now depends on and may overstate or understate the situation. (C) is appropriate once the PM knows what they are escalating, but passing raw, unanalysed bad news upward can trigger a disproportionate response. (D) mobilises people before the problem is scoped, risking a frantic, unfocused session. The fine margin is that A, C, and D are all sound second steps that a brief verification in B makes effective rather than premature.
