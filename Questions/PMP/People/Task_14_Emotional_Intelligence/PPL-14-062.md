# Question

During a sprint retrospective for a fintech product, a senior developer states that the Scrum Master "hid the dependency risk from the team," which is why the release slipped. The Scrum Master has the email trail showing the dependency was flagged twice and knows the claim is wrong. Two newer team members appear to be taking the accusation at face value. The Scrum Master notices a strong urge to set the record straight on the spot. What should the Scrum Master do?

## Options

- **A**: Acknowledge the developer's frustration, defer the factual rebuttal, and steer the team into examining how the dependency was tracked and surfaced.
- **B**: State plainly that the risk was flagged twice and offer to share the email trail, so the newer members are not left with a false account.
- **C**: Note that there are differing recollections, propose moving on with the retrospective, and take the specific disagreement up with the developer afterwards.
- **D**: Ask the team to help reconstruct the timeline of when the dependency was first raised and by whom, working it through together now.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The discriminator is self-regulation in service of the session's purpose, and each option is a defensible reading of how to handle it, so principle-spotting alone will not separate them. (A) regulates the defensive impulse, validates the emotion under the outburst, and redirects to the systemic question the retrospective exists to answer; the facts can be corrected later when emotions have cooled. (B) is factually justified and protects the record, but rebutting from a defensive footing in the moment reframes the retrospective as Scrum-Master-versus-developer and teaches the team that challenging the SM triggers a fight, which suppresses future candour. (C) keeps the peace but leaves an unaddressed accusation hanging in front of the newer members and converts a process-learning opportunity into a private dispute. (D) sounds collaborative, yet reconstructing the timeline live to settle who was at fault still centres the session on blame rather than on improving how dependencies are tracked. The fine margin is that the misperception can be corrected later, whereas a reactive defence in the moment does lasting damage to psychological safety.
