# Question

A project manager has just taken over a struggling agile team where the previous PM made promises that were not kept, and trust in the role is low. Two developers are openly guarded and a third quietly does the work but shares nothing in ceremonies. The PM wants to rebuild the kind of trusting relationships that let the team perform again, and has limited credibility to spend. Over the first few sprints, which approach will most durably strengthen these relationships?

## Options

- **A**: Make a small number of visible commitments to the team and meet every one of them, while showing consistent, genuine interest in each person.
- **B**: Run individual listening sessions early to surface each person's frustrations and signal that their concerns are heard.
- **C**: Bring the team together to co-create new working agreements so everyone shapes how the team will operate going forward.
- **D**: Quickly remove two long-standing impediments the team has complained about, demonstrating that the new PM gets results.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four are sound relationship and team moves, so the discriminator is what rebuilds trust most durably with a team that was burned by broken promises. (A) attacks the exact wound, unreliability, by pairing kept commitments with authentic interest; trust is rebuilt through repeated follow-through over time, which is the core of relationship management. (B) is valuable empathy and a good early step, but listening without subsequent reliable action repeats the prior pattern of words unbacked by deeds. (C) is a strong participatory practice, yet agreements the PM then fails to honour would deepen the cynicism; the agreements are only as good as the follow-through underneath them. (D) builds quick credibility through competence, but trust founded mainly on results is fragile and addresses capability more than the relationship itself. The fine margin is that consistent, demonstrated follow-through (A) is what converts the others into lasting trust rather than another round of unmet expectations.
