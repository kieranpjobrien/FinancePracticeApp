# Question

A project manager realises during a stressful product launch that they have snapped at two team members in as many days and that their tolerance for normal questions has dropped sharply. They are sleeping badly and aware their fuse is short. They want to manage this with emotional intelligence rather than let it harm the team. Which actions are appropriate? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Acknowledge to themselves the state they are in and consciously slow their responses, building in a brief pause before reacting to interruptions.
- **B**: Where they snapped unfairly, acknowledge it to those team members so the relationship is repaired and the behaviour is not normalised.
- **C**: Look at what is driving the overload and remove or delegate something to reduce the pressure feeding the irritability.
- **D**: Warn the team that they are under strain so people know to keep their distance and minimise questions until the launch is over.
- **E**: Push through until the launch is done, since the pressure is temporary and addressing it now would be a distraction.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Managing one's own emotional state well combines self-awareness (A), the humility to repair the harm done (B), and tackling the root cause rather than only the symptom (C). (D) offloads the PM's regulation onto the team by asking them to walk on eggshells, which damages psychological safety and openness. (E) ignores a self-awareness signal and lets the snapping continue, normalising harm to the team during the very period they most need support. The three correct options address the PM's state, the relationships, and the cause together.
