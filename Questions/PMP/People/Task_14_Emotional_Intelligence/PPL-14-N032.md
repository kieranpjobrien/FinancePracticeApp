# Question

During a sprint, a normally strong developer's velocity has dropped and they have introduced two avoidable bugs. In their one-on-one they seem tired and say only that they are "fine, just busy." The project manager senses there is more but also has a genuine delivery concern to raise. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Reflect back gently that they have seemed stretched lately and that it is fine to say so, creating space before turning to the recent issues.
- **B**: Address the velocity drop and the bugs directly but supportively, since avoiding the performance point would be doing the developer a disservice.
- **C**: Accept the "fine, just busy" response at face value and offer to help reprioritise their workload to ease the pressure.
- **D**: Hold off on the conversation entirely and revisit it next sprint if the pattern continues, to avoid pressing on something personal.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Empathy here means responding to the cue that "fine" is masking something, and opening a door rather than forcing it or ignoring it. (A) signals safety and invites disclosure first; if the developer opens up, the performance conversation can then be had with full context. (B) leads with the deliverable concern before understanding the cause, which may be exactly the wrong move if something serious is going on. (C) takes the deflection at face value and jumps to a fix that may miss the real issue. (D) avoids the conversation altogether, neglecting both the person and a legitimate quality concern. Acknowledging the emotional cue first does not mean abandoning the performance issue — it sequences it correctly.
