# Question

In a Scrum retrospective, a developer becomes visibly upset and starts blaming themselves for the team missing a sprint goal, despite multiple contributing factors including unclear requirements and a mid-sprint priority change. What should the Scrum Master do?

## Options

- **A**: Move the discussion forward to the next retrospective topic to spare the developer further public discomfort.
- **B**: Acknowledge the developer's feelings, then guide the team to look at contributing factors as a system so ownership stays collective.
- **C**: Directly tell the developer that the missed goal was not their fault and that they should not take it personally.
- **D**: Pause the retrospective and ask the developer to step out and continue the conversation one-on-one with the Scrum Master.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A retrospective is a learning event, and the Scrum Master's job is to keep it psychologically safe while still surfacing the system-level issues. (B) validates the emotion and reframes toward team ownership and continuous improvement — both core agile values. (A) avoids the moment and signals that emotion is not welcome. (C) contradicts the developer's feelings rather than acknowledging them, which rarely lands well in the moment. (D) removes the developer from a learning conversation they should be part of and skips the team's collective reflection.
