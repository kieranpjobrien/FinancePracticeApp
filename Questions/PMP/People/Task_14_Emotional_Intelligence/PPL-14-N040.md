# Question

During a critical go-live for a national payroll system, a serious data error surfaces an hour before cutover. A normally composed team lead becomes visibly panicked, voice rising, insisting the whole release be aborted. The project manager feels their own adrenaline spike but knows the team is watching how they react. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Make an immediate decision to abort the cutover, since a serious data error so close to go-live is too risky to proceed with.
- **B**: Tell the team lead to calm down and stop alarming the others, then take direct control of the situation themselves.
- **C**: Steady their own tone and body language first, calmly acknowledge the seriousness, and guide the lead and team to assess the scope of the error before deciding on cutover.
- **D**: Direct the team to push ahead with the cutover on schedule while the data error is investigated in parallel after go-live.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

In a crisis the leader's regulated state is contagious and is the precondition for clear collective thinking. (C) manages the PM's own arousal first, steadies the panicked lead by acknowledging rather than dismissing the fear, and moves to fact-based assessment before the cutover call — emotional intelligence and sound process together. (A) makes an irreversible decision under adrenaline before the error's scope is even known. (B) shames the lead publicly, which heightens panic and damages the relationship, the opposite of regulating the room. (D) presses ahead with a known serious data error in a payroll system, accepting unquantified risk for the sake of the schedule.
