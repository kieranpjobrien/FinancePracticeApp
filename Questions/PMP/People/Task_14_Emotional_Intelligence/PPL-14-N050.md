# Question

Coming out of a gruelling delivery phase, a project manager senses morale has dipped, though no one has said so. The PM cares about reading the team accurately before acting, but a demanding next phase starts in a few days and a misread now would be costly. The team is co-located and the PM sees them daily. How should the PM best gauge how the team is genuinely feeling?

## Options

- **A**: Over the next couple of days, attend closely to interactions and create low-pressure openings in normal conversation for people to say how they are doing.
- **B**: Hold short individual check-ins with each team member to hear directly and privately how the last phase landed for them.
- **C**: Raise it openly with the team, naming that the phase was hard and asking how everyone is holding up before the next push.
- **D**: Send a brief anonymous pulse survey so people can be candid without the PM's presence shaping their answers.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

All four are reasonable ways to read a team's emotional state, so the choice turns on what gives an accurate read fastest given a co-located team and a tight clock. (B) goes directly to each person in a private setting where individual signals surface most honestly, and it scales fine for a co-located team over a couple of days, which is why it is the most reliable read before a costly next phase. (A) is genuinely emotionally intelligent but passive; ambient observation may not surface much before the next phase begins. (C) invites honesty but a tired, guarded team often under-shares in a group, and individuals may mask how they really feel. (D) protects candour but loses the tone, nuance, and follow-up that direct contact provides, and treats a relational read as a data exercise. The fine margin is direct individual contact (B) over slower observation (A) or lower-fidelity channels (C, D).
