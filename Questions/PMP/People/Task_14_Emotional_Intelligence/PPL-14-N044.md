# Question

After a tense retrospective for an e-commerce platform, a respected team member tells the project manager privately that the PM came across as dismissive when ideas were raised. The PM is genuinely surprised; they felt they were keeping a meandering session on time. The PM has already heard the feedback out, thanked the person, and acknowledged the impact may have differed from the intent. The next retrospective is in two weeks. What should the PM do next?

## Options

- **A**: Ask the team member for a specific moment in the retro that felt dismissive, so the PM can see the behaviour through the team's eyes before changing anything.
- **B**: Privately review their own recollection of the retro against the feedback to identify which of their facilitation habits may have created that impression.
- **C**: Open the next retrospective by inviting the team to set a working agreement on how everyone, including the PM, signals when discussion needs to wrap up.
- **D**: Ask a peer to observe the next retrospective and give the PM candid feedback on how their facilitation actually lands with the team.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four are constructive, ego-free responses; the question is sequencing. The PM has acknowledged the impact but still cannot picture what they did, so the immediate next step is to get concrete data from the person who is standing right in front of them and willing to talk. (A) converts a vague impression into a specific, observable behaviour while the example is fresh and the source is present, which is what makes any later change accurate. (B) is useful reflection but risks reconstructing the event through the PM's own blind spot, which is precisely what the feedback exposed. (C) is a sound systemic fix but jumps to a solution before the PM understands the specific behaviour to address. (D) gathers good outside data but defers learning two weeks and adds an observer to fix something the PM could first understand from the willing colleague in front of them.
