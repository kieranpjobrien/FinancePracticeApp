# Question

A project manager prides themselves on quick thinking and tends to finish team members' sentences and jump in with solutions during refinement. A new team member, in a one-on-one, hesitantly says they sometimes find it hard to get a full thought out in meetings. The PM had not seen themselves this way. What is the most emotionally intelligent response?

## Options

- **A**: Explain that they finish sentences out of enthusiasm and a desire to move efficiently, so the team member understands it is not dismissiveness.
- **B**: Ask the rest of the team whether they share this experience, to gauge whether it is a genuine pattern or a one-off perception.
- **C**: Thank them genuinely for the courage to raise it, reflect that they may indeed jump in too quickly, and commit to creating more space — then watch their own behaviour in the next sessions.
- **D**: Apologise and resolve to stay silent in refinement from now on, letting the team lead the discussions entirely.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Self-awareness is acting on the gap between self-image and others' experience, especially when it comes from a junior voice that took courage to speak. (C) receives the feedback well, owns the likely pattern, commits to change, and adds self-monitoring — the complete emotionally intelligent response. (A) explains away the behaviour, which, however well-intentioned, dismisses the impact and discourages future candour. (D) overcorrects into silence, abandoning the PM's legitimate contribution rather than moderating it. (B) risks invalidating a vulnerable individual's experience by putting it to a group vote before even acknowledging it.
