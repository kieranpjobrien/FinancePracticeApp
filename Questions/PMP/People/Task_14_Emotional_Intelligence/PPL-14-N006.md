# Question

In a steering committee meeting for an aerospace tooling programme, a director unfairly blames the PM's team for a delay that was actually caused by a late vendor decision the director themselves made. The PM feels heat rising and the impulse to correct the record sharply in front of the executives. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Pause, keep their tone level, and present the documented timeline of decisions and dependencies so the cause is clear without accusation.
- **B**: Let the comment pass in the meeting to protect the relationship, then send a private note to the director with the correct sequence of events afterward.
- **C**: Respond firmly that the delay was caused by the vendor decision the director made, so the committee has accurate information.
- **D**: Acknowledge the team could have escalated the vendor risk sooner and commit to a recovery plan, leaving the cause aside.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Self-regulation under provocation, combined with a factual, non-blaming correction, protects both the truth and the relationship. (A) controls the emotional reaction and lets evidence speak, allowing the director to save face while the committee sees reality. (C) is accurate but the direct attribution in front of executives is likely to trigger defensiveness and damage the relationship unnecessarily. (B) leaves a false impression standing with the people who matter most and only corrects it where it has least effect. (D) accepts blame that is not the team's to absorb and obscures a genuine cause, which can distort future decisions.
