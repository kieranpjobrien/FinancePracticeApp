# Question

In a monthly governance review for a defence-sector programme, a senior stakeholder dismisses the project manager's carefully prepared risk analysis as "scaremongering" and moves to close the item. The PM is certain the risk is real and feels both embarrassed and indignant. The committee chair looks ready to move on. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Acknowledge that the analysis may have been pitched too strongly, and offer to bring a revised, more measured version to the next review so the committee can consider it without the present friction.
- **B**: Calmly walk the committee through the analysis so they can see the methodology is sound and judge the risk on its merits rather than on the stakeholder's characterisation of it.
- **C**: Defer to the chair's evident wish to move on, accept that the item is closed for today, and approach the dissenting stakeholder privately afterwards to understand the objection before deciding whether to re-table it.
- **D**: Steady themselves, briefly state the single most material consequence if the risk goes unaddressed, and ask that both the risk and the committee's decision on it be minuted.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Self-regulation under public challenge means containing the embarrassment and indignation, then acting on professional duty without escalating the conflict. (D) regulates, makes the consequence concrete and brief, and ensures the decision is recorded — discharging the PM's accountability without a fight. (A) reads as a constructive offer but quietly concedes the framing that the risk is overstated and defers a live, time-sensitive concern to suit the room rather than the facts. (B) is reasonable and evidence-led, but re-presenting the full analysis after it has just been rejected spends the committee's patience re-litigating method when the need is to register one decisive consequence and force a conscious decision. (C) protects the relationship and the chair's authority but lets a real risk lapse unrecorded on the strength of one stakeholder's reaction; the private follow-up is a sound later step, not a substitute for getting the decision minuted now. The margin is fine because (B) and (C) are both composed, defensible moves; (D) is best because it secures the PM's accountability in the moment without escalating.
