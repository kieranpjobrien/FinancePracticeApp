# Question

A project manager on a large pharmaceutical packaging programme has clashed repeatedly with one vendor's representative in steering meetings: exchanges between them grow heated, the PM talks over the representative, and two other attendees have privately asked the PM whether the meetings can be run more smoothly. The next steering meeting is in three days and the same representative will attend. The PM wants the meeting to run productively. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Reflect on what specifically about this representative provokes the PM's reaction, so the PM can recognise and check it before the next meeting.
- **B**: Speak with the representative privately beforehand to clear the air and agree how they will handle disagreements in the room.
- **C**: Restructure the meeting so the representative's items are chaired by a co-facilitator, removing the direct exchange.
- **D**: Set explicit ground rules for the steering meeting on interruptions and turn-taking, applying to everyone present.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four options are legitimate moves a competent PM might make, which is what makes the sequencing the discriminator. The visible problem is a two-way clash, but the only element the PM fully controls is their own reaction, and emotional intelligence begins with self-awareness: until the PM understands what triggers them and can regulate it, the other actions are built on an unmanaged variable. (A) does that internal work first. (B) is a sound relational step, but going into a clear-the-air conversation while still reactive risks reproducing the same dynamic one-to-one; it also presumes the representative is half the problem before the PM has examined their own contribution. (C) solves the symptom structurally but removes the PM from a situation they need to be able to handle and signals to the room that the PM could not manage the exchange. (D) is reasonable facilitation, yet ground rules will not stop the PM's own interruptions if the underlying reaction is unaddressed. The fine margin is that B, C, and D are all outward actions that work better once the self-awareness step in A has been taken.
