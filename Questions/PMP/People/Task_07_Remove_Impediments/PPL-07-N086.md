# Question

A newly formed agile gaming team raises in its daily scrum that a flaky continuous-integration pipeline keeps failing builds for unclear reasons, costing them an hour or more each day. One developer says she has a hunch about the cause and offers to dig into it. The Scrum Master has fixed a similar pipeline before and could likely resolve it within the day. The team has not tackled an infrastructure problem together before. What should the Scrum Master do?

## Options

- **A**: Resolve the pipeline personally, drawing on the previous experience to clear the daily cost quickly so the team can stay focused on its sprint commitments.
- **B**: Let the developer lead the investigation with time set aside for it, clear any external obstacles the team hits, and step in only if they stall.
- **C**: Log the flaky pipeline as an impediment and bring it to the retrospective so the team can plan a fix in the next sprint.
- **D**: Escalate the unstable pipeline to the platform-engineering group and ask them to take over diagnosing and fixing it.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A volunteer has stepped forward and the problem is within the team's reach, so the servant-leader builds the new team's capability by enabling them to solve it — making space for the investigation and clearing external blockers — rather than solving it for them (B). (A) is tempting because the Scrum Master is faster in the short term, but taking over denies a new team the chance to build its own troubleshooting capability when a member has volunteered, and the speed gain is one-off. (C) deferring a daily-cost blocker to the next sprint ignores that the team can act now. (D) outsourcing to platform engineering removes the learning opportunity and adds hand-off latency for a problem the team can likely solve. The margin is between the faster personal fix (A) and the slower investment in the team's self-sufficiency (B).
