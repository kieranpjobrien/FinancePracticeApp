# Question

During the daily scrum on a retail e-commerce team, a developer says she has been blocked for two days because her staging-database access request is sitting unactioned in the platform team's queue. A second developer notes he hit the identical block last month and it cleared only after someone chased the platform team's lead directly. The access governs work several others will pick up later this sprint. As Scrum Master, what should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Ask the two developers to sort it out between them, since one of them knows how it was cleared last time, and confirm tomorrow that her access is live.
- **B**: Take the chase as your own action, contact the platform team's lead now to clear her request, and raise that recurring access requests keep stalling in the queue.
- **C**: Escalate the recurrence to the platform team's manager, presenting both instances and asking for a faster standing route for access requests so the queue stops blocking the team.
- **D**: Reorder her work to unblocked stories for now and submit the access request fresh through the standard channel with a tracked follow-up date.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The same access has now blocked the team twice through the same queue, so the Scrum Master's job is to clear it now and surface the pattern — taking ownership of the chase and using the lead who is known to act, while flagging that the queue keeps stalling (B). (A) delegating the chase to a teammate offloads a servant-leader duty onto a developer and leans on a personal relationship that may not hold a second time. (C) escalating to the manager for a standing fix is the right move for the recurring pattern, but on its own it is a slower, structural play that does not get this developer working today — it is better paired with, or following, the immediate unblock. (D) reordering her work plus re-submitting concedes her current story and routes the request back through the same channel that already failed once. The decisive action clears the live blocker and names the recurrence; the structural fix in (C) is the next step, not the first.
