# Question

During planning for a predictive bridge-instrumentation rollout, a senior engineer mentions that the specialist calibration contractor the project will need in month four tends to get booked up well ahead. Nothing is blocked now, and the engineer notes there are one or two other firms that could do the work at a pinch. How should the project manager best handle this?

## Options

- **A**: Engage procurement now to secure a booking with the preferred contractor for the month-four window.
- **B**: Line up one of the alternative firms now as a standing contingency so a contractor is available whichever way it goes.
- **C**: Add the contractor availability to the risk register and set a review point a month out, before the booking window would typically close.
- **D**: Raise it with the sponsor now so budget can be pre-approved for premium or expedited scheduling if needed.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

This is a foreseeable resource constraint, and the most effective preventive action is to go after the primary resource itself — engage procurement now to book the preferred contractor for month four (A), removing the obstacle before it can form. (B) securing an alternative is reasonable contingency, but arranging a fallback before you have even tried to lock in the first choice is back-to-front; the alternative is what you reach for if the primary cannot be secured. (C) logging the risk and scheduling a review keeps it on the radar but defers the very action that would resolve it, leaving the booking exposed if the window closes between now and the review. (D) pre-clearing budget for premium scheduling jumps to a cost remedy before availability has even been pursued. The discrimination is between acting on the primary resource now (A) and the weaker options of contingency-first (B), watch-and-review (C), or spend-first (D).
