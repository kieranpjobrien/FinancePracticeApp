# Question

Your agile team in a large bank is consistently slowed because every code release must pass a heavyweight change-advisory board (CAB) that meets weekly, regardless of risk. The team's flow is healthy right up to this gate, where releases queue for days. The CAB exists for legitimate regulatory reasons, is mandated organisation-wide, and is outside your authority to change. What is the most effective action?

## Options

- **A**: Gather data on the queue time and its delivery cost and take it to the CAB's process owner, proposing a risk-based fast-track lane for pre-defined low-risk changes that still meets the board's governance intent.
- **B**: Work with the team to bring CAB-readiness forward — completing the change documentation, testing evidence and approvals checklist before submission — so items clear the board on first pass instead of bouncing.
- **C**: Engage the CAB chair to secure a standing pre-approval for a defined class of routine, low-risk change types your team ships repeatedly, removing those from the per-release queue entirely.
- **D**: Raise the systemic flow impact at the next governance forum where the process owner and other affected delivery leads are present, building broader support for revisiting the CAB's handling of low-risk changes.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

This is a fine-margin question among four constructive responses, all of which a good PM might pursue and several of which combine well. The obstacle is a systemic process constraint, and the most effective lever is an evidence-based proposal to the person who actually owns the process for a risk-based fast-track that preserves governance — A pairs the right target (process owner), the right currency (data), and the right ask (a proportionate change). (B) is genuinely valuable and reduces rework, but if the *board itself* only convenes weekly, cleaner submissions still wait days for the gate, so it eases the symptom rather than the constraint. (C) is very close to correct and is arguably part of the same fix, but a blanket standing pre-approval is a bigger governance concession that the chair is unlikely to grant cold; a defined fast-track lane with criteria (A) is the more credible, more likely-to-succeed first proposal. (D) is a legitimate way to build coalition and may be a useful follow-up, but a broad forum is slower and more diffuse than going directly to the accountable process owner with a concrete, costed proposal. The distinguishing factor is targeting the process owner with evidence and a proportionate, governance-preserving change.
