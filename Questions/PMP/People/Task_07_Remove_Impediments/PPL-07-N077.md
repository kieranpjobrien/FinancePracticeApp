# Question

A project manager on a hybrid renewable-energy project is logging this week's impediments and wants to assign the most appropriate response to each. Match each impediment to the response that best fits its nature.

## Items

- **1**: A single supplier shipment is late, threatening installation work due in four days; the procurement contact owes the project manager a callback and just needs chasing.
- **2**: The same approval step has delayed five consecutive work packages, signalling a flaw in how the approval workflow is designed.
- **3**: A risk has been identified that a key inspection body may be unavailable next quarter, though nothing is blocked yet.
- **4**: A team is briefly held up by an unfamiliar configuration tool that another team member has used extensively and is free this afternoon.

## Matches

- **A**: Log it and act proactively now to secure availability before it becomes a live blocker.
- **B**: Facilitate a direct peer knowledge transfer between the two team members.
- **C**: Directly chase the item through the contact you can already influence to unblock the imminent work.
- **D**: Investigate and redesign the underlying process so the recurring delay stops at source.

## Answer

1-C, 2-D, 3-A, 4-B

## Explanation

Each impediment calls for a response matched to its nature, not its wording. The late shipment is an immediate single blocker needing a nudge through an existing contact, so direct chasing fits (1-C). The approval step that has delayed five packages is a recurring process defect, so the durable answer is to investigate and redesign the workflow (2-D). The inspection-body concern is a future risk with nothing blocked yet, so the right move is to log it and act proactively to secure availability (3-A). The configuration-tool hold-up is a knowledge gap closable peer-to-peer (4-B). The likely confusion is between 1 and 2 — treating the recurring approval delay as just another item to chase, when its repetition demands a process fix rather than another one-off expedite.
