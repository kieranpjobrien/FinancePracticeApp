# Question

A Scrum Master on an agile media-streaming team is presented with four blockers in a single sprint. Each could be handled in more than one way, but one response best fits each, depending on where the obstacle truly sits and who can act on it. Match each blocker to its best first response.

## Items

- **1**: A developer is idle because a build artefact is stuck in an internal approval queue; the Scrum Master knows the approver personally and the item simply needs a nudge to move.
- **2**: The team keeps tripping over a flaky integration-test suite that costs everyone time every sprint, but no individual owns it and the team has the skill to stabilise it themselves.
- **3**: A mandatory quarterly platform freeze imposed by enterprise IT blocks all deployments for the team's entire sprint, and no single team can lift it.
- **4**: A junior developer is stuck on an unfamiliar caching pattern that a senior teammate with free time this afternoon has implemented many times.

## Matches

- **A**: Make the impediment visible to the team and facilitate them taking ownership to fix it within their own capability.
- **B**: Personally expedite the item through the channel you can already influence.
- **C**: Gather the cross-team impact and escalate it to leadership as an organisational constraint to be addressed.
- **D**: Connect the two team members so the knowledge transfers directly, peer to peer.

## Answer

1-B, 2-A, 3-C, 4-D

## Explanation

The skill is matching each blocker to where the obstacle and authority actually lie, not to surface wording. The stuck approval merely needs a push through a relationship the Scrum Master already has, so personal expediting fits (1-B). The flaky test suite is unowned but within the team's own skill to fix, so the right move is to make it visible and let the team own it (2-A). The enterprise freeze is an organisational constraint beyond any single team, so only evidenced escalation to leadership is durable (3-C). The caching gap is a pure knowledge transfer best closed peer-to-peer (4-D). The tempting traps are swapping 2 and 3 — escalating the test suite the team could fix, or asking the team to "own" a freeze they have no authority to lift.
