# Question

A scrum master maintains a visible impediment log with an owner and status for each item. Reviewing it, they notice several impediments owned by people outside the team have sat in "in progress" for over two weeks with no movement, while the team can see they are stuck and has started to disengage from raising new ones — "nothing happens to them anyway." What is the best action?

## Options

- **A**: Personally take back ownership of the stalled external impediments and drive each one to resolution yourself, updating the team as progress is made.
- **B**: Add a "days open" and "last update" field to each impediment and agree that any item past an age threshold is actively chased and, if still stalled, escalated to someone who can move it.
- **C**: Review the stalled impediments with the team at the next standup, agree which are still genuinely blocking, and close or de-prioritise the ones that have become stale or overtaken by events.
- **D**: Raise the pattern of stalled external impediments at the next retrospective so the team can examine why items get stuck and agree how to handle ageing blockers going forward.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The log is already visible — so this is not a "use an impediment board" definition question; it is a judgement call about why visible items still stall and how to restore the team's faith that raising them matters. The core failure is that ownership without follow-through lets items rot, so building in ageing visibility plus an explicit chase-and-escalate trigger directly fixes the mechanism that is breaking (B). (A) will likely clear these specific items and is a reasonable instinct, but absorbing every stalled external impediment personally does not scale and treats the symptom for this batch rather than the recurring gap in follow-up. (C) is good hygiene and worth doing, but pruning stale items addresses housekeeping, not the reason live blockers go unworked — and risks closing things that are still real. (D) is a sound systemic conversation and a strong second step, but it defers action to a future ceremony while the team is disengaging now and items already sit two weeks cold. The decisive factor is closing the follow-through gap with a concrete ageing-and-escalation mechanism, not just tidying or deferring.
