# Question

On an insurance-claims agile team, three developers mention in the daily scrum that the shared build pipeline has been failing intermittently for two days, forcing them to re-run merges by hand and costing roughly an hour each per day. They have been quietly working around it. The pipeline is maintained by a central DevOps group that also serves several other teams. What should the Scrum Master do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Have the team script their own automated merge-retry workaround so they stop losing the hour while the underlying fault is dealt with separately.
- **B**: Gather the specific error logs and failure frequency from the developers and take that evidence to the DevOps group as a defined, owned impediment to be fixed.
- **C**: Raise the pipeline instability with the DevOps group's manager and your own delivery lead, so it is prioritised against the other teams competing for the same DevOps attention.
- **D**: Ask the DevOps group for read access so the team can investigate the intermittent failures themselves and propose the fix rather than waiting in their queue.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The failures are real but undiagnosed, and the proportionate first move is to convert the developers' anecdotes into concrete evidence — logs and frequency — and hand them to the group that owns the pipeline as a clearly defined impediment they can act on (B). (A) a merge-retry script removes the daily hour but masks the fault, lets the team absorb a problem that is also hurting others, and addresses the symptom rather than the source. (C) escalating to two managers spends authority before anyone has even quantified the issue or given DevOps a fair chance to fix it; escalation is the next lever if the evidenced request stalls, not the first. (D) self-investigating another group's infrastructure overreaches into their domain and risks cutting across whatever they may already be doing. The fine margin is between defining and routing the impediment to its owner with evidence (B) and either coping around it (A) or jumping straight to escalation (C).
