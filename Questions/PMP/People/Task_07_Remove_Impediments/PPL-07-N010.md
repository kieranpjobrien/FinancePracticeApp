# Question

On a solar-farm project, three separate work packages have each slipped this month, and in every case the team attributed it to "late delivery of materials." The procurement lead insists every order was placed on time and the records appear to support that. The same pattern looks set to hit two upcoming packages. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Review the supplier's delivery performance against the contracted lead times to confirm whether the delays are genuinely supplier-side before deciding how to respond.
- **B**: Convene the procurement lead, the site receiving team and the main supplier together to reconcile their records and jointly pinpoint where the breakdown is occurring across the handoffs.
- **C**: Add a standard lead-time buffer to the upcoming material orders now to protect the next two packages from the same slippage while the cause is worked.
- **D**: Map the end-to-end flow from requisition through approval, ordering, supplier dispatch and site receipt for the three slipped packages to locate where the time is actually being lost, then target the fix at that constraint.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

There is no throwaway here — each option is a reasonable analytical or protective step, which is what makes it a fine-margin call. The same symptom across three packages, with order records that look clean, signals a systemic cause somewhere in the chain that has not yet been isolated; mapping the full requisition-to-receipt flow is the most direct way to find *where* the time is lost without pre-judging the culprit (D). (B) is a strong collaborative step and may well form part of the investigation, but pulling the parties together is most productive once you have framed the flow and know which handoffs to interrogate — otherwise it risks becoming a record-disputing session. (C) sensibly protects the next packages, but buffering an unknown cause masks the problem and may simply move the slippage rather than remove it. (A) is a sound check, yet it narrows straight to the supplier when the records suggest ordering was on time — the breakdown may be internal (late approvals, spec errors, receiving delays), so examining only the supplier could miss the real constraint. The decisive move is the whole-flow investigation that finds the actual bottleneck before acting.
