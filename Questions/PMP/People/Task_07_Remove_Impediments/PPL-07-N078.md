# Question

On an agile games-development team, velocity has quietly halved over three sprints. At the daily scrum everyone reports "no blockers", but the Scrum Master senses hesitation and notices that two experienced developers have gone unusually quiet. In a one-to-one, one developer hints that people are afraid to admit they are stuck because a previous manager treated it as incompetence. What should the Scrum Master do?

## Options

- **A**: Remind the team at the next daily scrum that they must report all blockers promptly so impediments can be removed.
- **B**: Introduce an anonymous impediment-reporting channel so people can flag blockers without revealing who is stuck.
- **C**: Raise the dropping velocity with the team's manager to investigate why the team is underperforming.
- **D**: Work to rebuild psychological safety so team members feel able to raise blockers, then address the specific impediments that surface.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The real impediment is not any single blocker but a culture of fear that keeps blockers hidden, so the Scrum Master must first rebuild psychological safety so people feel safe to surface problems, then tackle the specific impediments that emerge (D) — you cannot remove blockers you cannot see. (A) instructing people to report blockers ignores why they are silent and may deepen the fear. (B) anonymous reporting treats the symptom and can entrench the very lack of safety that caused the problem, rather than restoring open dialogue. (C) escalating "underperformance" to a manager risks reinforcing exactly the blame dynamic the team is afraid of. The hard insight is recognising a hidden-impediment problem rooted in safety and addressing the cause (D) before chasing individual blockers.
