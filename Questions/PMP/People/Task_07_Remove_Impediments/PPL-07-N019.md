# Question

A scrum master faces three obstacles, each blocking a different team this sprint. Every response below is a legitimate impediment-removal action — match each obstacle to the response that fits the nature of *that* obstacle best.

## Items

- **1**: A one-off blocker the team cannot clear itself: a developer's access has lapsed and reinstating it depends on a function outside the team.
- **2**: A blocker the team is fully equipped to resolve, but two members have reached an impasse between two workable options and progress has stalled.
- **3**: A blocker that keeps returning sprint after sprint from the same upstream source, repeatedly costing the team its goal.

## Matches

- **A**: Negotiate a durable, standing arrangement with the source so the obstacle stops recurring, treating the pattern rather than the instance.
- **B**: Personally take it to the owning party and drive it to closure now, confirming the team is unblocked.
- **C**: Facilitate a short, time-boxed resolution and ensure a clear owner is empowered to decide so work resumes.

## Answer

1-B, 2-C, 3-A

## Explanation

The matching turns on the *nature* of each obstacle, and the responses are deliberately phrased without echoing the items' wording so the candidate must reason rather than keyword-match. A one-off blocker that sits outside the team's authority (1) is best removed by the scrum master driving it directly to closure with the owning party now (B) — fast, decisive, single-instance. An impasse the team is capable of resolving (2) is a *decision* impediment, not a capability gap, so the right move is to facilitate a time-boxed resolution with an empowered owner (C) rather than taking it over or escalating it. A blocker that recurs from the same source every sprint (3) is systemic, so chasing each instance is wasteful — the durable fix is a standing arrangement with the source that stops it recurring (A). Applying B to a recurring problem, or A to a one-off, would each be a mismatch of effort to obstacle type.
