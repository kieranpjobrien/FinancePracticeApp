# Question

On a hybrid pharmaceutical-manufacturing project, an iterative software team cannot release a batch-tracking feature until a separate plant-automation team certifies an interface. That team has missed two committed dates and now says it cannot give a new date for "a few weeks". The release is on the critical path for a regulatory submission. The project manager has already raised the dependency informally with the other team's lead twice. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Continue working the dependency directly with the other team's lead and offer to lend a developer to help them finish the certification.
- **B**: Escalate the blocked dependency and its regulatory consequence to the manager who governs both teams so it can be prioritised and given a firm date.
- **C**: Replan the submission around the delay and formally extend the regulatory milestone to reflect the other team's uncertainty.
- **D**: Document the missed commitments in the issue log and the lessons-learned register to inform future cross-team planning.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The project manager has already exhausted the peer channel — two informal raises and two missed dates — and the blocker now threatens a critical-path regulatory milestone, which is exactly when measured escalation to the authority over both teams is warranted (B). (A) lending a developer is goodwill but assumes capacity is the constraint when the other team has given no date and may have other causes. (C) extending the milestone surrenders to the delay before testing whether prioritisation can recover it, and regulatory dates are rarely the project manager's to move unilaterally. (D) logging the history is bookkeeping that does nothing to unblock the release. The margin is between escalating at the right moment (B) and either over-helping, conceding, or merely recording.
