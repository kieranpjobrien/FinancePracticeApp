# Question

A Scrum Master on a logistics-software team faces four impediments in one week. Each could plausibly be handled several ways, but one response best fits each situation given who holds the authority and where the real obstacle sits. Match each impediment to its best first response.

## Items

- **1**: A developer is blocked awaiting a vendor licence; the renewal is in the procurement team's queue, the Scrum Master has a working relationship with that team, and the only issue is that it has not yet been actioned.
- **2**: Two delivery teams have stalled for three days over which of two incompatible API designs to adopt; both leads have dug in and neither will concede, and the architecture owner has not been involved.
- **3**: A three-week security-review step delays one story every sprint across several teams; the security team confirms the step is mandatory policy and outside any single team's authority to waive.
- **4**: A developer is stuck integrating an unfamiliar message-queue library; a teammate two desks away rebuilt that exact integration last quarter and has spare capacity this afternoon.

## Matches

- **A**: Compile the cumulative cross-team cost and take it to leadership, proposing a risk-tiered process change at the policy level.
- **B**: Personally pursue the item to completion through the channel you can already influence.
- **C**: Convene the parties with the person who holds deciding authority so a binding choice is made.
- **D**: Connect the two individuals so the one with the knowledge transfers it directly.

## Answer

1-B, 2-C, 3-A, 4-D

## Explanation

The skill is reading each impediment's authority and source, not matching keywords. The licence sits in a queue the Scrum Master can already influence and merely needs pushing, so personal pursuit fits (1-B). The API deadlock is a genuine decision with no agreed authority in the room, so it needs the deciding authority convened, not escalation for a policy change (2-C). The security step is a mandatory policy harming several teams and beyond any team's authority, so only evidence-based escalation for a process change is durable (3-A). The library gap is a pure knowledge transfer best closed peer-to-peer (4-D). The tempting errors are swapping 2 and 3 — escalating the deadlock to leadership when an architecture owner can decide it, or merely convening the security parties when the constraint is policy that leadership must change.
