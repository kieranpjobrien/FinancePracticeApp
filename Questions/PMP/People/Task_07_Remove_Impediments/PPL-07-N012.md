# Question

A capable team lead on a hybrid pharma-validation project routes every obstacle to you, the project manager — even small ones the team could clearly resolve — and pauses work until you respond. You have become a decision bottleneck and the team's flow is suffering. In this regulated environment some decisions genuinely do need your sign-off, but most do not. What is the BEST way to address this?

## Options

- **A**: Work with the team lead to define explicit decision rights — which obstacles they own outright, which they decide and inform you of, and which truly require escalation — and coach them to act within those boundaries.
- **B**: Create a simple decision-rights matrix yourself covering the common obstacle types, publish it to the team, and ask them to follow it so escalations are limited to genuine impediments.
- **C**: Agree a short daily window where the team lead brings the day's open obstacles in one batch, so you can quickly clear the few that need you and confirm they can proceed on the rest.
- **D**: Acknowledge to the team lead that, given the regulated context, you would rather they over-escalate than miss something — but commit to fast turnaround so the bottleneck eases.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

This is a fine-margin call between four reasonable management responses; the issue is which one durably removes the dependency on the PM rather than re-routing or formalising it. The real impediment is unclear authority boundaries, so jointly defining decision rights *and coaching the lead to exercise them* both fixes the cause and builds the lead's confidence to act, which lasts beyond any single list (A). (B) is close and genuinely useful, but a matrix the PM writes and hands down is a tool without the coaching — the lead who defaults to escalating needs support to internalise and trust their authority, not just a document. (C) is a sensible interim measure that consolidates the disruption, but every obstacle still flows through the PM, so the underlying lack of empowerment is unchanged. (D) is defensible caution given the regulatory stakes and may be right for the genuinely sign-off-required subset, but applied across the board it entrenches the bottleneck and treats fast response as the fix rather than reallocating the decisions. The distinguishing factor is shifting real decision authority to the team with the coaching to use it, while still reserving what regulation truly requires.
