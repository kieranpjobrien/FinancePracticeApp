# Question

An agile team developing a healthcare-scheduling product is consistently blocked by a centralised change-advisory board that meets fortnightly and must approve every production release, so finished increments wait up to two weeks to ship. The board is an enterprise governance body serving many teams; the Scrum Master has confirmed the constraint is real and organisation-wide. Which TWO actions best address this organisational impediment? (Choose two.)

## Options

- **A**: Accept the fortnightly cadence and align the team's release planning to the board's schedule.
- **B**: Have the team release without board approval when increments are low risk, to keep value flowing.
- **C**: Quantify the delivery delay and lost value across affected teams and present it to leadership, proposing a lighter-weight approval path for low-risk releases.
- **D**: Ask each team member to lobby the board members they personally know to fast-track this team's releases.
- **E**: Engage the change-advisory board to explore a standard pre-approval or risk-tiered process for routine, low-risk changes from mature teams.

## Answer

C, E

## Explanation

This is a systemic governance impediment beyond any single team, so the durable responses work the system: build the cross-team evidence and take a proposal for a lighter low-risk path to leadership (C), and engage the board itself to design a risk-tiered or pre-approval route for routine changes (E). (A) simply aligning to the cadence accepts the constraint rather than addressing it and concedes the delay permanently. (B) releasing without approval bypasses mandatory governance and is a serious compliance breach, especially in healthcare. (D) ad-hoc lobbying via personal contacts is inconsistent, undermines the governance process, and does not fix the constraint for anyone. The right pair changes the process legitimately through evidence and collaboration.
