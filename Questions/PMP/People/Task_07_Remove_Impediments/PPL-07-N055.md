# Question

On a hybrid fintech programme, your agile team is blocked because a shared data-warehouse change must be made by an internal analytics team, but that team's manager has deprioritised your request in favour of a regulatory reporting deadline of their own. Both deadlines are real and both teams report to a common director. Your sprint goal fails without the change this week. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Take the competing priorities and their business impacts to the director who owns both teams, asking them to arbitrate the sequencing.
- **B**: Work directly with the analytics manager to find whether a smaller, faster slice of the change could be delivered this week without derailing their regulatory work.
- **C**: Replan the sprint to pull forward stories that do not depend on the warehouse change, and re-forecast the dependent work to when the analytics team can take it.
- **D**: Reframe your request to the analytics manager around the regulatory exposure your own delivery carries, to compete for priority on the merits rather than on queue position.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Two legitimate priorities are competing for one constrained team, and neither manager can fairly adjudicate the other's regulatory commitment, so the decision belongs with the director who owns both and can weigh relative business value (A). (B) negotiating a smaller slice is a constructive move and may be part of the answer, but if the manager has already deprioritised you, you may simply get the same answer at the manager level; the trade-off ultimately needs the shared owner. (C) replanning around the dependency is prudent mitigation, yet on its own it concedes the sprint goal and leaves the priority conflict unresolved and invisible to the person who should decide it. (D) arguing the merits to the manager is reasonable but still asks one manager to rank your regulatory need above their own — a conflict they cannot impartially resolve. The fine margin is between routing a genuine priority conflict to the authority who can settle it (A) and pursuing manager-level moves that may not, and that leave the decision unmade.
