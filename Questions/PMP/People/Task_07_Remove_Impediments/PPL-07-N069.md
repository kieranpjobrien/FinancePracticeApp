# Question

An agile team building a public-transport ticketing app has been blocked for a week because a government data-sharing agreement the product owner is negotiating has not been signed. The Scrum Master raised it with the product owner, who said it is "in hand" but gave no date and asked the Scrum Master not to escalate further to avoid appearing to apply pressure on the partner. The sprint goal is now at serious risk. What should the Scrum Master do?

## Options

- **A**: Respect the product owner's request, hold the escalation, and continue offering the team support while the agreement is pursued.
- **B**: Make the blocker's delivery impact transparent to the product owner with specifics, and agree a date by which, absent the signature, they will jointly escalate to the sponsor.
- **C**: Escalate directly to the sponsor now, since a week-long blocker to the sprint goal overrides the product owner's reluctance.
- **D**: Re-plan the sprint to remove the dependent work and treat the agreement as an external risk owned entirely by the product owner.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The Scrum Master must keep the impediment transparent and moving without trampling the product owner; the strongest move is to lay out the concrete delivery impact and agree a joint trigger — a date after which they escalate together if the agreement is still unsigned (B). This preserves the working relationship while ensuring the blocker does not drift indefinitely. (A) simply deferring to "in hand" with no date abandons the Scrum Master's duty to surface and drive impediments. (C) escalating unilaterally now overrides the product owner before a reasonable, time-boxed agreement has been reached and can damage trust and the partner relationship. (D) re-planning and disowning the dependency treats the symptom and lets a critical blocker fester unaddressed. The hard line is balancing transparency and urgency with respect for the product owner by setting a shared escalation trigger (B).
