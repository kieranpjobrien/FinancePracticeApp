# Question

A scrum team building a customer portal is repeatedly delayed because every new code library must pass through a central security review that takes three weeks, even for trivial additions. This blocks roughly one story per sprint. The security team is firm that the review is mandatory policy. As Scrum Master, what is the most effective way to address this impediment?

## Options

- **A**: Front-load library decisions during backlog refinement so reviews are submitted three weeks before the dependent work is pulled into a sprint.
- **B**: Engage the security team and their leadership to co-design a faster, risk-tiered review path for low-risk libraries, framing the current lead time as an organisational impediment to delivery.
- **C**: Escalate the cumulative delivery cost to your delivery lead and the security team's manager and ask them to set a service-level target for the review.
- **D**: Bring the recurring delay to the retrospective so the team can agree how to plan and sequence around the three-week lead time each sprint.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The lead time is mandatory policy that no single team can waive, so the most effective and durable fix is to work with the people who own that policy to change the process itself — co-designing a risk-tiered fast path that preserves the control but cuts the latency for low-risk additions (B). (A) front-loading reviews is a genuinely sound coping practice and may run in parallel, but it accepts the three-week latency as permanent and breaks down whenever a library need emerges mid-sprint. (C) escalating for a service-level target presses on lead time without redesigning the path, and a faster SLA on a one-size review may not be achievable — it is a blunter lever than co-designing tiers and is better used if collaboration stalls. (D) using the retrospective to plan around the delay manages the symptom and normalises the waste rather than removing it. The fine margin is between changing the process at its source (B) and the legitimate-but-lesser moves of coping (A, D) or pressing without redesign (C).
