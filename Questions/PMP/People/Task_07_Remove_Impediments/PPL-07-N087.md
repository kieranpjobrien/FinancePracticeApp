# Question

A project manager leading a hybrid public-transport ticketing programme encounters four blockers in a single week. Each could be handled several ways, but one response fits best given where the obstacle truly sits and who can act on it. Match each blocker to its most appropriate first response.

## Items

- **1**: A team member cannot proceed because a third-party payment-gateway sandbox account has sat unprovisioned in the vendor's onboarding queue for several days, with no error or further information from the vendor.
- **2**: An iterative team and the operations group have spent four days unable to agree whether a feature is permitted to deploy during the current change-freeze, and each reads the policy differently.
- **3**: A mandatory accessibility audit is adding three weeks to every release across all of the programme's teams, and the schedule pressure is mounting across the board.
- **4**: A developer has been stuck for two days on an obscure fare-calculation edge case that another part of the organisation has dealt with before.

## Matches

- **A**: Connect the developer with the person elsewhere who has already solved this so the knowledge transfers directly.
- **B**: Personally chase the item to completion through the vendor relationship you already hold.
- **C**: Convene the disputing parties together with whoever has authority over the policy so a binding ruling is made.
- **D**: Assemble the cumulative programme-wide cost and escalate to leadership, proposing a risk-tiered change to the process itself.

## Answer

1-B, 2-C, 3-D, 4-A

## Explanation

The judgement is reading each blocker's true location and the authority needed, not pattern-matching words. The sandbox sits in a vendor queue the project manager can influence through an existing relationship, so a direct chase clears it (1-B). The change-freeze deadlock is a live decision the parties cannot settle because neither holds the authority, so the policy owner must be brought in to rule (2-C). The accessibility audit is mandatory regulation harming the whole programme and beyond any team's power to change locally, so only evidence-based escalation for a process change is durable (3-D). The edge case is a pure knowledge gap best closed peer-to-peer (4-A). The tempting errors are swapping 2 and 3 — escalating a decidable dispute to leadership, or merely convening people about a regulatory constraint that only leadership can change at the policy level.
