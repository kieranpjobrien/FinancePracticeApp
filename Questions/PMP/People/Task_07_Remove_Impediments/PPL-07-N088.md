# Question

On a large predictive defence-logistics programme, technical decisions stall for one to two weeks because every design choice must wait for a monthly architecture review board, and the queue of pending decisions is now delaying multiple work packages on the critical path. The board members are senior and busy, and the cadence is set by programme governance. As the project manager, which TWO actions most directly and durably reduce this impediment? (Choose two.)

## Options

- **A**: Propose to the governance owner a tiered decision-rights model in which only high-impact or irreversible choices need the full board, delegating routine ones to a named architect.
- **B**: Request that the architecture review board meet weekly instead of monthly so the decision queue drains faster.
- **C**: Have teams proceed on provisional designs while decisions are pending and rework later if the board rules differently.
- **D**: Maintain a prioritised decision log and lobby to have the most critical-path items taken first at each monthly board.
- **E**: Batch and pre-package decisions with full options and recommendations so the board can ratify them quickly rather than deliberate from scratch.

## Answer

A, E

## Explanation

The constraint is a low-frequency, high-authority decision body throttling flow, so the durable fixes either remove most demand from it (A, delegating routine decisions via a tiered model) or make each decision far cheaper to process (E, pre-packaged options and recommendations the board can simply ratify). (B) a weekly cadence helps but depends on senior people granting four times the meeting load and still routes every decision through the bottleneck. (D) prioritising the queue eases the worst pain but leaves the monthly throughput limit and the queue itself intact. (C) provisional designs keep work moving but manufacture rework and treat the symptom. The strongest pair shrinks the demand on the board and accelerates how it disposes of what remains.
