# Question

On a construction fit-out, the project manager learns that the permit required before electrical work can start has not yet issued. The electrical contractor mobilises in five days, the permit is on the critical path, and standing-time charges apply if the contractor arrives and cannot start. The permit was lodged on time; the authority's queue is the hold-up. What should the project manager do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Contact the permitting authority now to confirm exactly where the application sits, what (if anything) is outstanding, and whether issuance can be secured before the contractor's start date — logging it as an active issue.
- **B**: Ask the contractor to provisionally hold their mobilisation slot but stay ready to deploy at short notice, so you avoid standing-time charges yet lose no time if the permit lands on schedule.
- **C**: Re-sequence the programme to pull forward other trades that have no permit dependency, protecting overall progress while the permit clears.
- **D**: Notify the sponsor and client of the permit risk to the electrical start, with the potential schedule and standing-time cost, so they are not surprised if it slips.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four are things a competent PM would likely do, and B, C and D are genuinely useful — the test is sequence and root cause. The blocker is the permit, and the single most decisive first step is to engage its source to establish the real status and press for issuance in time; until you know that, every other action is built on guesswork (A). (B) is sensible contingency once you understand the timeline, but holding the contractor's slot without first finding out whether the permit can actually land in five days is managing the symptom blind. (C) is prudent schedule mitigation, yet re-sequencing trades does not move the electrical permit and risks knock-on clashes if done before you know the real date. (D) is appropriate communication and will likely be needed, but informing stakeholders of a risk before you have tried to clear it, or even confirmed its severity, is premature — you escalate with facts, which A is what gets you. The defining factor is acting on the constraint at its source before contingency, re-sequencing, or notification.
