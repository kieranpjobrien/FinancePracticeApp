# Question

Mid-sprint, two senior engineers are at an impasse over which caching approach to use, and progress on the sprint goal has stalled for a day and a half while they debate. Both approaches are technically reasonable and the disagreement is collegial, not personal. The team looks to you to help them move. What should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Ask the two engineers to write up the trade-offs of each approach and bring them to a short technical review with the wider team, so the decision is made with full peer input even if it takes another day.
- **B**: Give the engineers a clear deadline of end of day to converge themselves, making explicit that if they cannot, you will help them resolve it — preserving their autonomy while capping the delay.
- **C**: Facilitate a brief time-boxed session now, anchored on the few criteria that actually matter for the sprint goal, and if no consensus emerges by the end of it, ensure a single decision owner is empowered to choose so work resumes.
- **D**: Have each engineer spike their approach behind a shared interface for the rest of the day, then pick the winner on evidence, accepting the duplicated effort to get an objective answer.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Every option respects that this is a capable, self-organising team and none is a throwaway — the margin is about restoring flow fastest without either seizing the decision or letting the stall run on. The impediment is a *stalled decision*, not the technical merits, so the strongest move is to actively facilitate a focused, time-boxed resolution with a guaranteed tie-break, which unblocks today while keeping the choice with the team (C). (A) brings good rigour and wider input, but a full write-up plus review extends a stall that has already cost a day and a half, over a decision that does not need the whole team. (B) sensibly caps the delay and preserves autonomy, but "go away and converge by end of day" repeats the same unfacilitated dynamic that has already failed for a day and a half, betting it resolves itself this time. (D) yields the most objective answer, but spiking both approaches duplicates effort and still consumes another day before deciding, which is more than a choice of this size warrants mid-sprint. The decisive factor is facilitating the decision *now* with a fallback owner, rather than deferring, prototyping, or hoping.
