# Question

A Scrum Master on a payments team is reviewing how she handles impediments. During the current sprint she is facing: a developer stuck on an unfamiliar API that another team member has used before; a third-party outage that has halted integration testing; and a slow internal approval that recurs most sprints. Which THREE of her intended actions are sound impediment-handling for the role? (Choose three.)

## Options

- **A**: For the unfamiliar API, connect the stuck developer with the teammate who knows it and let them resolve it, rather than escalating or solving it herself.
- **B**: For the third-party outage, take ownership of contacting the vendor and pursuing the fix and any workaround, keeping the team informed of status while they work other items.
- **C**: For the recurring approval delay, gather the pattern across sprints and raise it with the approving function's leadership to seek a faster path, rather than only chasing this instance.
- **D**: For the unfamiliar API, escalate to the developer's line manager so additional expertise is formally assigned to the team.
- **E**: For the third-party outage, shield the team by holding back the news until she has a confirmed resolution, so their focus is not disturbed.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Each correct action matches the impediment to the right level of intervention: a knowledge gap the team can close itself is best resolved within the team (A); an external outage beyond the team's reach is one the Scrum Master should own and drive while keeping work flowing (B); and a recurring organisational delay warrants escalating the pattern, not just the instance (C). (D) escalating a one-off knowledge gap to a line manager overreaches and undermines self-organisation when a teammate can help immediately — it is plausible but the wrong altitude. (E) withholding the outage until it is resolved breaks transparency and prevents the team from adapting their plan; protecting focus does not mean hiding blockers. The trap options (D, E) are deliberately reasonable-sounding, so the distinction is altitude and transparency, not obvious right-versus-wrong.
