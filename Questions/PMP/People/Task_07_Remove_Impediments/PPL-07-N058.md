# Question

On a predictive aerospace-tooling project, you face three impediments on the same morning: a customs hold on imported fixtures (on the critical path, two days of float left), a disagreement between two engineers over a tolerance spec (not on the critical path), and a junior technician stuck on a calibration procedure a senior colleague nearby knows well. You can only personally drive one at a time. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Address the customs hold first because it threatens the critical path with only two days of float, then enable the technician's peer to help and facilitate the engineers' decision as capacity allows.
- **B**: Resolve the engineers' tolerance disagreement first, since technical disputes between seniors tend to escalate if left unattended.
- **C**: Help the junior technician first because it is the quickest to clear and gives an early win.
- **D**: Handle all three personally in sequence in the order they were reported to you this morning.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

When multiple impediments compete for the PM's attention, the one threatening the critical path with the least float takes priority, while the others are delegated or facilitated appropriately (A): the technician's gap is closed by directing a nearby peer, and the spec dispute is facilitated. (B) prioritises a non-critical-path issue over an imminent critical-path threat. (C) chasing a quick win lets the highest-impact blocker consume scarce float. (D) first-reported order ignores impact entirely and is a poor basis for triage.
