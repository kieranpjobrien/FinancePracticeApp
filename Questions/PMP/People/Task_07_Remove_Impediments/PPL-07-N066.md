# Question

At the daily scrum for an agile e-learning team, a developer says she is blocked because she lacks access to the content-management system she needs today. Getting access means raising a request with the system owner, who sits in another department. The developer has not dealt with the owner before; the Scrum Master has an established working relationship with them. What is the most appropriate action for the Scrum Master?

## Options

- **A**: Raise the access request directly with the system owner straight after the scrum, drawing on the existing relationship to get it cleared today.
- **B**: Have the developer raise the request herself and offer to step in personally if it stalls, so she builds her own link to the system owner.
- **C**: Coach the team to set up a standing access-provisioning arrangement with the owner's department so this stops recurring.
- **D**: Ask the developer to pick up a different ready story and pursue the access in parallel so today is not lost.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The blocker is live today and resolving it depends on a relationship the Scrum Master already holds and the developer does not, so the fastest reliable removal is for the Scrum Master to raise it directly with the owner now (A). (B) routing it through the developer to build her own contact is sound development thinking, but a same-day blocker is the wrong moment to optimise for relationship-building — it risks the day on an untested link when a quicker path exists. (C) a standing provisioning arrangement is a good systemic fix for recurrence, yet it does nothing for today's block and addresses a pattern the scenario has not established. (D) switching the developer to other work keeps her busy but is a workaround that leaves the actual impediment in place and is, at best, a complement to clearing it. The margin is between removing the live blocker through the channel that will actually work today (A) and options that prioritise growth (B), prevention (C), or coping (D) over the immediate need.
