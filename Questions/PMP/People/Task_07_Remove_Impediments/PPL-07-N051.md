# Question

A health-tech scrum team needs a clinician subject-matter expert to validate each triage-algorithm story before it can be marked done. The only assigned clinician is on a clinical rotation and can offer at most thirty minutes a fortnight, which is far less than the validation work the team is generating, so clinical stories are piling up unvalidated. The product owner shares the team's frustration. As Scrum Master, what should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Work with the product owner and the clinician's department to secure a reliable recurring block of expert time, or a named alternate clinician, sized to the team's actual validation demand.
- **B**: Sequence the backlog so only the highest-value clinical stories reach the clinician, and batch their validation into the fortnightly thirty-minute window.
- **C**: Stand up an asynchronous review flow where the clinician annotates completed work between rotation duties, converting the live session into written sign-off the team can act on.
- **D**: Split each validation into a triage step a trained clinical analyst can perform and a final confirmation the clinician signs off, reducing the senior clinician's time per story.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The impediment is a structural shortfall of a critical expert's time relative to demand, so the durable fix is to negotiate a reliable allocation or a named alternate sized to the real need (A); the other options optimise around a constraint they leave in place. (B) prioritising and batching squeezes more from thirty minutes a fortnight but accepts a severe ongoing bottleneck and starves lower-priority clinical work indefinitely. (C) asynchronous annotation can help at the margins but still depends on the same scarce clinician and assumes safety-critical sign-off can be done well between duties. (D) tiering the review is sensible only if a qualified analyst and an approved two-step sign-off genuinely exist, and it still relies on the same overloaded clinician for final confirmation. Only (A) addresses the supply of expert capacity that is the root cause.
