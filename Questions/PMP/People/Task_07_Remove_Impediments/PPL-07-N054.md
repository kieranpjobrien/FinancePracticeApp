# Question

A Scrum Master notices a pattern: team members rarely raise blockers in the daily scrum, yet stories regularly stall midway through the sprint and only surface as problems at review. The team is otherwise capable and delivering. What is the best first step to address this?

## Options

- **A**: Have a quiet conversation with a couple of trusted team members to understand why blockers are going unspoken, then act on what is actually driving the silence.
- **B**: Make impediment-raising an explicit, routine part of the daily scrum by asking directly what is in anyone's way and visibly treating raised blockers as helpful rather than as failings.
- **C**: Add a visible "blocked" state to the task board and a working agreement that any story stalled for more than a day must be flagged there.
- **D**: Reframe the daily scrum around flow by walking the board right to left so stalled work is discussed before anyone gives an individual status.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The Scrum Master is inferring that silence equals a safety problem, but does not yet know the cause — people may distrust the meeting, may not recognise what counts as a blocker, or may be quietly working around things. Understanding the real driver first (A) ensures the chosen intervention actually fits, and is the lightest, most respectful diagnostic step. (B) building a safe daily routine is a strong response and may well be right, but applied blind it assumes the cause is psychological safety; if the real issue is, say, unclear blocker criteria, it will not land. (C) and (D) are both sound structural changes that surface stalls through the board and flow-based facilitation, and either could form part of the fix — but imposing structure before understanding why people stay silent risks treating a symptom. The fine margin is between diagnosing the cause (A) and jumping to a plausible-but-assumed remedy (B/C/D).
