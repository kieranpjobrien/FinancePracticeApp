# Question

A scrum master is reviewing how the team handles impediments after several blockers lingered unresolved for multiple days before anyone acted. Which of the following are appropriate actions to improve how impediments are surfaced and removed? (Choose THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Examine recurring impediments together for common root causes and address the underlying systemic issue rather than each instance in isolation.
- **B**: Ensure every raised impediment has a named owner and a visible status, with stale items chased so none can quietly stall.
- **C**: Have the team attempt a reasonable workaround for each new blocker before logging it, so the impediment list stays focused on what genuinely cannot be self-resolved.
- **D**: Personally pursue impediments that fall outside the team's own authority, escalating with quantified impact where peer-level efforts do not move them.
- **E**: Establish that the daily scrum surfaces impediments and assigns first actions immediately, while reserving the retrospective for analysing patterns and systemic causes.

## Answer

B, D, E

## Explanation

The cluster here is genuinely close — every option describes something a competent scrum master might do, so the test is which three best fix the specific failure of *blockers sitting unactioned for days*. Owners with visible, chased status (B) directly attacks items quietly stalling; personally driving and escalating beyond-authority blockers with quantified impact (D) is core to removal; and making the daily scrum the point where impediments are surfaced and first-actioned, with the retro for pattern analysis (E), fixes the latency by acting as blockers arise. (A) is a real and valuable practice, but it targets *recurrence* over time, whereas the stated problem is *speed of first response* — it improves the system without fixing the immediate "nobody acted for days" gap, so it is the weaker fit here. (C) is the trap: encouraging the team to self-resolve where they can is healthy, but requiring a workaround attempt *before logging* suppresses transparency and lets some blockers go unrecorded — the opposite of what is needed when impediments are already going unseen and unacted. The discriminator is choosing the actions that surface and action blockers fast, over those that either work the long-term pattern (A) or risk hiding items (C).
