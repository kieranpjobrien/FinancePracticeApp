# Question

On a hybrid automotive-electronics programme, two team members have stopped collaborating after a heated disagreement, and a key integration task that needs both of their expertise has stalled for two days. Each separately tells the project manager that the other is being unreasonable. A third engineer could be brought up to speed on the task over several days, and the schedule has a little float. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Meet the two together to surface what is actually behind the disagreement and agree how they will work the task from here.
- **B**: Speak with each of them one-on-one first to understand each side before deciding whether to bring them together.
- **C**: Reassign the integration task to the third engineer so delivery is not held hostage to the personal friction.
- **D**: Set a short deadline for the two to resolve it themselves and step in only if the task is still stalled after that.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The impediment is an interpersonal conflict directly stalling shared work the two are best placed to do, and with the task already stalled two days the project manager's first move is to bring them together to surface the real issue and agree how they will proceed (A) — engaging the conflict is how the obstacle is removed. (B) separate conversations can have a place, but defaulting to shuttling between two people who each just blame the other tends to entrench positions and delays the joint conversation that has to happen anyway; here it postpones rather than resolves. (C) handing the task to a third engineer spends several days of ramp-up to avoid the conflict, leaves the underlying friction to resurface on the next shared task, and uses float that may be needed elsewhere. (D) giving them a deadline to sort it out themselves has already implicitly failed over two stalled days and risks the float draining with no movement. The discrimination is between addressing the conflict together now (A) and approaches that separate (B), bypass (C), or defer (D) it.
