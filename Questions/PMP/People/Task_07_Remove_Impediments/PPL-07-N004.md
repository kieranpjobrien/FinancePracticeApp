# Question

During daily scrum, a developer reports that the third-party API key the integration work depends on has expired. Renewing it requires a vendor-portal account that only the vendor-management office holds, and their published turnaround for such requests is two to three business days. Most of the sprint's remaining stories touch this integration. What is the most appropriate response from the scrum master?

## Options

- **A**: Have the developer who hit the blocker raise the renewal request and follow it up, since they have the technical context the vendor office will ask about, while you remove any obstacles they encounter.
- **B**: Submit the renewal request through the vendor-management office's standard intake and ask the team to swarm on the few stories that do not depend on the integration until the key returns.
- **C**: Raise the renewal directly with the vendor-management office now, press for an expedited turnaround given the sprint impact, and ask the team what work they can progress in parallel while the key is pending.
- **D**: Flag the dependency to the product owner so they can decide whether to re-order the sprint backlog around the expected two-to-three-day wait.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Every option here is a legitimate move and the request will be made one way or another — the margin is who drives it and how hard. The blocker stalls most of the sprint and the standard turnaround would consume much of it, so the scrum master should both push the owning office for an expedited resolution *and* keep the team productive in parallel — C does both and treats removal as active rather than process-default. (B) follows the standard channel and protects some flow, but accepting the full two-to-three-day turnaround without trying to compress it is passive when the sprint is at stake. (A) sensibly uses the developer's context, but delegating the *chase* to a blocked team member for an obstacle that needs cross-org weight is exactly the kind of impediment a scrum master should carry. (D) is reasonable transparency, yet it jumps to scope re-planning before any attempt has been made to shorten the wait, conceding the impact too early.
