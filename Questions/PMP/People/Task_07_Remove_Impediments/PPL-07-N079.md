# Question

On a predictive municipal-roadworks project, a team member reports that work is blocked pending a road-closure permit from the local council. The project has a named council liaison who is the day-to-day contact for permits, and the project manager also has a route to the sponsor, who sits on a joint steering group with senior council officials. The permit has now passed its expected issue date and is holding up the schedule. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Contact the council liaison to establish why the permit is delayed and press for an expedited issue date, quantifying the schedule impact.
- **B**: Escalate straight to the sponsor to raise the overdue permit with senior council officials through the steering group.
- **C**: Document the delay as a schedule risk and re-baseline the affected work to reflect the council's likely timeline.
- **D**: Re-sequence the team onto other available work and revisit the permit once the council responds in its own time.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The permit is outside the project manager's direct control, but the established channel for it is the council liaison, so the right first move is to work that channel — find out why it is delayed and press for an expedited date with the schedule impact made explicit (A). (B) going straight to the sponsor and senior officials skips the very contact whose job is permits and burns a high-level relationship before the normal route has been tried; it becomes appropriate only if the liaison cannot shift it. (C) recording a risk and re-baselining quietly concedes the slip instead of trying to remove the blocker, treating the symptom rather than the cause. (D) re-sequencing onto other work keeps the team busy but is a workaround that leaves the impediment unaddressed and accepts the council's unhurried timeline. The discrimination is between pursuing the right channel first (A) and over-escalating (B), conceding (C), or merely coping (D).
