# Question

On a predictive logistics-platform project, your team is blocked because the mandatory security review of a third-party component has not started, and the security function reports it is short-staffed for the next month. The review gates work on your critical path. Which TWO actions are the most appropriate for the project manager to take? (Choose TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Ask the security function whether a partial or risk-prioritised review of the highest-risk elements could clear the critical-path portion first, with the remainder reviewed later.
- **B**: Quantify the critical-path and cost impact of the month's wait and take it to the sponsor with a specific ask — prioritise this review, add temporary capacity, or fund an accredited external reviewer.
- **C**: Submit a change request to swap the third-party component for an already-approved equivalent that needs no new review, subject to stakeholder agreement.
- **D**: Identify lower-risk parts of the work that can proceed in parallel without the reviewed component, so progress continues while the review is arranged.
- **E**: Re-baseline the schedule to absorb the month's delay and notify stakeholders the date has moved, on the basis that the security review is mandatory and outside your control.

## Answer

B, D

## Explanation

This is a tight set — A, C and E are all plausible and none is an obvious throwaway, which is what makes the choice hard. Escalating with quantified impact and a concrete, options-based ask (B) targets the real constraint, and progressing unblocked low-risk work in parallel (D) limits the damage without touching governance; together they are the strongest, lowest-regret pair. (A) is genuinely reasonable and may help, but it depends entirely on security agreeing to split a mandated review and on such elements being separable — useful to *request* (and it overlaps with B's escalation), but not a reliable standalone action. (C) can be legitimate, yet swapping a core component late on a logistics platform is a significant scope and architecture change with its own risk and rework, so it is a fallback to weigh, not a first-line move. (E) is the subtle trap: re-baselining is sometimes unavoidable, but conceding the full month *before* attempting to remove or mitigate the blocker is premature surrender. The discriminator is acting to remove and work around the impediment first, rather than partially depending on others' agreement, re-architecting, or accepting the slip up front.
