# Question

On a retail-app team, the third-party payment sandbox has been flaky in four of the last five sprints, costing hours of debugging each time. The team has already accepted this is a recurring impediment, not a one-off, and wants a durable fix. The vendor acknowledges the instability but will give no firm date. Switching providers is a large, multi-sprint effort the product owner is reluctant to fund now. As Scrum Master, what is the best way to drive a lasting fix?

## Options

- **A**: Work with the team to build a reliable internal stub that mimics the sandbox for day-to-day development and testing, reserving the live sandbox for targeted end-to-end checks, so the team's flow no longer depends on the vendor's uptime.
- **B**: Keep escalating to the vendor with the cumulative impact data and press hard for a committed remediation date before pursuing any internal change.
- **C**: Build the business case for switching payment providers and ask the product owner to fund the migration as the only way to remove the root cause.
- **D**: Track every sandbox failure and its cost in detail so the team has a complete, evidenced record to justify action when the vendor or product owner is ready.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The most reliable durable fix is the one the team can control: decoupling routine work from the unreliable sandbox via an internal stub removes the dependency from the critical path without waiting on a vendor or a funding decision (A). (B) escalating to the vendor is worth doing in parallel, but it leaves the team's flow hostage to a supplier who has already declined to commit a date — it does not, by itself, remove the blocker. (C) a full provider migration may eventually be right, but it is a large, unfunded effort and treating it as the "only" route ignores cheaper containment the team can act on now. (D) detailed logging produces evidence but resolves nothing on its own. The fine margin is between taking ownership of a fix within the team's gift (A) and pursuing fixes that depend on others' decisions (B, C).
