# Question

On a large predictive programme, the project manager must route three blockers to the right level and timing of action. Each handling option below is a sound response in the right circumstances — match each blocker to the handling that fits its authority level and urgency.

## Items

- **1**: An obstacle the PM can clear using a routine channel already available to them, with no one else's authority required.
- **2**: An obstacle the PM has twice tried and failed to resolve at peer level, which sits across departments and now threatens a gate on the critical path.
- **3**: An obstacle that does not yet exist but is foreseeable — a likely future dependency surfaced while reviewing next quarter's plan, currently causing no delay.

## Matches

- **A**: Act now to coordinate with the other party so the situation is shaped before it can harden into a blocker.
- **B**: Take it to the sponsor with quantified impact and a specific request, because resolving it is beyond the PM's authority.
- **C**: Action it directly and confirm it is cleared, since it is fully within the PM's own remit.

## Answer

1-C, 2-B, 3-A

## Explanation

The match turns on authority level and timing, and the responses are worded so they do not name the obstacle type — forcing the candidate to reason rather than pattern-match. An obstacle the PM can clear through a channel already open to them (1) sits within their remit and should simply be actioned and confirmed (C). A cross-departmental conflict that peer-level attempts have already failed to fix and that now threatens the critical path (2) has demonstrably exceeded the PM's authority, so it warrants sponsor escalation with quantified impact and a concrete ask (B). A foreseeable dependency that is not yet causing delay (3) is best met by coordinating with the other party now to prevent it ever maturing into an impediment (A). Escalating (1), actioning (2) alone again, or waiting on (3) until it bit would each mismatch the response to the obstacle's authority level and timing.
