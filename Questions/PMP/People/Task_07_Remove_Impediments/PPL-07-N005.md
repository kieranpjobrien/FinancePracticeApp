# Question

On a predictive telecom rollout, your work package cannot proceed until another project in the same programme delivers a network interface. That project has just announced a four-week slip. The two projects share a programme manager who can re-prioritise across both. Your float on this dependency is one week, and the interface gates your critical path. What should you do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Spend a short, focused effort re-sequencing your work to consume the available float and listing activities that can proceed without the interface, so you can bring options alongside the problem when you raise it.
- **B**: Take the quantified critical-path impact of the four-week slip to the programme manager now and request a coordinated resolution — accelerating the other project, partial early delivery of the interface, or re-prioritising across the two.
- **C**: Convene a joint working session with the other project's lead to test whether the interface can be delivered incrementally, with the part you need first, before you involve the programme manager.
- **D**: Ask your team to build a temporary stub of the interface to defined contract so downstream work can continue against simulated data while the slip is worked.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

This is a fine FIRST-step margin: A, C and D are all sensible and a good PM would likely do several of them — the question is sequence. A four-week external slip against one week of float exceeds anything you can absorb or fix inside your own project, and only the shared programme manager has authority over both projects' priorities, so engaging them immediately with quantified impact is the correct first move (B). (A) is genuinely good practice — arriving with options is better than arriving with only a problem — but the gap is far too large for float optimisation to close, and a four-week critical-path threat should not wait while you polish options before alerting the one person who can act. (C) is a strong mitigation and may well be where the solution lands, but going peer-to-peer first on a slip this size, when a shared authority exists, risks an agreement that cannot be resourced and delays the escalation. (D) is a legitimate technical mitigation to put on the table, but a stub against a contract that is itself slipping carries real integration risk and is something to weigh with the programme manager, not to launch before the dependency is even coordinated.
