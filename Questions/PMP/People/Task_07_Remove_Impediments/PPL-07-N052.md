# Question

On a predictive offshore-wind project, commissioning is blocked because the grid operator must energise the export cable, and they have pushed your slot back six weeks citing their own works. Your contract with them has no remedy for this, your sponsor has a board commitment tied to the original date, and your written request for an earlier slot has already been refused at the operator's scheduling level. What should the project manager do NEXT?

## Options

- **A**: Build a concise impact case — board commitment at risk, cost of the slip, options already explored — and escalate to your sponsor so they can apply executive or commercial pressure on the grid operator above the level that refused you.
- **B**: Re-baseline commissioning to the new date and brief the sponsor that the slip is grid-operator-driven and outside your control, having exhausted the routes available to you.
- **C**: Resequence remaining works to compress the post-energisation programme and partially recover the six weeks once the slot does open, then brief the sponsor on the residual exposure.
- **D**: Approach the grid operator commercially to negotiate an incentive or alternative arrangement for an earlier slot, given your contract offers no remedy.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

You have exhausted your own leverage at the operator's working level and the blocker now threatens a board commitment, so the decisive next step is to equip your sponsor — who can reach a level you cannot and bring commercial weight — with a sharp impact case (A). (B) re-baselining concedes the date before giving the sponsor any chance to act and treats an organisational blocker as immovable too early. (C) downstream compression is prudent contingency planning but only mitigates the consequence; it neither recovers the energisation date nor engages the party who controls it. (D) negotiating an incentive may well form part of the response, but pursuing a commercial deal yourself, after a refusal, without the sponsor's authority and visibility is premature; the sponsor must be brought in first. The fine margin is between escalating to the one person who can move the operator (A) and acting alone through mitigation or commercial routes that do not.
