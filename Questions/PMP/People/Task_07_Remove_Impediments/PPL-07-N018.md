# Question

On a hybrid insurance-claims modernisation programme you face three obstacles at once: a developer waiting on access credentials you can request through the standard channel; an architectural question the team is capable of resolving but is circling on; and a regulatory interpretation that only external counsel can clarify, which gates a release in two weeks. Your time this week is limited. Which TWO actions reflect the soundest impediment handling? (Choose TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Action the credential request through the standard channel now and confirm the developer is unblocked, since it is quick, fully within your reach, and stops a person sitting idle.
- **B**: Facilitate a short, time-boxed session to help the team converge on the architectural decision themselves, stepping in to name a decision owner only if they cannot.
- **C**: Engage external counsel on the regulatory interpretation now, because only they can resolve it, legal turnaround is uncertain, and it gates a release just two weeks out.
- **D**: Make the architectural call yourself to remove that obstacle quickly and free your limited time for the credential and regulatory items.
- **E**: Delegate chasing the credential request to the blocked developer so you can concentrate your week on the regulatory question, which carries the highest stakes.

## Answer

A, C

## Explanation

This is deliberately hard because B, D and E are all reasonable-sounding and none is a pure throwaway — the test is matching the response to the *nature and authority level* of each obstacle. The credential is a quick, in-reach external unblock that should simply be actioned now (A), and the regulatory interpretation is genuinely beyond anyone internal and gates a near-term release, so engaging counsel immediately is right (C); these are the two clearest, lowest-regret moves. (B) is actually the *correct* way to handle the architectural item and would be a strong answer in isolation — but the team can resolve it and it is not the most pressing of the three, so against the credential and regulatory obstacles it is the weaker pick this week. (D) deciding it yourself removes a decision the team can and should own, undermining their autonomy to save time. (E) wrongly delegates a cross-system access chase to the very person it is blocking, and an idle developer waiting on a fast unblock should not be parked; the credential is quick enough that you should just clear it (A). The discriminator is taking the two actions that match obstacle to owner — drive what is yours and what only externals can do — over imposing, deferring, or mis-delegating.
