# Question

As Scrum Master across two teams, you have already flagged to leadership that the most damaging recurring impediments — slow security reviews and a shared-services queue — stem from organisational structures, not the teams. Leadership agreed the problems are real but no change has followed, and the impediments keep recurring. There is an existing agile transformation steering group, chaired by a sponsor, that owns cross-team process improvements. What is the best next move?

## Options

- **A**: Bring a quantified case — cumulative delay, cost, and a specific proposed change for each impediment — to the transformation steering group that owns cross-team process, so the issue moves from acknowledged to actioned through the body empowered to change it.
- **B**: Continue raising each impediment with leadership as it recurs, with sharper impact data each time, until the weight of evidence prompts them to act.
- **C**: Implement the changes you can within your two teams — local fast-track agreements with security and the shared-services owners — and let other teams adopt them if they wish.
- **D**: Coach both teams to plan around the known delays so their forecasts stay reliable while you keep pressing leadership for the wider fix.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Leadership has acknowledged the problem but acknowledgement is not change; the gap is converting it into action, and the most effective route is the body that actually owns cross-team process improvement — armed with quantified impact and a concrete proposal it can decide on (A). (B) repeating the same escalation to general leadership has already stalled once; without routing it to an empowered forum with a specific ask, more evidence alone may not break the inertia. (C) local fast-track deals are a worthwhile interim move and may seed the broader fix, but on their own they leave the systemic constraint in place for everyone else and depend on voluntary adoption. (D) planning around the delays accepts the waste and reduces the pressure for systemic change. The fine margin is between driving the agreed problem into an empowered decision channel (A) and continuing to push it through the channel that has already failed to act (B).
