# Question

An agile team keeps surfacing the same obstacle in every retrospective — security review approvals routinely take five to eight days, stalling stories near the end of each sprint. The team has started treating the wait as "just how things are here." You have a good working relationship with the security review lead, who is also stretched. Which action best addresses the impediment?

## Options

- **A**: Pull security-dependent stories to the start of each sprint so the review wait overlaps with other in-progress work and stops landing on the critical end-of-sprint path.
- **B**: Sit down with the security lead to map what actually drives the five-to-eight-day wait, then jointly agree a faster path for routine low-risk changes — such as pre-classification, a standing review slot, or delegated sign-off for defined cases.
- **C**: Set a team policy that any story needing security review must enter review by sprint mid-point, with the scrum master chasing each submission so reviews are requested early and tracked to closure.
- **D**: Bring the recurring delay and its cost in lost throughput to the next steering committee, asking leadership to direct the security function to resource reviews faster.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

All four are reasonable and none is a throwaway — the margin is about which one actually removes the recurring constraint versus working around or merely escalating it. A recurring, systemic delay is best dissolved by understanding its drivers and agreeing a durable faster path with the people who own the process, which is exactly what B does and what the existing good relationship makes feasible. (A) is a sound flow tactic and genuinely reduces the pain, but it only re-times the same wait rather than shortening it, so the constraint persists. (C) improves discipline and early submission — also helpful — but if the review itself takes five to eight days, requesting it earlier does not remove the bottleneck, it just front-loads it. (D) is a legitimate escalation and may be needed if collaboration stalls, but going to the steering committee first, over a partner you can work with directly, is heavier than warranted and risks the relationship; it is the fallback, not the opening move.
