# Question

A pharmaceutical analytics team repeatedly pulls stories into the sprint that turn out to be understood differently by developers, the product owner and the data steward, causing mid-sprint churn. The scrum master wants to strengthen how shared understanding is built before work starts, without slowing delivery to a crawl. Which actions would genuinely help? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Hold regular refinement sessions where developers, the product owner and the data steward jointly examine upcoming stories
- **B**: Agree a definition of ready so a story is only pulled in once its intent and acceptance criteria are commonly understood
- **C**: Co-write concrete examples or scenarios with the three roles to pin down the intended behaviour of ambiguous stories
- **D**: Have the product owner author fuller, more detailed story descriptions and circulate them for the team to read before planning
- **E**: Add the data steward as a required approver who signs off each story's wording before it enters the backlog

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

The churn comes from divergent interpretations entering the sprint, so the effective actions build a common picture multi-way and before commitment: joint refinement (A) gets the three perspectives into one conversation; a definition of ready (B) gates the pull until that understanding actually exists; and co-written examples (C) make ambiguous intent concrete and testable with all three contributing. Fuller written descriptions circulated to read (D) is tempting and not useless, but it keeps the exchange one-way — better prose from one author does not make three people converge, and the same silent misreads survive a passive read-through. Making the data steward a sign-off gate (E) adds a single approver's view and a serialised handoff, which can even mask divergence behind one person's tick; it substitutes individual approval for the shared conversation that the developers and product owner also need to be inside.
