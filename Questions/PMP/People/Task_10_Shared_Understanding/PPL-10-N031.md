# Question

A project manager joins an agile team building a clinical-trials data platform. Recurring rework stems from members holding different understandings of the regulatory rules the platform must enforce, and the rules are genuinely complex and externally defined. The team is collaborative and communicates well, but no one on it is a regulatory expert, and the product owner is a clinician, not a compliance specialist. The project manager wants to build a durable shared understanding of these rules within the team. Which three actions best achieve that here? (Choose three.)

## Options

- **A**: Run a working session with a regulatory subject-matter expert who walks the team through the rules using concrete trial scenarios the team works through together
- **B**: Co-create a shared, examples-based reference of how each rule applies, that the team authors with the expert and maintains as rules change
- **C**: Have the team co-author acceptance examples for each story and confirm them, drawing on the agreed regulatory reference
- **D**: Have the product owner write fuller story descriptions of the rules from their own clinical knowledge and distribute them before refinement
- **E**: Encourage each developer to interpret the regulations themselves and raise anything they are unsure about in standup

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

The gap is real external knowledge the team does not yet hold, so building shared understanding here requires getting that knowledge in and making it common: an expert-led session grounded in concrete scenarios (A) brings the knowledge in interactively, a co-authored examples-based reference (B) makes it durable and shared, and co-authoring story acceptance examples against that reference (C) applies it where work actually happens. Option D is the tempting near-miss: it looks like making the rules explicit, but the product owner is a clinician, not a compliance specialist, so fuller descriptions risk propagating an authoritative-looking but unreliable interpretation, and it is one-directional. Option E pushes interpretation onto individuals with no expert grounding, which is exactly how the divergent readings arose, and standup is not where deep rules get reconciled. Three options build correct shared understanding from a reliable source; D and E spread or entrench unreliable understanding.
