# Question

In a sprint retrospective, the team realises a feature was built and discarded because two members each assumed the other was handling a key integration, so neither did, and downstream work was based on that wrong shared assumption. Looking back over the last few sprints, the team finds this is not the first time a silent assumption about ownership, sequencing, or interfaces went unchallenged until it caused rework. The team is keen to prevent recurrences. What should the project manager encourage the team to focus on?

## Options

- **A**: Assign a single named owner to every task so responsibility for each piece of work is never ambiguous
- **B**: Maintain a visible assumptions-and-dependencies log that the team reviews and updates at each planning session
- **C**: Adopt a practice of stating key assumptions and dependencies aloud during planning and confirming who owns each before work starts
- **D**: Add a dependency-check item to the definition of ready so no story is started until its cross-team dependencies are confirmed

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The retrospective has already diagnosed the real pattern — assumptions go unspoken — so the most effective focus is the practice that directly changes that behaviour for the broadest range of cases. Stating assumptions and dependencies aloud and confirming ownership in planning (C) targets the habit itself and catches the whole class of silent-assumption failures, not just integrations. Single named owners per task (A) fixes this specific ownership ambiguity but does nothing for the many other unspoken assumptions a team makes about sequencing, interfaces, or intent. An assumptions log (B) is a strong supporting artefact, yet a log only captures what someone already thought to write down — it does not by itself create the act of voicing what was previously assumed silently. A dependency check in the definition of ready (D) guards cross-team dependencies but, like A, addresses one slice rather than the underlying habit. C changes the behaviour that produced the failure, which is what the team identified.
