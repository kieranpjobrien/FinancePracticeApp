# Question

On a predictive railway signalling upgrade, the operations director has emailed insisting the cutover happen on a weekend to minimise service disruption, while the safety regulator has emailed insisting it happen on a weekday when their inspectors are on duty. The two emails crossed; neither stakeholder has seen the other's reasoning. The cutover is eight weeks away and the project manager wants both parties to reach a shared, workable decision. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Bring both stakeholders together to explain the reasoning behind each position and have each confirm they understand the other's
- **B**: Establish with the regulator what is actually driving the weekday requirement, and whether any part of it is a fixed condition of approval, before framing the discussion between the two
- **C**: Propose a Friday-evening cutover that falls outside peak service yet overlaps regulator working hours, and ask both to respond
- **D**: Escalate to the project sponsor with both positions, since the two requirements appear mutually exclusive

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Both messages read as flat insistence, but a regulator's "must" and an operations director's "must" are not the same kind of must: one may be a hard condition of approval that bounds every option, the other a strong business preference. Until the project manager knows which, bringing the parties together to "understand each other" as if both positions were equally movable can mislead the conversation and spend the operations director's goodwill on flexibility that may not exist. Probing what truly drives the regulator's stance, and whether any of it is non-negotiable, is what lets the discussion be framed honestly. Option A is the right move once that is known, but run first it presumes both positions are negotiable; if the regulator's is fixed, the session raises a false hope of compromise. Option C jumps to one specific solution before anyone knows whether the regulator can move at all, so it may propose something impossible. Option D escalates while the decisive fact is still ungathered; the sponsor would rightly ask whether the regulator's stance is actually fixed, which has not been checked. All four are legitimate, but only B settles the fact on which the whole conflict hinges before acting on it.
