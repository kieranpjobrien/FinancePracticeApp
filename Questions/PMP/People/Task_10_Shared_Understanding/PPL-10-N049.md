# Question

On an agile team, the developers and product owner have argued twice in two days about how one complex story should behave at its boundaries. Each side restates its view more firmly each time, the disagreement is now visibly tense, and the story is needed in the sprint starting tomorrow. The Scrum Master has facilitated discussion, but the talk keeps circling the same abstract points. What technique should the Scrum Master use to move them to a shared understanding fastest?

## Options

- **A**: Work the story through specific concrete examples of input and expected behaviour, building the agreed cases together until the boundaries are pinned down
- **B**: Have each side state its position and reasoning in full, then look for the underlying interest both are trying to protect
- **C**: Capture the points of agreement and disagreement on a shared board so the group can see where exactly they diverge
- **D**: Time-box the debate and, if unresolved, have the product owner make the call as the accountable decision-maker

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The discussion is stuck because it stays abstract, and abstract argument about edge cases rarely converges — moving to concrete examples (specification by example) forces both sides to say exactly what should happen for specific inputs, which is where vague disagreement resolves into a precise, jointly agreed answer. Surfacing underlying interests (B) is the right tool when a conflict hides incompatible goals, but here both parties want the same thing — correct behaviour — and simply picture it differently, so interest-mining does not address the gap. Visualising points of agreement and disagreement (C) helps see *where* they differ but does not itself settle *how* the boundaries should behave; it can leave the team staring at a well-drawn impasse. Deferring to the product owner's authority (D) can break a deadlock and is a reasonable last resort, but it imposes an answer the developers may still not understand or agree with, which is the opposite of the shared understanding needed to build the story right.
