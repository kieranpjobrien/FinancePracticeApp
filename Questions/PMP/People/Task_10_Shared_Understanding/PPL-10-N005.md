# Question

On a hybrid product programme with members in three countries, the offshore engineering team consistently delivers work the onshore business analysts say is "not what we asked for", while the engineers say they followed the requirements precisely. Written specifications exist for every item. Investigating, the project manager finds the specs rely on idioms and implied context that are read differently in each location, and crucially the engineers are confident they understood — they do not realise their reading differs. What is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: Rewrite specifications in plain literal language and maintain a shared glossary of agreed terms across both teams
- **B**: Hold a joint live walkthrough of each requirement set where both teams restate the intended behaviour until their interpretations are confirmed to match
- **C**: Have the analysts add concrete acceptance examples to each requirement showing exactly what correct output looks like
- **D**: Pair an analyst and an engineer to co-review each spec before development and sign off that both read it the same way

## Answer

B

## Explanation

All four attack the implied-context problem, so the discriminator is which reliably detects a divergence the engineers do not know they have. Because the engineers are confident they understood, any method that depends on them noticing ambiguity will silently fail — a glossary and plain language (A) reduce ambiguity in the author's terms but cannot surface a confident misreading, and concrete examples (C) help only if the analysts happen to write the very edge case where interpretations split. Paired co-review with sign-off (D) is closer, but a busy pair confirming "we read it the same" still risks both nodding without testing it on specifics. A joint live walkthrough where each side restates the intended behaviour back (B) actively forces the hidden mismatch into the open in real time and across locations, which is the only one of the four that verifies rather than assumes a shared reading. The others are useful reinforcements layered on top.
