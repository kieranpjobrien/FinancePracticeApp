# Question

At the start of a sprint, the project manager notices the team has a list of committed stories but no shared sense of why those stories were chosen together or what outcome they should produce. During the sprint, members make conflicting trade-off decisions when problems arise, each optimising for something different. Which practice would most directly give the team a common basis for these decisions?

## Options

- **A**: Have the team and product owner agree a sprint goal that states the single outcome the sprint is meant to achieve, so trade-offs can be weighed against it
- **B**: Have the product owner rank the committed stories by priority so the team knows which to protect when conflicts arise
- **C**: Add a brief "why this sprint" statement to the sprint plan explaining the business reason the stories were selected
- **D**: Capture the team's working agreements on how to resolve trade-offs, such as favouring the highest-value story when time is short

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The team is making divergent trade-offs because they lack a shared objective to judge against, so the most direct fix supplies that objective. A jointly agreed sprint goal (A) gives every member the same outcome to test decisions against in the moment, which is exactly the missing piece. Priority-ranking the stories (B) helps when the conflict is simply which story to drop, but many in-sprint trade-offs are within or across stories where ranking gives no guidance on the intended outcome. A "why this sprint" statement (C) is close and adds useful context, but a passive explanation of past selection rationale is weaker than an active, agreed goal the team commits to steer by. General trade-off working agreements (D) provide a procedure for resolving conflicts but no shared sense of what the sprint is for, so members still apply the rule toward different ends. A delivers the unifying purpose; the others give priority, context, or process without it.
