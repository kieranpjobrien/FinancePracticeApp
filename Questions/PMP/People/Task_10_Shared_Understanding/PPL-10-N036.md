# Question

On a large predictive port-expansion programme, several alignment problems have emerged among stakeholders. Each could be approached several ways, so match each problem to the action the project manager should take first to rebuild shared understanding.

## Items

- **1**: Two department heads hold firmly opposed positions, and neither has explained the constraint driving their stance
- **2**: A signed charter is too high level, so different teams describe the programme's scope inconsistently
- **3**: Engineers and operators nod agreement in reviews but then deliver and expect different things
- **4**: A new contractor keeps cutting across how the programme works — reporting lines, sign-off steps, escalation paths — that the incumbent teams treat as obvious but never spelled out to them

## Matches

- **A**: Facilitate joint creation of concrete shared artifacts and play understanding back to expose hidden mismatches
- **B**: Facilitate a dialogue that surfaces the underlying interests and constraints behind each position
- **C**: Collaboratively develop a more specific, jointly endorsed scope statement to cascade consistently
- **D**: Make the unstated expectations explicit by agreeing and documenting them with the parties involved

## Answer

1-B, 2-C, 3-A, 4-D

## Explanation

Opposed positions with unstated reasons require first surfacing the underlying interests and constraints so the parties can genuinely understand each other (1-B); jumping to artifacts or scope wording would not address why they are dug in. Inconsistent scope descriptions from a vague charter call for co-developing a more specific, jointly endorsed scope statement to cascade (2-C). Apparent agreement masking divergent expectations is exposed by building concrete shared artifacts and replaying understanding to reveal the mismatch (3-A); a scope statement alone would not catch the silent divergence between two technical groups. Norms breached because they were never stated are an unwritten-expectations gap, fixed by making those expectations explicit and documented with the parties (4-D). The challenge is that several actions look applicable to each problem, but only one targets its actual cause.
