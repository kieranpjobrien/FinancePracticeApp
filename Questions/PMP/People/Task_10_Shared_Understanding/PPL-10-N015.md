# Question

The project manager must brief a steering committee of non-technical executives on why a proposed architecture change is necessary. The lead architect's dry-run uses slides dense with technical diagrams, and the PM is convinced the executives will not grasp the implications and may reject a sound proposal for the wrong reasons. The decision is needed at next week's committee meeting. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Prepare an executive summary that leads with the recommendation and its business consequences, attaching the architect's full slides as supporting material
- **B**: Work with the architect to reframe the briefing around business impact, risk, cost, and the options, keeping the technical detail in an appendix for questions
- **C**: Translate each technical diagram into a plain-language equivalent the architect can present, preserving the original flow of the briefing
- **D**: Pre-brief one or two influential committee members individually so they can help interpret the proposal for their peers during the meeting

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The committee can only reach a sound decision if the content is pitched at what they can act on, so the discriminator is which option most fully rebuilds the briefing around their decision frame rather than dressing up the existing technical one. Reframing the whole briefing around business impact, risk, cost, and options with technical depth held in reserve (B) restructures the material to the audience's needs and equips them to decide, which is the core of building shared understanding across a technical divide. An executive summary bolted onto the unchanged deck (A) helps but still routes the committee into dense slides for the substance, so comprehension stalls the moment they go past the summary. Translating diagrams one-for-one (C) keeps a flow designed for engineers, fixing vocabulary but not the framing — executives need the "so what", not a clearer picture of the architecture. Pre-briefing allies (D) is a useful tactic for building support but outsources the committee's understanding to intermediaries instead of communicating the proposal clearly to the whole group. B addresses the actual barrier most directly.
