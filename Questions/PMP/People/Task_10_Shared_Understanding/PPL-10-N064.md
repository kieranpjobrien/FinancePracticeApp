# Question

An agile team building a logistics tracking platform has delivered three sprints. The business stakeholders approve written sprint summaries each time, yet at a quarterly checkpoint several declare the product "not at all what we expected" and ask for substantial changes. The team is frustrated, having reported progress diligently throughout. What change to how understanding is built would most likely have prevented this?

## Options

- **A**: Add a quarterly milestone demo where stakeholders see the assembled product before each governance checkpoint
- **B**: Require stakeholders to sign off on each story's acceptance criteria before its sprint begins
- **C**: Run a working-software sprint review each sprint where stakeholders interact with the increment and give feedback on the real thing
- **D**: Circulate richer sprint summaries with annotated screenshots and have a business analyst walk stakeholders through them

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The stakeholders signed off on descriptions but never used the running product, so their mental model drifted unchecked for three sprints — putting the actual increment in front of them every sprint (C) is what surfaces "that's not what we meant" while only a sprint's worth of change is at stake. A quarterly milestone demo (A) does let them see real software and is better than nothing, but it inspects at the same coarse interval at which the gap already detonated; a quarter of divergence is exactly what proved expensive. Acceptance-criteria sign-off (B) sharpens intent before each sprint and is genuinely valuable, yet agreed criteria on paper still describe expectation, not built reality, and the mismatch this team hit only appears once something runs. Richer summaries with a walkthrough (D) improve the abstraction and add a voice, but a more detailed account of the wrong picture is still not the stakeholders seeing and handling the thing itself.
