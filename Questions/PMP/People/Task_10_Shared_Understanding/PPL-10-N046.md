# Question

A construction project manager is collecting requirements for a hospital extension. The clinical director wants single-occupancy rooms throughout for infection control and cites an applicable health regulation; the facilities director wants a mix of single and shared rooms to hit the funded bed-capacity target within budget. Each has separately told the project manager their requirement is non-negotiable. Both report to the same sponsor. What is the best way for the project manager to proceed?

## Options

- **A**: Bring both directors together to clarify the actual regulatory constraint and the bed-capacity target, then explore designs that satisfy the binding requirements of each
- **B**: Confirm the regulation's exact requirement with the compliance authority first, then bring the directors the verified constraint to plan within
- **C**: Bring both directors together to negotiate a compromise on the single-versus-mixed split that each can live with
- **D**: Escalate both positions to the shared sponsor with options and a recommendation, since the two directors have declared deadlock

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The two "non-negotiable" positions are stated as room-mix preferences, but underneath sit two interests that may both be satisfiable — a genuine regulatory floor on infection control and a funded capacity target — and the project manager's job is to bring the parties together to separate the truly binding constraints from the negotiable design choices and find an option that meets both. Verifying the regulation first (B) is sensible diligence and the regulatory facts do matter, but going to the authority before the directors have jointly framed the problem treats the conflict as purely a compliance lookup and delays the alignment conversation that has to happen anyway. Negotiating a compromise on the split (C) bargains over positions and is likely to leave both half-satisfied, whereas exploring interests can surface a design (e.g. single rooms only where infection risk requires them) that meets each need in full. Escalating to the sponsor (D) is a legitimate fallback once the project manager has genuinely tried and failed to reconcile the interests, but doing it now abdicates the facilitation role and hands up a conflict that has not yet been properly explored.
