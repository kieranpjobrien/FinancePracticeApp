# Question

Midway through an agile programme delivering a clinical scheduling system, the project manager observes a recurring pattern: stories are accepted at sprint review, then reopened days later because clinicians say the outcome does not match what they meant. The product owner relays requirements verbally from clinician meetings the developers do not attend, and acceptance criteria are written by the product owner alone. Which TWO actions would most directly build a durable shared understanding of requirements?

## Options

- **A**: Invite a clinician representative and one or two developers to participate together in story refinement so intent is confirmed at the source
- **B**: Require the product owner to write more detailed acceptance criteria and have a second person proofread them
- **C**: Introduce example mapping or concrete worked examples during refinement so the team and clinicians agree on specific expected behaviours
- **D**: Add a formal change-control board to review every reopened story before it can be re-accepted
- **E**: Lengthen the sprint so there is more time to clarify requirements after development begins

## Answer

A, C

## Explanation

The mismatch persists because requirement intent passes through the product owner as a single relay and is never confirmed directly between the people who hold the need and the people who build it. Bringing a clinician and developers into refinement together (A) closes that gap at the source, and using concrete examples (C) forces the abstract wording into specific agreed behaviours that expose hidden differences in interpretation before build. More detailed criteria with a proofreader (B) still keeps the product owner as the sole interpreter and does not verify clinician intent. A change-control board (D) adds governance to manage the symptom rather than building shared understanding. Lengthening the sprint (E) gives more time to clarify after the misunderstanding has already shaped the work, which is later and more wasteful than aligning upfront.
