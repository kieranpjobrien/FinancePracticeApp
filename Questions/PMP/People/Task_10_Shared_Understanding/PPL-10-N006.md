# Question

A Scrum team and its product owner repeatedly leave refinement sessions believing they have agreed on the scope of upcoming stories, only to discover during the sprint that members held different understandings. The project manager wants to introduce practices that genuinely confirm shared understanding before the sprint starts. Which actions would best achieve this? (Select TWO.)

## Options

- **A**: Use example mapping or a similar technique to capture concrete examples and rules for each story during refinement
- **B**: Have the product owner restate the agreed scope at the end of the session and have team members confirm it in their own words
- **C**: Require every team member to sign a digital attendance log for each refinement session
- **D**: Increase the length of refinement sessions so more stories can be covered
- **E**: Record the refinement sessions so members can re-watch them if confused later

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

Example mapping (A) forces hidden disagreements into the open by grounding each story in concrete examples and rules, which is one of the most effective ways to build shared understanding. Having members restate the scope in their own words (B) verifies comprehension rather than assuming it, surfacing mismatches before the sprint. An attendance log (C) records presence, not understanding. Longer sessions (D) cover more stories but do nothing to confirm members actually agree on each one. Recordings (E) are a passive fallback that depend on members realising they were confused — they do not actively close the gap during refinement.
