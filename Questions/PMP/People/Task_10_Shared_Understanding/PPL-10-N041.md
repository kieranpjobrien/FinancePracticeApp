# Question

During backlog refinement on a Scrum team building a logistics platform, a user story draws estimates of 2, 8 and 20 points. The Scrum Master has the high and low estimators explain their thinking, and the discussion reveals the real reason for the gap: the developers disagree about whether the story includes integrating a third-party carrier API or merely stubbing it, and the product owner's own answer keeps shifting as the trade-offs are raised. A re-vote still comes back split. The product owner wants the story in tomorrow's sprint. What should the Scrum Master encourage the team to do next?

## Options

- **A**: Take the average of the latest estimates and commit the story, treating the residual spread as normal estimating noise
- **B**: Have the product owner decide on the spot whether the carrier integration is in or out, then re-estimate against that single agreed scope
- **C**: Split the story so the carrier integration is a separate item the team can size on its own terms
- **D**: Pull the story from the sprint and raise a spike to investigate the carrier API before it is estimated again

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The split persists not because the work is technically unknown but because the story's *scope* is still undecided — the same story means two different things to different people, and the product owner is the one who can collapse that ambiguity by ruling the carrier integration in or out. Once scope is fixed, a re-estimate can converge on a shared number, which is the understanding the team actually lacks. Averaging the spread (A) commits a number to a story nobody agrees the meaning of, baking the ambiguity into the plan. Splitting the story (C) is a sound technique and may well follow, but splitting before the product owner has decided what the story covers just defers the same in-or-out question to the new items. A spike (D) is for genuine technical unknowns; here the developers understand the API options perfectly well, so the blocker is a scope decision the product owner can make now, not research that needs a sprint.
