# Question

A project manager is starting a six-month predictive office relocation involving facilities, IT, HR, and three department heads who have never worked together. The charter is approved and a detailed plan exists. During execution, cross-functional decisions will arise where two department heads' priorities pull in opposite directions, and the project manager wants the group to be ready to handle those moments smoothly when they come. Which action best addresses this at the outset?

## Options

- **A**: Circulate a RACI matrix for the major decisions and ask each department head to confirm their assignments
- **B**: Meet each department head individually to understand their priorities and explain how the project will accommodate them
- **C**: Hold a facilitated kickoff that works the group through the escalation and tie-breaking approach for conflicting decisions until all confirm the same understanding
- **D**: Publish a decision log from the outset so every decision and its rationale are visible to all stakeholders

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The concern is specifically how contested cross-functional calls get resolved, and divergent unspoken assumptions about that are best surfaced and reconciled with the whole group present and confirming one approach. Option A is the closest miss: a RACI names who is responsible, accountable, consulted, and informed for each decision, but it does not define what happens when two accountable heads disagree, and people routinely confirm a RACI without aligning on the tie-breaking mechanism. Option B builds each head's individual understanding and the project manager's, yet the heads never hear each other, so incompatible assumptions between them survive. Option D improves transparency once decisions are being made but establishes nothing about how conflicts will be resolved beforehand. All four are reasonable; only C aligns the group itself on the resolution approach up front.
