# Question

Two months into a predictive project to build a community sports centre, the project manager discovers the local council has been assuming an Olympic-size pool, while the architectural drawings approved by the sponsor specify a smaller recreational pool. Construction of the pool has not started. The council part-funds the project; the sponsor holds the budget and signed the drawings. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Ask the architect for the cost and schedule impact of the larger pool so the decision can be made with numbers in hand
- **B**: Convene the council and sponsor to surface why each expected a different pool and agree a single documented requirement before any further design or costing
- **C**: Review the original requirements and approvals to confirm which pool the project is currently baselined and contractually obligated to deliver
- **D**: Raise a change request to enlarge the pool, since the council is a funder whose expectation should be met

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A genuine, two-month-old divergence in expectations between two parties who both hold power over the project must be reconciled before any costing, change, or contractual reading is meaningful, because the right outcome depends on understanding what each party actually needs and why. Option C is sound diligence and the approved drawings do define the baseline, but starting there frames a stakeholder-alignment problem as a compliance one; "you signed the small pool" can harden the council's position before anyone has heard its reasoning, and the council may have a legitimate need the sponsor never saw. Option A prices one option before the requirement is even agreed, and numbers presented early can entrench positions. Option D commits to the council's expectation before testing whether it is valid, funded, or required, risking an unnecessary and costly change. All four are legitimate steps; aligning the two stakeholders on the requirement first is what makes any of the others sound.
