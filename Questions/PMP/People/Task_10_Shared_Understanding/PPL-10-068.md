# Question

Twelve months into an 18-month programme, the PM realises the success measures in the business case are no longer specific enough to test at closure: they read like slogans ("improved customer experience"), with no baseline or target. The benefits owner has not pushed for more. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Proceed as planned and, at closure, interpret the existing phrasing in the most reasonable way the evidence allows, describing the outcomes in terms the data supports.
- **B**: Add specific measures at closure based on whatever data the programme has produced, so that reported benefits match the actual outcomes the team has been able to deliver.
- **C**: Let the benefits owner define the measures independently without PM involvement so that the PM preserves independence from the benefits reporting at the end of the programme.
- **D**: Work with the benefits owner and sponsor to define specific measures, baselines and targets now, track them for six months, and agree the closure basis in writing.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

PMI treats benefits measurement as a shared, defined, baselined commitment — vague slogans are a benefits-realisation failure waiting to happen, and the time to fix it is as early as possible. (A) lets vague phrasing be retrofitted to whatever happened, which is not measurement. (B) is outcome-to-measure fitting, which destroys the integrity of reported benefits. (C) misreads independence — the PM is accountable for ensuring benefits are measurable, not separated from the conversation.
