# Question

In a scaled agile programme delivering a banking platform, three teams consume a shared data contract, and each has implemented it against a slightly different reading, causing integration failures every few sprints. Each team is confident its own interpretation is right. The contract is genuinely under-specified in places and will keep evolving as the platform grows. The project manager wants a durable shared understanding of the contract across the teams. What is the best approach?

## Options

- **A**: Convene the three teams to jointly define the contract with concrete examples that resolve the ambiguous cases, and agree how they will keep that shared definition current as it changes
- **B**: Have the teams nominate a small cross-team working group that owns the contract specification and circulates an authoritative version for the teams to implement
- **C**: Stand up a shared contract-test suite, agreed by all three teams, that runs on every integration so any divergence from the contract is caught immediately
- **D**: Designate a lead architect to define the contract and approve all changes, giving the teams a single authoritative source to build against

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The failures come from three teams holding genuinely divergent readings of an under-specified contract, so a durable fix has to make them converge on the same understanding and keep converging as it evolves; jointly working the ambiguous cases with concrete examples builds that shared understanding in the teams themselves and gives them a way to maintain it. Option B is a real scaling pattern and better than nothing, but a working group that hands down an "authoritative version" still transmits a specification the other teams may read differently, recreating the interpretation gap one level up unless they also reconcile understanding directly. Option C is valuable and worth doing, but tests detect divergence after it is built; they enforce the contract without ensuring the teams comprehend it, so the same misreadings keep surfacing as failures to fix. Option D gives a single source of truth, yet a contract defined by one architect and approved top-down does not guarantee three teams understand it the same way, and it adds a change bottleneck. All four are legitimate governance moves; only A produces shared understanding the teams own rather than authority they merely follow.
