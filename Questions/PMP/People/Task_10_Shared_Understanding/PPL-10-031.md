# Question

An integration project links five systems owned by different teams. In design discussions, the same word — say, "transaction" — means slightly different things in each system, and the teams have started arguing past each other. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Adopt the dominant team's vocabulary as the project standard and require the other teams to translate their terms.
- **B**: Limit cross-team meetings and have each team document their interface contracts in writing for the others to review.
- **C**: Run collaborative workshops to build a shared glossary, agree naming conventions for integration points, and validate definitions with concrete examples.
- **D**: Assign a senior architect as the single point of integration design authority and have all teams defer to their definitions.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Cross-team integration depends on a shared vocabulary that neither team owns alone; collaborative glossary work surfaces hidden distinctions and produces definitions everyone agrees to. (C) does this. (A) imposes one team's worldview and loses the legitimate distinctions the other teams encode in their language. (B) reduces the meetings that are needed for the shared model to form and assumes written contracts will somehow carry the meaning that face-to-face discussion is currently failing to. (D) elevates one expert and bypasses the dialogue that produces real alignment, leaving the other teams disengaged.
