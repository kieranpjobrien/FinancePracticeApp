# Question

A project manager is overseeing a fixed-price contract with an external supplier. A dispute has arisen: the supplier insists a particular feature is out of scope and an extra, while the project manager's team is certain it was included. Both sides point to the same statement of work, reading one clause oppositely. Work on that feature has paused while the rest of the contract proceeds. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Have both parties exchange written statements of their interpretation and the supporting clauses, then review them side by side to pinpoint exactly where the readings diverge
- **B**: Convene a working session with the supplier's delivery lead to talk through the disputed clause and reach a shared view of what the requirement was meant to cover
- **C**: Ask the team that wrote the statement of work to confirm the originally intended meaning, then put that interpretation to the supplier for agreement
- **D**: Log the dispute as a formal issue and request the supplier's contract manager and your procurement lead to jointly determine the correct interpretation

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The clause is being read two opposite ways, which is a shared-understanding failure, and the most direct first step is a live working conversation with the people actually delivering the work, where each side can explain its reasoning, test the other's, and converge on what the requirement was meant to cover before the matter is escalated or formalised. Exchanging written interpretations and comparing them (A) is a reasonable way to expose where the readings differ, but trading documents about a document that is already being read two ways tends to entrench positions and lacks the back-and-forth that actually resolves meaning. Asking the authoring team to confirm intent and then presenting that to the supplier (C) privileges one side's reading as the starting point rather than building a genuinely joint understanding, and invites the supplier to dig in against an interpretation handed to them. Routing it to the contract and procurement managers to determine (D) is a legitimate next step if the delivery-level conversation fails, but moving to a formal contractual determination first escalates a disagreement the two working parties have not yet tried to talk through, and treats a meaning gap as a contract-administration matter before it needs to be.
