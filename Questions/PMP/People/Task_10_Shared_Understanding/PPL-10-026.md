# Question

Stakeholders signed off the scope statement four weeks ago, but during execution they keep saying "I assumed feature X was included." The PM has the signed scope to show the contrary. What should the PM do on the next project to prevent this?

## Options

- **A**: Insist on more detailed written scope statements with each feature explicitly listed in or out of scope.
- **B**: Reduce the initial project scope so stakeholders are less likely to be surprised by eventual exclusions.
- **C**: Require multi-level sign-off so each layer of stakeholder formally approves the scope statement together.
- **D**: Walk stakeholders through visual artefacts to validate understanding of what is in and out before sign-off.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The pattern of "signed but surprised" means the document didn't transfer the picture in stakeholders' heads to the team or vice versa; visual artefacts make scope concrete in a way text struggles to. (D) attacks the comprehension gap. (A) adds words to a medium that already failed and assumes the issue was missing detail rather than missing shared understanding. (C) adds approval layers without changing whether anyone actually understood what they signed. (B) trims the work to mask the symptom and shrinks business value to avoid an avoidable problem.
