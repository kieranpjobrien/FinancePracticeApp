# Question

A technical project manager presented a network migration plan to non-technical business managers using acronyms and engineering terminology. The managers approved it, but their follow-up questions reveal they misunderstood which of their teams' systems will and will not be affected, and when. The migration starts in three weeks. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Reconvene the managers, walk through the migration in plain language using a per-team impact view of what changes and when, and have each manager confirm the impact on their own area in their own words
- **B**: Re-present the plan in plain business language with concrete examples, and ask whether there are any further questions before proceeding
- **C**: Send each manager a tailored written summary of how the migration affects their team and ask them to confirm they have read it
- **D**: Produce a plain-language FAQ and glossary addressing the points the follow-up questions revealed they had misunderstood

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The managers approved something they did not understand, so the project manager must both communicate in their language *and* verify that understanding has actually landed for each decision-maker — having each manager restate the impact on their own area in their own words is what exposes any remaining gap rather than assuming it has closed. Re-presenting in plain language and asking for questions (B) is a real improvement but "any questions?" tends to draw silence and confirms nothing; managers who misunderstand often do not know what to ask. A tailored written summary (C) communicates the impact but reading-and-confirming is passive, and these are the same managers who already approved without grasping the detail. An FAQ and glossary (D) addresses the specific points raised but leaves the burden on the audience and still does not check that the corrected understanding is shared. Active, confirmed restatement is what turns a one-way correction into genuine shared understanding among the people making the decision.
