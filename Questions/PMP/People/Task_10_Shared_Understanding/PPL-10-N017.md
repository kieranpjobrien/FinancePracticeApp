# Question

On a predictive project to replace a claims-processing system, business users have approved a detailed written requirements specification, but the project manager senses from their hesitant questions that they cannot fully picture how the future system will work. The PM wants to confirm the users and the team truly share the same understanding before development locks in. Which approach best achieves this?

## Options

- **A**: Run a facilitated workshop where the team walks the users through each requirement and the users restate in their own words how the system will behave
- **B**: Build low-fidelity wireframes or a clickable prototype of the key workflows and walk the users through them to confirm the design matches their intent
- **C**: Develop detailed scenario walkthroughs and use cases describing step by step how users will perform key tasks, and review them with the users
- **D**: Build the highest-risk workflow first as a working slice and demonstrate it to the users for feedback before building the rest

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The users can approve text but cannot visualise the resulting system, so the best approach gives them something concrete to react to early and cheaply. Low-fidelity wireframes or a clickable prototype (B) make the design tangible without committing build effort, letting users recognise mismatches they could never spot in prose. A requirements-restating workshop (A) is valuable but still operates on abstract descriptions — restating words they could not visualise does not overcome the visualisation gap. Scenario walkthroughs and use cases (C) are more concrete than the spec but remain narrative text the users must mentally render, which is the very step they are struggling with. Building a real working slice first (D) does produce something concrete, but constructing production functionality to test understanding is far more expensive and slower than a throwaway prototype, and is better reserved for validating high technical risk than for confirming requirements comprehension. B confirms shared understanding earliest and at least cost.
