# Question

A project team optimises for technical excellence and resists deadline pressure; the business stakeholders optimise for time-to-market and resist anything that delays launch. Each side feels the other doesn't understand what success means. How should the PM build shared understanding?

## Options

- **A**: Ask the sponsor to make a binding decision on whether quality or speed wins, and communicate it as the project's success criterion.
- **B**: Allow each side to drive their own areas of the project to keep both perspectives represented in the outcome.
- **C**: Facilitate a joint conversation that surfaces the trade-offs each side actually faces and produces a shared definition of "good enough" success.
- **D**: Have the team and stakeholders agree to escalate any quality-versus-speed disagreement to the steering committee.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

PMI's servant-leadership and stakeholder-engagement principles say the PM should bring the parties together and help them build a shared model of success rather than picking a winner or escalating. (C) treats both perspectives as legitimate and ends with a shared agreement on trade-offs. (A) imposes a decision without the dialogue that creates buy-in, and the losing side will keep relitigating it. (B) institutionalises the conflict by giving each side their own turf. (D) routes the work the PM should be doing — facilitating alignment — to the steering committee, which is escalation without first attempting resolution.
