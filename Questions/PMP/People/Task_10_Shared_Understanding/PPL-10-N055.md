# Question

A newly formed agile team has just finished a turbulent first sprint. In the retrospective, members describe the same events in incompatible ways — what one called "helpful initiative" another called "ignoring our agreement". There is clearly no common picture of how the team is meant to work together. What should the project manager facilitate next?

## Options

- **A**: A working-agreement session where the team co-creates and records explicit norms for how they collaborate and make decisions
- **B**: A facilitated discussion of the specific clashes from the sprint to agree, case by case, what should have happened
- **C**: A roles-and-responsibilities mapping (e.g. a RACI) so it is clear who owns and decides what
- **D**: Adoption of a recognised team working-agreement template that the team reviews and adjusts to fit

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The members are interpreting the same behaviour in opposite ways because the team has no agreed norms yet, and co-creating an explicit working agreement builds the common picture while giving the team ownership of standards they will actually honour. Debating the specific clashes case by case (B) is a useful input and may feed the agreement, but resolving individual incidents does not establish the general norms needed to prevent the next, different clash. Mapping roles and responsibilities (C) clarifies who owns what — valuable, but a narrower slice than the broader question of how the team collaborates, communicates and decides, which is where the divergent interpretations arose. Adopting and tailoring a template (D) is a reasonable accelerator, but for a brand-new team starting from a borrowed document yields weaker ownership and understanding than building the agreement themselves; the act of co-creating is what makes the norms shared.
