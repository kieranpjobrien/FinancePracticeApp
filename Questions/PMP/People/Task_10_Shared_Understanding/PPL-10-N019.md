# Question

A project manager wants to deliberately build shared understanding at different points across a hybrid delivery. Match each situation to the technique that most directly creates a common interpretation among the people involved.

## Items

- **1**: A new cross-functional team is unclear how the day-to-day pieces of the system fit together and who depends on whom
- **2**: A user story's wording is interpreted differently by the developer and the tester, and they need to agree exactly how it should behave
- **3**: Two business units disagree on what a key term in the requirements actually means, each citing their own reading

## Matches

- **A**: Facilitate a session to surface each party's assumptions and jointly agree a single explicit definition
- **B**: Run a collaborative modelling or system-mapping workshop so the team builds one shared picture of the whole
- **C**: Write concrete worked examples of expected behaviour and confirm them together before development

## Answer

1-B, 2-C, 3-A

## Explanation

Each technique targets a different kind of gap. A team that cannot see how the parts and dependencies fit needs a shared visual model of the whole, so collaborative system mapping (B) fits item 1. A developer and tester disagreeing on precise behaviour for one story are best aligned with concrete worked examples that pin down expected behaviour (C), matching item 2. Two units disputing the meaning of a term are in a definitional conflict, resolved by surfacing assumptions and agreeing one explicit definition (A), matching item 3. Swapping them — for example, using example mapping for a whole-system orientation, or a system map to settle a single term — would address the wrong scale of misunderstanding.
