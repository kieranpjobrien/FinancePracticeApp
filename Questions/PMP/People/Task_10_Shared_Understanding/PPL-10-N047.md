# Question

On an agile e-commerce team, developers repeatedly finish a story and only discover at review that the product owner expected edge-case behaviour the developers never thought to ask about. The product owner already writes acceptance criteria, but they list the happy-path outcomes and the surprises are always in the exceptions and boundaries no one raised. What practice would most directly reduce these surprises?

## Options

- **A**: Hold three-amigos refinement where a developer, a tester and the product owner work each story through concrete examples, deliberately probing edge cases together
- **B**: Have the product owner expand the acceptance criteria to enumerate the edge cases before refinement
- **C**: Ask the testers to derive edge-case test scenarios from each story and review them with the product owner before development
- **D**: Adopt a story template that requires an explicit "edge cases and exceptions" section for every story

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The gap is not the absence of acceptance criteria but that the exceptions live in nobody's head until someone with a different perspective asks; a three-amigos session deliberately puts the developer's "what if" instinct, the tester's failure-mode thinking and the product owner's intent in the same conversation, working concrete examples so the unknown unknowns surface and become shared before any code is written. Having the product owner enumerate edge cases alone (B) still depends on one person foreseeing exceptions they may not think of, which is the exact limitation that failed. Testers deriving scenarios and reviewing them (C) brings in failure-mode thinking but as a downstream check the developers building the story are not part of, so the developers can still hold a different picture. A template section for edge cases (D) prompts the question but, filled in by one author, again relies on a single perspective rather than the cross-role conversation that actually exposes the mismatches.
