# Question

Over several sprints on a media-publishing platform, a team's retrospectives keep surfacing the same complaint: features get reworked because "we did not all understand what was being asked." The team raises it each time as a fresh annoyance and moves on without connecting the instances. As the project manager facilitating the retrospective, what is the most effective thing to do?

## Options

- **A**: Guide the team to see that the complaint is recurring, choose one concrete practice change to address it, and agree to review its effect at the next retrospective
- **B**: Facilitate a root-cause analysis of the rework this sprint so the team identifies why the misunderstanding happened before deciding what to change
- **C**: Propose the team adopt confirming intent with worked examples in refinement, the standard remedy for this kind of misunderstanding, and add it as an action
- **D**: Have the team raise the recurring rework with the product owner, since clearer upstream stories would prevent much of the misunderstanding

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The team's real failure is treating a systemic pattern as isolated incidents, so the facilitator's highest-value move is to make the pattern visible, have the team commit to one specific change it owns, and close the loop by checking the effect next time, which is exactly what makes a retrospective improve anything. Option B is genuinely good facilitation and often a step within A, but root-causing only this sprint's instance, without first naming that the problem recurs, risks producing yet another one-off fix; the missing ingredient here is recognising and tracking the pattern, not analysing a single occurrence. Option C jumps to a sound remedy but supplies it as the project manager's prescription rather than letting the team diagnose and own the change, and an action adopted without the team seeing why it matters tends not to stick or get reviewed. Option D may help, but it offloads the cause onto the product owner before the team examines its own part in the misunderstanding, and clearer stories alone do not guarantee shared interpretation. All four are reasonable facilitator actions; only A turns a recurring complaint into an owned, verifiable improvement.
