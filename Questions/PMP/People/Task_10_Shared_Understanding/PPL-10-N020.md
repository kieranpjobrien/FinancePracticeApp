# Question

On a predictive project to replace a retailer's point-of-sale terminals, the requirements were signed off by store-operations managers months ago. As the build nears completion, the project manager senses the managers pictured a different workflow than what the document literally specifies, though no one can point to a specific error. The managers are busy and reluctant to re-engage with a 90-page document. What is the best way to confirm a shared understanding before the build is finalised?

## Options

- **A**: Hold a focused workshop on only the highest-risk workflows, walking the managers through the documented steps and asking them to describe how they expected each to work
- **B**: Have a business analyst produce annotated screen-flow diagrams of the key workflows and review them with the managers in a short session
- **C**: Walk the managers through an interactive demonstration of the near-complete key workflows and have them confirm or correct the behaviour against what they expect
- **D**: Demonstrate the build to the managers and, for any workflow they flag, trace it back to the signed requirement to determine whether it is a build defect or a misunderstanding

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The risk is a gap between what the managers imagined and what the text specifies, and with the build nearly complete the most direct confirmation is to let them experience the actual behaviour. An interactive demonstration of the near-complete workflows (C) makes the real system tangible across the full set of key workflows, so the managers can recognise a mismatch they could never see in prose — and the working software already exists, so this costs little. Limiting the session to high-risk workflows (A) is efficient but narrows the check and may miss a divergence in a workflow not pre-judged as risky, where managers' silent assumptions are just as likely to differ. Tracing flagged items to the signed requirement (D) is a sound follow-up once a mismatch is found, but it presumes the managers can already articulate what is wrong, which they cannot yet. Annotated diagrams (B) are more concrete than the document but still require the managers to translate static representations into a mental model — the exact step that failed the first time. C surfaces the divergence most reliably and exploits work already done.
