# Question

A predictive infrastructure project is two months from a major milestone. The project manager has been reporting "on track" against a scope baseline signed off at initiation. In a steering meeting the sponsor expresses surprise and frustration, saying the delivered design "is not at all what the business expected" and points to capabilities that were never in the baseline but were, in the sponsor's mind, always assumed. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Hold a working session with the sponsor and key business stakeholders to walk through the baseline and capture where their expectations and the documented scope diverge
- **B**: Raise a change request for the additional capabilities and route it through the change control board
- **C**: Commission a gap analysis comparing the current design against the sponsor's stated expectations and present the findings at the next steering meeting
- **D**: Re-plan the remaining work to absorb the expected capabilities before the milestone is missed

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The root issue is a gap between the sponsor's mental model of the project and the documented scope, and that gap must be understood *with the sponsor* before any corrective action can be sensibly scoped. A working session (A) surfaces exactly where and why understanding diverged and rebuilds a shared picture face to face. A gap analysis (C) is genuinely useful and gets at the same divergence, but doing it as a desk study delivered later keeps the project manager interpreting the sponsor's expectations second-hand and defers the direct alignment conversation by a full reporting cycle when the relationship is already strained. Jumping to a change request (B) or a re-plan (D) presumes you already know which expected capabilities are real, agreed and in scope — the very thing the working session exists to establish — so both act on an understanding that has not yet been rebuilt.
