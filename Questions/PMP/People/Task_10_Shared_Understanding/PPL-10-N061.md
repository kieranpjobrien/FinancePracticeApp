# Question

During a refinement session for a mobile banking app, a developer says she can start a payments-limit story straight away, while a tester insists it is far too vague to estimate. The product owner says the intent is plain to him and the backlog is already crowded for the upcoming sprint. The story sits near the top of the priority order. What should the agile team do first?

## Options

- **A**: Work through the story together now until they agree its scope and acceptance criteria
- **B**: Ask the product owner to talk the tester through the intent, then let the developer pull it in
- **C**: Co-write two or three concrete examples of the expected behaviour and attach them to the story
- **D**: Pull the story into the sprint and have the developer and tester resolve the detail as the first task

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Three people reading the same story three ways is a divergence the team must close before commitment, and the lever that closes it is the whole team agreeing scope and acceptance criteria together (A) — that is the conversation refinement exists for, and it reconciles all three views at once rather than any single pair. Having the product owner brief the tester (B) is faster but treats the gap as the tester's alone; it leaves the product owner's "obvious" intent untested against the developer's read and can reproduce the same ambiguity one-way. Co-writing concrete examples (C) is a strong technique and may well form part of the conversation, but reaching for it first presumes the disagreement is only about behaviour in edge cases when the parties have not yet agreed the story's basic scope. Pulling it in and resolving detail as the first sprint task (D) honours the crowded backlog and the developer's readiness, but it moves an unresolved divergence inside the commitment boundary, where a wrong shared assumption becomes rework rather than a refinement note.
