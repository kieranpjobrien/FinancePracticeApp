# Question

A project manager is starting a cross-departmental process improvement initiative. Stakeholders from finance, operations and IT have each described the project's purpose in their own words, emphasising quite different outcomes. The sponsor has already drafted a one-line objective. The project manager has time before detailed planning to do one of the following first. Which best builds a genuinely shared understanding of the project's direction?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate a workshop where the three departments and the sponsor reconcile their views into one agreed vision and set of objectives they all sign up to
- **B**: Interview each department in turn, synthesise their inputs into a draft vision, and circulate it for the group to confirm
- **C**: Adopt the sponsor's drafted objective as the anchor and ask the three departments to align their outcomes to it
- **D**: Facilitate a workshop for the three departments to agree a vision, then present the agreed vision to the sponsor for endorsement

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Because the divergence spans the departments *and* the sponsor, the understanding has to be built with all of them in the room so the single vision is co-owned and no key party is reconciling to a picture they did not help shape. Synthesising from interviews (B) is collaborative in input but the project manager authors the vision and the parties merely confirm it, which secures less genuine convergence than building it together. Anchoring on the sponsor's draft (C) privileges one view and asks the departments to bend to it rather than truly aligning. Aligning the departments first and only then presenting to the sponsor (D) risks the sponsor — the person who owns the mandate — having to react to a finished vision rather than helping form it, reopening the divergence at the top. Including every diverging party in the same co-creation is what makes the understanding shared rather than imposed.
