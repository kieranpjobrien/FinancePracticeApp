# Question

Two senior stakeholders on a hospital systems migration disagree sharply about what "go-live readiness" means. The clinical director believes it means zero open defects; the IT director believes it means all critical-path defects closed with workarounds documented for the rest. Each has cited the project charter to support their position, and the disagreement is now blocking the schedule. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Escalate to the project sponsor, who owns the charter, to confirm the intended readiness definition and unblock the schedule
- **B**: Bring both stakeholders together to surface the assumptions behind each interpretation and jointly define readiness criteria
- **C**: Convene a small working group to draft explicit, measurable readiness criteria and circulate them to both stakeholders for sign-off
- **D**: Propose readiness criteria under which all high-severity defects are closed and lower-severity ones are documented with workarounds

## Answer

B

## Explanation

All four are legitimate ways to settle the dispute, so the discriminator is what comes first when two owners hold genuinely different meanings. The disagreement is about interpretation, not authority or wording, so the priority is to expose why each stakeholder reads readiness as they do and let them build a shared definition together (B); this both resolves the immediate block and prevents the next ambiguity from re-igniting it. Escalating to the sponsor (A) can produce a fast ruling, but a definition imposed over two senior owners who never aligned leaves residual conflict that resurfaces at go-live — reasonable as a fallback, not as the first move. Drafting criteria in a working group for sign-off (C) skips straight to authoring an answer before the two principals' underlying concerns are understood, so the "sign-off" risks being reluctant rather than genuine. Proposing specific criteria (D) — which happens to match the IT director's position — is a plausible outcome but pre-empts the clinical director's reasoning and is likely to read as taking sides. B understands before deciding, which the others reverse.
