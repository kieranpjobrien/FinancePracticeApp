# Question

Two senior stakeholders on a predictive airport-terminal refurbishment repeatedly clash in meetings, each convinced the other is undermining their objectives. The project manager has already met each one separately and understands both positions well, and has already set ground rules that keep the meetings civil, yet the clashes continue because neither stakeholder understands the other's actual goals and constraints. Which two actions now most directly build the mutual understanding needed to reduce the conflict? (Choose two.)

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate a joint session where each stakeholder explains their own objectives and constraints and then plays back what they understood of the other's
- **B**: Share with each stakeholder a validated summary of the other's interests and where the two sets of interests actually overlap, and confirm they take it on board
- **C**: Continue meeting each stakeholder separately and have the project manager relay each one's position to the other between meetings
- **D**: Escalate the clash to the sponsor and ask the sponsor to direct the two stakeholders to align
- **E**: Reinforce the meeting ground rules and add a neutral chair so future clashes are contained when they arise

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

The project manager already holds both positions and the meetings are already civil, so the remaining gap is purely that the two stakeholders do not understand each other; the right actions transfer that understanding between them. A joint articulate-and-playback session (A) does this live, and a validated, shared summary of each other's interests and overlaps (B) does it in a form each must take on board. Option C keeps the project manager as a permanent go-between, which can perpetuate misunderstanding through second-hand relay and never lets the stakeholders build a direct understanding; it also does not scale. Option D pushes for an imposed alignment from above without either party actually understanding the other, so the underlying friction survives the directive. Option E doubles down on containing the symptom — the meetings are already civil — without addressing why the two keep clashing. C, D, and E are all things a project manager might do, but only A and B close the comprehension gap that is the actual cause.
