# Question

An agile team complains that stories regularly stall mid-sprint because, once development starts, the developers realise they and the product owner pictured the feature differently and have to stop and re-discuss. Refinement does happen, but stories are pulled into sprints while the team still has open questions, and everyone assumes the gaps will sort themselves out during the sprint. The Scrum Master wants a durable fix. What is the best approach?

## Options

- **A**: Agree a Definition of Ready, so a story may be pulled into a sprint only once the team confirms it has a shared understanding and no open questions
- **B**: Restructure refinement so each story is worked through concrete examples until the team and product owner agree how it should behave
- **C**: Ask the product owner to write fuller, more detailed acceptance criteria for every story before it reaches refinement
- **D**: Have the team review each candidate story against a readiness checklist that the product owner completes ahead of sprint planning

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The breakdown is that stories cross into a sprint while shared understanding is still incomplete, so the durable fix is a gate that *prevents commitment* until that understanding exists and is confirmed — exactly what a Definition of Ready does, by making "we all understand this and have no open questions" the explicit, agreed condition for sprint entry. Working stories through concrete examples (B) is a powerful way to build understanding and may well be how the team satisfies the gate, but improving the refinement conversation does not by itself stop an under-understood story being pulled in anyway, which is the specific failure here. Fuller acceptance criteria from the product owner (C) put more detail on paper but are authored by one party, so the team can still picture the story differently and the gap survives the better document. A readiness checklist the product owner completes alone (D) introduces a gate but one owned by a single person, so it certifies the product owner's view of readiness rather than the *shared* readiness across the team — and shared understanding is precisely what was missing.
