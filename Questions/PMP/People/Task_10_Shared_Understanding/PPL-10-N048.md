# Question

A globally distributed team rarely meets live and makes most decisions in a chat channel. A significant architectural decision was discussed there and the project manager believed it was agreed because no one objected. A week later, two engineers in another region began building against a different assumption, saying they "didn't take silence as agreement". The project manager wants a protocol that produces durable shared decisions without forcing everyone into live calls. Which is the best approach?

## Options

- **A**: For each significant decision, post it as a proposal with a response deadline and require an explicit acknowledgement (agree / disagree / need-to-discuss) from every affected party before it is considered decided
- **B**: For each significant decision, set a response deadline and treat no objection by the deadline as agreement, having explicitly told the team that silence will count as consent
- **C**: Assign a single accountable decision-owner per area who decides after inviting input in the channel, removing reliance on group consensus
- **D**: Keep deciding in chat but record every decision in a shared decision log so anyone building against it can check the agreed position

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The failure is that agreement was inferred from silence in a channel where people may simply not have read the thread, so the fix must make each affected party's position observable; requiring an explicit acknowledgement from everyone affected, against a deadline, turns "we all agree" into something visible rather than assumed. A "silence equals consent" rule (B) is a real and sometimes-used convention, but it still lets a decision pass with people who never engaged — exactly the situation that bit the team — and is weaker precisely where the stakes are high. A single decision-owner (C) is legitimate for many decisions and removes consensus ambiguity, but it replaces shared understanding with a one-person call and does not ensure the people who must *build* to the decision actually understood and bought into it. A decision log (D) is valuable and worth doing, yet it only records the conclusion; it does not establish that everyone agreed in the first place, so on its own it documents the same ambiguity that caused the problem.
