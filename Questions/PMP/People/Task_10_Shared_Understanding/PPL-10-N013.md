# Question

A project manager is preparing to facilitate a workshop to resolve conflicting understandings of the project's scope among five stakeholders from finance, operations, and IT. Which actions would most help the workshop produce a genuinely shared understanding rather than a surface-level truce? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Have each stakeholder articulate their current understanding of the scope and the reasoning behind it before any debate
- **B**: Capture points of agreement and disagreement visibly so the group works from the same picture
- **C**: Decide the contested scope items in advance and present them for ratification to save time
- **D**: Close the workshop by having stakeholders restate the agreed scope in their own words and confirm it
- **E**: Allow the most senior stakeholder to set the direction whenever the group reaches an impasse

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

Surfacing each stakeholder's understanding and reasoning (A) exposes the hidden assumptions driving the conflict, which is prerequisite to resolving it. Making agreements and disagreements visible (B) keeps everyone working from one shared representation rather than separate mental models. Closing with stakeholders restating the scope in their own words (D) verifies that the alignment is real and not merely polite nodding. Pre-deciding contested items (C) produces ratification without genuine understanding and undermines the workshop's purpose. Letting seniority break impasses (E) substitutes authority for shared understanding, leaving the underlying disagreement unresolved and likely to resurface.
