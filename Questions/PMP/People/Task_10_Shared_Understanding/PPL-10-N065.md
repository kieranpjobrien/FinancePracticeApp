# Question

A utilities company has engaged a vendor to replace its meter-reading system under a fixed-scope contract. Reviewing the signed requirements, the project manager spots a clause requiring the system to support "real-time" readings; the internal team reads this as sub-second, while informal remarks from the vendor suggest they mean readings refreshed every few hours. Both have proceeded as if their interpretation is correct, and design work is already under way on each side. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Have the contract administrator give the vendor formal written notice of the company's required interpretation under the clause
- **B**: Ask the vendor to confirm in writing what their delivery will provide for that requirement
- **C**: Bring both parties together to agree and document a single, testable definition of the requirement
- **D**: Raise a change request to replace "real-time" with a specific refresh-rate target and route it through change control

## Answer

C

## Explanation

One word carries two materially different meanings and each side is building to its own, so the first need is a common, verifiable picture — convening both parties to agree and document a single testable definition (C) reconciles the divergence and produces a basis everyone can be held to at acceptance. Issuing formal notice of the company's reading (A) asserts a contractual position before the parties have even confirmed they mean different things; the vendor priced against another meaning, so leading with notice invites a claim rather than resolving the misunderstanding. Asking the vendor to confirm their interpretation in writing (B) is a reasonable early step and surfaces the gap, but it captures only one side's view and documents the divergence rather than closing it. Raising a change request now (D) presumes a particular answer and pushes cost and scope changes through governance before the parties have agreed what was originally intended — sequencing matters, and the definition has to be settled before deciding whether anything actually needs to change.
