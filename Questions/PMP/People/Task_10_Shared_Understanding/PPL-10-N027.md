# Question

On a streaming-media analytics product, the team pulls a mix of loosely related stories into each sprint and completes most of them, but the product owner is repeatedly frustrated that the sprint produces nothing coherent to demonstrate to stakeholders. The team feels it delivered exactly what it committed to and does not see the problem. As the project manager, what is the most effective way to close the gap between the team and the product owner?

## Options

- **A**: Reduce the sprint's story count so the team concentrates on a smaller, more cohesive slice of work
- **B**: Have the product owner rank the backlog so the highest-value related items rise to the top and get pulled together
- **C**: Ask the product owner to review and approve the proposed sprint backlog before planning closes each time
- **D**: Coach the team and product owner to set one clear sprint goal each planning session that expresses the outcome the selected stories should jointly achieve

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The team and product owner disagree because they do not share an understanding of what the sprint is meant to achieve; a sprint goal supplies that shared intent and gives the team a basis to judge whether a coherent outcome will result. Option B improves what gets prioritised, and ordering by value with related items together is a real and tempting move, but a well-ordered backlog can still be sliced into an incoherent sprint if no unifying outcome is named. Option C adds an approval checkpoint that may catch an incoherent plan, but it makes the product owner the gatekeeper rather than creating shared purpose the team owns, and approval without a stated goal still leaves "coherent" undefined. Option A can raise focus, but smaller does not mean coherent; the team could still complete a tidy set of unrelated items. All four are plausible agile moves; only D builds the shared sense of purpose that is actually missing.
