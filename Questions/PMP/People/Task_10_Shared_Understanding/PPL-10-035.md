# Question

The PM is facilitating a priority-setting workshop. Two senior stakeholders are dominating air time; three others have barely spoken. The PM suspects the quiet stakeholders disagree but won't say so in front of the dominant pair. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Take silence as alignment, conclude the workshop on time, and follow up individually with the quiet stakeholders later.
- **B**: Ask the dominant stakeholders to step out of the room while the others contribute, then bring them back to share the conclusions.
- **C**: Pause the workshop, speak privately with the quiet stakeholders during the break, and reflect their views back into the discussion afterwards.
- **D**: Use silent-writing first, anonymous polling, or directed invitations to draw out every view, then surface the differences openly.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

PMI expects facilitation techniques that surface every voice in real time so genuine alignment can be achieved. (D) does this without isolating anyone or creating side channels. (A) treats silence as consent, which it almost never is in this dynamic, and pushes resolution to private conversations that don't build group understanding. (C) breaks the workshop momentum and still relies on the PM relaying others' views rather than the stakeholders speaking for themselves. (B) excludes some stakeholders from a shared discussion, which creates trust problems and risks making the dominant pair feel ambushed.
