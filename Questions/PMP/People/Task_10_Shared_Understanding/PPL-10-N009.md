# Question

In a project status meeting, a functional manager and the project manager have been arguing for several minutes about whether a deliverable is "complete". It emerges that the manager means code-complete while the PM means tested and deployed. Other attendees are waiting and tension is rising. What is the best immediate action for the project manager?

## Options

- **A**: Name the misunderstanding aloud — that the two are using "complete" to mean different states — and propose the group agree a shared set of status terms before continuing
- **B**: Acknowledge the manager's meaning is valid, restate the PM's, and ask the group which definition the project should adopt as its standard going forward
- **C**: Suggest the project adopt the team's existing definition-of-done as the agreed meaning of "complete" and proceed on that basis
- **D**: Capture the two interpretations in the minutes and continue the status review, agreeing to settle the standard definition immediately after the meeting

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Once the clash is revealed to be definitional, every option here is a constructive, non-autocratic response, so the discriminator is which best restores shared understanding for the people in the room right now. Explicitly surfacing that "complete" is being used for two different states and inviting the group to align terms (A) makes the hidden mismatch visible to everyone and rebuilds a common vocabulary before more confusion compounds. Asking the group to pick a standard (B) jumps to choosing a winner before the participants have a shared grasp of what each definition actually entails — useful, but premature. Adopting the existing definition-of-done (C) imports a possibly-fit answer without confirming it covers what this dispute is about. Deferring to after the meeting (D) is reasonable but lets the remaining status review run on the very ambiguity that just derailed it. A names and dissolves the misunderstanding in the moment, which the others either skip past or postpone.
