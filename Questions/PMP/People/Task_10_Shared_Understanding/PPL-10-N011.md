# Question

A long requirements meeting with a new client covered many decisions, several of them nuanced trade-offs reached after debate. The project manager wants to confirm both sides genuinely left with the same understanding of what was agreed, not just a list of bullet points. Which step best achieves this?

## Options

- **A**: Send the client a written summary of the decisions and the reasoning behind each, asking them to confirm it matches their understanding or correct it
- **B**: Send the client the meeting minutes recording what was decided and ask them to confirm the record is accurate
- **C**: Distribute the summary to the client and project team together and ask anyone to raise points that look wrong
- **D**: Schedule a short follow-up call to verbally walk the client through the key decisions and confirm agreement

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four close the loop in some way, so the discriminator is which most reliably verifies a shared understanding of the nuanced trade-offs rather than just the conclusions. Minutes that record what was decided (B) let the client confirm the outcome but not the reasoning, so a divergence in why something was agreed can pass unnoticed and resurface later. Capturing the rationale alongside each decision and asking the client to confirm or correct it (A) tests the understanding itself, surfacing mismatches in interpretation, not just in record-keeping. Broadcasting to the whole team for open comment (C) dilutes ownership and relies on someone happening to spot an issue. A follow-up call (D) is a fine reinforcement but provides no written artefact the client has explicitly endorsed, and verbal "yes" in the moment often masks unconfirmed assumptions. A both documents and validates the meaning, which the others partially miss.
