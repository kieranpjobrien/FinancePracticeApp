# Question

An agile team building a renewable-energy monitoring dashboard has been given a product vision, but it is a one-line slogan ("help operators act before faults cost money"). The team can recite it, yet still builds technically impressive features the product owner says miss the actual customer need, because nobody can translate the slogan into concrete judgements about what matters. As the project manager, which three actions best turn this vision into shared understanding the team can actually act on? (Choose three.)

## Options

- **A**: Arrange for the team to observe or hear real operators describe the faults that cost them money and how they currently respond
- **B**: Facilitate the team and product owner to define a few concrete value measures the dashboard should improve, against which feature ideas can be judged
- **C**: Work through several recent feature decisions as a team, having the product owner explain which served the vision and why, to calibrate the team's judgement
- **D**: Have the product owner repeat and emphasise the vision statement at the start of every planning session
- **E**: Display the vision slogan prominently on the team's board so it stays front of mind during the sprint

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

The team already knows the words; what it lacks is the concrete grounding to apply them, so the useful actions make the vision operational. Hearing real operators' costly faults (A) gives the team the customer reality behind the slogan, defining concrete value measures (B) turns the vision into criteria features can be judged against, and calibrating on past decisions with the product owner's reasoning (C) trains the team's own judgement. Options D and E are the traps: both keep the same abstract slogan in front of the team more often, but repetition and a poster do not add any of the concrete meaning that is missing, so the team can recite the vision even more fluently and still misjudge value. The discriminator is not direct-versus-indirect but whether the action adds operational substance (A, B, C) or merely re-displays an abstraction the team has already memorised (D, E).
