# Question

At the kickoff for a hospital wing refurbishment, the project manager notices the clinical lead, the facilities team and the main contractor each describe the project's primary objective in subtly different terms — patient experience, energy efficiency and schedule certainty respectively. The charter, signed by all three, lists these as objectives but sets no order of precedence between them. The kickoff agenda still has scope and milestones to cover. What is the project manager's best immediate move?

## Options

- **A**: Pause the agenda and facilitate the group to agree one statement of priority among the three objectives now
- **B**: Speak with each lead separately after kickoff to understand the gap before deciding how to reconcile it
- **C**: Note the divergence and raise it with the sponsor, since ranking the objectives sets the project's value trade-offs
- **D**: Capture the three readings in the kickoff minutes and schedule a dedicated objectives workshop within the week

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The differing emphases are surfacing live, with the right people in the room and a charter that left precedence unstated, so facilitating them to agree one priority statement now (A) builds the shared understanding at the cheapest possible moment and stops the divergence from quietly steering early decisions. Speaking to each lead privately first (B) gathers nuance but trades away the chance to reconcile the views collectively while everyone is present, and risks the project manager brokering a position no one jointly owns. Escalating to the sponsor to rank the objectives (C) is a real consideration because the ordering does encode value trade-offs the sponsor may own — but raising it before the team has even attempted to align is premature, and a ruling handed down does not make these three understand each other. Deferring to a dedicated workshop (D) is defensible if the conflict were deep, yet it lets the misalignment persist for days and surrenders the assembled audience the project manager already has; the gap here is narrow enough to close in the room.
