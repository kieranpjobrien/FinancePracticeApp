# Question

A project manager is coaching an agile team and must pick the single most fitting technique for each of several understanding gaps. The gaps are similar enough that more than one technique could plausibly apply, so match each to the technique that most directly addresses its specific cause.

## Items

- **1**: Developers and the product owner keep disagreeing, sprint after sprint, on whether a story counts as finished
- **2**: For one story, two developers read the same written acceptance criteria and built different things
- **3**: Stakeholders give vague, shifting feedback because they cannot picture how the proposed feature will behave in use
- **4**: A new joiner keeps tripping over how the team operates — branching, review etiquette, when to ask versus decide — that longstanding members simply take for granted

## Matches

- **A**: Co-author concrete worked examples for the story to make its intent unambiguous
- **B**: Review a low-fidelity prototype together so reactions are grounded in something tangible
- **C**: Agree and post a shared definition of done
- **D**: Document and make visible the team's ground rules and working agreements

## Answer

1-C, 2-A, 3-B, 4-D

## Explanation

Each gap has a distinct cause that selects one best technique even though others look tempting. Recurring disagreement about completion is a team-wide standards gap, resolved by a shared definition of done (1-C), not by fixing any single story. Two developers diverging on one story's written criteria is a story-level intent gap, best closed by co-authoring concrete examples that pin down meaning (2-A); a definition of done would not disambiguate that particular story. Stakeholders who cannot visualise a feature need something tangible to react to, which a prototype provides (3-B); examples in text help less when the issue is visualisation. A newcomer making wrong assumptions about unwritten norms is a working-agreements gap, addressed by documenting and surfacing the ground rules (4-D). The discrimination is matching the technique to the precise kind of understanding that is missing.
