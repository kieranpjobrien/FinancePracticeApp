# Question

On a hybrid telecom network-upgrade programme, the dashboard shows "62% complete". In a governance meeting the engineering director reads this as 62% of sites physically upgraded, the finance lead as 62% of budget consumed, and the sponsor as 62% of business benefit realised. All have been making decisions on their own reading for weeks, and this is the third reporting metric to have caused such confusion. What should the project manager change?

## Options

- **A**: Replace the single percentage with three separate indicators for scope, cost and benefit
- **B**: Establish with stakeholders an agreed meaning and calculation for each reported metric, so a number means the same thing to everyone
- **C**: Add a clear definitions panel to the dashboard stating precisely what each figure measures and how it is derived
- **D**: Open each governance meeting by walking the group through what every reported metric means before discussing it

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A single number carried three private meanings and the pattern has now repeated three times, so the change that holds is to agree with stakeholders what each metric means and how it is derived (B) — definitions reached jointly are ones the parties internalise, and they cover every metric on the report and every decision taken between meetings, not just this dashboard. Splitting completion into three indicators (A) is a genuine improvement and directly addresses this instance, but it is a design fix for one figure; without agreed definitions the three new indicators are simply three more numbers open to the same divergence, and the recurring pattern would continue. A definitions panel (C) documents the meaning and is worth doing, yet a panel is something stakeholders can read past — much as a footnote let this run for weeks — so on its own it does not guarantee the shared understanding that agreeing the definitions together creates. Walking the group through the metrics each meeting (D) helps in the room but depends on the project manager repeating it indefinitely and leaves between-meeting reading exposed; it manages the symptom rather than settling the definitions once.
