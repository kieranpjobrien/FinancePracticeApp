# Question

A newly formed agile team building a hospital scheduling system is partway through its second sprint. The product owner attends refinement, writes clear acceptance criteria, and answers every question promptly, yet developers still occasionally build solutions the sponsor later says miss the point. The team gets along well, the acceptance criteria check out as accurate when reviewed, and the builds that miss are ones that technically satisfied them. As the project manager, what is the best step to reduce these misses?

## Options

- **A**: Have the product owner co-author concrete acceptance examples with the developers for every story during refinement, so each story's stated intent is pinned down before build
- **B**: Add a checkpoint where the developers walk the half-built increment past the product owner partway through each story to catch a drift before it is finished
- **C**: Facilitate the team and sponsor to articulate and confirm the product's underlying value priorities, so developers have a shared basis for the judgement calls a story cannot spell out
- **D**: Have the product owner sit with the developers during build for the first few sprints to steer the trade-off calls in real time

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The diagnostic detail is that refinement is already strong — clear criteria, a responsive product owner, a team that communicates — yet solutions still miss the point, which means the gap is not in the stated parts of a story but in the unstated judgement calls developers make while building, where no criterion reaches. A shared, confirmed sense of what the product values gives them a durable basis to make those calls the way the sponsor would. Option A is the strongest distractor and genuinely good practice, but it only sharpens each story's explicit intent; the misses arise precisely in the choices a story does not articulate, so tighter examples leave that gap open. Option B catches drift after it has happened on each story rather than equipping the team to choose well in the first place, and it adds a recurring inspection without building judgement. Option D embeds the product owner as a real-time crutch, fixing calls without growing the team's own understanding and not scaling past a few sprints. All four are reasonable, but only C closes the gap where it actually opens.
