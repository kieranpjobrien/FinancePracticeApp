# Question

A project manager is launching a six-month initiative with a newly assembled virtual team across four time zones who have never worked together. She wants to give the team the best chance of a shared understanding of goals, roles and ways of working from the outset. Which actions best support that aim? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate a kickoff where the team co-creates the goals, working agreements and communication norms
- **B**: Distribute a thorough project handbook covering goals, roles and process, and ask members to raise anything unclear
- **C**: Establish a shared, always-current information radiator (e.g. a project workspace) the whole team can see
- **D**: Hold short one-on-one onboarding calls with each member to brief them on goals and their role
- **E**: Confirm understanding of roles and decision rights explicitly rather than assuming people infer them

## Answer

A, C, E

## Explanation

For a brand-new virtual team, shared understanding is built by participation and visible, confirmed information: a co-creation kickoff (A), a shared information radiator keeping everyone on the same current picture (C), and explicit confirmation of roles and decision rights so nothing is left to inference (E). A handbook with an invitation to ask questions (B) is one-way and passive; comprehensive reading material is not the same as agreement, and members rarely surface the gaps they do not know they have. One-on-one onboarding calls (D) are interactive and genuinely helpful for individuals, but briefing each person separately builds a set of individual understandings, not the *shared* picture across a dispersed team — people never hear the same framing together or align with each other, which is precisely what a co-creation kickoff and a common radiator provide.
