# Question

A product owner communicated the release roadmap to senior stakeholders in a slide deck and considers them aligned. The project manager separately hears three stakeholders each describe what they expect from the next release, and all three differ from the roadmap and from each other. The release is six weeks away and the product owner is confident the deck settled it. What is the project manager's best first move?

## Options

- **A**: Privately brief the product owner on the three divergent expectations, then have the product owner convene the stakeholders to reconcile and agree what the release will deliver
- **B**: Convene the stakeholders and product owner together immediately to surface the differences and agree the release scope in one session
- **C**: Confirm with each of the three stakeholders directly what they expect and feed a consolidated picture to the product owner to address
- **D**: Raise the misalignment at the next steering committee so it is owned and resolved at governance level

## Answer

A

## Explanation

There is a real collective misalignment that must be reconciled with the stakeholders together — but the product owner, who owns the roadmap and the stakeholder relationship, currently believes everyone is aligned and has not seen the divergence the project manager has. Briefing the product owner first and then having them convene the reconciliation (A) respects that ownership, equips the product owner to lead the session credibly, and avoids the project manager appearing to ambush or undercut them. Calling everyone together immediately (B) gets the right people in the room but springs the conflict on a product owner who walks in thinking it is settled, risking a defensive, poorly-led session. Gathering each stakeholder's view and consolidating it for the product owner (C) usefully sharpens the picture, but staying in one-to-one mode leaves the stakeholders unaware of each other's competing expectations and defers the joint reconciliation the situation needs. Taking it to steering (D) escalates a problem the product owner has not yet been given a chance to own and resolve, and is disproportionate as a first move.
