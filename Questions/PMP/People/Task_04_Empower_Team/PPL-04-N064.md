# Question

Early in a predictive project, team members keep waiting for the project manager to approve routine technical choices that sit well within their expertise, which is slowing progress. The PM stated at kick-off that "these calls are yours to make", and in conversation most can readily name which decisions are theirs. In one-to-ones, several describe seeking sign-off as "just safer", and one notes that on their last project someone was singled out in front of peers after a self-made decision later proved wrong. What is the most effective action?

## Options

- **A**: Tell the team plainly that they are trusted to make these calls and that you will not hold an honest, well-reasoned decision against them if it later turns out wrong.
- **B**: The next time a team member's reasoned decision does not pan out, treat it openly as a normal cost of the work — no fault assigned — so the team sees how mistakes are actually handled here.
- **C**: Introduce a brief team norm of recording the reasoning behind routine decisions, so anyone whose call is later questioned can show it was sound at the time.
- **D**: Offer to act as a quick sounding board for any decision the team feels unsure about, so they can sanity-check their thinking with you before they commit.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The team already knows its authority and can name its decisions, so the barrier is not clarity — it is a fear, carried in from a previous project, that an honest mistake will be punished. Three of the four options engage that fear, which is what makes this a fine-margin call rather than "spot the psychological-safety option". (B) is strongest because safety is established by what the team *sees happen*, not by what it is told: the first time a real decision goes wrong and is met without blame, the lesson lands far harder than any assurance. (A) says the right thing, but a verbal promise of "I won't hold it against you" is exactly the kind of statement the team has heard before and will not trust until it is demonstrated — and the kick-off message already showed words alone are not moving them. (C) gives the team defensive cover, but institutionalising "keep evidence in case you are questioned" tacitly affirms that being questioned is the threat, reinforcing the fear rather than dissolving it. (D) is well meant but rebuilds a check-in habit and signals their judgement still wants your endorsement, preserving the dependency. The finest margin is B over A: both aim at the fear, but A asserts safety where B proves it, and a team burned once will believe the demonstration, not the declaration.
