# Question

Your self-organising mobile team has authority over its own definition of done. Over three sprints you notice they have quietly relaxed their automated-test coverage bar to hit sprint commitments, and defect escape rate is creeping up, though no release has failed yet. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the defect-escape trend and the coverage change to the team, and let them decide how to respond and how the signal stays visible in their decisions.
- **B**: Raise the defect trend privately with the team's tech lead and ask them to steer the team back toward a sustainable coverage bar.
- **C**: Add defect-escape rate and test coverage to the metrics reviewed in the team's retrospective so the trend is in front of them every sprint.
- **D**: Ask the product owner to factor the rising defect rate into sprint commitments so the team has the capacity to keep coverage up.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The team owns the definition of done, so the empowering response keeps the decision with the whole team while making sure they have the information they currently lack — none of these options overrides them, so the discrimination is about which best returns the problem to the team that owns it. (A) does this most directly: it puts the specific evidence in front of the whole team and hands them both the decision and the responsibility for keeping the signal visible. (B) routes a team-wide quality decision through one person, which empowers the tech lead rather than the team and risks the rest not owning the outcome. (C) is sound practice and makes the trend visible, but adding a metric to the retro does not actually prompt a decision or assign responsibility for acting on it — visibility without ownership of the response. (D) reframes the issue as insufficient capacity, which may not be the cause (they chose to relax the bar to hit commitments) and shifts the lever to the product owner instead of the team owning its own quality standard. The margin is between giving the whole team the evidence and the decision (A) and either narrowing the decision to one person (B) or stopping at visibility (C).
