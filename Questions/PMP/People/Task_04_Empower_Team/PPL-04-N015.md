# Question

An agile programme delivering a mortgage-origination platform is organised into component teams (a database team, a UI team, a services team), and every feature requires hand-offs across all three. Features routinely stall waiting on another team, and when one slips no single team sees itself as owning the feature. Leadership asks the PM how to address this. What should the PM recommend?

## Options

- **A**: Establish a dependency-coordination role and a shared hand-off board so cross-team work is sequenced and visible.
- **B**: Reorganise into cross-functional feature teams that can each deliver an end-to-end slice of value.
- **C**: Introduce hand-off contracts with agreed interfaces and lead times so each team knows exactly what to deliver to the next and by when.
- **D**: Have each component team commit its hand-off dates in a joint planning session and hold them to those commitments.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The stalls and the ownership gap both stem from the component structure itself: no single team can finish a feature, so each waits on others and none owns the whole. (B) removes the cause by reorganising around value — cross-functional feature teams deliver a slice end-to-end, which both eliminates the cross-team hand-offs and gives each team clear ownership of the feature. (A), (C) and (D) are all legitimate dependency-management techniques and each would smooth the hand-offs somewhat — coordination and visibility, interface contracts, committed dates — but every one of them leaves the component structure in place, so features still cross three teams and accountability for a whole feature still belongs to no one. The discrimination is recognising that the question describes a structural problem with ownership, which optimising the hand-offs cannot fix, rather than a coordination problem that better process would solve.
