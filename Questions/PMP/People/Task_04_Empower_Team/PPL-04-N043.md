# Question

Midway through a release, a senior business stakeholder begins emailing the development team directly to reprioritise items in the sprint, bypassing the product owner. The developers are unsure whose priorities to follow, and the product owner is frustrated that backlog ordering keeps changing without their knowledge. The stakeholder's underlying needs are legitimate and time-sensitive. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Speak with the stakeholder to redirect their requests to the product owner, and help the product owner set up a fast path to weigh and respond to urgent input.
- **B**: Reaffirm with the developers that they take backlog order from the product owner only, so the inconsistency stops while the stakeholder issue is worked out.
- **C**: Ask the product owner and stakeholder to meet and agree directly how urgent reprioritisation requests will be handled going forward.
- **D**: Add the stakeholder to the next sprint review and backlog refinement so their priorities are heard and ordered through the proper forum.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Two things need fixing at once: the product owner's authority over the backlog is being bypassed, and a legitimate, time-sensitive need is going unmet, which is what is driving the bypass. (A) addresses both first, it closes the side channel by redirecting the stakeholder and equips the product owner to respond quickly, so the stakeholder gets responsiveness through the right person rather than around them. (B) stops the developer-level confusion but does nothing about the stakeholder's unmet need, so the pressure to bypass remains. (C) sensibly puts the two parties together, but handing the product owner straight into a direct negotiation with a powerful stakeholder, without the PM first reinforcing the role and the fast path, risks the product owner being steamrolled. (D) routes priorities to the correct forums but refinement and review are too slow for time-sensitive requests, so the stakeholder will keep going direct in the meantime. (A) is best because it protects the product owner's decision right while solving the responsiveness problem that caused the breach.
