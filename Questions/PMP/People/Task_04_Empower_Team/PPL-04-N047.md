# Question

During a retrospective, an agile team decides to spend roughly a fifth of the next sprint stabilising an unreliable automated test suite that keeps producing false failures. A delivery manager outside the team tells the project manager the team should not "waste" capacity on internal work and should keep shipping features for an important release. The PM judges the team's reasoning to be sound. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Support the team's decision and help them show the delivery manager, in delivery terms, how an unstable suite is already costing feature throughput and risking the release.
- **B**: Ask the team to take the test work as a smaller slice this sprint and finish stabilisation over the next two, so feature delivery is less disrupted while the work still happens.
- **C**: Bring the delivery manager into a short conversation with the team so they hear the reasoning first-hand and the team hears the release pressure, then let the team finalise their plan.
- **D**: Back the team but offer the delivery manager a commitment that feature scope for the release will be protected, with the test work absorbed without moving the release date.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

How the team allocates its own sprint capacity is the team's to decide; the PM's job is to protect that while addressing the legitimate concern behind the pushback. (A) does both directly: it upholds the team's ownership and reframes the work in the delivery manager's own currency (throughput and release risk), which is the cleanest way to convert opposition into support. (C) is good servant-leadership and often wise, but staging a meeting is a means, not the resolution, and it can expose the team to direct pressure to change a decision that is theirs. (B) and (D) both quietly negotiate the team's decision on their behalf, trading away or rescheduling work the team chose, which softens the external conflict at the cost of the team's authority over how they work. (A) is best because it defends the decision and satisfies the concern without bargaining the team's ownership away.
