# Question

A Scrum team delivering a logistics tracking platform keeps stalling mid-sprint because technical approach choices are routinely brought to you for sign-off, even minor library selections. Velocity is dropping. When you ask why, developers say a past architecture decision they made was reversed by you after the fact, so now they check first to avoid rework. The increments themselves are sound. What should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Work with the team to define which categories of technical decisions they own outright and which genuinely need wider input, then step out of the routine ones.
- **B**: Tell the team plainly that all technical approach choices are theirs to make, and commit that you will not reverse a decision they have taken.
- **C**: Have the team's most senior engineer act as the technical decision-maker so choices are resolved within the team without waiting on you.
- **D**: Set up a short architecture working agreement up front each sprint so the team and you align on direction before the work starts, removing the need for mid-sprint sign-off.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The root cause is not just a bottleneck but uncertainty about where the team's authority actually ends — one past reversal taught them to check everything. (A) fixes exactly that by agreeing explicit categories of owned-versus-escalated decisions, so the team knows its boundaries and stops escalating the routine ones. (B) gives the right message but a blanket "all yours, never reversed" removes your ability to flag the genuinely consequential calls and overcorrects from the past reversal; it leaves no agreed line for the decisions that really do warrant wider input. (C) resolves choices faster but concentrates authority in one person, which empowers the senior engineer rather than the team and recreates a single gatekeeper inside it. (D) is a reasonable supporting practice, but aligning on direction up front does not tell the team which mid-sprint choices are theirs to make alone — it can even reintroduce an approval step at planning. The margin is between defining graduated authority (A) and conceding blanket authority (B).
