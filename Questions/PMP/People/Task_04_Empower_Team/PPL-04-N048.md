# Question

A project manager empowered a junior engineer to own the rollout of a new monitoring tool. The engineer made a configuration choice that caused a non-critical dashboard outage for half a day before they detected and fixed it themselves. The sponsor, who heard about the outage, asks the PM whether delegating to the junior engineer was a mistake and suggests the PM take the work back. The remaining rollout still carries a few changes of similar risk. What is the best response by the project manager?

## Options

- **A**: Take the rollout back yourself for the remaining high-risk changes, keep the engineer involved as your second pair of eyes, and hand ownership back once the riskier steps are done.
- **B**: Set out for the sponsor that the engineer caught and resolved the outage themselves and is folding the lesson into their approach, and keep them as owner with support sized to the work still ahead.
- **C**: Keep the engineer as owner but agree with the sponsor that the engineer will walk you through each remaining configuration change before applying it.
- **D**: Keep the engineer as owner and add a peer pre-deployment review of config changes by another engineer, so a second person checks the riskier steps without the decision returning to you.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

All four keep the engineer involved and none is reckless, so the decision turns on how much of the recovered authority to claw back for a half-day, self-corrected slip. (B) treats it as ordinary growth: the owner detected and fixed their own error, the sponsor's concern is met with facts and a credible adjustment, and support is scaled to the residual risk without diluting ownership. (A) pulls the most consequential remaining work back to the PM, so the engineer owns the rollout in name while the PM owns its hardest parts, which is the lesson that ownership evaporates under scrutiny. (C) routes every remaining change through PM pre-approval, reasonable-sounding but converting the delegation into a sign-off gate held by the PM. (D) adds a proportionate safeguard and keeps the decision out of the PM's hands, the closest rival, but it institutes a standing control in response to a single recovered incident and still narrows the engineer's autonomy more than the evidence warrants; (B) preserves ownership while leaving room to add such a check only if a pattern emerges.
