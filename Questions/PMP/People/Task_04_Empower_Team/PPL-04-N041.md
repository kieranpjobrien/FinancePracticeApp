# Question

On a Scrum team building a logistics app, the developers keep messaging the project manager to approve which third-party mapping library to adopt for each new feature. Architecture guardrails exist but are scattered across old wiki pages, and two recent library choices were quietly reversed by the team's tech lead after the PM had already approved them. The PM agrees library selection sits within the team's remit. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Tell the team in the next standup that library choices are theirs and they should stop routing them through the PM from now on.
- **B**: With the tech lead, consolidate the architecture guardrails into one current reference the team trusts, then confirm that choices meeting those guardrails are the team's to make and record.
- **C**: Ask the tech lead to be the approver for library choices instead of the PM, since the tech lead is already vetting them after the fact.
- **D**: Keep approving the choices but add the tech lead as a required reviewer before each library is adopted, so the later reversals stop.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The interruptions persist because the team lacks a trustworthy boundary to act against: the guardrails are stale and decisions are being reversed, so developers seek cover from the PM. (B) fixes the actual blocker, gives the team a reference they can own decisions against, then confirms the authority, which is what makes empowerment real rather than nominal. (A) declares the authority but leaves the team with no reliable guardrail, so they will either keep asking or make choices that get reversed again. (C) and (D) both keep an approval gate, swapping or adding an approver rather than enabling the team to decide within clear limits, which centralises the very decision that should sit with them.
