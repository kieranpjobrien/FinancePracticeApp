# Question

You empowered a junior developer on a Scrum team to own the spike for a new payment integration. She made a design call the team later found will need reworking, costing about two days. In the retrospective she is visibly deflated, and two teammates make pointed remarks about the "wasted effort". As the Scrum Master, what is the best thing to do?

## Options

- **A**: In the retrospective, frame the spike as having done its job — surfacing that the design wouldn't hold — capture what the team now knows, and address the "wasted effort" framing by reinforcing that exploring and discarding an option is how a spike pays off.
- **B**: Acknowledge the rework cost openly, ask her to walk the team through her reasoning and what she would do differently, and keep the next spike with her.
- **C**: Name that the spike succeeded in de-risking the design, then privately check in with her afterwards to make sure the teammates' remarks haven't dented her willingness to take the next one on.
- **D**: Redirect the retrospective away from blame by focusing the team on the lesson learned and the improved approach going forward, rather than dwelling on individual decisions.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Two things are live: a junior who must stay empowered, and teammates publicly reframing a legitimate spike outcome as waste — a norm that, left standing, will chill everyone's willingness to take ownership. (A) handles both in the room: it reframes the result as the spike succeeding (a discarded option is a result, not a failure) and corrects the "wasted effort" narrative for the whole team, which is what protects psychological safety beyond just her. (B) is good practice — owning the reasoning and keeping the work with her — but having a deflated junior publicly recount "what I'd do differently" while teammates snipe risks reinforcing the very framing that she erred, and it leaves the team's unhealthy norm unchallenged. (C) gets the reframe right but defers the teammate dynamic to a private chat, so the corrosive "waste" message stands publicly and the other developers learn the wrong lesson. (D) avoids blame but does so by steering *away* from the decision, which implicitly concedes it was a misstep to be glossed over rather than a normal, even valuable, spike outcome. The discriminator is whether the response also repairs the team norm in the moment, not just supports her.
