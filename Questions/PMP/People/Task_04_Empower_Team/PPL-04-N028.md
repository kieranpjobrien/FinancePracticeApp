# Question

During backlog refinement, your development team proposes deferring a complex feature to reduce technical risk and instead delivering two smaller items the product owner had ranked lower. The product owner pushes back because the complex feature was promised verbally to a key customer. Both positions are reasonable. The team and product owner have aired their views but cannot converge, and both now look to you. What is the best move?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate the product owner and team through the trade-off together so the priority decision is reached by the product owner, informed by the team's risk view.
- **B**: Ask the product owner to make the priority call now, since ordering the backlog is their accountability and the team has already given its risk input.
- **C**: Help the product owner reconnect with the key customer to test whether the verbal promise allows a short, transparent delay, then let priority follow what that conversation reveals.
- **D**: Propose a middle path yourself — start the smaller items now and time-box a spike on the complex feature — and put it to both of them.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Backlog priority is the product owner's to own, so the PM should neither seize the decision nor simply order them to decide in isolation; the value here is helping them decide well with the team's input genuinely weighed. (A) does this: it keeps the decision with the product owner while facilitating a structured trade-off so the team's risk view actually shapes the call — empowerment of the right owner, supported. (B) lands the decision with the correct owner, but having "aired their views" is not the same as having reconciled the risk and the commitment; pushing for an immediate solo call risks the product owner deciding without truly absorbing the team's input, and can feel like you washing your hands of a stuck room. (C) is a smart way to dissolve the real constraint — the verbal promise — but it presumes the customer commitment is the binding factor and may not be feasible in the moment; it is a strong next step once facilitation shows the promise is the crux, not the first move. (D) inserts your own compromise, which quietly relocates the priority decision from the product owner to you, however collaboratively framed. The margin is between facilitating the owner to decide (A) and prompting them to decide unaided (B).
