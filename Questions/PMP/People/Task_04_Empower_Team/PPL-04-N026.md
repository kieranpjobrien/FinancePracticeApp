# Question

A capable new Scrum Master joins one of the teams in your programme and, to prove value early, begins assigning tasks to individual developers in sprint planning and asking for twice-daily updates. The team was self-organising and is visibly frustrated, though the sprint is on track. The Scrum Master genuinely believes this is helping. As the programme PM, what is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: In a private one-to-one, explore what outcome the Scrum Master is trying to achieve, then coach them toward enabling the team to pull and self-assign work as the way to deliver it.
- **B**: Bring the Scrum Master and team together to co-create a working agreement that defines how work is allocated and how progress is made visible.
- **C**: Coach the Scrum Master to drop the task assignment but keep the twice-daily check-ins for now, since the extra visibility is reassuring while they build trust.
- **D**: Let the team raise the friction with the Scrum Master directly in their retrospective, intervening only if they cannot resolve it themselves.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The Scrum Master is well-intentioned but acting on a flawed model, so the first move is to understand their goal and coach them toward servant leadership — addressing the root cause while developing them, which is what restores the team's ownership of how work is allocated. (B) is a reasonable next step, but jumping straight to a co-created agreement before the Scrum Master understands why directive assignment undermines self-organisation risks formalising a watered-down compromise; the coaching conversation should come first. (C) removes the assignment but keeps an intrusive monitoring cadence the team has not asked for, leaving part of the disempowerment in place. (D) can work for minor friction, but it asks the frustrated team to manage a new colleague's behaviour the PM is better placed to coach, and lets the wrong model run another iteration. The fine margin is between coaching the individual first (A) and structurally co-designing with the group (B) — and the cause here is one person's misunderstanding, so A leads.
