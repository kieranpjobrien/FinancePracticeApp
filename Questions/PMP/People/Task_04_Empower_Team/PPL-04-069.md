# Question

Your three mobile teams across a product company each solve the same problems differently and reinvent solutions every quarter. Engineers have asked for "more coordination" but previous attempts at central mandates have failed. You run one of the three teams; the other two have peer PMs. What should you propose?

## Options

- **A**: Propose the mobile engineers self-form a cross-team guild that sets shared conventions through their own consent, with the three PMs sponsoring the guild's time and decisions.
- **B**: Ask your CTO to issue a set of mobile engineering standards across the three teams so that a senior technical authority settles the coordination question decisively for all engineers.
- **C**: Form a small steering group of the three PMs to agree a mobile engineering policy and publish it to the three teams so that coordination is driven by the leaders of each team.
- **D**: Ask each team to nominate a senior engineer for a technical-decision board that rules on cross-team conventions and produces a quarterly shared convention set enforced by the three PMs.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

(A) is the PMI community-of-practice empowerment pattern: engineers own the conventions they will have to live by, PMs sponsor rather than direct, and consent-based decision-making produces norms that actually stick. Previous central mandates have already failed — a clear signal. (B) repeats the central-mandate approach the team has said does not work. (C) has the PMs agreeing engineering conventions, which they are not best placed to author and which removes engineer ownership. (D) creates a representative board that rules rather than convenes, recreating the central-authority dynamic in a different forum.
