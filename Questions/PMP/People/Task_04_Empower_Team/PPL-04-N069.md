# Question

A project manager is calibrating how much decision authority to hand to three team members. Readiness matters, but so does the nature of the decision: some calls are easily reversible, others carry regulatory or safety consequences that the PM remains accountable for regardless of who decides. For each situation, match the most appropriate level of empowerment.

## Items

- **1**: A seasoned engineer with a strong track record on identical work wants to own a recurring, low-stakes and easily reversible technical choice.
- **2**: That same seasoned engineer now wants to own a one-off decision that carries a regulatory-compliance consequence the PM is personally accountable for.
- **3**: A capable mid-level analyst, proven elsewhere but new to this platform, wants to take on a key but reversible configuration decision.

## Matches

- **A**: Delegate fully and step back, intervening only if outcomes drift.
- **B**: Delegate the decision but agree it stays within explicit compliance boundaries and that they brief you on how those are met before it takes effect.
- **C**: Grant ownership with a light safety net and an agreed early check-in so platform-specific gaps surface before they bite.

## Answer

1-A, 2-B, 3-C

## Explanation

Empowerment is calibrated to *both* readiness and the stakes of the decision — it does not map cleanly onto competence alone, which is the trap in this item. For the same expert, the low-stakes reversible call (1) warrants full delegation (A), but the regulatory decision (2) warrants delegation bounded by explicit constraints and a compliance briefing (B) — not because the engineer is less capable, but because the PM stays accountable for an irreversible regulatory consequence and must keep visibility of the boundary while still leaving the decision with them. That is the distinction many candidates miss: confirming constraints are met (B) is empowerment, whereas retaking the decision would not be. The platform-new analyst on a reversible call (3) is best given ownership with a light safety net and an early check-in (C), catching environment-specific gaps without second-guessing their proven competence. Note that 1 and 2 involve the *same person*, so anyone matching purely on the individual's readiness will mismatch — the decision's reversibility and accountability profile is what moves the dial.
