# Question

A newly formed Scrum team is enthusiastic but keeps routing small decisions to the project manager: how to split a story, which teammate takes which task, and whether a minor refactor is worth doing. The PM wants to build a genuinely self-organising team without leaving them unsupported. Which actions support that goal? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Work with the team to make explicit which decisions are theirs to own, so the boundaries of their authority are clear rather than assumed.
- **B**: When they bring an in-scope decision, ask what they think the right call is and back their reasoning rather than supplying the answer.
- **C**: When they ask you to make an in-scope call, give them your view but tell them the final decision is theirs and you will support whatever they choose.
- **D**: Make sure the team has the information and access they need to decide well, such as visibility of priorities and the relevant technical context.
- **E**: Encourage them to bring decisions they are unsure about to you early, so you can validate their thinking before they commit and they avoid costly missteps.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

Self-organisation is built by clarifying decision rights (A), coaching the team to reach and own their own answers (B), and equipping them with the information to decide well (D) — together these transfer real authority with a safety net. (C) is tempting because it ends with "the decision is theirs", but leading with your view on a routine in-scope call anchors a brand-new, deferential team to your answer; the empowering move is to draw out and back *their* reasoning (B), not to supply yours and then hand it back. (E) sounds supportive but institutionalises a validate-before-committing habit on small decisions, which keeps the team checking in for cover and is exactly the dependency the PM is trying to dissolve — a safety net belongs on genuinely uncertain, high-stakes calls, not on routine ones. Both wrong options keep authority nominally with the team while quietly preserving the PM as the reference point.
