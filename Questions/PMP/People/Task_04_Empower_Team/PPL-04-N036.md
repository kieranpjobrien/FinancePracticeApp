# Question

A senior stakeholder funding your e-commerce checkout work tells you she has been burned before by teams that "looked busy but missed dates", and asks you to assign each backlog item to a named developer so she can track who is accountable for what. Your team has delivered reliably while self-organising. How should you respond?

## Options

- **A**: Acknowledge her need for predictable accountability, explain that the team self-selects work and commits to a sprint goal as a unit, and set up a board view and forecast that let her see commitment and progress without per-person assignment.
- **B**: Assign named owners to each backlog item for this stakeholder's visibility, while telling the team they can still reorganise the work among themselves as they go.
- **C**: Have the team self-select as usual, then produce a per-developer breakdown after planning so the stakeholder gets the named accountability she asked for.
- **D**: Invite the stakeholder to attend sprint planning and review so she can see the team commit and self-organise, and address her concern through direct observation.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The stakeholder's real need is confidence that delivery is predictable and accountable, not literally a name against each task; empowerment means protecting the team's ownership of how work is distributed while genuinely meeting that need. (A) does both — it reframes accountability at the team-and-sprint-goal level and gives her real visibility through a board and forecast. (B) creates named assignments that either constrain the team or become fiction the moment they reorganise, so it satisfies the stakeholder in appearance only and quietly erodes self-organisation. (C) reverse-engineers per-person ownership the team did not actually take on, which is misleading and reintroduces individual task-tracking by the back door. (D) is a reasonable supporting move, but exposing her to planning without first addressing how accountability is evidenced may amplify her anxiety rather than resolve it, and on its own it does not give her the ongoing visibility she wants. The margin is between meeting the underlying need (A) and conceding the literal request (B)/(C).
