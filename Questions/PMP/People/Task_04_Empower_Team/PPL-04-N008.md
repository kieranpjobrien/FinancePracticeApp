# Question

A PM delegated supplier negotiation on a predictive defence-logistics project to a team member, who agreed terms that later proved unfavourable on delivery penalties. A senior stakeholder now demands the PM "take negotiations back in-house". The team member is shaken but otherwise strong, and there are three more negotiations ahead. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Take the remaining negotiations back to protect the project and reassure the senior stakeholder.
- **B**: Debrief the outcome with the team member to extract the lessons, then keep them on the remaining negotiations with closer support and a review checkpoint.
- **C**: Move the team member off negotiations entirely but keep them on the project in a non-decision role to preserve their confidence.
- **D**: Defend the team member's decision to the stakeholder and proceed unchanged, since second-guessing would undermine the delegation.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Empowerment includes letting people learn from mistakes while adding support, not withdrawing trust at the first poor outcome — (B) captures the lesson, retains the development opportunity, and adds a checkpoint that also reassures the stakeholder. (A) reverses the delegation and signals that one mistake ends autonomy, discouraging the whole team from taking ownership. (C) removes the person from the very skill they need to develop, which is over-correction. (D) ignores a genuine performance gap and the stakeholder's legitimate concern; empowerment is not the same as refusing to adjust.
