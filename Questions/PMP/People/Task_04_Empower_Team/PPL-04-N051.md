# Question

A functional manager presses the project manager to have the Scrum team commit to twelve specific backlog items in the next two-week sprint because a customer demo has been promised for the end of it. The team's velocity history points to about eight items. The product owner can flex which items are in scope but not the demo date. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Ask the team whether, just for this sprint, they are willing to stretch to ten with the product owner pre-agreeing which two items can drop if needed.
- **B**: Bring the velocity evidence to the team, manager, and product owner together, and have them re-shape the demo around what the team can credibly commit to, whether by trimming scope to the highest-value items or staging the rest.
- **C**: Have the product owner pick the eight highest-value items so the demo shows the most important features, and tell the manager the rest will follow next sprint.
- **D**: Let the team set their own commitment as usual, and separately advise the manager to set demo expectations with the customer based on the team's forecast.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The team owns what it can credibly commit to; the PM's role is to defend that authority while constructively solving the real constraint, a fixed demo date. (B) does both, putting the evidence in front of everyone and letting the people who own the decisions (team for capacity, product owner for scope) re-shape the demo collaboratively, which protects the forecast and addresses the deadline. (A) keeps the team's consent in the loop, but it starts from the manager's number and nudges the team to stretch above their evidence-based forecast, subtly relocating the commitment. (C) makes a sensible scope choice but the PM is effectively deciding the sprint content and managing the manager's expectations unilaterally, sidelining the team and product owner from a decision that is theirs. (D) respects the team's authority but stops short of helping solve the demo problem, leaving the manager to handle a hard conversation alone. (B) is best because it couples protection of the team's commitment with active, shared problem-solving.
