# Question

You empowered a developer to own integration with a third-party payments API. He chose an approach that failed in the sprint, causing a missed increment and some embarrassment in the review. He is now reluctant to make decisions and keeps asking you to approve his choices. Other developers are watching how this plays out. You want to keep him owning the work. What should the PM do first?

## Options

- **A**: Reaffirm his ownership and agree that for the next approach he will run a spike to validate it before committing the sprint to it.
- **B**: Reaffirm his ownership, and have him present the failure and what he learned from it to the team in the retrospective.
- **C**: Reaffirm his ownership, and offer him a sounding-board session with you to think through the next approach before he decides.
- **D**: Reaffirm his ownership, and arrange for him to pair with a developer experienced in payments integrations for the next attempt.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four keep ownership with him — the disempowering "take it back" options are deliberately off the table — so the discrimination is about what most directly rebuilds his confidence to decide. The reluctance traces to a technical bet that failed, so (A) attacks that at source: a spike de-risks the next decision with evidence, lowers the stakes of getting it wrong, and the choice still stays his — it is the move most likely to get him deciding again. (B) is healthy blameless-culture practice and good for the watching team, but asking the developer who is already smarting from public embarrassment to present his own failure may deepen his hesitancy before it helps, so it is not the first step to restore his decision-making. (C) supports him, but a sounding-board with you keeps your judgement in the loop and can quietly recreate the approval-seeking you want to end. (D) gives real capability help, yet pairing him with an expert for "the next attempt" risks him deferring to that expert, which dilutes the very ownership you are trying to rebuild. The margin is between de-risking his own decision (A) and routing his decision through someone else's input (C)/(D).
