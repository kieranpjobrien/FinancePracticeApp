# Question

On an insurance-claims automation product, the development team is repeatedly held up because provisioning a test environment requires a manager two levels up to approve each request individually, and approvals take a day or more. The team raises it most stand-ups. The manager is willing to help but says blanket self-service access is off the table for audit reasons. The Scrum Master has limited standing with that manager. As the person serving the team, what is the best action?

## Options

- **A**: Negotiate with the manager to delegate test-environment approval authority to the team's own tech lead within agreed limits, so the team approves its own routine requests.
- **B**: Ask the manager to pre-approve a standing pool of environments at the start of each sprint that the team can draw down without further sign-off.
- **C**: Bring the recurring impediment to the next scrum-of-scrums or management review so a sponsor with the standing to change the policy can act on it.
- **D**: Arrange for the manager to commit to a guaranteed same-day turnaround on the team's approval requests so the delay no longer disrupts the sprint.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The audit constraint rules out blanket self-service, so empowerment has to come through giving the team a controlled form of standing authority rather than just speeding up the existing dependency. (A) delegates the approval decision itself to the team's tech lead within limits — the team now approves its own routine requests, which is genuine transferred authority and preserves an audit trail, and it is achievable directly with the willing manager. (B) is a strong, defensible move that removes most per-request friction, but a pre-approved pool still caps the team at someone else's forecast and leaves them dependent on the next allocation once it is exhausted — it eases the impediment without giving the team the decision. (C) is the right escalation route when the blocker needs a policy change above the team, but here a sponsor-level intervention is heavier than needed and slower than working with the cooperative manager who can already act. (D) keeps every request flowing through the manager as an approver; faster turnaround manages the symptom but transfers no authority to the team and breaks the moment the manager is unavailable. All four are reasonable responses to a recurring blocker; only (A) actually moves the authority to the team within the stated audit limits.
