# Question

You are setting up decision-making authority for a newly formed agile team building a customer onboarding portal. You want the team genuinely empowered while keeping appropriate organisational guardrails. Which actions best support real empowerment? (Choose two.)

## Options

- **A**: Agree explicitly which decisions the team makes alone, which it consults on, and which require escalation, then make this visible.
- **B**: Ask the team to record the decisions it takes in a shared log and submit that log to you each week so you can confirm they were the right calls.
- **C**: Give the team authority to choose its own technical practices and tools within agreed architecture and security constraints.
- **D**: Have the team's most experienced member hold decision authority on the team's behalf and bring the others' views to them.
- **E**: Tell the team it is fully autonomous and may decide anything it wishes, so it can move fast without waiting on the wider organisation.

## Answer

A, C

## Explanation

(A) and (C) together define clear boundaries and grant real authority within sensible constraints — the essence of bounded empowerment. (B) looks lightweight because it is just a log, but submitting it for you to "confirm they were the right calls" turns delegated decisions into ones you still vet after the fact, which undercuts the ownership; a transparency log the team keeps for itself would be fine — retrospective approval is the problem. (D) keeps decisions inside the team but concentrates authority in one person, so the team as a whole is not empowered and the rest become advisers rather than owners. (E) sounds maximally empowering but removing reference to organisational standards is abdication, not empowerment, and sets the team up to collide with architecture, security, or compliance constraints it is still bound by. The two distractors that tempt are (B), which feels like a minor reporting ask, and (D), which feels like sensible delegation — both quietly remove real authority from the team.
