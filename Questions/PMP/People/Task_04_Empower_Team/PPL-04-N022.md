# Question

On a predictive hospital fit-out project, you delegated commissioning coordination to an experienced site engineer two weeks ago. You have just learned that a critical inspection booking was missed: the engineer assumed the mechanical subcontractor was raising it, and the subcontractor assumed the engineer was. The schedule slips by a week. The engineer is capable and the rest of the commissioning scope is progressing well. What is the best action?

## Options

- **A**: Meet the engineer to map exactly which interface items sit with them versus the subcontractor, agree how those hand-offs are tracked, and let them keep ownership.
- **B**: Ask the engineer to produce a full commissioning RACI covering every deliverable and every party, and review it with you before they continue.
- **C**: Recover the immediate slip yourself by rebooking the missed inspection and chasing the subcontractor, then hand coordination back to the engineer once the schedule is stable.
- **D**: Keep the engineer on coordination but add a weekly checkpoint with you where they walk through upcoming inspection bookings.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The miss came from an ambiguous interface between the engineer and the subcontractor — a "someone else had it" gap — not from the engineer being incapable. (A) targets exactly that gap: it clarifies the specific hand-off boundaries that failed and the tracking for them, while leaving ownership intact, so it both prevents recurrence and keeps the engineer empowered. (B) addresses the same boundary issue but a full RACI over every deliverable is disproportionate to one interface gap and routes it back through your review, which dilutes the ownership you delegated. (C) is a defensible short-term recovery, but stepping in to run the coordination — even temporarily — signals the engineer cannot be trusted through a stumble and delays restoring their accountability; the problem is a boundary to fix, not a capability to backfill. (D) adds useful visibility but a generic weekly walkthrough does not resolve the engineer-subcontractor ambiguity that actually caused the miss, and risks becoming a status ritual. The margin is between fixing the precise interface boundary (A) and either over-formalising it (B) or temporarily taking it back (C).
