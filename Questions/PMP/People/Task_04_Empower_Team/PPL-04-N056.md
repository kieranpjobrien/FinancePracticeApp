# Question

A project manager delegated authority over minor design decisions to the lead designer, defining "minor" as anything not affecting cost, schedule, or agreed scope. The lead designer has just approved a visual change they classed as minor, but a key stakeholder believes it alters the agreed scope and is unhappy it went ahead without consultation. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Reverse the designer's decision immediately and apologise to the stakeholder for the designer overstepping their authority.
- **B**: Clarify with the stakeholder and designer whether the change actually crosses the agreed boundary, resolve this specific case fairly, and sharpen the definition of "minor" so the boundary is clearer next time.
- **C**: Revoke the designer's delegated authority over design decisions to prevent any similar dispute recurring.
- **D**: Tell the stakeholder that the designer had authority to make the call, so the change stands and there is nothing further to discuss.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

(B) addresses the real issue, a genuine ambiguity at the edge of the delegated boundary, by resolving the instance and tightening the rule, which preserves the delegation while satisfying the stakeholder. (A) treats the designer as having overstepped before establishing whether the boundary was actually crossed, undermining them unfairly. (C) over-corrects by stripping authority over a single boundary dispute, punishing the whole arrangement for an unclear line. (D) dismisses a legitimate stakeholder concern and hides behind the letter of the delegation rather than resolving the disagreement.
