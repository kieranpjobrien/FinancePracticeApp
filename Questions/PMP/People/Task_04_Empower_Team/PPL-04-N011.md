# Question

A PM on a smart-metering deployment is deciding how to handle three upcoming decisions with the same team member. Match each decision (left) to the delegation approach (right) that best fits it. Each approach is used exactly once.

## Items

- **1**: Choosing which of two approved component suppliers to use for the next batch of meters — the team member has placed dozens of these orders without issue and either supplier meets spec.
- **2**: Selecting the data-encryption library for the meters' firmware, an area the team member has worked in only twice; the choice shapes later work but can be revisited at some cost if it proves wrong.
- **3**: Committing to a single regional rollout sequence that locks in subcontractor mobilisation across three states and cannot be unwound once contracts are signed.

## Matches

- **A**: Delegate fully; the team member decides and proceeds without checking back.
- **B**: Have the team member work up the options and a recommendation, then decide it together before acting.
- **C**: The PM owns the decision but seeks the team member's analysis and view first.

## Answer

1-A, 2-B, 3-C

## Explanation

The right delegation level turns on weighing how reversible and how consequential each decision is against how much the person has proven in that area — none of which is labelled, so each must be read from the scenario. (1) is something the team member has done repeatedly with interchangeable, spec-meeting options, so full delegation (A) is appropriate and empowering; reserving it would be needless control. (2) shapes downstream work but is recoverable and sits in a developing area, so a decide-together approach (B) grows the person while sharing the call — full delegation would over-reach their experience, and PM-decides would waste a development chance. (3) is irreversible once contracts sign and carries cross-state exposure the PM must answer for, so the PM owns it while still drawing on the team member's input (C). The discrimination is matching the approach to the blend of reversibility, stakes and demonstrated competence in each case, not to a stated risk label.
