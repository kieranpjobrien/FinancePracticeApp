# Question

A long-tenured contractor on your team has declined every training opportunity you offer, saying "my agency manages my development." She produces solid work but has been visibly disengaged from team rituals for months. Her contract runs for another year. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Respect her position and stop offering training since she has clearly stated her preference that her agency manages her development and not the customer project she is engaged on.
- **B**: Raise her disengagement with her agency manager so that the agency can address both the training question and her visible withdrawal from the team rituals on the programme.
- **C**: Meet her privately, name that you have noticed the withdrawal, ask what she values in team engagement, and co-design what shared team membership looks like for a long-term contractor.
- **D**: Accept that long-term contractors often remain outside team life and focus your empowerment efforts on the permanent staff where that energy will have a more lasting organisational impact.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

(C) is the PMI inclusion pattern: name what you have observed, ask what matters to her, and co-design membership — empowerment applies to contractors too, especially on a year-long engagement. (A) uses her stated preference as an excuse to disengage and misses that "training" may not be what she actually needs. (B) outsources a conversation the PM can have directly and treats her agency as her only legitimate connection. (D) writes off a long-term contributor on the basis of a category assumption about contractors.
