# Question

A PM running a predictive utilities billing-system upgrade is constantly interrupted by team members asking whether they can approve minor change requests, small purchases, and day-to-day risk responses. Each interruption is small but the volume is slowing everyone down, and a few people have privately said they ask first because they are unsure where their authority ends. What is the best action?

## Options

- **A**: Agree and publish defined authority thresholds for each decision type, stating who can approve what up to which limit without coming to the PM.
- **B**: Tell the team they are trusted to use their judgement on anything minor and to come to the PM only when they are genuinely unsure.
- **C**: Delegate a senior team member to field and approve these routine requests on the PM's behalf so the PM is no longer the bottleneck.
- **D**: Hold a short daily session where the team brings all pending approvals so the PM can clear them in one batch rather than as interruptions.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

People are asking because the limits of their authority are undefined, so they ask to stay safe — naming the thresholds removes the cause and lets them act on their own within clear limits. (A) does this directly. (B) extends trust but still leaves "minor" and "genuinely unsure" to each person's interpretation, so the cautious will keep asking and others may approve things beyond their remit — the underlying ambiguity is unresolved. (C) lifts the load off the PM but simply relocates the approval bottleneck to one person rather than empowering each individual to act within defined limits. (D) makes the interruptions tidier but keeps every decision flowing through the PM, so nothing is actually delegated.
