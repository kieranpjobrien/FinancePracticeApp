# Question

A Scrum Master notices that the project manager habitually answers every question raised in the daily scrum and assigns the follow-ups, leaving the developers passive. The PM genuinely wants the team to own the event and coordinate their own work. What is the best step for the PM to take?

## Options

- **A**: Have the Scrum Master facilitate the daily scrum and coach the developers to raise and resolve their own follow-ups, with the PM attending as a participant.
- **B**: Ask the developers to direct their updates to each other rather than to the PM, while the PM keeps attending and captures the follow-ups that come out of it.
- **C**: Stop directing the daily scrum and let the developers run it among themselves, holding back on questions and follow-ups and stepping in only to help clear impediments they surface.
- **D**: Agree with the team a simple format for the event, who they talk to, how they pick up follow-ups, then attend while they get used to running it.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Ownership of the daily scrum transfers when the team actually runs it and the PM stops being the person things flow to. (C) makes that switch most cleanly: the developers coordinate themselves and the PM is recast as a supporter who removes blockers, which is the self-organising end state. (B) redirects the talking but leaves the PM still capturing the follow-ups, so the team is performing for the PM rather than owning the coordination. (A) is a reasonable transitional move, yet it shifts the lead to the Scrum Master rather than to the developers themselves, so the team is still being facilitated rather than self-running. (D) is sensible scaffolding but agreeing a format with the PM present keeps the PM in the room as the reference point; the format helps only once the team is actually running it. (C) is best because it puts the event genuinely in the team's hands while keeping the PM available for the one thing that is theirs to do, clearing impediments.
