# Question

You manage a large predictive infrastructure programme with eight work packages. Every cost variance over £5,000, regardless of package, currently needs your approval, and you have become a bottleneck — approvals are queuing and packages are waiting. The package managers vary: most are highly experienced, but two are new to running a package at this scale. What is the most appropriate way to relieve this while keeping control?

## Options

- **A**: Delegate a single, uniform variance-approval limit to all eight package managers, with consolidated reporting to you and escalation above the limit.
- **B**: Set each manager's delegated approval limit according to their experience and package risk, with consolidated reporting and escalation above each limit.
- **C**: Delegate approval authority to the experienced managers and continue approving variances for the two newer managers' packages yourself for now.
- **D**: Form a small approvals board of the senior package managers that decides variances above £5,000 for all eight packages.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The aim is to remove the bottleneck by delegating real authority while keeping control proportionate to risk and capability. (B) does this best: tailoring each limit to the manager's experience and the package's risk empowers everyone to some degree, stretches the newer managers within a safe boundary, and still surfaces the largest exposures to you — bounded empowerment matched to context. (A) is a strong move and removes the bottleneck, but a uniform limit either sets the bar low enough to under-empower the experienced managers or high enough to over-expose the two new ones; one threshold cannot fit both. (C) keeps the newer managers fully dependent on you and singles them out, denying them any delegated authority and leaving you a partial bottleneck. (D) relieves your load but concentrates authority in a few seniors deciding over their peers' packages, which empowers a clique rather than the package owners and can undercut their ownership. The margin is between a uniform delegation (A) and a risk-and-capability-calibrated one (B).
