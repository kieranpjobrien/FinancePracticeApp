# Question

On your 9-month government identity services programme, your team includes four employees and five contractors from the same agency. A significant design decision is due next week and the two most knowledgeable engineers on the relevant technology are contractors. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Keep the final decision with the employee engineers since they carry the long-term accountability for the identity services after the contractors have rolled off the programme.
- **B**: Route the decision through a contractor technical lead so that the agency's best expertise is formally represented while the employee engineers provide a sanity check on the outcome.
- **C**: Have both groups jointly contribute but let the employee engineers vote at the end, so the final call rests with the people staying on the programme after the contractors depart.
- **D**: Run the decision as one team, let the two contractors lead the technical proposal based on their expertise, and have the team commit jointly to the outcome regardless of contract status.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

(D) is the PMI one-team empowerment pattern: contract status should not dictate decision authority when expertise and outcome ownership are shared during the engagement. (A) privileges tenure over expertise on a decision where expertise is the critical factor for the design. (B) creates a parallel governance path that will feel artificial and excludes employees from the real decision conversation. (C) gives contractors the work of thinking and employees the work of voting, which is the opposite of genuine empowerment.
