# Question

On a hybrid infrastructure project, some team members hesitate and bring routine decisions to the project manager, while others have made calls that turned out to be the PM's or the sponsor's to make. Both the delays and the overstepping trace back to people being unsure where their authority ends. The PM wants the team to act confidently without crossing lines. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Hold a session where the team talks through the recent cases of hesitation and overstepping and works out, from those examples, where the boundaries should sit.
- **B**: Give the team a clear default rule, act within budget and agreed scope, escalate anything that touches cost, scope, or other teams, and refine it as cases arise.
- **C**: Set up a quick channel where members can sanity-check a decision before acting whenever they are unsure it is theirs to make.
- **D**: Work with the team to agree and write down, decision type by decision type, which they own, which they consult on, and which the PM or sponsor retains.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

Two failure modes are happening at once, under-acting and over-acting, and both come from an unclear map of authority. (D) produces that map explicitly and by decision type, which is the only option that simultaneously tells the hesitant members what they may do and tells the over-reachers what they may not, removing both problems at the source. (A) is a strong method and may well feed into (D), but on its own a discussion of past cases can leave the agreed boundaries unrecorded and contested later. (B) gives a useful heuristic, yet a single rule of thumb blurs exactly the grey areas where the overstepping occurred and will need case-by-case interpretation. (C) reduces overstepping but reinforces checking-before-acting, which keeps the delay problem alive. (D) is best because clear, agreed, recorded boundaries are what let people act independently and correctly.
