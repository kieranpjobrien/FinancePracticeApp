# Question

After a two-hour design debate your 8-person security-tools team has converged on approach A. One engineer, who argued strongly for approach B, is refusing to participate in the implementation and has told you privately he thinks the team is wrong. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Escalate the decision to the architect so that approach B gets a fair hearing from a more senior technical voice outside the team and the dissenting engineer feels validated.
- **B**: Send the team back to re-debate approach A versus B until they reach genuine consensus, since a single strong dissenter suggests the team has not actually settled the question properly.
- **C**: Meet the engineer, acknowledge his argument genuinely, introduce the disagree-and-commit norm, and ask him to support execution of the chosen approach while his concerns are documented.
- **D**: Remove the engineer from the implementation stream so that the rest of the team can execute approach A without him and he can work on an unrelated area he has no objection to.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

(C) is the PMI empowerment-with-accountability pattern: disagree-and-commit preserves team agency and individual integrity, and documenting the concern leaves a learning trail if approach A underperforms. (A) overrules a team decision with an external voice on a solved question. (B) rewards dissent with rework and encourages future holdouts to block decisions. (D) treats individual disagreement as grounds for removal, which punishes exactly the candour healthy teams need.
