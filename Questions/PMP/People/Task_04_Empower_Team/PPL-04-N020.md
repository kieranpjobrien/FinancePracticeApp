# Question

A PM on a predictive government-payroll modernisation project has a mid-level engineer who is technically excellent but says in a one-to-one that the work feels routine and they are thinking of leaving. Two pieces of work are coming up: an integration with an unfamiliar third-party system that no one has volunteered to lead, and a refactor of the core calculation module that this engineer knows better than anyone. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Offer the engineer ownership of the unfamiliar integration as a supported stretch into new territory.
- **B**: Give the engineer the calculation-module refactor to lead, since it lets them set the technical direction on work they already command.
- **C**: Ask the engineer which of the two pieces they would rather take the lead on, and assign accordingly.
- **D**: Have the engineer mentor a junior through the integration while staying hands-on with the calculation work themselves.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The disengagement is about the work feeling routine, so the motivating move is genuinely new territory with ownership — (A) gives the engineer a fresh, challenging workstream to own, which is what addresses "routine", and the support manages the unfamiliarity. (B) hands them more authority but over work they already command, so it relieves none of the staleness that is driving them out. (C) sounds empowering but defers the PM's read of what will re-engage the engineer; offered a comfortable option, a tired high-performer often retreats to the familiar, and the question is what best re-engages them, not what they will pick. (D) keeps them anchored to the calculation work they are bored of and reduces their integration role to oversight rather than ownership. The margin is between new-and-owned (A) and familiar-and-owned (B)/(D) — novelty with ownership is the stronger retention lever here.
