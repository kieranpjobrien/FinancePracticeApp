# Question

A high-performing developer tells the project manager privately that they feel like "just a pair of hands": their work is fully specified by a business analyst, signed off by the PM, and they have no say in how the solution is shaped. Their engagement is visibly dropping. The project is predictive, but within the agreed requirements there is genuine latitude in how solutions are designed. What is the best action for the project manager?

## Options

- **A**: Invite the developer into the analyst's specification sessions so they can contribute their views on the solution before the specs are finalised.
- **B**: Ask the developer to bring forward design improvements they would like, which the PM and analyst will review and approve before the developer proceeds.
- **C**: Broaden the developer's role to include code reviews and mentoring of juniors, giving them more scope and visible influence on the team.
- **D**: Give the developer ownership of the design of the components they build within the agreed requirements, so the "how" is genuinely theirs to decide.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The disengagement has a specific cause, no ownership over the "how", and the cure is to restore decision authority over exactly that, which (D) does within the project's predictive boundaries and is the strongest intrinsic motivator available here. (A) gives the developer a voice into the specs but the analyst still owns the outcome, so it is influence, not ownership, and may not resolve the "pair of hands" feeling. (B) keeps every design choice subject to PM and analyst approval, which dresses up the same disempowerment as participation. (C) adds responsibilities but in a different area; it may help engagement generally, yet it sidesteps the actual grievance about owning the design of their own work. (D) is best because it returns authority over the precise dimension the developer is being denied.
