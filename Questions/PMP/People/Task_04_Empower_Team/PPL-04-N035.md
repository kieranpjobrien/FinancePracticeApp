# Question

Eager to empower a junior coordinator, you handed her sole ownership of stakeholder communications for a regional rollout. Two weeks in, she is clearly overwhelmed: messages are going out late, tone is inconsistent, and she has not asked for help. The communications are starting to confuse stakeholders, and the next major announcement is due in three days. You want to keep developing her. What is the best action?

## Options

- **A**: Keep ownership with her, and pair her with an experienced communicator as an ongoing coach, agreeing together what support she needs to succeed.
- **B**: Keep ownership with her, but agree that for now you will review and sign off each communication before it goes out, easing the reviews back as she steadies.
- **C**: Keep ownership with her, and give her a set of approved templates and a tone guide so her messages become more consistent without added oversight.
- **D**: Keep ownership with her, and have her shadow you drafting the next major announcement so she learns the approach by example.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The cause is over-delegation beyond her current capability, not unwillingness, so the strongest move keeps her owning the work while building the capability that lets her actually deliver. (A) does this best: an ongoing coach develops her judgement across the whole job — tone, timing, stakeholder read — and she helps define the support, so it is sustained empowerment rather than a one-off prop. (B) keeps her ownership nominally but inserting your sign-off on every message makes you the de facto author and can dent her standing with stakeholders; it stabilises output at the cost of her autonomy. (C) is a genuinely useful aid and may fix tone consistency, but templates alone do not address the lateness or the underlying overwhelm — it treats a capability-and-capacity problem as a formatting one. (D) builds skill by example but is a single touchpoint on one announcement; it does not give her the continuing support she needs across the rollout, and learning by watching you can tip into you doing it. The margin is between developing her capability with sustained coaching (A) and the lighter or more controlling supports (B)/(C)/(D) that either over-ride or under-serve her.
