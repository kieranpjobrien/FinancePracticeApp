# Question

Three sprints into a new agile delivery, the development team keeps asking the Scrum Master at planning which backlog items to pull next and how to divide the work among themselves, and the Scrum Master has been answering each time so planning stays on track. Velocity is stable and the burndown is healthy. The team is technically strong and was told at chartering that pull order and task allocation are theirs to own. The Scrum Master wants to move the team toward genuinely making these calls itself without destabilising the steady delivery. What should the Scrum Master do next?

## Options

- **A**: At the next planning, decline to assign the work and ask the team to agree the pull order and the task split themselves, stepping in only if they stall.
- **B**: In the next retrospective, ask the team what is leading them to route these calls to the Scrum Master rather than settling them together, and act on what surfaces.
- **C**: Run a short working session on a lightweight ordering technique — sequencing by value and dependency — so the team has a shared method it can apply to reach its own decision.
- **D**: Reaffirm at planning that these decisions belong to the team, then have it make the next pull-order and allocation call with the Scrum Master in the room but silent unless invited.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The team already holds the authority (granted at chartering) and the capability (technically strong, delivery is healthy), yet still defers — so something is driving the deference that the Scrum Master has not yet identified. All four options are credible empowerment moves and any of them could be right *once the cause is known*; the discriminator is which one the Scrum Master should do next, before acting on a guess. (B) finds the cause first: a self-organising team is built by understanding why it is not self-organising, then matching the intervention to what is actually in the way — an avoided disagreement, a confidence gap, or a habit formed in the first sprints would each call for a different response. (A) hands the decision back cold; if the block is interpersonal or a lack of confidence, withdrawing support mid-sprint risks a stall or a rushed call and can read as abandonment rather than empowerment. (C) supplies a technique, but there is no sign the team lacks a method — it decides nothing, which points to willingness or dynamics rather than know-how. (D) restates authority the team already has and stages a decision, which may simply surface the same deference in a new setting without addressing its source. The finest margin is B over A: both push ownership back to the team, but A acts blind where B diagnoses first, and the right empowerment move is only as good as the cause it is aimed at.
