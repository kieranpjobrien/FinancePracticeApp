# Question

A senior business stakeholder on an agile programme repeatedly messages the developers directly to demand that their features jump the queue, bypassing the Product Owner. The developers, wanting to be helpful, have started quietly reworking the in-progress sprint to fit some requests. The Product Owner is frustrated that the backlog she owns is being reshuffled without her, and the stakeholder genuinely believes his items are urgent for the business. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Sit down with the Product Owner and the stakeholder together to re-establish that she owns prioritisation, and agree how he brings urgent needs into refinement so she can weigh them against everything else.
- **B**: Coach the development team to redirect any direct requests to the Product Owner, so the stakeholder quickly learns the proper channel and the backlog stops being reshuffled mid-sprint.
- **C**: Privately reassure the Product Owner that you back her authority, and take on engaging the stakeholder yourself so she is not drawn into a confrontation with a senior figure.
- **D**: Ask the Product Owner to meet the stakeholder one-to-one to hear his priorities and agree a way of working, stepping in only if they cannot reach an understanding.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The problem has two live parts: the Product Owner's authority is being bypassed, and a senior stakeholder has a real need with no legitimate route in. (A) addresses both at once and, by convening the two of them, visibly reinforces her authority *to* the stakeholder while equipping her to handle similar approaches herself — empowerment plus repair of the channel. (B) fixes the leak through the team but leaves the stakeholder with no constructive path, so the pressure simply resurfaces, and it does nothing to back the Product Owner directly. (C) protects her but solves the problem *for* her by taking over the stakeholder relationship, which undercuts the very authority you are trying to reinforce. (D) keeps her in the lead but sending a junior-to-the-org Product Owner alone to face a pushy senior stakeholder, without first jointly resetting the ground rules, sets her up to be steamrolled; the PM's presence to frame the boundary first is what makes her ownership stick. The finest margin is A over D — both leave the decision with her, but only A establishes the authority in the room before relying on her to defend it.
