# Question

On a hybrid programme you are calibrating how much authority to grant for four upcoming decisions. Each could plausibly sit at more than one level. Match each situation to the most appropriate level of authority to grant.

## Items

- **1**: An experienced developer picking an internal caching approach that stays within the agreed architecture but would set a pattern other teams are likely to copy
- **2**: A team lead proposing to swap a contracted vendor's deliverable for a cheaper equivalent, within budget but altering an agreed statement of work
- **3**: The team choosing to extend its sprint length from two weeks to three to steady its flow, affecting only its own cadence
- **4**: A coordinator deciding whether to bring forward a regulator-facing milestone demo by two weeks

## Matches

- **A**: Full authority — decide and act without consulting you
- **B**: Consult first — recommend, discuss with you, then proceed
- **C**: Escalate — bring to you (and the sponsor) to decide

## Answer

1-B, 2-B, 3-A, 4-C

## Explanation

The calibration turns on reversibility and who bears the consequence, not merely on how "big" each item sounds. The caching approach (1) is technically inside the developer's remit, but because it will set a cross-team pattern its blast radius extends beyond their own work, so consult-first lets the wider implication be weighed before it propagates. The vendor substitution (2) is within budget yet changes a contractual statement of work, so it is not a free internal call — consult-first surfaces procurement and obligation impacts while still letting the lead drive it. Changing the team's own sprint length (3) affects only the team's cadence and is readily reversed, so full authority genuinely empowers self-organisation. Moving a regulator-facing milestone (4) carries external commitment and compliance exposure the coordinator cannot own, so it must escalate to you and the sponsor. Two items legitimately sit at consult-first, which is where uniform "low impact equals full authority" reasoning would go wrong.
