# Question

On a hybrid construction-and-software project, the client's operations manager is a key stakeholder who will inherit the delivered system. He keeps deferring every acceptance decision back to you — "you're the expert, whatever you recommend" — and you sense it is partly that the technical detail of the acceptance criteria feels over his head. You are concerned this hands-off stance will create problems at handover when his team has to live with choices they never engaged in. What should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Bring his operational team into the acceptance reviews and structure each decision around how it will affect their day-to-day running of the system, so the people who must live with the choice actively shape it.
- **B**: Translate each acceptance decision into the operational trade-offs he cares about, walk him through the options in those terms, and ask him to make the call so he builds ownership of the outcome.
- **C**: Invite him to co-define the acceptance criteria up front in plain operational language, so the later sign-offs become decisions he already understands and owns.
- **D**: Propose a few sessions where you brief him on the technical background behind each decision, so he is equipped to engage with the acceptance choices himself.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

His deferral has a specific cause — the *criteria* feel too technical, so by acceptance time the decisions are already framed in language he can't own. (C) attacks that cause earliest and most durably: by co-defining the criteria in operational terms up front, every subsequent sign-off becomes a decision he already understands, so ownership is built in rather than coaxed out at each gate. (A) brings his team in, which is valuable, but routes ownership around him rather than equipping the accountable stakeholder himself, and still presents decisions framed by the project. (B) is a strong move and very close — translating each decision into operational terms and having him decide — but it works decision-by-decision at the back end, repairing the framing repeatedly instead of fixing it at the source where the criteria are set. (D) misreads the gap as missing technical knowledge; loading him with technical background is the opposite of meeting him in his own domain and is unlikely to make him comfortable owning the calls. The finest margin is C over B — both build his ownership, but C removes the barrier at the point it is created.
