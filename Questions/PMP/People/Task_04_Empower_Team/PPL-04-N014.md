# Question

A PM on a predictive aviation-maintenance software project wants to delegate ownership of the release-readiness sign-off to a team lead, but is unsure the lead is ready. The PM has roughly three release cycles before the handover needs to be complete. Which actions genuinely build toward empowered, durable delegation? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Clarify with the lead exactly what release-readiness criteria the sign-off must satisfy.
- **B**: Have the lead shadow the next sign-off, then make the following call as a recommendation the PM reviews, before owning it outright.
- **C**: Have the lead make each sign-off but require the PM to countersign every release before it ships, keeping this in place once the handover is "complete".
- **D**: Agree the boundaries of the lead's authority and the specific conditions under which they should escalate to the PM.
- **E**: Hand the lead the full sign-off now and have a peer from another team review their decisions for the first few releases instead of the PM.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

Durable delegation transfers the decision while building the lead's readiness in steps. (A) clarifies the standard the lead must own, (B) is a graduated handover (shadow, then reviewed recommendation, then full ownership) matched to the three-cycle runway, and (D) defines the authority and the escalation conditions so the lead knows exactly where their remit ends — together these create empowered, lasting ownership. (C) is tempting because countersigning feels like prudent safety oversight, but making it permanent means the authority never actually transfers — the lead carries the accountability without the final decision, which is delegation in name only. (E) looks like a supported handover, but skipping the graduated build-up and dropping full sign-off on an unready lead at once is premature; substituting a peer reviewer does not close the readiness gap and muddies who actually owns the decision. The discrimination is between steps that move real authority across on a sensible ramp (A, B, D) and two plausible-sounding options that either never transfer the decision (C) or transfer it before the lead is ready (E).
