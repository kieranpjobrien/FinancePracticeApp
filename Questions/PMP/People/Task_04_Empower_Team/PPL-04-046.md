# Question

A senior engineer on your 12-person healthcare platform team has asked whether he should "run every architectural decision past you first" to avoid surprises. He has the experience to make most of these calls himself. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Ask him to run every architectural decision past you first, as he suggested, so that you stay informed and can intervene if a decision is heading somewhere unexpected.
- **B**: Co-create a short decision-rights map with him — what he decides, what he consults on, what needs you involved — and publish it so the team understands the shared expectation.
- **C**: Tell him he is empowered to make all architectural decisions without involving you at all so that he is trusted to exercise his own judgement on the platform team's technical direction.
- **D**: Ask him to write a weekly summary of any significant architectural decisions he has made so that you are kept informed without slowing any individual decision down in the moment.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

(B) is the PMI empowerment pattern: empowerment is about clarity of decision rights, not about blanket approval or blanket autonomy. A published map lets him act with confidence. (A) accepts his suggestion at face value and embeds the PM as a bottleneck in decisions he is qualified to make. (C) overcorrects to full autonomy and ignores that some architectural decisions genuinely do need wider engagement. (D) keeps him fully autonomous but substitutes reporting for clarity, which does not prevent decisions that should have been shared.
