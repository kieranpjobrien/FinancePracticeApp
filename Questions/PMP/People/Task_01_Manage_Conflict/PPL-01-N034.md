# Question

During requirements workshops for a hospital records system, the clinical lead and the IT security lead repeatedly clash: clinicians want fast bedside access, security wants strict authentication at every step. Each frames the other's priority as obstructive, and the workshops are stalling. As project manager, what is the best approach to move this forward productively?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate the two leads to articulate the underlying need behind each stated position, then guide them towards a solution that satisfies both rapid clinical access and the security obligation.
- **B**: Bring in a clinical-systems security specialist to present proven authentication patterns used in other hospitals, so the leads negotiate around concrete, field-tested options.
- **C**: Capture both as formally conflicting requirements with their respective risks, and put the trade-off to the project's clinical-governance board to rule on.
- **D**: Ask each lead to bring the specific regulatory and patient-safety standards their position rests on, so the discussion is anchored in mandatory constraints rather than preferences.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four are credible and could feature in a real resolution, so the question is what does the most to unlock value first. The leads are stating opposed positions that actually rest on reconcilable interests — protected data and fast bedside care — so surfacing those interests and problem-solving toward a solution that meets both (A) is the integrative move that can dissolve the apparent conflict (for example via proximity or context-aware authentication). (B) introducing expert options is a strong supporting tactic, but parachuting in patterns before the leads have articulated their own underlying needs can feel imposed and may solve for the wrong constraints. (C) escalating to governance treats the clash as a binary either/or and abandons the chance to satisfy both needs before any integrated option has been explored. (D) grounding the debate in mandatory standards is sensible and may form part of step (A), but on its own it sharpens the contest between two "must-haves" rather than searching for the option that honours both.
