# Question

One hour before a fixed go-live for a payroll system migration, the test lead and the release manager disagree sharply over whether an unresolved medium-severity defect should block the release. The defect mis-calculates overtime for a small group of contractors. The go-live date is contractually fixed, a hold has cost and reputational consequences, and a go/no-go decision is needed within the hour. As project manager, what is the BEST way to resolve the disagreement in the time available?

## Options

- **A**: Agree to go live on schedule with the affected contractors' overtime corrected manually for the first pay cycle and the permanent fix scheduled immediately after, so the date holds and the impact is contained.
- **B**: Get the test lead, release manager and the contractor group's business owner on a call now to weigh the defect together and reach a go/no-go all three support before the window closes.
- **C**: Treat an unresolved payroll-calculation defect as a release blocker, hold the go-live, and notify the stakeholders that the date moves until the fix is verified.
- **D**: Have the release manager and test lead jointly assess the defect against the documented release exit criteria, and let that pre-agreed threshold decide whether it blocks or not.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

With a one-hour window and a genuinely contested, material point, the move is to find a position that meets both parties' core needs quickly: the release manager keeps the fixed date, and the test lead's concern is contained by manually protecting the affected contractors until the permanent fix lands. (A) does exactly that and is the proportionate, decisive choice under hard time pressure. (B) is the ideal collaborative approach in principle and you would prefer it with more runway, but pulling a third party in to deliberate risks consuming the very window the decision has to fit inside, for a defect of this scale. (C) applies a clean rule — never ship a known payroll-calc defect — but treats a contained, mitigable medium-severity issue as an absolute blocker and slips a contractually fixed date without weighing the small, workable impact. (D) is sound governance and, if the exit criteria are clear and decisive, could resolve it fast; the catch is that exit criteria rarely adjudicate a borderline medium-severity defect cleanly, so it can stall inside the hour without producing the agreed call. The fine margin is between brokering a workable middle position now (A) and the options that need time you may not have (B, D) or override the trade-off by rule (C).
