# Question

A powerful department head keeps directing your team members to make "small" changes outside the approved baseline, and when the team pushes back he accuses them of being unhelpful. Team members are now anxious and unsure whether to comply. The sponsor is aware the baseline is at risk. As project manager, what is the best course of action?

## Options

- **A**: Meet the department head to acknowledge his needs, explain the change-control process and its purpose, and agree that future requests are routed through you and into it.
- **B**: Brief the sponsor, who already knows the baseline is at risk, and ask the sponsor to reinforce with the department head that changes must go through governance.
- **C**: Log each instructed change as a formal change request showing its baseline impact, so the head's requests are visible and assessed rather than absorbed unseen.
- **D**: Tell the team to direct any out-of-baseline instruction to you, and step in personally as the single point of contact for the department head's requests.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

This is a relationship and governance problem with a stakeholder who has legitimate needs but is bypassing the baseline, and the most complete first move is to address him directly: acknowledge what he is trying to achieve, explain change control, and agree that future requests come through the PM and into the process (A) — this protects the baseline and shields the team while preserving the relationship, and it does the one thing the others assume has happened. (B) enlisting the sponsor's authority is a sound escalation, but reaching for it before the PM has had a direct, good-faith conversation skips the peer-level step and risks souring an influential relationship the PM still has to work with. (C) logging the requests as change requests is exactly the right mechanism and will likely follow, but applied on its own without the conversation it is procedural — it documents the breach rather than resolving why he keeps going around the process. (D) becoming the single contact point genuinely protects anxious team members and is part of the answer, but as a standalone it manages the symptom (the team being pressured) without addressing the head's behaviour or his underlying need. The fine margin is that all four are constructive, but only A engages the source of the conflict directly while setting up the process and the protection the others provide only in part.
