# Question

A normally vocal QA analyst has gone quiet across the last three sprint reviews after a developer publicly dismissed one of her defect reports as "noise". She still completes her work but no longer challenges anything, and defect escapes have ticked up since. As scrum master, what should you do first?

## Options

- **A**: Speak with the QA analyst privately to understand how she is experiencing the team dynamic, and agree with her how to restore her voice in reviews.
- **B**: Speak with the developer privately about the impact of dismissing a colleague's findings in front of the team, so the behaviour that triggered her withdrawal is addressed.
- **C**: At the next retrospective, raise as a team norm that every defect report deserves a hearing, without naming the incident, so the expectation is reset for everyone.
- **D**: In the next sprint review, deliberately invite the QA analyst's assessment of the build directly, so she has a clear opening to contribute again.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Each option targets a real part of the problem — her, him, the team norm, and the forum — so the question is where to start. Her withdrawal is a conflict-avoidance response to a specific harm, and she is the only person who can tell you how she is actually experiencing it and what would help, so the private, empathetic conversation with her comes first (A) and shapes whatever else you do. (B) addressing the developer is likely necessary, but acting on the trigger before you have understood the affected person's perspective risks misreading the situation and acting over her head. (C) a generic retrospective reminder resets the norm but does nothing for the individual injury and may feel hollow to her. (D) putting her on the spot in the very forum where she was dismissed, before any private repair, can deepen her discomfort rather than draw her out.
