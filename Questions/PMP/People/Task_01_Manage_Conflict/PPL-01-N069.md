# Question

You lead a hybrid programme with a co-located business team in Sydney and an offshore development team in Manila. Tension is building: the Manila team feels its concerns raised in writing are ignored, while the Sydney team feels Manila stays silent in the joint video calls and then misses commitments. You suspect differences in communication style and the time-zone split are amplifying the friction. Which actions would help you manage and reduce this conflict? (Select THREE.)

## Options

- **A**: Establish a team working agreement, co-created by both locations, covering how decisions are confirmed, how written concerns are acknowledged, and expected response times across time zones.
- **B**: Rotate the joint-meeting time periodically so the inconvenience of out-of-hours calls is shared rather than always falling on the Manila team.
- **C**: Appoint a respected Manila team member as a liaison who gathers that team's concerns ahead of each call and presents them on the team's behalf, so the points land even if individuals stay quiet.
- **D**: Create a shared, visible record where written concerns are logged and explicitly responded to, so the Manila team can see its input is being actioned.
- **E**: Move the substantive decisions out of the live video calls and into a written, asynchronous channel both teams can contribute to on their own hours, with the calls used only to confirm what was agreed.

## Answer

A, B, D

## Explanation

The friction is driven by communication-style differences and time-zone asymmetry, so the most effective responses build shared agreements, share the load fairly, and make participation visible — without removing the channels where trust gets built. (A) gives both teams joint ownership of explicit norms, directly addressing the "ignored concerns" and "silent in calls" patterns. (B) shares the burden of inconvenient meeting times, signalling respect and improving Manila's engagement. (D) makes written input visibly acknowledged and actioned, removing the sense of being ignored. (C) is plausible but institutionalises the silence rather than resolving it — funnelling Manila's voice through one proxy can entrench the very disengagement you want to reverse and mask who actually holds a concern. (E) over-corrects by hollowing out the synchronous calls entirely; some real-time contact is what surfaces tone and builds the relationship, and pushing all decisions to async can slow the work and let misunderstandings harden unseen. The better mix keeps the calls but makes them fairer and pairs them with visible written follow-through.
