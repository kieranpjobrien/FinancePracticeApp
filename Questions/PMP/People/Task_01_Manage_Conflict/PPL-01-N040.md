# Question

Your agile team has become noticeably polite and quick to agree. Design choices that once drew vigorous debate now pass with little discussion, and two members privately tell you they hold back objections to "keep the peace". As scrum master, what is the most appropriate action?

## Options

- **A**: Model and reinforce that dissent is welcome — surface the muted concerns yourself, thank those who raise them, and agree team norms that protect disagreement.
- **B**: Introduce a structured technique such as silent brainstorming or anonymous input into refinement so quieter objections reach the table without anyone having to confront a peer.
- **C**: Assign a rotating "devil's advocate" each sprint whose job is to challenge the leading proposal, so a dissenting view is always voiced.
- **D**: Raise the pattern with the whole team in the next retrospective and ask them to examine why objections are being withheld and what would change it.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The objections exist but are being suppressed because voicing them feels unsafe, so the most effective action targets that cause: the scrum master makes disagreement visibly safe by raising the muted concerns, rewarding those who speak up, and agreeing norms that protect dissent (A) — this rebuilds the conditions for genuine debate rather than engineering a substitute for it. (B) is a strong technique and a real aid for quieter voices, but it routes around the fear instead of reducing it; the team can still avoid open disagreement, and the underlying reluctance persists once the technique is set aside. (C) guarantees a dissenting voice is heard, yet a mandated contrarian produces obligatory, often low-stakes opposition and signals that real disagreement still is not safe to own personally. (D) surfacing the pattern in a retrospective is a legitimate and collaborative move and may well accompany the fix, but asking a team that already feels unsafe to publicly dissect why it stays silent can reproduce the very dynamic it is probing unless safety is established first. The fine margin is that all four address the missing debate, but only A works on the safety deficit that is causing the silence.
