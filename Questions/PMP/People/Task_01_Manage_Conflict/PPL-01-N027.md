# Question

On a construction fit-out project, the mechanical and electrical subcontractor foremen are in a heated standoff over sequencing of ceiling-void work, each insisting their own pipework or conduit must go in first and that the other should yield. Both are experienced, both have raised it loudly, and the rest of the trade is watching. As project manager stepping in, what is the most useful first thing to establish before trying to resolve the dispute?

## Options

- **A**: Which foreman has the stronger personality and is driving the standoff, so you can manage that relationship directly.
- **B**: Whether the two foremen are actually working to the same current revision of the coordination drawings.
- **C**: How much clear working space the ceiling void genuinely has, so you can decide how to allocate it between the trades.
- **D**: Whether either subcontractor's contract or the construction programme already states who has install precedence in the void.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Before mediating positions you need to know whether the two foremen are even arguing about the same design — a coordination dispute that looks like a clash of wills is very often two parties reading different drawing revisions, which is quick to check and, if true, dissolves much of the standoff at once. So confirming they are on the same current coordinated drawing (B) is the most useful first thing to establish, because it is both highly probable here and the fastest to verify. (A) sizing up personalities reframes a likely technical-information problem as an interpersonal one and risks intervening on the wrong axis. (C) the void's true clearance does matter, but measuring and allocating space is premature until you know both foremen are reading the same coordinated layout — the "conflict" may not be a real space constraint at all. (D) install precedence in the contract or programme is worth knowing and may resolve genuine sequencing rights, but it is slower to run down and only helps if the dispute survives the much more likely revision mismatch.
