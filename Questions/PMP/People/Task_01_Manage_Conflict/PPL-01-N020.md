# Question

A capable new team member confides that on her last project the manager treated any objection as disloyalty, so she has learned to stay quiet when she disagrees. She is clearly still wary. You want her to start contributing dissenting views on your team, where constructive disagreement is genuinely welcome. What is the most effective thing for the project manager to do?

## Options

- **A**: The next time she raises even a small dissenting view in a team setting, engage it seriously on its merits in front of the others and act on it where it is right.
- **B**: Tell her directly that on your team disagreement is valued, give her recent examples of when challenging a decision changed the outcome for the better, and encourage her to speak up.
- **C**: Add reviewing decisions and inviting objections as a standing step in team meetings, so dissent becomes an expected routine rather than something individuals must risk initiating.
- **D**: Ask another respected team member to mentor her and model how people on this team raise and debate disagreements openly.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four genuinely help build psychological safety, so the margin is which most directly converts this wary individual's first attempt into evidence that dissent is safe here. (A) is best: acting on her own dissent visibly, in the moment, gives her direct personal proof that outweighs any assurance — it is her experience, not a general norm, that has to change. (B) is helpful and well-targeted, but it is still words and second-hand examples to someone whose lived experience taught her the opposite; it sets expectations without yet testing them. (C) builds a durable team-wide habit and lowers the cost of speaking up, but a structural routine works slowly and does not by itself prove to her that her objections will be received well. (D) is a reasonable reinforcement, yet it routes her growth through a proxy and relies on her trusting the modelling before she has tried it herself.
