# Question

On a predictive infrastructure upgrade, a senior network engineer who is your only resource for a critical-path firewall migration has been pulled onto an urgent operational incident by her functional manager, with no return date given. The migration window is fixed by a regulatory deadline four weeks away. You have already raised the dependency informally with the functional manager twice, each time getting verbal reassurance but no commitment. What is the BEST next step?

## Options

- **A**: Meet the functional manager with the schedule impact quantified and the regulatory deadline shown, and jointly agree either a firm return date or a named backfill with a defined handover.
- **B**: Re-sequence the plan to pull forward the non-firewall work the engineer is not needed for, buying time, and lock in her firewall availability for a specific later date with the functional manager.
- **C**: Take the resource conflict to the sponsor now with the deadline and the two prior raises documented, and ask the sponsor to set the priority between the incident and the migration.
- **D**: Identify and brief an external contractor or a second internal engineer who could run the firewall migration, so a viable alternative exists whether or not the engineer is freed in time.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Two informal raises have produced reassurance but no commitment, so the next move is a structured, evidence-based negotiation with the person who actually controls the resource — quantifying the critical-path impact turns a vague ask into a concrete decision, and insisting on a date or a named backfill protects the deadline either way, while keeping the relationship intact for the work still to come. (A) does exactly that and is the proportionate next step before escalating over anyone's head. (B) is sound scheduling that you would likely do as well, but on its own it still leaves the firewall task with no committed resource — it manages the symptom and defers the core negotiation. (C) jumps to the sponsor before a fact-based negotiation with the functional manager has been properly attempted; escalation is a legitimate later step if (A) fails, but going there first strains the working relationship and may prove unnecessary. (D) lines up a fallback resource, which is prudent contingency, but commits to replacing the engineer before you have even tried to secure her or an agreed backfill through the manager — it can be premature, costly, and signals you have written off the negotiation. The fine margin is between negotiating the actual blocker now with the right person (A) and the options that either work around it (B), over-escalate it (C), or pre-empt it (D).
