# Question

Two developers have clashed repeatedly across two facilitated conversations and a refreshed working agreement, and their disputes are now derailing iterations and demoralising the wider team. As scrum master, what is the most appropriate next step?

## Options

- **A**: Hold a third facilitated session, this time setting an explicit behavioural agreement with a defined review point and consequences if it is not met.
- **B**: Escalate to the developers' functional manager and HR, sharing your documented attempts, so intervention beyond your authority can be considered.
- **C**: Restructure assignments so the two work on separate components with minimal overlap, removing the friction points while you decide what else to do.
- **D**: Meet each developer one-on-one to understand what is actually driving the clash before choosing how to proceed.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The discriminator is that facilitation has already been tried twice and a working agreement refreshed, yet the team is now being harmed — the conflict has outgrown what the scrum master can resolve, so the responsible move is to escalate to the line manager and HR with the documented record (B), bringing in the authority and tools (performance management, reassignment with due process) that sit above the role. (A) is a reasonable next facilitation refinement and would be right earlier, but a third variation of an approach that has failed twice, while the team suffers now, persists with a method whose limit has been reached. (C) physically separating them relieves the symptom and can be a sensible interim step, but on a small team it is often impractical, leaves the underlying conflict unresolved, and quietly rewards the behaviour. (D) one-on-one diagnosis is the correct opening move for a fresh conflict and may surface a hidden cause, but here the scrum master has already worked the issue through two facilitations; returning to diagnosis understates how far the matter has progressed. The fine margin is that A, C and D are all legitimate conflict tools, but only escalation matches a documented, twice-attempted, team-harming impasse.
