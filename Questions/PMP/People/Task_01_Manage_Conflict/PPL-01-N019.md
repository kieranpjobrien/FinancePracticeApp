# Question

Near the end of the sprint, the product owner wants a user story shown as completed work in tomorrow's demo to a key customer, but the agreed definition of done requires automated tests that are not yet written. The developers say presenting it as finished is misleading and risky; the product owner says the customer relationship depends on showing visible progress. The two are now arguing in front of the team. As Scrum Master, what should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Help the product owner and developers separate "what we can honestly demo" from "what counts as done," so they agree to show the working functionality tomorrow while being clear the automation is still outstanding.
- **B**: Facilitate a quick conversation on whether the team can complete the missing automated tests before the demo, and only if it cannot, agree together how to represent the story's status to the customer.
- **C**: Remind both that the definition of done is the team's agreed standard and cannot be waived for one story, then ask the product owner how they would like to handle the demo within that constraint.
- **D**: Suggest the team pull the missing tests into this sprint as its immediate focus so the story can legitimately be called done in time for tomorrow's demo.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four respect the definition of done and avoid faking "done", so the margin is which best resolves the real tension — an honest customer demo without breaching the standard or burning the remaining hours chasing the wrong fix. (A) is best: it reframes the dispute as demo content versus completion status, which lets the customer see genuine progress tomorrow while the team keeps its standard intact, resolving the conflict rather than racing the clock. (B) is reasonable and even shares (A)'s endpoint, but it leads with a feasibility scramble — if the tests cannot be finished, the team has spent its scarce time discovering that instead of preparing an honest demo. (C) correctly protects the standard but hands the demo problem back to the product owner alone, stopping short of the joint solution the situation needs. (D) commits the team to a late-sprint push for one story, which may displace other committed work and gambles the customer demo on finishing tests under pressure.
