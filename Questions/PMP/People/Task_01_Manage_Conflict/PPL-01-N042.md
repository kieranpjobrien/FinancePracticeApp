# Question

On a predictive pharmaceutical validation project, your lead automation engineer is being pulled onto a separate line-of-business initiative by her functional manager, who outranks you. The engineer is caught between you both and her work on your critical path has stalled. You have already shown the functional manager the quantified schedule impact, but he maintains his priority stands. What should the PM do NEXT?

## Options

- **A**: Escalate to the sponsor with the quantified critical-path impact and request a decision on the competing priorities.
- **B**: Re-sequence your schedule to work around the engineer's absence and absorb the delay within available float.
- **C**: Ask the functional manager to agree a part-time split of the engineer's hours that keeps your critical-path tasks moving.
- **D**: Negotiate with the functional manager again, this time offering to release the engineer fully once your milestone clears.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The PM has already negotiated at peer level and reached a firm impasse on a genuine resource conflict affecting the critical path, so the next move is (A): escalate to the sponsor, the one party with the organisational authority to arbitrate between two competing mandates. (C) is a credible negotiated middle ground and would be a strong move had the first negotiation not already failed — but the functional manager has explicitly held his priority after seeing the impact, so proposing another split is unlikely to break a deadlock he has chosen to maintain, and a half-resourced critical path may still slip. (D) likewise reopens a negotiation that has just failed, offering a future concession with no new leverage. (B) silently accepts a delay the sponsor may not tolerate and leaves the underlying contention unresolved, so it treats the symptom rather than the priority clash. The distinction the question turns on is that A, C and D are all legitimate, but only escalation introduces the authority needed once peer negotiation is exhausted.
