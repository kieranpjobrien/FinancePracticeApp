# Question

A hybrid programme is moving a previously waterfall finance team onto two-week iterations. The long-serving business sponsor keeps demanding a fixed, fully-detailed plan for all twelve months, while the delivery lead insists only the next two iterations can be committed in detail. Their disagreement is now stalling the kickoff. What should the PM do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Explore the sponsor's underlying need behind the demand for certainty, then show how a rolling-wave plan can meet it within the iterative approach.
- **B**: Produce a high-level roadmap with target dates for the twelve months, detailed only for the near term, and present it as the plan to both parties.
- **C**: Arrange for the sponsor to sit in on the next two iterations so the cadence builds their confidence in the adaptive approach.
- **D**: Bring the sponsor and delivery lead together to negotiate how much of the plan is fixed versus elaborated over time.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The clash is positional — "twelve months fixed" versus "two iterations only" — and masks an interest: the sponsor wants predictability and assurance. The first step is to uncover that interest and reframe it as something a rolling-wave, progressively-elaborated plan can satisfy (A), which dissolves the standoff rather than bargaining across it. (B) is the subtlest distractor because a rolling-wave roadmap is very likely the eventual artifact — but producing and presenting it before understanding why the sponsor wants certainty risks solving the wrong problem; if the real need is, say, a board commitment or budget assurance, a roadmap handed over without that conversation may be rejected as still not "fixed" enough. (C) is a reasonable confidence-building move but is slow and presumes the issue is unfamiliarity with cadence rather than an unmet need the PM has not yet surfaced. (D) jumps to negotiating the fixed-versus-flexible split before the sponsor's actual concern is on the table, so it bargains over positions instead of addressing interests. The fine margin is that B, C and D could all form part of the solution, but only A starts by learning what the sponsor truly needs.
