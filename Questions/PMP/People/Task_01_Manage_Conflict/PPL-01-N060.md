# Question

On a Scrum-based logistics platform, several conflicts arise. Each could plausibly be handled by more than one party. Match each conflict to the party who most appropriately owns resolving it in an agile context.

## Items

- **1**: The product owner and a developer disagree over whether a story is "done"; the dispute is really about whether it meets the agreed Definition of Done.
- **2**: Two developers are in a recurring personal clash that they have tried and failed to settle, and it is now degrading the team's collaboration.
- **3**: Sales has committed a feature to a customer that conflicts with the priorities the product owner has set for the shared backlog.

## Matches

- **A**: The development team and product owner, against the agreed Definition of Done
- **B**: The Scrum Master, coaching and facilitating resolution
- **C**: The product owner with business stakeholders, via backlog and governance

## Answer

1-A, 2-B, 3-C

## Explanation

Each case is deliberately mis-assignable, so the test is who is genuinely accountable. A "done or not" dispute is not a Scrum Master call and not a business-escalation; it is resolved by the team and product owner checking the work against the agreed Definition of Done (1-A) — the artifact exists precisely to settle this objectively. A recurring interpersonal conflict the pair cannot resolve themselves is where the Scrum Master coaches and facilitates (2-B); routing it to business stakeholders or forcing the team to "self-organise" it again would both be wrong. A sales commitment that cuts across backlog priorities is a business-alignment problem the product owner must reconcile with stakeholders through governance (3-C), not something the Scrum Master or development team owns. Misrouting any of these — escalating the Definition-of-Done question, or treating the sales conflict as a team facilitation issue — is the classic error.
