# Question

You are coaching a project manager on conflict-handling modes. In each situation more than one mode could be defended; match each to the single mode that best fits the stakes, the time available, who holds authority, and how much each party's position genuinely matters.

## Items

- **1**: A vendor and your integration lead are deadlocked on which system owns master data. Both designs are credible and the choice will shape the platform for years. You are early in design with real slack, and the vendor is pressing to lock its design in now so it can release its team to other work.
- **2**: Your strongest engineer and a junior both want the lead on a six-week module. The engineer is the safer pick and assumes it is hers. The junior has been waiting for a stretch opportunity, you have the bandwidth to back them closely, and you have been looking for ways to deepen the team beyond its one or two stars.
- **3**: A commissioning engineer wants to keep a gas line energised to save a day; your safety officer says it must be isolated first. The hazard is live, the procedure already requires isolation, and the decision is yours to make. The engineer is well regarded and you will be relying on their goodwill next week.
- **4**: Two team leads are stuck on which team absorbs an unplanned 30 hours of rework. Both have fair points, there is no slack for a long worked-through session, and you need them aligned and moving by end of day.

## Matches

- **A**: Collaborating
- **B**: Forcing (directing)
- **C**: Accommodating
- **D**: Compromising

## Answer

1-A, 2-C, 3-B, 4-D

## Explanation

Each item is built so a second mode tempts, and the detail decides. In 1 the stakes are high and long-lived, both designs are credible, and there is time — collaborating (A) is worth the investment for a durable, jointly owned answer; the vendor's push for a quick split is the lure toward compromising, which is exactly what you should resist on a master-data design that constrains the platform for years. In 2 the obvious call is to give the lead to the stronger engineer (which reads as forcing or simply the efficient pick), but the situation is weighted toward developing the bench and you have the capacity to support the junior — yielding the immediate lead to grow them is accommodating (C) in service of the larger aim. In 3 the hazard is immediate, procedure mandates the action, and the authority is yours: that is the justified use of forcing/directing (B), and the goodwill note is there to tempt you into accommodating on something non-negotiable. In 4 two fair positions, no time for full collaboration, and a need to move today point to compromising (D) — a split both can live with beats chasing the perfect answer. The same surface dispute can demand different modes depending on stakes, authority and what you are optimising for.
