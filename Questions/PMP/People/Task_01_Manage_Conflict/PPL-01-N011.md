# Question

In sprint review, a contractor on your team argues to replace a safety-critical design rule with a cheaper alternative that the regulator has not approved, and several developers find the cost argument persuasive. The release that depends on this is committed for next sprint. As the team lead, the regulation leaves no room to deviate. How should you handle the disagreement?

## Options

- **A**: State plainly that the compliant rule is non-negotiable for regulatory reasons, then redirect the team's energy to finding the most cost-effective compliant design within that constraint.
- **B**: Facilitate an open working session for the team to weigh the contractor's alternative against the current rule and reach a shared decision the whole team can own.
- **C**: Add the contractor's proposal to the backlog as a spike so the team can investigate whether the cheaper alternative could be made acceptable before the release.
- **D**: Acknowledge the cost concern, then defer to the contractor's judgement on this item since they hold the relevant specialist expertise and the team is divided.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The discriminator is that the regulation is genuinely non-negotiable, which narrows when collaboration is appropriate. (A) is correct: where a safety or regulatory requirement admits no deviation, the leader forces the compliant position and then channels the team's legitimate cost concern into compliant alternatives — directing on the boundary, collaborating within it. (B) treats a settled constraint as an open question and invites the team to "decide" something it has no authority to change, which wastes effort and risks legitimising a non-compliant path. (C) spends a spike investigating an option the regulator has already foreclosed. (D) defers to expertise on the wrong axis — the contractor's skill does not override a regulatory limit, and yielding here trades compliance for harmony.
