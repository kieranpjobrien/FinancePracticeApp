# Question

A test engineer reports to you on a regulatory reporting project and to another project manager on a separate initiative. He tells you, visibly stressed, that both of you have asked him to treat your respective work as "top priority" this week and he cannot do both. What is the best first action?

## Options

- **A**: Reassure him you will sort it out, then ask him which of the two sets of tasks he could realistically complete this week so you can plan around his view of the load.
- **B**: Work with him to map exactly what each project needs from him this week and the true deadlines, so you have a clear picture before approaching the other manager.
- **C**: Meet the other project manager to compare the two projects' deadlines and dependencies and agree how to share the engineer's time, then communicate a clear plan back to him.
- **D**: Escalate to the resource manager who owns the shared engineer, asking them to set the priority between the two projects for this week.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

All four take the engineer's distress seriously; the margin is where the conflict actually gets resolved. (C) is best because the clash is between two managers competing for one resource, and only direct negotiation between those managers can settle it — after which a clear shared plan relieves the engineer, who should never be left to adjudicate between two bosses. (B) is sensible preparation and would reasonably precede (C), but on its own it stops short of the conversation that resolves anything. (A) effectively asks the engineer to choose between his two managers' priorities, pushing the decision onto the person least able to make it. (D) escalates to the resource manager before the two PMs have even attempted to coordinate, which is premature when a peer conversation could resolve it.
