# Question

A PM on a telecoms rollout handles three disputes in one day. Each could be argued more than one way; match each to the conflict-handling mode that best fits what the situation actually allows.

## Items

- **1**: Two vendors both want their integration slotted into this week's window. The PM is indifferent to which goes first, the work can flex either way, and both vendor leads are getting impatient and want an answer before they leave the call.
- **2**: A junior tester is right that a senior architect's code breaches an agreed naming standard. The architect is touchy about being corrected by a junior, the build cuts over to production this evening, and the standard can be tidied in a follow-up.
- **3**: The PM and the QA manager are at odds over how much regression to run before release. A miss here could ship a real defect, each has data behind their view, and the go/no-go is still several days away.

## Matches

- **A**: Collaborate (problem-solve)
- **B**: Compromise (reconcile)
- **C**: Smooth / accommodate

## Answer

1-B, 2-C, 3-A

## Explanation

The same surface — a disagreement to settle — calls for different modes once you read what each situation permits. In 1 the PM has no stake, the timing can bend, and two impatient parties want a fast answer; a quick split both can accept is compromise (1-B). The pull toward collaboration is the trap: building an integrative win-win for a simple sequencing call no one cares about would be effort out of all proportion. In 2 a correct but minor point sits against a prickly relationship hours before cutover; accommodating (2-C) lets the small standard ride and be fixed afterwards, protecting both the relationship and the deadline — forcing the correction now, or opening a full problem-solving session, spends capital and time the moment does not have. In 3 the stakes are real, both sides hold evidence, and there are days of runway; that is where collaborating to the best-supported scope earns its cost (3-A), and meeting in the middle would trade away genuine risk coverage to feel fair. Note that none of the three is decided by the loudest cue alone — each could be misread as a neighbouring mode, and stakes, time and relationship together settle it.
