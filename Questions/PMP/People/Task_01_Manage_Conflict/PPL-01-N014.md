# Question

Two developers are arguing because each believes the other agreed to own the database migration, and the migration is now overdue. As you listen, it becomes clear they each left a prior meeting with a genuinely different understanding of the decision. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the two together to talk through how they each understood the decision, and have them agree ownership and next steps between themselves.
- **B**: Check the meeting notes and decision log to establish what was actually agreed, then confirm ownership with both developers.
- **C**: Sit with both developers now to agree who takes the migration from here and a recovery plan, treating who-said-what as secondary to catching up the overdue work.
- **D**: Ask each developer to write down their recollection of the decision, then compare the accounts to pin down where the understanding diverged.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

This is a conflict rooted in differing information, not personality or values, and every option engages with that — so the margin is which gets to a reliable shared fact fastest. (B) is best: the decision record is the objective source that settles what was actually agreed, removing the ambiguity at its source before ownership is reconfirmed. (A) relies on reconciling two contradictory memories with no external reference, which can simply re-run the original disagreement. (C) is pragmatic about recovery but assigns ownership while leaving the root misunderstanding unexamined, so the same gap can recur on the next hand-off. (D) surfaces where perceptions diverged but still rests on memory rather than the authoritative record, and is slower than simply consulting the log.
