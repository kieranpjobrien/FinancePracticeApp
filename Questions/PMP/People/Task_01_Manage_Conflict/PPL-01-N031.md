# Question

In a daily stand-up you notice two team members exchanging short, clipped remarks and avoiding eye contact after a disagreement yesterday over who would take a shared task. The tension is mild but visible to the rest of the team. As scrum master, what is the most appropriate action?

## Options

- **A**: Catch the two members briefly together just after stand-up to surface the friction and help them settle who owns the task.
- **B**: Speak with each of the two members separately first to understand the disagreement, then bring them together if needed.
- **C**: Wait and watch through today's stand-up, ready to step in only if the friction grows, since over-reacting to minor tension can amplify it.
- **D**: Raise with the wider team, without naming anyone, that task ownership disagreements should be settled in refinement rather than carried into stand-up.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The friction is mild, the cause is known and specific (an unsettled task owner), and it is already visible — so the proportionate move is a quick, private, same-day intervention with the two of them to resolve the concrete question (A) before it hardens. (B) separate conversations are sometimes warranted, but for a small, transparent dispute over one task they are heavier than needed and delay a resolution the pair can reach together in minutes. (C) "wait and watch" lets a visible friction sit unaddressed and risks it festering across the day, which is the opposite of resolving conflict early while it is cheap. (D) a team-level reminder generalises a two-person issue to everyone and still leaves the actual task ownership unresolved.
