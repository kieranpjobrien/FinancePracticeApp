# Question

On a Scrum team building an insurance quoting engine, the product owner has begun directing developers on technical implementation details mid-sprint, and the developers are frustrated because it cuts across the design they agreed at planning. Tension surfaced in today's stand-up. The product owner believes she is simply clarifying priorities and keeping the work aligned to business value. What should the Scrum Master do?

## Options

- **A**: Help the product owner reframe her concerns as outcomes and acceptance criteria she can add to the stories, rather than implementation instructions.
- **B**: Coach the product owner privately on the boundary between the "what" she owns and the "how" the development team owns, then reaffirm it with the team.
- **C**: Surface the role friction openly at the next stand-up so the whole team can recalibrate how they work together.
- **D**: Facilitate a short session for the product owner and developers to talk through the design disagreement directly and realign.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The trouble is a recurring role-boundary breach the product owner does not recognise as one, so the Scrum Master's core job — coaching toward correct roles — is best served by addressing her privately first to protect her standing, then reinforcing the boundary with the team (B); this treats the pattern, not just today's instance. (A) is a genuinely good technique and would likely form part of the coaching, but offered on its own it equips her to express intent better without naming the boundary she keeps crossing, so the overreach can simply recur in better-worded form. (D) sensibly resolves the specific design clash, yet it frames a "who decides how" problem as a one-off disagreement to be talked through, leaving the underlying role confusion intact for the next sprint. (C) raises a coachable individual behaviour in front of the team before any private conversation, inviting defensiveness. The fine margin is that A, B and D are all constructive and non-escalatory, but only B targets the repeating cause — the boundary itself — rather than its current symptom.
