# Question

During a sprint review for a mobile banking app, the product owner tells a developer in front of the stakeholder guests that the demoed feature "is not what the business asked for", and the developer replies that the acceptance criteria for that story were never written down. Both are partly right: the feature does miss an expectation, and the criteria genuinely were never recorded. The room goes quiet and roughly fifteen minutes of the review remain. As the Scrum Master, what should you do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Acknowledge that there is a real gap to close, ask the stakeholders to give their feedback on the increment as demoed, and carry both the missing-criteria issue and the way it surfaced into the retrospective.
- **B**: Note the gap aloud, ask the product owner to state briefly what the business actually expected, and have the stakeholders react to that expectation so the review still produces usable direction.
- **C**: Let the demo stand, ask the stakeholders whether the feature as shown meets their need, and use their answer to decide on the spot whether the story is done or needs rework.
- **D**: Move the review forward to the remaining items now, and speak with the product owner and developer privately straight after to deal with both the call-out and the undocumented criteria.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The sprint review exists to inspect the increment and gather stakeholder feedback, and with the room already tense the first job is to keep that purpose intact while routing the real problems to where they belong. (A) gets the feedback the review is for, names the gap honestly so it is not buried, and sends both the process failure (missing criteria) and the interpersonal one (the public call-out) to the retrospective, the proper venue for team-process conflict. (B) is reasonable and keeps the review productive, but reopening "what the business expected" mid-demo invites the stakeholders into refereeing a live scope dispute and keeps the developer exposed. (C) is also defensible — stakeholder reaction is exactly what tells you if a story meets the need — but settling done-or-not on the spot, off an undocumented expectation, converts the feedback session into a verdict and bypasses the product owner's ownership of acceptance. (D) handles the people side well but abandons the review's purpose for the remaining items by treating "keep moving" as enough, when the gap needs to be acknowledged in the room, not just offline. The margin is between protecting the review's feedback purpose while deferring the disputes (A) and the variants that either resolve too much live (B, C) or under-handle the moment in the room (D).
