# Question

On a hybrid public-sector digitisation programme, two senior stakeholders disagree over release sequencing: the service director wants the citizen-facing portal first for visible wins, while the operations director wants the back-office system first to avoid manual workarounds. You are mediating, and both will have to live with and support whatever sequence is chosen. Which actions will most help reach a durable resolution? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Draw out the underlying interests behind each stakeholder's stated position.
- **B**: Establish objective criteria, such as value delivered, risk, and dependency, to evaluate the sequencing options against.
- **C**: Explore whether an incremental release can deliver elements that serve both interests sooner.
- **D**: Take the sequencing decision to the programme board with a recommendation, so a governance ruling settles it authoritatively.
- **E**: Alternate the order — portal in this release, back-office in the next — so each director gets their priority in turn.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

A durable resolution that both stakeholders will support comes from principled negotiation: surface the interests beneath the positions (A), agree objective criteria to judge the options (B), and generate creative incremental options that serve both needs sooner (C). (D) and (E) are not illegitimate — a genuine impasse may need governance, and alternating is a real compromise device — but each is premature here, where the parties have not yet been mediated. Escalating to the board before exploring interests outsources a decision the stakeholders could own and is more likely to leave the "loser" disengaged; alternating splits the timeline by position without testing whether an incremental design could give both something now, and it bakes in a sequence that may ignore real dependency or risk. The reason to prefer A, B and C is precisely the durability constraint: solutions the parties shape and that rest on agreed criteria stick, whereas an imposed ruling or an arbitrary split tends not to.
