# Question

Your agile team serves a single backlog, but two product owners — one for the mobile app, one for the web platform — have been giving the team contradictory priorities, and developers are caught switching focus mid-sprint based on whoever spoke to them last. Morale is suffering and nothing is finishing. As Scrum Master, what is the best course of action?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate the two product owners to merge their items into a single ranked backlog and agree how they will settle future priority calls between them, escalating to their shared sponsor only if they cannot align.
- **B**: Take the conflict to the sponsor both product owners report to and ask them to designate a single decision-maker for the backlog, since two equal owners over one team is a governance gap only the sponsor can close.
- **C**: Split the team into a mobile squad and a web squad so each product owner has a dedicated backlog and the competition for the team's capacity is removed at source.
- **D**: Establish an interim rule that priorities are fixed at sprint planning and cannot change mid-sprint, giving the developers a stable order to work to while the two owners reconcile their longer-term plans.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The root problem is structural — one team, one backlog, two equal voices — and the most direct fix is to get those two voices to produce a single ranked order and an agreed way to resolve future contention, with escalation kept in reserve. (A) does this and keeps prioritisation with the people accountable for value. (B) correctly reads it as a governance gap, and if the two cannot align the sponsor must indeed step in, but going to the sponsor first to impose a single decision-maker skips the facilitation the Scrum Master can run and surrenders a conflict the owners can likely resolve themselves. (C) genuinely removes the competition by giving each owner a dedicated team, yet it is a heavyweight reorganisation that may not be warranted, can fragment a shared codebase, and treats a prioritisation problem with a restructuring; it dissolves the conflict rather than teaching the owners to manage one backlog. (D) is a sensible stabiliser — protecting the sprint from mid-flight churn addresses the most visible damage — but freezing priorities only contains the symptom for one sprint and leaves the unresolved question of whose work ranks higher to resurface at the next planning.
