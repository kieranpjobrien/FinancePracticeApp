# Question

On a hospital systems integration, two technical leads are in conflict over whether to use a message queue or direct API calls between modules. The argument has become heated and is now consuming most of each daily call. Both positions have technical merit, and the architecture decision genuinely affects the next two sprints. What should the PM do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Have the leads agree the criteria that matter — latency, reliability, operational cost, delivery timeline — and evaluate both options against them together.
- **B**: Move the architecture debate off the daily call into a dedicated design session so the standup stays focused on delivery.
- **C**: Bring in the enterprise architect to facilitate the leads' evaluation and lend authority to whichever option they land on.
- **D**: Have each lead build a quick spike of their approach and compare the results before the next decision point.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The disagreement is task-based with two credible positions, so the first step is to depersonalise it by having the leads agree the decision criteria and weigh both options against them together (A); this converts a clash of advocacy into a shared evaluation and is what makes any later step — a spike, a facilitator — actually conclusive. (B) is a sensible piece of meeting hygiene and should probably happen, but relocating the argument does not resolve it; the same stalemate just moves to a different room unless the basis for deciding changes. (C) brings in a capable facilitator, yet doing so before the leads have structured their own evaluation risks the decision being seen as imposed and skips the step that lets the team own the outcome. (D) is a strong technique for genuine uncertainty but is premature here: with no agreed criteria, two spikes produce data nobody has agreed how to judge, so the duel continues over the results. The fine margin is that all four are legitimate, but only A establishes the shared basis the others depend on.
