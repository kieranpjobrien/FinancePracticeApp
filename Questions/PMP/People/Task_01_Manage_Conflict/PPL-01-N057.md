# Question

A normally cohesive agile team supporting a retail loyalty app has become conflict-prone over the last two sprints, with bickering in stand-ups and several heated exchanges. The friction is recent and the team had been working well before it. As the team's project manager, what should you do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Speak privately with several team members, review what changed around the time the friction began, and observe the next stand-ups before deciding on a response.
- **B**: Raise the rising friction at the next retrospective and have the team itself name what is driving it and propose actions to reduce it.
- **C**: Reseat the most frequently clashing pair onto separate stories to relieve the immediate tension while you work out what is going on.
- **D**: Bring the two or three people involved in the sharpest exchanges together to talk it through with you facilitating, and agree how they will work together going forward.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The friction is recent and its cause is not yet known, so the most effective first move is to understand it from several angles — private perspectives, a scan of what changed in scope, deadlines or team composition, and direct observation of when it surfaces — before choosing a fix. (A) does exactly this and keeps every later option open. (B) is a strong, closely competing choice: the retrospective is the team's natural improvement forum and team-owned actions stick, but handing the diagnosis to the whole team in an open session assumes the cause is something the group can name aloud, when it may be an individual workload or interpersonal issue people will not raise in front of each other — useful soon, but it can fix the wrong thing if run before you know the driver. (C) buys immediate calm but acts on a guess that the problem is a single pair, and if the real driver is, say, an ambiguous priority hitting everyone, reseating two people masks the signal. (D) goes straight to a facilitated reconciliation of the people who clashed loudest, which presumes the conflict is interpersonal and between them specifically, before you have established whether it is. The fine margin is between understanding first across multiple sources (A) and the options that act on one hypothesis early — the team (B), a pair (C), or the loudest two (D) — any of which may be right once the cause is known.
