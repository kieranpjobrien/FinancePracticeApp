# Question

On a predictive grid-modernisation programme, the regulatory affairs lead and the engineering lead are in entrenched conflict: regulatory wants additional documentation cycles that engineering says will slip the in-service date by a quarter. Both have legitimate organisational mandates and neither will move. You have already facilitated two structured sessions between them without progress. The steering committee meets next week. What is the BEST approach for the PM?

## Options

- **A**: Prepare a decision paper setting out both positions, the trade-offs, and options, and bring the unresolved conflict to the steering committee for a governance decision.
- **B**: Convene one more facilitated session, this time with a neutral subject-matter expert, to test whether a phased documentation approach could close the gap.
- **C**: Reframe the dispute around objective criteria — compliance exposure, schedule risk, cost — and have the two leads re-evaluate the options against them.
- **D**: Add half the requested documentation cycles as a compromise, accepting a partial slip so both leads get part of what they want.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Two stakeholders with legitimate, conflicting mandates have held firm through two structured facilitations, so the conflict now exceeds what the parties can resolve and what the PM has authority to settle; the BEST move is to frame the trade-offs and escalate to the body empowered to set the priority (A). (B) and (C) are the strongest distractors because each is a real collaboration technique and a single failed attempt would not justify abandoning them — but here facilitation has already been tried twice and the impasse is a clash of mandates, not a gap that one more session or a fresh criteria exercise is likely to bridge before the committee meets; persisting risks running out the clock on a decision only governance can make. (D) imposes a split on something that may not be cleanly divisible, pleasing neither lead while still slipping the date, and it makes a risk-balancing call that properly belongs to governance. The fine margin is that A, B and C are all legitimate in principle, but the exhausted facilitation and the authority gap make escalation the right move now rather than another round of problem-solving.
