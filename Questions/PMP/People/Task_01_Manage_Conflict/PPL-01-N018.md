# Question

A heated conflict between two team leads over a missed integration deadline was resolved last week with a workable agreement, and delivery is back on track. Reviewing what happened, you realise the same type of clash has now occurred three times this year between different people, always around hand-offs between the design and build phases. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Run a focused retrospective with the design and build leads on the recurring hand-off clashes to find the systemic cause, and change the hand-off process so the conflicts stop recurring.
- **B**: Clarify and document the roles and responsibilities at the design-to-build hand-off so each side knows exactly what it owns, removing the ambiguity that keeps sparking disputes.
- **C**: Introduce a joint design-to-build review checkpoint where both leads confirm readiness before each hand-off, so gaps are caught before they become conflicts.
- **D**: Add a standing agenda item to monitor the relationships between the design and build leads so any emerging clash is surfaced and addressed early.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Three similar conflicts signal a process defect rather than a run of personality issues, and the strongest response first diagnoses the cause rather than presuming it. (A) is best: a focused retrospective surfaces the actual systemic driver of the hand-off clashes with the people who live them, then fixes the process — diagnosis before solution. (B) and (C) are both plausible fixes, but each assumes a specific cause (unclear ownership; missing readiness gate) before it has been confirmed, and may treat the wrong thing if the real driver is, say, schedule pressure or incomplete design inputs. (D) only watches for the symptom and helps catch clashes early, leaving the underlying hand-off flaw that keeps generating them untouched.
