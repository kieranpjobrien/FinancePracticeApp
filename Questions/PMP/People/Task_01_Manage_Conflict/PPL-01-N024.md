# Question

A long-simmering personality clash between two developers boiled over during today's retrospective, with raised voices and a personal jibe before you closed the session early. Both are skilled and central to the iteration, and the rest of the team looked shaken as they left. As scrum master, which two actions are the most appropriate immediate next steps? (Select two.)

## Options

- **A**: Speak with each developer individually first to understand each perspective before deciding how to bring them together.
- **B**: Reference the team's working agreement on respectful conduct with the two developers and reinforce that the agreement applies to everyone.
- **C**: Bring the two developers together yourself within the next day to talk through the incident while it is still fresh, before hearing them out separately.
- **D**: Open the next retrospective by inviting the whole team to set norms for how heated disagreements are handled, treating today as a learning trigger for everyone.
- **E**: Document the incident factually in your own records in case the pattern continues and later needs escalation.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

Every option here is something a competent scrum master might do at some point; the question is which two are the right immediate moves. Understanding each person privately first (A) and grounding the conversation in the team's own working agreement (B) are the proportionate, de-escalating steps that address the behaviour and prepare a productive joint conversation. (C) forcing the two together the next day while emotions are still raw, and before you understand each side, risks re-igniting the clash — the private conversations in (A) should precede the joint one, so (C) directly contradicts the better sequence. (D) a team norms session is reasonable later, but making the whole team process two individuals' personal clash so soon is premature and exposes them publicly before the principals have been spoken to. (E) quiet factual notes are sensible if it recurs, but documentation is a safeguard, not one of the two actions that actually resolve the immediate situation.
