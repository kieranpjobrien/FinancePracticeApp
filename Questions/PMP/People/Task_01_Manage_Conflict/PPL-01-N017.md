# Question

During the daily scrum, a developer repeatedly drags the team into detailed design debates with one teammate, overrunning the time-box and frustrating the rest of the team, who have stopped contributing. This has happened for several days. As Scrum Master, what is the most effective response?

## Options

- **A**: When the debate starts, name it as a deeper topic and ask the team to capture it in a visible "parking lot" you work through together immediately after the daily scrum.
- **B**: Acknowledge the topic as important, ask the two involved to take the design discussion to a focused follow-up session straight after the scrum, and reinforce the event's purpose with the team.
- **C**: Raise the recurring overruns in the next retrospective and have the team agree how it wants to handle design deep-dives that surface in the daily scrum.
- **D**: Speak to the developer privately, recognise the value of his design questions, and agree with him how he will raise them outside the daily scrum in future.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

All four are legitimate facilitation responses, so the margin is which resolves the recurring disruption most directly and promptly. (B) handles it in the moment by redirecting only the two people involved to a follow-up, protecting the rest of the team's time while honouring the design need, and reaffirms the event's purpose for everyone — the standard pattern. (A) is close and also sound, but pulling the whole team into the parked discussion straight afterwards keeps everyone tied up in a conversation that concerns only two, which is part of the original problem. (C) defers a daily, ongoing disruption to a retrospective that may be days away, leaving the team to suffer it meanwhile. (D) is a reasonable parallel step but, alone, addresses only the one person and skips redirecting the conversation and resetting the norm for the team.
