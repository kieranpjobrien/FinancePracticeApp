# Question

On a manufacturing automation deployment, a robotics technician and the site safety officer are arguing on the floor about whether a guard interlock can be temporarily bypassed to meet today's commissioning window. A live cell is energised nearby. The technician insists a documented procedure permits a supervised bypass; the safety officer disputes that it applies here. The argument is going in circles and the window is closing. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Direct that commissioning pauses with the interlock in place until the dispute over the procedure is resolved, then address how the two work together afterwards.
- **B**: Defer to the safety officer's call to keep the guard in place, since safety authority sits with that role, and note the schedule impact for the sponsor.
- **C**: Ask the technician to walk the safety officer through the documented bypass procedure so they can jointly confirm whether it applies before anyone proceeds.
- **D**: Escalate to the plant's safety governance contact for an immediate ruling on whether the procedure covers this situation.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A live hazard with an unresolved dispute calls for the PM to remove the immediate danger first by directing a pause with the protective interlock in place (A); this stops the clock from forcing an unsafe shortcut and creates the space to resolve the procedural question and the working relationship without risk. (B) reaches the same physically-safe state and is genuinely tempting — deferring to safety authority is correct in principle — but as written it settles the dispute by rank rather than by halting and verifying, which leaves the technician's documented-procedure claim untested and the conflict unaddressed; the PM's job here is to make the situation safe, not to declare a winner. (C) and (D) are both legitimate paths to resolve whether the procedure applies — and one of them will likely follow — but doing either while the cell stays energised and the argument continues keeps people working a contested safety question in a live hazard; the verification must happen from a safe state, not before reaching one. The fine margin is that all four are defensible PM instincts, yet only A secures safety first and then sequences the resolution.
