# Question

On a globally distributed predictive project, a designer in one region feels a reviewer in another region is being rude and dismissive in written feedback, while the reviewer believes they are simply being direct and efficient. The friction is starting to slow review cycles. As project manager, what is the best first action?

## Options

- **A**: Speak with each of them to understand how they each intend and interpret the feedback, then help the team align on shared expectations for review communication.
- **B**: Facilitate a conversation between the two of them so they can hear each other's intent directly and work out how to communicate going forward.
- **C**: Propose the team adopt a lightweight review etiquette guide (for example, leading with what works, framing changes as questions), so written feedback follows a shared, neutral convention.
- **D**: Add a short synchronous review call to each cycle so the designer and reviewer can talk through the harder feedback live, where tone is easier to read.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Each option is a legitimate communication intervention, so the question is sequencing. The friction is a mismatch of intent and interpretation shaped by differing cultural communication norms, and you have only heard the designer's framing — so understanding how each person both means and reads the feedback, then aligning expectations (A), is the necessary first step that everything else depends on. (B) a direct joint conversation is a likely next move, but bringing them together before you understand each side privately risks the designer feeling ambushed and the reviewer defensive, hardening positions. (C) an etiquette guide is a sensible structural aid, yet imposing a convention before understanding the actual gap may not fit the real problem and can feel like a rebuke to the reviewer. (D) adding synchronous calls can help convey tone, but it adds cross-time-zone burden and sidesteps building the shared understanding of written feedback the distributed team still needs.
