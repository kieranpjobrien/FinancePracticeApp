# Question

Midway through a sprint, a powerful stakeholder contacts a developer directly and presses her to add an "urgent" feature before the sprint ends. Wanting to keep the stakeholder onside, she has quietly started the work, putting the committed sprint goal at risk. You learn of this at the daily scrum. The stakeholder genuinely believes the feature is time-critical. As Scrum Master, what should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the developer and the product owner together now to assess the started work against the sprint goal, and have the product owner decide whether it stays in this sprint or returns to the backlog for prioritisation.
- **B**: Coach the developer on protecting the sprint commitment, then arrange for the stakeholder and the product owner to discuss the request so it is evaluated and ordered in the backlog through the proper channel.
- **C**: Raise the situation in the team's next interaction with the stakeholder, explaining as a team how mid-sprint requests are handled, so the stakeholder hears the boundary directly.
- **D**: Ask the product owner to talk to the stakeholder, confirm whether the feature is urgent enough to justify changing the sprint, and let the product owner adjust the sprint scope if it is.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Every option keeps backlog decisions with the product owner and avoids simply rewarding the out-of-band request, so the margin is fine and turns on addressing both the behaviour and the demand at their source. (B) is best: it coaches the developer on why the sprint commitment matters (the behaviour that let this happen) and reconnects the stakeholder to the product owner for proper evaluation (the demand), restoring the right flow without alienating anyone. (A) handles the immediate disposition of the started work well, but it skips coaching the developer and leaves the stakeholder still expecting direct access, so the pattern recurs. (C) usefully sets the boundary with the stakeholder, but doing it as a future team event delays resolution and does nothing about the work already eroding this sprint. (D) appropriately routes the urgency question to the product owner, yet by framing a possible mid-sprint change first it under-weights protecting the current commitment and again omits coaching the developer.
