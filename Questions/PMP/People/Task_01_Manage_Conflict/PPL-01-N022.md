# Question

On a predictive pharmaceutical packaging project, the validation lead and the production lead both insist they need the same two technicians during the same fortnight. Each has a credible deadline tied to a regulatory submission. They have raised it with you separately, each framing the other as unreasonable. What is the best first action?

## Options

- **A**: Allocate the technicians to validation, because regulatory validation activities are on the critical path and slipping them risks the submission date.
- **B**: Bring both leads together to examine the two work packages against the schedule and submission requirements so the genuine constraints and any sequencing flexibility surface jointly.
- **C**: Split the two technicians, giving one to each lead, so both teams make some progress and neither feels their concern was dismissed.
- **D**: Ask the sponsor to authorise short-term contract technicians so the resource contention disappears without either lead having to give ground.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Each lead has only given you a one-sided account, so the first step is to get both in the room with the actual schedule data to confront the problem (not each other) and discover whether the apparent clash is real or resolvable by re-sequencing. (A) commits to a winner before the facts are jointly examined and assumes critical-path status you have not yet verified with both leads. (C) splitting a resource as a "fairness" compromise often leaves both work packages under-resourced and unfinished. (D) jumps to a procurement solution with cost and onboarding lead-time before confirming the constraint cannot be solved by sequencing — and trained validation technicians are rarely available at short notice.
