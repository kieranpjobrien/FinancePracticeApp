# Question

In a sprint retrospective, a tester says the latest release broke in production "because the developers never write proper unit tests." A developer responds that QA "signs off on everything in five minutes without reading it." The exchange is getting personal, the two are talking past each other, and other members have gone quiet. As the agile team lead facilitating the session, what is the best response?

## Options

- **A**: Acknowledge that both feel let down by the other's part of the process, and ask each to say plainly what they need from the other to trust the hand-off.
- **B**: Ask the team to walk the path the defect actually took — where it should have been caught and where it slipped — and pick one change to the workflow to try next sprint.
- **C**: Name that the conversation has turned personal, agree a working norm of critiquing the process not the person, then resume on that footing.
- **D**: Park the released-defect topic, keep the retrospective on track for the rest of the team, and pick the quality issue up with the two of them and the data straight after.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The blame is flying because the underlying question — how a defect escaped to production — is real and unexamined; the fastest way to both de-escalate and get value is to turn the team to the system rather than the people. (B) does this: tracing the defect's actual escape path makes the discussion blameless by construction and yields a concrete workflow change, which is the retrospective's purpose. (A) is a legitimate interpersonal repair and the trust between the two does need rebuilding, but opening on "what each needs from the other" keeps the lens on the two individuals and can deepen the personal framing before the shared problem is on the table. (C) rightly resets the norm to process-over-person, yet a norm reminder alone redirects tone without giving the team the systemic task that actually channels the energy into improvement, so the friction can resurface. (D) protects the rest of the session and is reasonable if time is short, but deferring the one issue the team is most charged about reads as avoidance in the moment and forfeits the chance to convert a live conflict into a collective fix while everyone is present.
