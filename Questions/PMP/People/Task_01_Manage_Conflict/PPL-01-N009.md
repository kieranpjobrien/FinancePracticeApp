# Question

On a construction fit-out, your mechanical subcontractor and electrical subcontractor are in open dispute over who is responsible for a ceiling-void clash that is now blocking both trades. Each points to the other's drawings. Work in that zone has halted and costs are accruing daily. The contracts are firm-fixed-price. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Convene both subcontractors and the design coordinator over the coordinated drawings to establish where the clash originated before any responsibility is assigned.
- **B**: Direct both trades to resume work in the zone on a without-prejudice basis now, and have the cost responsibility determined once the clash is investigated, so the daily standstill cost stops accruing.
- **C**: Have the design coordinator re-run the clash detection on the latest models, since the dispute hinges on whose drawing introduced the conflict and the coordinator owns the federated design.
- **D**: Direct the trade whose work sits lower in the void to proceed first to keep the sequence moving, and reserve the cost question for the change process once the cause is known.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Responsibility cannot be assigned fairly while the cause is contested, and every credible remedy depends on the same missing fact — which drawing introduced the clash — so the first move is to get the disputing parties and the technical authority over the coordinated drawings together. (A) does this: joint examination both establishes the cause and starts to defuse the blame, and it is the input B, C, and D all presuppose. (B) is a genuinely tempting way to stop the bleeding, and a without-prejudice restart is real practice, but resuming before the cause is even examined risks building in the wrong sequence and trading rework against the standstill cost it saves. (C) correctly identifies the coordinator as the owner of the federated model, yet routing the question to the coordinator alone produces a technical finding without the two subcontractors present to see and accept it, so the dispute can simply reignite when the result lands. (D) keeps the trades moving on a plausible lower-then-higher sequencing rule, but acting on an assumed sequence before the clash is understood pre-judges the very point in dispute and can compound the rework if the assumption is wrong.
