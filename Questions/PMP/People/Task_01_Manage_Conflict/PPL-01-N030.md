# Question

You manage a multi-team release. Match each conflict situation to the conflict-handling mode that best fits that specific context.

## Items

- **1**: Two leads are pushing competing wordings for one line in a board summary you are about to send; you think both readings are fine and the substance is identical, but each wants you to back theirs as the "right" call.
- **2**: An outage is live with customer data at risk; two engineers each insist their rollback path is safer and are still debating, and as incident lead you are accountable for the next action.
- **3**: Your team and a partner team disagree on a shared interface they will both build against for the coming year; your team holds the authority to set it, and the design freeze is still weeks away.

## Matches

- **A**: Collaborate (work it through to an integrated solution both own)
- **B**: Force/direct (decide and impose the next action yourself)
- **C**: Smooth/accommodate (let the minor point go rather than contest it)

## Answer

1-C, 2-B, 3-A

## Explanation

The pull in each case is towards a different mode than the best one, which is what makes the match non-obvious. (1) It is tempting to convene the two leads and reconcile the wording, but the substance is identical and the only thing at stake is whose phrasing "wins" — investing in collaboration here wastes goodwill on nothing, so smoothing (let it go, pick one, move on) is proportionate. (2) Collaboration looks attractive because both engineers have expertise, but a live incident with data at risk and a single accountable lead cannot wait for consensus — directing the next action is the responsible mode, and you reconcile views afterwards. (3) You hold the authority and could simply impose the interface, which is the tempting shortcut, but both teams must live with it for a year and there is time, so forcing it now buys a fast decision at the cost of buy-in and quality — collaborating to a jointly owned contract is what pays off. Choosing collaboration in (2) or force in (3) would each fit the surface cue but misread what the context actually needs.
