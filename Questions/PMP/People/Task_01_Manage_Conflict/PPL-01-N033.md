# Question

Your newly formed agile team has had three avoidable flare-ups in two weeks, all triggered by ambiguity over who decides what and how disagreements should be raised. You want to reduce recurring conflict structurally rather than firefighting each instance. Which two actions best support that goal? (Select two.)

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate the team to create a working agreement that makes explicit how decisions are made and how disagreements are escalated.
- **B**: Add a standing retrospective item to inspect team interactions and adapt the working agreement as the team learns.
- **C**: Define a clear decision-rights map (who is consulted, who decides) for the recurring decision types that have been triggering the clashes.
- **D**: Run a one-off team-building workshop to strengthen interpersonal relationships across the new team.
- **E**: Bring in your manager to set out the team's decision-making rules so there is clear authority behind them.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

The flare-ups stem from ambiguous norms in a brand-new team, and the two strongest structural responses are a team-built working agreement that codifies decision-making and escalation (A), plus a recurring retrospective check that lets the team inspect and adapt those norms as it matures (B) — together these create self-owned, evolving structure. (C) a decision-rights map is genuinely useful and tempting, but on its own it addresses only the "who decides" half and not how disagreements are raised, and it is better produced as part of the working agreement than as a separate edict; between the options it is the next-best, not one of the top two. (D) relationship-building can help a new team but does little about the structural ambiguity that is actually driving the conflicts. (E) having a manager impose the rules undermines the self-organisation that makes the norms stick and is less durable than a team-owned agreement.
