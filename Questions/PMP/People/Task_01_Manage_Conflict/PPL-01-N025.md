# Question

On a predictive infrastructure project, your integration vendor and your in-house network team have now failed three scheduled handovers in a row, each blaming the other's incomplete documentation. The slippage is threatening the go-live date, and the vendor's contract does specify documentation standards for handover. As project manager, what is the most effective first step?

## Options

- **A**: Convene a joint working session with both sides to walk through the last failed handover and agree exactly which artefacts each party must provide, in what form, and by when.
- **B**: Hold separate conversations with the vendor lead and your network lead to hear each account candidly, then decide how to bring them together.
- **C**: Review the handover requirements already defined in the contract and the project's handover plan, and check the last handover against them to establish what was actually missing.
- **D**: Invoke the contract's documentation clause and require the vendor to remediate their deliverables at their own cost before the next handover attempt.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

All four are plausible, and the contract genuinely is in play, which makes the contractual option tempting. But three consecutive failures point to a process and interface that both sides are interpreting differently, so the most effective first step is to get them in one room to reconstruct the failure and agree concrete, mutual deliverables (A) — this fixes the shared defect and ends the blame loop. (B) hearing each side separately is reasonable, but it delays the joint problem-solving and risks you carrying distorted second-hand accounts between two parties who need to align face to face. (C) checking the documented requirements is useful input, yet on its own it adjudicates "who breached" rather than repairing the handover, and the failures plainly involve both sides, not just the vendor's artefacts. (D) reaching for the clause treats a two-sided collaboration problem as a one-sided compliance dispute, hardens the vendor's stance, and does nothing about your own team's contribution to the failures.
