# Question

This is the third standup running in which the same two developers reopen the caching-approach argument, each time pushing the e-commerce replatforming team past its timebox while the others wait. The decision genuinely affects the current sprint's stories, the two have already met offline twice about it without converging, and the rest of the team is visibly disengaging. As the Scrum Master, what should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Close the standup on time, then convene a focused decision session with the two developers, the product owner and anyone else with a stake, with a clear way for the call to be made if they still cannot agree.
- **B**: Close the standup on time and ask the two developers to bring the caching question to the product owner today, since it affects sprint scope and the product owner can set the direction.
- **C**: Use the remaining standup minutes to let the team hear both positions once and take a quick decision so the question stops recurring and the sprint can move.
- **D**: Raise the recurring pattern at the next retrospective so the team agrees how technical disputes are handled, and ask the two to keep working the caching question between themselves until then.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Two offline attempts have failed, the dispute is now blocking sprint work and eroding the standup, so the need is a structured path to an actual decision with the right people and a defined tie-breaker if they still deadlock. (A) protects the timebox and sets that up, which is what an unresolved, work-blocking technical dispute requires. (B) is close and sensible — the product owner does own scope and should be involved — but routing it to the product owner alone may not resolve a technical design disagreement the two developers hold, and it omits the explicit decision mechanism that two failed attempts show is needed; (A) brings the product owner in and adds the path to a decision. (C) forces a binding technical call inside the standup among people who may lack the context, trading a real resolution for a fast one and undercutting the two developers' ownership. (D) correctly identifies the retrospective as the place for the behavioural pattern, but parking a live, sprint-blocking decision until then leaves the work stuck while the already-failed offline approach continues. The fine margin is between convening the right group with a tie-breaker now (A) and the variants that hand it to one role (B), force it in the wrong forum (C), or defer it (D).
