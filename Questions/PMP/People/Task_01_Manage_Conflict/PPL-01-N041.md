# Question

During sprint planning for a mobile banking app, two senior developers disagree sharply over the story-point estimate for a payments feature: one argues five points, the other thirteen. After two rounds of planning poker the gap has not narrowed, both are now repeating the same arguments, and the team is disengaging. The sprint goal depends on committing this item today. What should the Scrum Master do?

## Options

- **A**: Have each developer state, in one sentence, the single assumption that most drives their number, then re-vote once on that basis.
- **B**: Time-box a short technical huddle for the two of them to reconcile their approaches, and have the team estimate the remaining backlog while they do.
- **C**: Split the item into a thin, well-understood slice the team can estimate confidently and a follow-on for the contested complexity.
- **D**: Take the average of the two estimates as the commitment so the team can move on and adjust velocity from actuals next sprint.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

A persistent estimate gap that survives two poker rounds and has stalled into repetition signals the story bundles a known part and a genuinely uncertain part; splitting it (C) lets the team commit the understood slice today and isolate the contested complexity, which resolves the disagreement and protects the sprint goal without guessing. (A) is sound facilitation and may yet converge a normal disagreement, but the assumptions have already been aired across two rounds, so surfacing them once more is the weaker bet for a gap this entrenched. (B) keeps the team productive and is reasonable, yet it defers the very decision the sprint goal depends on today and pulls two seniors out of planning on a disagreement that is really about hidden scope, not approach. (D) manufactures a number neither developer believes; averaging a 5 and a 13 hides the structural uncertainty the gap is pointing at and distorts velocity, even with later correction.
