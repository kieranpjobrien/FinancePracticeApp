# Question

Two Scrum teams building a shared logistics platform are in open conflict over a shared API contract. The payments team says the routing team changes the contract mid-sprint and breaks their builds; the routing team says payments stops attending the joint design sessions where those changes are agreed, then objects after the fact. Each product owner has complained to you separately about the other, and the next sprint starts in two days. As the Scrum Master supporting both teams, what should you do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Get both teams together to lay out what each side is actually experiencing, then have them agree how shared-contract changes will be proposed, reviewed and communicated from the next sprint on.
- **B**: Facilitate a working session focused on the API contract itself — walk the current interface, agree which parts are stable, and have the teams define a versioning approach so changes stop breaking consumers mid-sprint.
- **C**: Raise the recurring dependency at the next scrum of scrums and ask the programme to confirm a shared expectation that contract changes are agreed jointly before either team builds against them.
- **D**: Have both product owners meet with you to reconcile their two accounts and commit to a single way of handling contract changes that they will then take back to their teams.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

There are two one-sided accounts and a process failing at both ends — changes made without consumer input, and a consumer skipping the forum where input happens — so the first move is to bring the people doing the work into one room to establish a shared picture and let them own the fix, which is the self-organising agile pattern. (A) does this directly and addresses both halves of the breakdown. (B) is a strong, closely related move and you may well run it next, but jumping straight to the technical mechanics (versioning, stable surfaces) treats a collaboration breakdown as purely a design problem and skips surfacing the behaviour — payments not attending — that the contract approach alone will not fix. (C) escalates to the programme before peer resolution has been tried and seeks an imposed expectation rather than a team-owned agreement, which is heavier than the situation yet warrants. (D) sensibly involves both product owners, but resolving it between the two POs and handing the outcome down keeps the teams who actually hit the problem out of the conversation, weakening ownership of whatever is agreed. The fine margin is between convening the whole working group to co-own the resolution (A) and the variants that narrow it to the interface (B), escalate it (C), or settle it above the teams (D).
