# Question

On a hospital construction fit-out, the mechanical and electrical subcontractors are in escalating conflict over who must re-route services around a clash that neither flagged during coordination. Each blames the other, work in that zone has slowed, and both have hinted at a claim if forced to absorb the cost. You want a resolution that actually holds and keeps the trades working together for the rest of the fit-out. As project manager, which TWO actions best serve that aim?

## Options

- **A**: Convene both subcontractors with the coordinated services drawings and the contracted scope splits, and work the problem from the documented facts rather than the competing accusations.
- **B**: Have your design coordinator establish which trade's model was revised last before the clash appeared, and ask that subcontractor to take the re-routing on that basis so work can resume.
- **C**: Steer the discussion onto what each party is really worried about — cost exposure and programme slippage — and search for a re-routing solution and cost basis both can live with.
- **D**: Refer the responsibility question to the contract administrator for a formal determination under the subcontracts, and stand the zone down only until the determination is issued.
- **E**: Propose splitting the re-routing cost and labour evenly between the two trades so neither concedes fault and the zone can restart today.

## Answer

A, C

## Explanation

A resolution that holds and preserves the working relationship has to rest on objective facts and on what each party actually needs, not on assigning blame or buying speed. (A) grounds the conversation in the drawings and contracted scope, dissolving the he-said-she-said and giving any outcome a defensible basis. (C) moves the parties off their positions ("not my fault") onto their underlying concerns (cost and programme exposure), which is where a durable, mutually acceptable answer becomes possible. (B) reaches a one-sided verdict on a single piece of evidence and assigns the work before either party has been heard, so it is the option most likely to trigger the claim each has hinted at. (D) is a legitimate fallback if facilitation fails, but going straight to a contractual determination hardens the dispute, sidelines the relationship, and leaves the zone idle while it runs. (E) restarts work fast but settles on positions with no factual basis for the split; a 50/50 with no reference to who actually bears the scope tends to unravel once the real cost lands, and rewards whichever trade was at fault. The trap is reaching for the quick restart (E) or the fast verdict (B); the pairing that both resolves and endures is facts plus interests (A, C).
