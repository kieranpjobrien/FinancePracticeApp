# Question

In an agile data-analytics team, two senior analysts have a long-running personality clash that resurfaces in every retrospective, turning improvement discussions into pointed exchanges the rest of the team now dreads. The friction is between the two of them specifically, not about the work, and it has been going on for several iterations. As the team's agile coach, what is the BEST first approach?

## Options

- **A**: Meet each analyst privately to understand the friction from their side, then bring the two together to agree concrete ways they will interact going forward that they both commit to.
- **B**: Facilitate the next retrospective more actively yourself — name behaviours as they occur, redirect personal remarks back to the work, and model the tone you want the two to adopt.
- **C**: Have the team co-create a working agreement on how disagreements are raised and handled, so the expectation applies to everyone rather than singling the two analysts out.
- **D**: Bring the two analysts together directly to talk it through with you facilitating, surfacing the issue between them without the separate one-on-ones first.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

This is a persistent interpersonal conflict between two specific people, so the most effective opening is to understand each perspective privately and then broker a concrete agreement between the two who actually own the problem — private first lowers defensiveness and lets each speak candidly before they are in the same room. (A) does this and targets the real source. (B) is a legitimate facilitation response and you may use it alongside, but tighter facilitation manages the symptom in the session without resolving the underlying relationship, so the clash will keep resurfacing. (C) is a reasonable team-level move, yet a general working agreement treats a two-person relationship problem as a team-norms gap; it can help the room but is unlikely to fix friction that is specifically between these two, and may feel like a public workaround aimed at them. (D) gets to the right two people but skipping the separate conversations risks the joint session reigniting the very exchange you are trying to defuse, with neither having had space to be heard first. The fine margin is between addressing the two individuals privately then jointly (A) and the options that work the symptom in the room (B), generalise it to the team (C), or go joint without the groundwork (D).
