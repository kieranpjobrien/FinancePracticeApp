# Question

On a predictive defence-systems project, a conflict between the systems-engineering lead and the test lead over interface responsibilities has been unresolved for two weeks. You are deciding whether to escalate above the two leads. Which factors, on their own, legitimately support escalating now? (Select all that apply.)

## Options

- **A**: Direct facilitation between the leads has been attempted more than once and the positions have not moved.
- **B**: The unresolved item now sits on the critical path and is consuming schedule float that cannot be recovered locally.
- **C**: Resolving it requires reassigning a responsibility that is fixed in another team's charter, which neither lead can change.
- **D**: The dispute has run for two weeks, which exceeds the resolution time your team norms set for open conflicts.
- **E**: A higher-graded programme director has the relevant interface expertise and could give a faster technical answer than the leads will reach.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Each option is a real consideration, so the test is whether it independently justifies going over the leads' heads. (A) exhausted lower-level resolution, (B) genuine and unrecoverable project impact, and (C) a decision that exceeds the parties' authority are the three classic, sufficient triggers. (D) is a near-miss: a breached time-box is a prompt to act, but elapsed time alone does not say the leads cannot still resolve it — it tells you to intervene, not necessarily to escalate, and the right intervention might be a sharper facilitation deadline. (E) is the subtlest trap: escalating because a senior person could decide faster or knows more bypasses the leads who own the work and turns escalation into convenience; speed and expertise elsewhere do not make a matter beyond the parties' remit. Escalation is driven by exhausted alternatives, real impact, and lack of authority — not by a clock or by who could answer quickest.
