# Question

During a sprint retrospective for an e-commerce platform, a developer blames a tester by name for a defect that reached production, and the tester reacts defensively. The mood in the room turns tense and other team members fall silent. What should the Scrum Master do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Acknowledge the frustration, then steer the discussion to how the defect slipped through the process and what the team can change to catch the next one.
- **B**: Ask the team to capture the defect as an improvement item and move on to the rest of the agenda so the session does not stall on one incident.
- **C**: Pause the retrospective and arrange to meet the developer and tester separately afterwards to work through the personal friction.
- **D**: Name the working agreement on respectful communication, invite the team to recommit to it, and then resume the discussion.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A blame exchange in a retrospective is best handled in the moment by validating the emotion and redirecting to a systemic, no-fault analysis (A) — this restores psychological safety for the watching team and converts the incident into a real improvement action. (B) defers the live tension to a backlog item and presses on, leaving the blame framing and the chilled room unaddressed. (C) is the right vehicle for a deeper interpersonal rift, but reaching for a separate meeting first abandons a teachable moment the whole team is witnessing and treats a normal blame-slip as a private dispute. (D) invokes the agreement but stops short of redirecting the actual conversation, so the systemic learning is missed and the blame can resurface.
