# Question

During sprint planning for a payments platform, two senior developers disagree sharply on whether to refactor the legacy authentication module now or defer it. Both have credible technical arguments grounded in real constraints, the debate has run for twenty minutes, and the rest of the team has gone quiet. The product owner is in the room and the sprint goal is still undecided. What should the Scrum Master do next?

## Options

- **A**: Frame the open question for the product owner in terms of value and risk, and ask them to choose whether this sprint's goal targets the refactor or the feature work the two approaches are competing to serve.
- **B**: Have the two developers each prepare a short proposal and bring it to the next backlog refinement, deferring the sprint goal decision until the wider team can weigh both in detail.
- **C**: Ask the team to agree a small, time-boxed investigation this sprint to gather the missing data, and set a sprint goal that delivers value regardless of how the refactor question later resolves.
- **D**: Surface that the disagreement is really about sequencing risk, and have the team commit to the refactor now since deferring technical debt on an authentication module raises the security exposure.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

All four are things a capable Scrum Master might do, which is why the call is fine. (C) is best: it unblocks planning immediately by separating the sprint goal from the unresolved refactor, while a time-boxed probe produces the evidence the opinion-versus-opinion standoff lacks — the team keeps moving and the decision waits for data. (A) hands a technical-sequencing call to the product owner, who owns value and ordering but not how the team structures its engineering work. (B) defers the whole sprint goal to a future event, stalling a planning session that should still produce a commitment today. (D) forces a resolution on a plausible-sounding security rationale, but it overrides one developer's credible case without the evidence to justify it.
