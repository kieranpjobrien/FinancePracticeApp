# Question

On a predictive infrastructure project, your lead network engineer is shared 50/50 with an operations team. Her functional manager has just reassigned her full-time to an urgent outage for the next two weeks, which puts a critical-path milestone at risk. The functional manager outranks you and has already informed the engineer. The sponsor expects the milestone on the committed date. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Meet the functional manager to understand the outage's expected duration and severity and whether any of the engineer's project time can be protected, then weigh the options together.
- **B**: Ask the engineer what critical-path tasks could still progress in her remaining capacity, and reshape the next two weeks around what she can realistically deliver.
- **C**: Replan the critical path to reflect the lost capacity, then take the revised milestone date and the resourcing cause to the sponsor for a decision.
- **D**: Have a second engineer begin shadowing the critical-path tasks now so the work can continue at reduced pace if she is unavailable for the full two weeks.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The conflict crosses an organisational boundary — a peer manager has redirected a shared resource over a competing priority you do not yet understand — so the first move is to engage that manager directly and establish the facts before committing to any plan. (A) does this: it surfaces the outage's real duration and severity and tests whether some project time can be preserved, which is the input every other option depends on; the two managers may resolve a partial-availability split between themselves without escalation at all. (B) is a sound mitigation, but reshaping the engineer's workload assumes the full reassignment is fixed and skips the manager conversation that might recover some of her time. (C) is reasonable once the impact is known, yet replanning and presenting a slipped milestone now treats the reassignment as settled and concedes the date before any attempt to resolve the conflict at source. (D) sensibly protects continuity, but committing a second engineer to ramp up on critical-path work pre-empts the conversation that might make it unnecessary and spends capacity before the competing priority is even understood.
