# Question

On a construction management software implementation, the client's operations director and the vendor's solution architect are in open disagreement about whether a reporting requirement was in scope. Both reference the same signed requirements document but interpret one clause differently, and the build is paused pending resolution. What should the PM do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Ask the business analyst to produce a written interpretation of the disputed clause for both parties to respond to.
- **B**: Convene both parties to walk through the disputed clause together and establish the shared facts before deciding scope.
- **C**: Raise a change request so the build can resume now, with the scope-and-cost question settled in parallel.
- **D**: Refer the clause to the vendor's account manager and the client's sponsor under the contract's escalation provision.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The dispute is a live, two-party disagreement over what a clause means, so the first step is to bring the disputants together to surface the facts of what the clause actually says and was intended to cover (B); doing this jointly often dissolves the disagreement and, where it does not, produces the shared understanding any later step depends on. (A) gathers a useful input but has a third party interpret the clause in the disputants' absence, so it risks producing a verdict neither side feels heard on and can harden positions rather than resolve them. (C) presumes the requirement is a change — prejudging the very question in dispute — and may concede cost or scope that a joint reading would have shown was already covered. (D) escalates to senior parties before the two who actually disagree have jointly examined the document, which is premature for a clause-interpretation question they may well settle themselves. The fine margin is that A, B and D all engage the disagreement legitimately, but only B has the parties confront the facts together first.
