# Question

A junior business analyst on your team disagrees with how you have worded the risk section of a status report due to the steering committee in thirty minutes. She feels strongly about her phrasing; you prefer yours. You have reviewed both and each conveys the same risk and severity accurately, with no difference in meaning the committee would act on. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Use the analyst's preferred wording for this report, and note the difference in approach to discuss with her after the committee meeting.
- **B**: Spend a few minutes now walking her through why your phrasing is clearer, aiming to reach a wording you both genuinely prefer before the report goes out.
- **C**: Combine the strongest elements of both versions into a single jointly agreed sentence so neither of you has simply conceded.
- **D**: Keep your wording for this report because you are accountable to the committee, and invite her to propose phrasing improvements for the next reporting cycle.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Each option is something a reasonable PM might do, so the margin is fine and turns on stakes versus time. (A) is best: the issue is trivial for you but matters to her, the two versions are genuinely equivalent, and the deadline is imminent — accommodating yields at no real cost, builds goodwill with a junior member, and the deferred conversation preserves the coaching point. (B) is a sound collaborate move in general, but spending the last thirty minutes debating wording with no material difference is disproportionate and risks the deadline for nothing. (C) manufactures a compromise sentence where one clean, accurate version already suffices, adding edit churn under time pressure. (D) is defensible on accountability grounds, but asserting positional preference over a wording the committee could not distinguish spends relationship capital with a junior analyst for no decision-relevant gain.
