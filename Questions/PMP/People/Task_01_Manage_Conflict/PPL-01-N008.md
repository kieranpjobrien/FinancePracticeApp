# Question

You facilitate a distributed agile team split between offices in Berlin and Bangalore. The Berlin members say the Bangalore members "never push back and just agree to everything," while the Bangalore members privately tell you the Berlin members "talk over us and decide before we've finished thinking." Velocity is dropping and trust is eroding. Which TWO actions should the Scrum Master take to address the conflict at its root? (Choose two.)

## Options

- **A**: Establish team working agreements that build in deliberate space for asynchronous input and explicit turn-taking, so quieter contributions are not overrun by faster real-time debate.
- **B**: Rotate the facilitation of ceremonies between the two sites so each location regularly runs the room and sets the pace.
- **C**: Surface the differing communication norms openly with the whole team and co-create how decisions will be made and dissent will be voiced going forward.
- **D**: Shift the daily scrum and key ceremonies to a time that sits within both sites' working hours so neither group is joining tired or rushed.
- **E**: Bring the two most vocal Berlin members and two Bangalore members together as a small working group to draft proposals the rest of the team then ratifies.

## Answer

A, C

## Explanation

The conflict stems from a mismatch in communication and decision-making norms, not individual fault, so the root-cause fixes are the two that change how the whole team interacts and decides. (A) restructures participation so faster real-time debate no longer crowds out quieter input, and (C) makes the implicit norms explicit and lets the team jointly redesign them — both collaborative and aimed at the cause. The others are all reasonable distributed-team practices but treat symptoms or sit beside the issue: (B) rotating facilitation helps balance airtime but does not, by itself, resolve the decide-before-others-finish dynamic or the trust gap. (D) a fairer meeting time addresses fatigue, a real distributed problem, but not the participation imbalance described here. (E) a drafting sub-group risks hard-wiring the existing imbalance — the vocal Berlin members shaping proposals first — rather than rebuilding shared norms.
