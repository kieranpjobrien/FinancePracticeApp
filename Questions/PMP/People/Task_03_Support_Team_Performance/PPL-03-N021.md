# Question

Three sprints into a 14-sprint mobile banking project, the team's velocity has been 32, 30, then 18 story points. The most recent drop coincided with two team members being pulled onto a production incident for four days. At the next sprint planning, the team is anxious that the lower number will be read as a performance decline and wants to commit to its full historical capacity again. As Scrum Master, what should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Plan the upcoming sprint against the team's available capacity for that sprint, and ensure the velocity trend is reported to leadership with the incident noted as the cause of the dip.
- **B**: Let the team commit to its pre-incident velocity of around 31 points, since the incident is over and the members are now back on the project full-time.
- **C**: Use the team's three-sprint average of about 27 points as the commitment, so the planned figure smooths out the one-off incident rather than overreacting to it.
- **D**: Forecast the remaining release using only the two pre-incident sprints, treating the incident sprint as an outlier that would otherwise understate the team's true throughput.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

(A) plans against the capacity actually available for the coming sprint and keeps the trend transparent with context — the genuine support move, since velocity is a planning and communication tool, not a target to defend. (B) is tempting because the members are back, but committing to a number anchored on a different period ignores the actual capacity and known carry-over of the coming sprint, risking another shortfall. (C) and (D) both manipulate the data set — one averaging the dip in, the other discarding it — to produce a more flattering planning figure, when the honest approach is to use real upcoming capacity and annotate, not reshape, the history. The fine margin is between planning on actual capacity (A) versus reverse-engineering a comfortable number from past sprints (B, C, D).
