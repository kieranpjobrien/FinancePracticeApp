# Question

Two new developers joined your established Scrum team at the start of this sprint. By mid-sprint the team's flow has slowed noticeably as experienced members spend time answering the newcomers' questions, and the two new developers seem hesitant to pick up stories. Which THREE actions best support the team's performance through this transition?

## Options

- **A**: Pair each new developer with an experienced team member on stories so they learn the codebase and practices in context while contributing.
- **B**: Acknowledge with the team that a temporary dip during onboarding is expected, and adjust the sprint forecast to reflect the reduced near-term capacity.
- **C**: Ensure the new developers have access to the team's working agreements, Definition of Done, and key documentation, and orient them to where to find answers.
- **D**: Assign the two new developers only isolated, low-priority tasks for their first few sprints so they do not slow the experienced members down.
- **E**: Ask the experienced members to limit time spent helping the newcomers so the team's velocity recovers to its previous level this sprint.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Integrating new members is a known, temporary cost worth investing in: (A) pairing transfers knowledge fastest while keeping the newcomers productive, (B) honestly resets capacity expectations rather than pretending nothing changed, and (C) gives them the references and norms to become self-sufficient. (D) isolating them on trivial tasks delays real integration and signals they are not trusted. (E) rationing help to chase this sprint's velocity sacrifices the newcomers' ramp-up and the team's long-term throughput for a short-term number.
