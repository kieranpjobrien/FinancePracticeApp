# Question

Three sprints into a new mobile-banking release, the team's burndown is consistently flat for the first half of each sprint and then drops sharply at the end. Stories are being completed, but always in a late rush, and the Scrum Master notices testers sitting idle early in the sprint while developers are overloaded. The team has not raised this as a problem. What should the Scrum Master do first?

## Options

- **A**: Raise the late-finish pattern as an observation in the next retrospective and let the team examine how work is being sequenced within the sprint.
- **B**: Ask developers to break stories into smaller tasks so that completions register earlier on the burndown.
- **C**: Reassign some testing-phase stories to the idle testers to balance the load mid-sprint.
- **D**: Introduce a mid-sprint checkpoint where each developer reports percentage complete so progress is visible earlier.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The pattern points to a team-level flow problem — work is being batched so that testing only begins once development is nearly done — and the team itself should own the diagnosis rather than have a fix imposed. Surfacing it in the retrospective (A) supports performance by helping the team see and solve its own bottleneck, which is the Scrum Master's role as a facilitator. Smaller tasks (B) would smooth the chart but not address why testers are idle early. Reassigning stories (C) is the Scrum Master acting as a resource manager and bypasses the team's self-organisation. A percentage-complete checkpoint (D) introduces command-and-control reporting that agile deliberately avoids and treats the symptom (chart shape) rather than the flow issue.
