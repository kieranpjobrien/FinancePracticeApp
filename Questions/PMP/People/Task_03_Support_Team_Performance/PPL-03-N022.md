# Question

On a predictive infrastructure upgrade, a senior engineer delivers high-quality work but finishes her critical-path tasks two to three days late each time. You met with her privately last week; she explained that incomplete inputs from the design team force her to chase clarifications mid-task, and you both agreed she would flag input gaps earlier. This sprint she flagged nothing and slipped again, and downstream engineers are now quietly building around her late outputs. The milestone is in four weeks. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Meet with her again to understand why the agreed early-flagging did not happen before deciding on any further step.
- **B**: Work with her and the design lead to put the missing-input handoffs on a tracked checklist with dates, so the gaps that cause her slippage surface at the source rather than relying on her to raise them.
- **C**: Adjust the schedule so her critical-path tasks start only once the design team's inputs are confirmed complete, removing the mid-task chasing that drives the slippage.
- **D**: Bring the recurring slippage and the agreed action into the next team meeting so the downstream engineers stop building around her and the handoff is fixed openly with everyone present.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The cause is already known — incomplete design inputs — and a private agreement relying on her to flag them has already failed once, so repeating the conversation (A) re-diagnoses something you have diagnosed and burns a week against a four-week deadline. (B) fixes the actual mechanism: making the input handoff a tracked, dated obligation on the design team surfaces the gaps at their source instead of depending on her goodwill, supporting both her and delivery. (C) is genuinely tempting and partly right, but gating her start on "confirmed complete" inputs can idle her and the critical path while design catches up, trading her slippage for a different delay rather than fixing the handoff. (D) airs one person's repeated miss in front of the team — even framed as a process fix it exposes her and reads as a reprimand, when the handoff can be fixed with her and the design lead directly. The margin is between fixing the upstream handoff that causes the slip (B) and either re-talking (A), shifting the delay elsewhere (C), or fixing it publicly (D).
