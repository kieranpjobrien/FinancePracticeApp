# Question

A Scrum team's velocity has been steady at roughly 32 points for six sprints, but in the last retrospective two developers said the estimates feel arbitrary and that they pad them to avoid being blamed for overruns. The product owner is pressing you to raise the velocity target so more roadmap items fit the next quarter. What should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Coach the team to recalibrate their estimation as a group so the numbers reflect real effort, and explain to the product owner that velocity is a planning input, not a performance lever to be turned up.
- **B**: Set a stretch velocity target of 38 points for the coming sprint to signal ambition, then review whether the team rises to meet it before deciding on further increases.
- **C**: Keep the velocity target where it is but ask each developer privately why their individual estimates have been inflated, so you can address the padding at its source.
- **D**: Replace story points with hours so the product owner gets a more concrete, defensible basis for fitting roadmap items into the quarter's capacity.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Velocity measures a team's own historical throughput for forecasting; raising the target externally corrupts the metric and the trust behind it, so (A) both fixes the estimation problem and protects velocity's purpose. (B) treats velocity as a dial to push, which is exactly what drives the padding the team described. (C) singling out individuals over a team-owned estimate undermines collective ownership and amplifies the blame the developers already fear. (D) swapping units does not resolve the trust issue and discards the relative-estimation benefits the team built up.
