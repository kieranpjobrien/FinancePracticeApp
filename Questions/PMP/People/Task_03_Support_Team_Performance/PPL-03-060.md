# Question

On your analytics programme, new engineers currently take four months to reach full productivity according to your delivery data — well above the team's two-month target. Team members repeatedly say "we don't have time to onboard people properly." Sprint commitments keep missing as a result. What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Keep hiring at the current rate and accept the four-month ramp as an unavoidable cost of growth on the programme; the productivity curve will eventually smooth out over time.
- **B**: Invest sprint capacity explicitly in onboarding — dedicated pairing time, a written starter-pack, and named buddies — and measure ramp time as a team-level performance metric.
- **C**: Pause hiring entirely until the current team has worked through its backlog so that onboarding bandwidth is free enough to support future joiners without stretching senior capacity.
- **D**: Outsource onboarding to HR by formalising a corporate onboarding programme so that the delivery team can focus on delivery while others handle the new-joiner ramp-up conversation.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

(B) is the PMI team-performance pattern: capability investment is sprint work, not after-hours extra, and measurable ramp time makes the investment visible. This is the classic "slow down to speed up" move. (A) accepts the cost without attempting to reduce it and lets poor performance become normal. (C) pauses hiring instead of fixing the system and leaves the team chronically under-capacity. (D) treats technical onboarding as an HR function, which it is not, and breaks the pairing relationship new engineers actually need.
