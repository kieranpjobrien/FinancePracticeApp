# Question

Your scrum team has just delivered a difficult release that succeeded because two developers quietly refactored a fragile module mid-sprint and the team swarmed to cover the resulting test load. You want your recognition to reinforce those specific behaviours so they recur. The team is mixed: some members value public credit, while the two who did the refactoring are known to be uncomfortable being singled out in front of others. What is the most effective way to recognise the team?

## Options

- **A**: In the retrospective, name the specific behaviours that drove the success — the proactive refactor and the swarming on tests — as team strengths to repeat, and thank the two developers privately and concretely for the refactor.
- **B**: At the next all-hands, publicly credit the whole team for the release and call out the two developers by name for the refactor, so the behaviour is visibly rewarded across the organisation.
- **C**: Give the whole team equal public recognition for the release without singling out individuals, so cohesion is protected and no one is made uncomfortable.
- **D**: Privately and specifically thank each contributor for what they individually did, keeping all the recognition one-to-one so the reserved developers are never exposed.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Effective recognition is timely, specific to the behaviour you want repeated, and delivered in a form the recipient actually values — and here recipients differ, so one channel cannot fit all. (A) reinforces the exact behaviours where the team learns from them (the retrospective) while routing the individual credit for the two reserved developers into the private, concrete form they prefer — matching method to person. (B) publicly naming the two developers rewards the behaviour visibly but ignores that they are uncomfortable being singled out, so the "recognition" may embarrass the very people you most want to encourage. (C) equal, generic team credit protects cohesion but is so unspecific that it reinforces nothing in particular and lets the standout behaviours disappear into a blanket thank-you. (D) private specific thanks suits the two developers but, by keeping everything one-to-one, denies public credit to teammates who value it and loses the team-level reinforcement of the behaviours. The margin is between tailoring both the specificity and the channel to who is being recognised (A) and applying a single mode that misfits some of the people or blurs the behaviour (B, C, D).
