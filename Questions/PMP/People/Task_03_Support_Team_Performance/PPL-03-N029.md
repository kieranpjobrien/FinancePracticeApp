# Question

Midway through a construction fit-out project, the schedule performance index (SPI) for the joinery work package has dropped to 0.82 while the cost performance index (CPI) remains at 1.0. The joinery crew lead insists his team is "working flat out", and you believe him. What should the project manager do first to support the team's performance?

## Options

- **A**: Walk the joinery work package with the crew lead to see what is constraining throughput despite full effort, examining upstream dependencies, material availability, and the flow of information and approvals to the crew.
- **B**: Authorise overtime for the joinery crew to lift the schedule performance index back toward 1.0 before the next reporting period.
- **C**: Bring an additional joinery sub-crew onto the work package to add capacity and recover the slipping schedule through crashing.
- **D**: Re-sequence the joinery activities to run more of them in parallel, fast-tracking the work package to claw back the schedule variance.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

An SPI below 1.0 while the crew is genuinely at full effort points to a constraint outside the crew's control — a late predecessor, missing materials, or held-up information — not to low productivity. (A) diagnoses that constraint first, which is the genuine support move because adding effort or capacity does nothing if the team is blocked. (B) overtime piles hours onto a team already flat out and will not help if the bottleneck is upstream supply or information. (C) crashing by adding a sub-crew assumes the work is labour-limited and spends money before the cause is known, risking congestion on the same blocked front. (D) fast-tracking adds rework and coordination risk and, again, presumes the constraint is sequence rather than an external feed. All three corrective actions are legitimate schedule-recovery techniques, but each is premature until the actual impediment is found; the margin is diagnosis-before-action (A) versus applying a recovery lever blind (B, C, D).
