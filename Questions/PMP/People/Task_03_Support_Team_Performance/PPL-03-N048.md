# Question

Your self-organising development team agreed to swarm on the highest-priority story each sprint. In practice, the same two senior members settle the technical decisions in a daily side-channel and the rest implement what they are told. Sprint goals are being met. A mid-level developer confides to you, asking you not to name them, that the team no longer feels self-organising and that juniors' ideas get dismissed. The retrospective is four days away. As Scrum Master, what is the best action?

## Options

- **A**: At the next retrospective, present the decision-making pattern you have independently observed and facilitate the team to agree how technical calls get made inclusively, without attributing the concern to anyone.
- **B**: Before the retrospective, have a private coaching conversation with the two senior members about how the side-channel is narrowing participation, so the dynamic is addressed without exposing it to the whole group.
- **C**: Introduce a lightweight working agreement that significant technical decisions are raised in the daily scrum where the whole team can weigh in, then let the team refine it.
- **D**: Create space for the quieter members by facilitating a session where juniors lead the technical discussion on the next story, shifting the participation balance through practice rather than discussion.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A Scrum Master fosters self-organisation by making a dysfunction visible to the whole team and facilitating *them* to own the remedy, so (A) surfaces the pattern from the SM's own observation (protecting the confider) and lets the team redesign how decisions are made — the most durable fix and the one consistent with the role. (B) coaching the seniors privately can be a useful complement, but as the primary move it keeps the issue between the SM and two people, substitutes the SM's read for the team's, and denies the whole team the chance to reset its own norms; it can also feel like the seniors are being singled out. (C) is a reasonable agreement and may well emerge from the retro, but pre-writing the rule yourself imposes a structure the team has not chosen and treats a participation problem as a process tweak. (D) rebalancing through practice is a strong technique, yet engineering who leads before the team has even acknowledged the pattern can feel contrived and may provoke the seniors without a shared understanding of why. The margin is between letting the team see and fix the dynamic itself (A) and the SM stepping in to fix it for them through a private word, a rule, or a staged exercise (B, C, D).
