# Question

An agile team developing an e-commerce checkout flow has a member whose velocity contribution has steadily declined over three sprints. In a one-to-one, the developer explains they feel stuck on unfamiliar payment-gateway technology and are reluctant to ask the team because they fear looking incompetent. As a servant leader, which actions best support this team member's performance? (Select two.)

## Options

- **A**: Arrange a pairing or mentoring arrangement with a teammate experienced in the payment-gateway technology.
- **B**: Reframe the team's norms so that asking for help is treated as a strength, reinforcing psychological safety for everyone.
- **C**: Quietly reduce the developer's story allocation each sprint so their lower output is less visible.
- **D**: Raise the developer's struggle in the next retrospective so the team can collectively decide how to help.
- **E**: Set the developer a personal velocity target to recover their previous contribution level.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

The root causes are a genuine skill gap and a fear that suppresses help-seeking, so the supportive responses tackle both. Pairing or mentoring (A) directly closes the capability gap with hands-on, low-stakes learning, while reinforcing that asking for help is valued (B) removes the psychological barrier and strengthens the whole team's safety. Reducing the allocation covertly (C) hides the problem, denies the developer growth, and risks resentment if discovered. Naming the individual's struggle in a retrospective (D) breaches the confidence of a private conversation and could deepen the very fear of looking incompetent. An individual velocity target (E) misuses a team flow metric as a personal performance stick and adds pressure rather than support.
