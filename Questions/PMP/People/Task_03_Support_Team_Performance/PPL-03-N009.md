# Question

During a sprint, a developer on your agile team raises in stand-up that the shared test environment — owned and run by a separate infrastructure group — has been unstable for two days, costing the team hours of rework. The team starts debating workarounds at length and the stand-up is running well over its timebox. What is the most appropriate action for the project manager?

## Options

- **A**: Close out the stand-up by having the team take the workaround discussion offline, and take ownership yourself of getting the environment stabilised with the infrastructure group.
- **B**: Direct the team to a specific workaround you used on a previous project so they can stop debating and keep delivering the sprint.
- **C**: Let the team keep working the problem themselves, trusting a capable self-organising team to find its own way around the unstable environment.
- **D**: Extend the sprint by two days to absorb the time lost to the unstable environment, then pursue the root cause after the release.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The instability sits in an environment another group owns, so it is an organisational impediment the team cannot clear on its own however self-organising it is — and the stand-up is meant for coordination, not extended problem-solving. (A) protects the timebox and removes the cross-team obstacle through the PM's reach, which is the distinctive value the PM adds here. (B) hands the team a solution and undermines its autonomy on a technical call it is capable of making. (C) respects self-organisation but misapplies it: the team can choose a workaround, but it cannot fix an environment outside its control, so leaving the whole problem with it abandons the part only the PM can move. (D) shifts the sprint boundary to mask lost time and defers the actual fix, leaving the same instability to recur. The discriminator is recognising which slice of the problem is the team's to solve and which slice the PM must take off their plate.
