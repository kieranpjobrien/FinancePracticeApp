# Question

A developer has been blocked for three days waiting on a database access grant from the security team, and work is now stacking up behind that task. You raised it with the security team's lead two days ago and got an acknowledgement but no date. The developer has found a way to keep progressing using a shared read-only test account, but it would mean the story is only partly verifiable until the real access lands. As the agile lead, what should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Have the developer proceed on the read-only account to keep the story moving, and in parallel go back to the security lead to agree a firm date and surface the cross-team delay to your manager if one is not given.
- **B**: Have the developer switch to the next-highest-priority story that has no external dependency, and leave the blocked story until the access grant arrives.
- **C**: Press the security lead again for a firm commitment and hold the blocked story open until access is granted, keeping the team focused rather than splitting work across a workaround.
- **D**: Escalate the three-day delay to the sponsor now so leadership can direct the security team to prioritise the grant and prevent further slippage to the team's flow.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The block is real and stacking up, but a partial workaround exists and the working-level channel is not yet exhausted, so (A) keeps flow moving while pursuing a firm date and escalating only if the working level fails to commit, which is proportionate. (B) protects this person's time but lets the dependent story and everything behind it stall when a viable workaround is available. (C) keeps the team pure of workarounds but accepts continued idle time and the stacking queue for a verification gap that can be closed once real access lands. (D) jumping to the sponsor before the security lead has been given a clear deadline-or-escalation choice overreaches and spends escalation capital prematurely.
