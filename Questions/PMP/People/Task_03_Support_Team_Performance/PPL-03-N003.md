# Question

Your Scrum team has run the same retrospective format for nine sprints. The same two people speak, the action items from the last three retrospectives were never completed, and in the corridor a developer tells you most people now see the meeting as a box-ticking exercise. The Scrum Master, who is new to the role, asks for your view as the project manager supporting the team. What is the most effective response?

## Options

- **A**: Encourage the Scrum Master to vary the retrospective format and rotate facilitation so the session feels fresh and more people are drawn into the conversation.
- **B**: Suggest the team start each retrospective by reviewing the status of prior action items, so the group sees whether its own decisions are actually changing anything.
- **C**: Propose the Scrum Master give each new action item a named owner and a due date, so accountability for follow-through is unambiguous.
- **D**: Recommend the team agree to take on at most one or two improvement actions per sprint, so the actions are small enough to actually complete alongside the sprint work.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

All four are legitimate retrospective-health moves, but the team's core complaint is that the meeting changes nothing — the credibility of the whole ceremony has collapsed because past decisions evaporate. (B) confronts that head-on: opening with action-item status makes the cost of unfinished work visible to the team itself and is what restores belief that the session matters, which in turn pulls participation back. (A) freshens the experience but a livelier meeting that still produces nothing will lose the team again. (C) and (D) are both sound mechanics for getting actions done — ownership and limiting WIP on improvements — but they are downstream tactics that only bite once the team is actually inspecting its follow-through; without (B) the new owners and limits go unwatched just as the previous actions did.
