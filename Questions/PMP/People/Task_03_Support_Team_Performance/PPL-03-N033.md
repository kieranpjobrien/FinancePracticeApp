# Question

As an agile coach you support four teams, each reporting a different complaint. For each team, you must choose the single most useful next diagnostic. All four teams already track velocity, maintain a board, and hold the standard Scrum events, so simply "looking at velocity" is available to every one of them. Match each team's complaint to the diagnostic that would most directly reveal its underlying cause.

## Items

- **1**: "Our velocity per sprint is steady, but features still take ages to reach stakeholders from the moment we start them."
- **2**: "Each sprint we plan a sensible amount, yet a few large stories carry over almost every time while small ones fly through."
- **3**: "The team's pace is fine, but the sponsor keeps asking, and we cannot confidently say, whether the scoped release lands by the deadline."
- **4**: "Stakeholders accept our demos, but weeks later they report the delivered features behave wrongly in real use."

## Matches

- **A**: Throughput and story-size distribution across recent sprints
- **B**: Cycle-time breakdown by workflow state on a cumulative flow diagram
- **C**: Release burn-up of completed scope against remaining scope and target date
- **D**: Definition of Done compared against what "accepted at demo" actually verifies

## Answer

1-B, 2-A, 3-C, 4-D

## Explanation

The trap is that a steady *velocity* looks reassuring in several of these, so the right diagnostic is the one that exposes what velocity hides. (1) Steady velocity but long elapsed time is a flow problem: a cycle-time breakdown by state (B) shows where work waits — velocity and a burn-up would both look healthy. (2) Consistent carry-over of large stories with small ones flowing is a sizing/throughput pattern; throughput with story-size distribution (A) reveals that big items should be split, whereas raw velocity masks it by averaging. (3) Date confidence against a fixed scope needs a burn-up of done-versus-remaining scope to the target (C); velocity alone gives a rate but not the scope-versus-deadline picture the sponsor is asking for. (4) Features accepted at demo but failing in real use point to acceptance that does not test real conditions, i.e. a weak Definition of Done (D), not a throughput metric at all. Each pairing turns on choosing the diagnostic that surfaces the cause the team's existing velocity view is concealing.
