# Question

On a predictive pharmaceutical validation project, two senior team members who must co-author the same regulated deliverable have long-standing personal friction. Their individual work is excellent, but the joint deliverable is two weeks late because they avoid working together and have been duplicating overlapping sections differently. A milestone review with the regulator is in three weeks, and the deliverable is genuinely integrated — its sections cross-reference and must be internally consistent. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Meet with both to anchor on the shared regulatory goal and define explicit section ownership, interfaces, and a joint review point, keeping the conversation on the work rather than the relationship.
- **B**: Facilitate a direct conversation between the two to surface and work through the personal friction, since the interpersonal issue is the underlying cause of the duplication and delay.
- **C**: Split the deliverable into each person's strongest sections and take on the cross-reference reconciliation yourself, so they can each work without interacting before the review.
- **D**: Have a senior reviewer integrate and reconcile their two separate drafts into one consistent deliverable, so the friction never has to be resolved before the deadline.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

With three weeks to a regulatory milestone, the immediate need is a consistent, integrated deliverable, and both members respect the work even if not each other. (A) gets delivery moving through the work itself: clear section ownership, defined interfaces, and a joint review point remove the duplication and force the consistency the regulator requires, without demanding they first repair the relationship. (B) tackling the personal friction head-on is tempting and addresses the deeper cause, but forcing a relationship conversation under deadline pressure is slow, may inflame things, and is not required to produce a consistent deliverable — the friction can be worked later. (C) splitting the work and personally owning reconciliation can paper over a genuinely integrated deliverable, but it makes you a bottleneck on cross-references you do not own and abandons the collaboration the regulated document needs to be coherent. (D) routing both drafts to a third reviewer institutionalises the avoidance, adds a late integration step on a critical-path deliverable, and pulls another senior off their own work. The margin is between driving the integrated work through structure now (A) and either fixing the relationship first (B) or engineering around the collaboration entirely (C, D).
