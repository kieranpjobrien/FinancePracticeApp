# Question

On a predictive infrastructure programme, a specialist whose work sits on the critical path has begun consistently delivering late and with rework, jeopardising a regulatory go-live in ten weeks. A peer has quietly told you the specialist seems to be struggling with a new toolset introduced last month, though you have not confirmed this. A contractor with proven experience in that toolset is available now but would need two weeks to onboard. The functional manager who owns the specialist's appraisal is on extended leave. What should you do first?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the contractor on now to run the critical-path tasks in parallel, so the regulatory date is protected whatever the cause turns out to be, and reassess the specialist's role once you understand the problem.
- **B**: Have a direct, supportive conversation with the specialist to understand the obstacle and agree what support or training would help, before deciding any schedule or staffing response.
- **C**: Add the slipping critical-path tasks to the risk register and raise the schedule threat at the next steering meeting so a resourcing decision can be made with the programme's governance.
- **D**: Arrange targeted training on the new toolset for the specialist straight away, since the peer's account points to a capability gap and training addresses it directly.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

You have a plausible root cause but only second-hand, so the first step that both confirms the cause and can restore output is the supportive performance conversation in (B): it tells you whether this is the toolset, workload, a personal issue, or something else, and lets every subsequent move (training, contractor, escalation) be the right one. (A) protecting the date with the contractor is a legitimate parallel action and may follow, but committing two weeks of onboarding and effectively sidelining the specialist before you have spoken to them risks losing domain knowledge over a problem targeted training might fix in days. (C) raising it through governance is sound for a critical-path threat, but escalating for a resourcing decision before you have even diagnosed the issue is premature and skips the PM's own coaching role. (D) jumps straight to a solution on the strength of one unverified peer comment; if the real cause is workload or a personal matter, training wastes time the ten-week window cannot spare. The margin is between confirming the cause with the person first (B) and acting on an assumed cause — by replacing, escalating, or training — before it is verified (A, C, D).
