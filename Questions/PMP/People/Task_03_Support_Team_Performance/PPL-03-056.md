# Question

You introduced pair-programming across your 12-person backend team six weeks ago to lift code quality. Four senior engineers have quietly stopped pairing and the defect rate on their work has begun diverging upward from the rest of the team. What should the PM do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Mandate pair programming across the team with attendance tracked so that the four senior engineers no longer have the option to quietly opt out of the agreed practice.
- **B**: Open a team discussion at the next retrospective about pair programming, using the defect divergence data to explore why the practice is not landing for part of the team.
- **C**: Drop pair programming since it clearly does not suit all engineers, and replace it with mandatory peer review of every pull request as an alternative quality control.
- **D**: Meet the four engineers privately to understand their objection, acknowledge their experience, and agree which practices (pairing, peer review, design spikes) they will own to hit the quality bar.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

(B) is the PMI inspect-and-adapt move with data: the retrospective was designed for exactly this conversation and the defect divergence gives the team an evidence base. Opening it publicly also tests whether the resistance is specific or broader. (A) imposes compliance on a resisted practice and erodes trust. (C) abandons a useful practice prematurely before understanding why it is not working. (D) addresses only the four and lets the rest of the team continue pairing without the evidence-driven conversation the whole team needs.
