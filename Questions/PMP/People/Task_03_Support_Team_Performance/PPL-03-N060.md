# Question

A project manager has three team members whose performance has dipped, each for a different underlying reason. For each person, choose the support response most likely to restore their performance. Each response is used at most once; one response fits none of them well.

## Items

- **1**: A senior engineer who is fully capable has gone quiet and is doing only the minimum since the client overruled their design recommendation without explanation two sprints ago.
- **2**: A developer who was a strong performer has started missing details; in a one-to-one they reveal they have been covering a colleague's tasks on top of their own and are stretched thin.
- **3**: A mid-level developer keeps producing work that misses the mark, not from lack of effort but because they consistently misunderstand what "good" looks like for this client's standards.

## Matches

- **A**: Clarify expectations and give concrete worked examples and reference points for the standard, with closer review until their output reliably hits the mark.
- **B**: Re-engage them by bringing them back into design decisions, giving them a voice upward, and restoring ownership over technical choices.
- **C**: Rebalance the workload, hand the colleague's tasks back or reassign them, and protect the person's capacity before the strain causes more errors.
- **D**: Recognise their contribution publicly and set a stretch goal to rebuild their momentum.

## Answer

1-B, 2-C, 3-A

## Explanation

Each person's dip has a different root cause, and the response must target that cause rather than the surface symptom of "reduced output." Person 1 is able but demotivated by being overruled, so the fix is re-engagement and restored ownership (B), not more direction or recognition. Person 2's errors stem from being overloaded, so the fix is a workload intervention (C); coaching or recognition would not relieve the capacity problem. Person 3 has a genuine capability/standards gap, so clarified expectations, examples, and review (A) address it directly. Recognition and a stretch goal (D) fits none here — it would frustrate the overloaded person, ring hollow for the overruled one, and ignore the standards gap of the third; recognition is not a substitute for diagnosing why each person slipped.
