# Question

On a predictive ERP migration, one developer is the sole expert on the legacy data-mapping layer. He is highly productive but every mapping issue routes to him, his components are the only ones no one else can review, and he is showing clear signs of burnout. He has previously declined to pair, saying it slows him down and he is the only one who can hit the dates. Go-live is in three months and the mapping layer is on the critical path. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Agree with him a lightweight knowledge-capture approach he can sustain — recorded walkthroughs and reviewed documentation of the mapping layer — so the knowledge spreads without the live-pairing he resists.
- **B**: Reach a clear agreement with him and his functional manager to ring-fence dedicated time for him to pair and cross-skill a second developer on the mapping layer, accepting the short-term hit to his throughput.
- **C**: Assign a capable second developer to shadow his mapping work and pick up the lower-risk mapping tickets, building their capability from the live queue without adding meetings.
- **D**: Reduce his burnout load now by routing all non-mapping requests away from him, freeing him to both deliver the mapping work and bring someone else up to speed.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Two risks must be solved together — an unmaintainable single-point-of-failure on the critical path and his burnout — and three months is enough time to build real redundancy if protected time is secured. (B) does that: a firm agreement with the person who controls his capacity ring-fences time to actually transfer the skill to a named second developer, which is the only option that durably removes the silo. (A) is tempting and respects his resistance, but documentation and recordings alone rarely transfer tacit mapping knowledge, leave no second person who can *do* the work, and still load him as the only one who can act. (C) shadowing plus low-risk tickets is a sound mechanism, but without ring-fenced time it competes with his deadline pressure and he will deprioritise it exactly as he has deprioritised pairing — it needs the protected-time agreement (B) to hold. (D) relieves load and is reasonable, but offloading other work assumes he will then choose to cross-skill, when he has already said pairing slows him down; it treats the burnout symptom without securing the knowledge transfer. The margin is between locking in protected time to build a second capable person (B) and approaches that capture artefacts, rely on unprotected shadowing, or only relieve load (A, C, D).
