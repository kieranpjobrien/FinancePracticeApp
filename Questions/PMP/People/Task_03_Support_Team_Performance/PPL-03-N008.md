# Question

A project manager is supporting four team members, each of whom is missing the same agreed delivery deadlines. The slippage looks identical on the schedule, but the underlying cause differs in each case. Match each team member to the response that best fits the actual cause of their slippage.

## Items

- **1**: A developer who hit every deadline for months has slipped on the last three; she is quieter than usual and her commit times show she is working late into the night.
- **2**: A capable analyst keeps missing deadlines because she keeps accepting extra ad-hoc requests from a stakeholder and will not say no, so her committed work is crowded out.
- **3**: A new engineer is missing deadlines on a task type he has never done before; his work is correct but he is reworking it repeatedly to get it right.
- **4**: An experienced specialist is missing deadlines on tasks he considers low-value busywork that he openly thinks should not be in scope at all.

## Matches

- **A**: Coach him on the specific technique and pair him with someone experienced in this work so he builds speed without sacrificing quality.
- **B**: Explore privately whether something outside work is affecting her, and adjust load or offer support rather than treating it as a discipline issue.
- **C**: Help her protect her committed capacity, agreeing how she and you will handle incoming ad-hoc requests so they stop displacing planned work.
- **D**: Hear out why he sees the work as low value, and connect the tasks to the outcome they serve — or escalate descoping them if he is right.

## Answer

1-B, 2-C, 3-A, 4-D

## Explanation

Identical-looking slippage has four different causes, and the same blunt remedy would misfire on three of them. Member 1's sudden change from a strong baseline plus late-night work points to a personal or burnout factor, so a supportive private conversation (B) fits — not coaching or expectation-setting, which would misread it as a capability or discipline problem. Member 2 is missing deadlines because of unmanaged inflow, not capability, so the fix is protecting her committed capacity and managing the ad-hoc requests (C). Member 3's accurate-but-repeatedly-reworked output on unfamiliar work is a genuine proficiency gap, addressed by targeted coaching and pairing (A). Member 4's slippage is rooted in disagreement about value, so hearing his reasoning and either re-anchoring the work to its purpose or descoping it (D) is what re-engages him. The distractors are deliberately interchangeable on the surface — coaching, a private welfare chat, and capacity protection could each plausibly be offered to several of them — so only the cause, not the symptom, identifies the right match.
