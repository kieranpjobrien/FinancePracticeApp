# Question

A maturing scrum team is split on which of two architectures to use for a new payments integration. The debate has run two days, the sprint is stalling, and the team is genuinely unsure which option is better — both have credible advocates and the trade-offs are real, not a matter of preference. A junior developer asks you, as Scrum Master, to "just decide for us". What is the best way to support the team's performance?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate the team time-boxing a spike that builds a thin slice of each option against agreed decision criteria, so the choice is made on evidence and stays the team's own.
- **B**: Facilitate the team scoring both options against weighted criteria they agree on now, so they converge on a decision today without spending sprint time on a spike.
- **C**: Bring the technical architect in to advise the team on the trade-offs and recommend an option, leaving the team to make the final call.
- **D**: Ask the team to pick the option that is cheaper to reverse and proceed, treating the decision as a two-way-door they can revisit once they learn more in build.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The team is genuinely uncertain because the trade-offs are real and unresolved by argument, so the support move is to convert opinion into evidence while keeping ownership with the team. (A) does exactly that: a time-boxed spike that tries a thin slice of each against agreed criteria settles a real architectural unknown with data and ends the stall without the Scrum Master deciding for them. (B) criteria-scoring is faster and sometimes sufficient, but here the dispute is over genuine technical trade-offs the team cannot resolve by discussion — scoring opinions they already hold may just re-stage the same deadlock without new information. (C) the architect's input is legitimate and may help, but reaching for an external authority before the team has tried to resolve a question within its own capability weakens the team's growth and ownership. (D) the reversibility heuristic is a sound real tool for low-stakes, easily reversed choices, but a payments architecture is typically expensive to unwind, so "pick the cheaper-to-reverse one and move on" risks anchoring on the wrong axis for a consequential decision. The margin is between resolving a real technical unknown with a quick experiment the team owns (A) and re-scoring opinions, outsourcing the call, or applying a heuristic that does not fit the stakes (B, C, D).
