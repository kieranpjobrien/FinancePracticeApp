# Question

Your 9-person Scrum team's velocity has been stable for ten sprints but cycle time for each story has crept up by 40% over the same period. The team insists nothing has changed. The product owner is asking why features feel slower despite the consistent velocity. What should the PM do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Reduce story size guidance at the next refinement so that stories break down more finely and cycle time trends downward as individual stories shrink.
- **B**: Increase capacity by asking the functional manager to add a tenth engineer so the team can carry the additional work implied by the increased cycle times.
- **C**: Present the velocity data to the product owner to demonstrate that delivery is genuinely stable and that the cycle time signal is being misread as a slowdown.
- **D**: Run a focused diagnostic workshop — value-stream map the last two sprints — to find where time is actually being spent before changing any process or capacity.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

(D) is PMI's "diagnose before treating" applied to delivery metrics: cycle-time drift with stable velocity is a classic WIP or blocker signal, and value-stream mapping surfaces where time is actually going. (A) treats a symptom (story size) before the cause is understood. (B) adds capacity to a system that may be bottlenecked, not under-resourced, which often makes cycle time worse. (C) dismisses a legitimate product-owner observation and misreads what velocity and cycle time each measure.
