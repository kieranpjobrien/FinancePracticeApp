# Question

A new hire is six weeks into a 12-week ramp-up plan and is still slower than expected. Two senior engineers have started complaining privately to the PM that supporting him is eating their sprint time. What should the PM do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Tell the new hire he must close the ramp-up gap by the end of next sprint or be moved off the team.
- **B**: Ask the senior engineers to step back from support so the new hire is forced to learn faster on his own initiative.
- **C**: Review the onboarding plan and redistribute mentorship more evenly, adjusting sprint commitments for onboarding cost.
- **D**: Escalate the slower-than-expected ramp-up to the hiring manager so they can decide whether the hire was a mistake.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

A six-week ramp-up is normal for a non-trivial role and the issue is uneven mentor load and unrealistic sprint planning, both of which (C) addresses. (A) sets an arbitrary deadline that is likely to fail and damages psychological safety. (B) abandons the new hire and risks slower ramp-up still. (D) jumps to a hiring decision before the PM has worked the onboarding mechanics that are within their control.
