# Question

On a hybrid programme, an agile software squad consistently hits its sprint goals while the predictive hardware workstream it depends on keeps slipping its milestones, leaving the squad blocked and idle for days at a time. Tension is rising between the two groups, each blaming the other for the programme's poor overall performance. Which THREE actions should the programme manager take to support performance across both?

## Options

- **A**: Establish joint planning and a shared, visible roadmap so both workstreams' timelines, dependencies, and handoffs are aligned and transparent to everyone.
- **B**: Investigate the root causes of the hardware milestone slips with that workstream's lead, rather than assuming the team is simply underperforming.
- **C**: Reframe success around shared programme outcomes and facilitate a session for the two groups to align on common goals instead of trading blame.
- **D**: Reallocate the idle software squad's budget to the hardware workstream to pressure it into delivering its milestones faster.
- **E**: Have the software squad stop raising the dependency delays so the friction between the two groups subsides and they can focus on their own work.

## Answer

A, B, C

## Explanation

Cross-workstream friction in a hybrid programme is usually a coordination and goal-alignment problem: (A) makes dependencies and timelines visible so the squad is not blindsided, (B) diagnoses why the hardware slips instead of scapegoating, and (C) rebuilds a shared definition of success to replace blame. (D) cutting one team's budget as leverage is punitive, harms capacity, and does nothing to fix the dependency. (E) silencing the squad's legitimate dependency signals hides the real problem and removes the information the programme needs to manage it.
