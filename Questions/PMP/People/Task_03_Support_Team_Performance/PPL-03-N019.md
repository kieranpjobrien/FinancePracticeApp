# Question

On a predictive aerospace-software project, one developer delivers his own components early and to a high standard, and his personal metrics are the best on the team. His components are written so that only he can readily integrate or extend them, he routes every related query through himself, and two colleagues have quietly asked to be moved off work that depends on him. He has said he hits his dates precisely because he is not slowed down by others. The integration milestone is two months out. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Reframe his targets so that integratable handoffs and a reviewable, shared codebase are explicit deliverables he is measured on, not just the speed and quality of his own components.
- **B**: Have a direct conversation to understand what is driving the siloing before deciding whether the issue is his choice, the way his work is structured, or pressure he is under.
- **C**: Restructure the work so his components have defined interfaces and a second developer reviews and co-owns the integration-critical parts, building shared capability into the workflow.
- **D**: Keep routing self-contained components to him where his speed pays off, and assign the integration-heavy, shared-ownership work to developers who collaborate readily.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Two months from an integration milestone, the real risk is not his output but a codebase only he can integrate and a single point of failure the team is already routing around. The support question is what most reliably removes that risk. (C) attacks the mechanism: defined interfaces plus a co-owning reviewer make the work integratable and spread the knowledge structurally, regardless of his disposition — it changes the system, not just the person. (A) is tempting and partly right — measuring the right outcomes signals the expectation — but rewriting targets alone, without building the interfaces and second owner into the work, leaves the actual integration dependency in place and relies on him choosing to change. (B) a diagnostic conversation is reasonable, but the cause is already largely visible (he optimises his own metrics by not depending on others) and talking first, with the milestone close, defers the structural fix that is needed either way. (D) plays to his strength but permanently entrenches the silo on exactly the integration-critical work that most needs to be shared, trading a short-term throughput gain for the delivery risk you are trying to remove. The margin is between building integratability and shared ownership into the work itself (C) and re-measuring (A), re-diagnosing (B), or accommodating the silo (D).
