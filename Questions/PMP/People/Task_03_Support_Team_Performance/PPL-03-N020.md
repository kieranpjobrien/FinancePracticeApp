# Question

Midway through a high-pressure agile programme, a normally upbeat team member has become withdrawn, his velocity is erratic, and he has snapped at colleagues twice this week — behaviour entirely out of character. When you asked him quietly after stand-up whether he was alright, he said everything was fine and ended the conversation. You are also aware the whole team has been running hot for several sprints and his snapped-at colleagues are starting to keep their distance. As the project manager, what is the best way to support the situation now?

## Options

- **A**: Respect that he has declined to discuss it, and instead remove pressure where you can — easing his immediate commitments and watching whether the behaviour settles before raising it again.
- **B**: Tell him directly but privately that you have noticed a specific, sustained change, that you are concerned rather than checking up on his output, and that you want to understand what would help.
- **C**: Treat it as a team-level signal: surface the sustained pace and rising friction in the retrospective so the team can address the conditions affecting everyone, this member included.
- **D**: Speak quietly with the colleagues he snapped at to understand what they have observed and contain the friction, before deciding how to approach him.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A sharp, sustained, out-of-character change is a signal you should not let drop, and a deflecting one-line "I'm fine" after stand-up is not a genuine decline — it is a brush-off in a setting that gave him no real opening. (B) gives him that opening: a private conversation that names the specific change, makes clear it is concern not performance scrutiny, and invites him to say what would help — the most direct route to the cause and the support he may need. (A) respects his "I'm fine" too literally; easing his load without understanding why guesses at the problem, can read as being quietly sidelined, and lets a possibly deepening issue run unaddressed. (C) the team pace is a real and worth-addressing factor, but routing an individual's acute, visible distress into a group retrospective neither reaches what is specifically wrong with him nor is a safe place to expose it. (D) talking to the colleagues first gathers second-hand observation and risks the conversation looking like it is about his impact on others rather than about him, when the person himself is the one to ask directly. The margin is between a direct, specific, private approach to the individual (B) and acting on the team signal, on his colleagues, or on his deflection instead of on him (A, C, D).
