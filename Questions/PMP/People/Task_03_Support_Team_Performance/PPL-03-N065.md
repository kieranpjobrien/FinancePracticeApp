# Question

A hybrid programme delivering a regional logistics platform measures each delivery team against on-time task completion. One team consistently hits its completion targets, yet defects traced to that team's work are rising and other teams are spending time fixing them. The team in question believes it is performing well because its agreed metric is green. What is the most appropriate action for the programme manager?

## Options

- **A**: Instruct the team to slow down and prioritise quality over hitting their completion dates.
- **B**: Introduce a defect-rate penalty into the team's targets to discourage the rework they are causing.
- **C**: Reassign the defect fixing back to the originating team so they bear the cost of their own errors.
- **D**: Review the performance measures with the team so they reflect quality and downstream impact, not just on-time completion, and agree what "good performance" should mean.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The team is optimising honestly against a measure that rewards the wrong thing, so the fix is to correct the measurement system collaboratively so it captures quality and downstream effect (D) — this supports genuine performance rather than gaming of a narrow metric. Simply telling them to slow down (A) is a vague directive that leaves the misaligned incentive in place. A defect-rate penalty (B) is punitive and bolted on, and treating performance as something to penalise undermines trust without helping the team understand the goal. Reassigning the fixes (C) addresses cost allocation but not the behaviour, and the team would still chase completion dates at the expense of quality. The skill is recognising that a team rewarded for the wrong outcome needs better-aligned measures, agreed with them, not blame.
