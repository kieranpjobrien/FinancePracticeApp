# Question

A technically strong new member of your agile team keeps pulling backlog items that interest him over the ones the team prioritised for the sprint goal. You have already reminded him twice which items matter most this sprint, and he agrees in the moment — but the next day he drifts back to the work he finds engaging, and the sprint goal is now at risk. What should the project manager do to support his performance?

## Options

- **A**: Explore with him why the prioritised items keep losing out to the others, and work out together what would help him stay anchored to the sprint goal.
- **B**: At the next planning session, have the team make the sprint goal explicit and agree as a group that members pull the highest-priority ready item next, so the working agreement guides everyone including him.
- **C**: Ask the product owner to break the priority items into smaller, more interesting slices so the work he is drawn to and the work the team needs become the same thing.
- **D**: Reframe his role so he is accountable for a priority outcome the team is counting on, giving his pull choices a purpose he is bought into rather than just a ranked list.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A simple context gap would have been closed by the first reminder; it was not, so repeating the message (or restating priority in new forms) is not the lever. The behaviour persists despite his agreement, which means the PM does not yet understand what is actually driving it — pull of interesting work, weak buy-in to the goal, avoidance of harder items, or something else. (A) surfaces that cause with him and co-creates a fix, which is the necessary first step before any specific mechanism. (B) is a sound working-agreement move and may well help, but applied now it is a team-level guess at an individual cause the PM has not diagnosed. (C) and (D) are both plausible interventions — reshaping the work or giving him ownership of an outcome — but each presupposes a particular cause (boredom; lack of ownership) that may be wrong; choosing among them only makes sense after (A) reveals which one fits.
