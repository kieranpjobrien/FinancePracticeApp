# Question

Three weeks before a major release on a hybrid project, you notice two of your core engineers have been logging twelve-hour days and working weekends to hold the date. Quality has held so far, but in stand-up both seem exhausted and short-tempered, and one made an uncharacteristic mistake yesterday. The sponsor is adamant the date cannot move. What should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Reprioritise the remaining scope with the product owner to protect a sustainable pace, descope or defer lower-value items, and make the trade-off and its risks explicit to the sponsor.
- **B**: Thank the two engineers for their commitment and bring in pizza and a bonus to keep morale up through the final push to release.
- **C**: Hold the line on the date and scope but ask the two engineers to take the first week after release off to recover from the crunch.
- **D**: Quietly redistribute some of the two engineers' tasks to other team members so the heavy load is shared without changing the plan with the sponsor.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Unsustainable pace is already producing fatigue and errors, the leading edge of a quality failure; (A) protects the people and the release by adjusting scope and being transparent with the sponsor about the real trade-off. (B) rewards and thereby normalises the overwork without reducing it. (C) defers recovery until after the risk has peaked and does nothing about the error-prone final week. (D) spreading the same excessive load may just exhaust more people, and hiding the strain from the sponsor denies them the information needed to make the scope-versus-date call that is theirs to make.
