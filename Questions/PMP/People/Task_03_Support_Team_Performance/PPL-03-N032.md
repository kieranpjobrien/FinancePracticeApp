# Question

Your scrum team's velocity has fallen for two sprints. The cause is external: a partner team owns an API your stories depend on and delivers it late and incomplete every sprint, forcing your developers into rework. You have already raised it informally with the partner's Scrum Master twice with no change, and your team is demoralised at being judged on outcomes they cannot control. Both teams report into the same delivery manager. What should the Scrum Master do?

## Options

- **A**: Bring the dependency to the delivery manager with the rework data, proposing the two teams' priorities and sprint plans be aligned so the API lands before your dependent stories each sprint.
- **B**: Stop committing dependent stories until the partner's API is delivered, pulling independent work forward so your team's flow and morale are no longer hostage to the partner's lateness.
- **C**: Set up a recurring cross-team planning session with the partner team so the API and your dependent stories are sequenced together every sprint going forward.
- **D**: Have your team build temporary stubs against the expected API contract so they can complete their stories on time, then integrate once the partner delivers.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Direct, peer-level engagement has already been tried twice and failed, and the real lever — the two teams' relative priorities — sits with the shared delivery manager, not with either Scrum Master. (A) escalates correctly: it takes evidence to the one person who can actually align the teams' priorities and makes the external cause visible so the team is not judged unfairly, addressing the systemic constraint. (B) re-sequencing your own backlog to independent work is a sensible interim tactic and protects flow, but it does not fix the partner's chronic lateness and only delays the dependent stories, which must eventually be done. (C) a recurring cross-team planning session is exactly the right durable mechanism — but the partner has already ignored two direct approaches, so without the delivery manager aligning priorities first, a standing meeting has no authority to make them deliver on time. (D) stubbing to the contract keeps your team moving but defers integration risk and depends on a contract the partner has been delivering incompletely, so the stubs may not match what finally arrives. The margin is between escalating to the authority who can realign priorities after peer attempts failed (A) and tactics that buffer your own team or rely on cooperation the partner has not given (B, C, D).
