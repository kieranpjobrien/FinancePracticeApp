# Question

On a pharmaceutical validation project, two specialists who must hand work between them have a strained relationship, and their friction is slowing the validation pipeline and starting to affect a third colleague who sits between their tasks. Both specialists are individually competent and meeting their own deliverables. The project manager has observed the tension but neither specialist has raised it. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Bring both specialists together immediately to resolve their differences in a facilitated joint session.
- **B**: Speak with each specialist separately to understand their perspective on the handover difficulties before deciding how to address the friction.
- **C**: Restructure the workflow so the two specialists no longer hand work directly to each other.
- **D**: Continue monitoring, since both are meeting their individual deliverables and may resolve the tension themselves.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The friction is now affecting team performance and a third person, so it cannot simply be left to resolve itself — but the project manager does not yet understand the cause, so the first step is to gather each perspective privately (B) before choosing an intervention. Forcing an immediate joint session (A) risks escalating an emotionally charged situation without knowing what is driving it and could entrench positions. Restructuring the workflow (C) engineers around the conflict rather than addressing it and may not be feasible given the required handover. Continuing to monitor (D) ignores that the impact has already spread beyond the two individuals; meeting individual deliverables does not mean the team is performing well. Understanding before acting is what allows a proportionate, effective response.
