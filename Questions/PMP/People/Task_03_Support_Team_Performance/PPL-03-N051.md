# Question

You lead a matrixed project. A team member's functional manager rates them a top performer, but on your project the same person repeatedly misses commitments and their work needs heavy rework. When you first raised it, the functional manager dismissed your feedback, saying the person is excellent and the issue must be your project's expectations. The deliverable is now at risk. What should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Go back to the functional manager with specific, dated examples of the missed commitments and the rework, and propose working jointly on a concrete plan to bring the person's performance on your project up.
- **B**: Sit down with the team member directly to clarify your project's expectations, agree what is getting in the way, and set checkpoints, treating it as a performance issue you own day-to-day regardless of the appraisal.
- **C**: Document the pattern thoroughly and escalate to the project sponsor and functional manager's chain so the resourcing and performance issue is resolved at a level that has authority over both of you.
- **D**: Re-plan the at-risk deliverable to reduce reliance on this person — reassigning the riskiest tasks within your team — while you pursue the performance issue in parallel.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

In a matrix the PM lacks formal authority over the appraisal but owns project performance and has already had one feedback exchange rebuffed by assertion, so the next step is to convert the disagreement from opinion to evidence: (A) brings specific, dated facts and proposes a *joint* improvement plan, which both makes the problem undeniable and keeps the functional manager — whose cooperation the PM needs — inside the solution. (B) the direct conversation with the team member is necessary and will likely happen too, but as the immediate next move it risks blindsiding the functional manager you have already clashed with and can be undercut by a manager who has told their report they are excellent; aligning the manager first makes the direct conversation stick. (C) escalating is a legitimate fallback, but going over the functional manager's head after a single dismissed exchange — before presenting hard evidence and attempting a joint plan — burns goodwill and is disproportionate. (D) protecting the deliverable by re-planning is prudent risk response and may run in parallel, but on its own it works around the person without addressing the performance problem or the manager's misread. The margin is between grounding the next conversation in evidence and shared ownership (A) and acting before that — going direct to the report, escalating, or routing around them (B, C, D).
