# Question

On a predictive infrastructure programme, a senior network engineer who is widely respected has missed three consecutive milestone commitments, each time giving a credible technical reason only after the deadline has passed. His tasks sit on the critical path, and other team members have started quietly padding their own estimates so they are not caught the same way. The next milestone is three weeks out. As project manager, what is the best course of action?

## Options

- **A**: Have a direct private conversation with the engineer about the pattern, and agree how he will surface emerging risk before a deadline rather than explaining it afterwards.
- **B**: Move his critical-path tasks to other capable team members for the next milestone so the schedule is protected while you look into the misses.
- **C**: Introduce a standing rule that the whole team reports any at-risk commitment at the daily check-in, so slippage surfaces early and the same expectation visibly applies to everyone.
- **D**: Bring the engineer and his functional manager together now to agree how his commitments will be met, since the line manager controls his priorities and competing demands.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Two things are going wrong: the engineer discloses risk only after the fact, and the team is starting to pad estimates in response — both behaviours the PM owns within the team, and both fixable by changing how risk is raised, not by treating the engineer as a delivery liability. (A) goes to the person and the actual behaviour (late disclosure) and resets it to early flagging, which also removes the example the rest of the team is reacting to. (B) protects the immediate milestone but pulls a respected specialist off the work he is best placed to do without ever addressing why the misses happen, and it signals to the team that missing commitments gets you quietly sidelined — reinforcing the padding. (C) is a defensible transparency mechanism, but imposing a blanket reporting rule on everyone in response to one person's pattern can read as collective punishment and still leaves the individual conversation unhad. (D) involves the functional manager prematurely: there is no evidence yet that competing line priorities are the cause, and going around the engineer before speaking with him directly damages the relationship the PM should manage first. The discriminator is whether the first move addresses the individual's behaviour directly or reaches instead for a structural, schedule, or escalation lever.
