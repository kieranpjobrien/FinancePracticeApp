# Question

A project manager on an aerospace systems project observes that a previously high-performing engineer's output has dropped noticeably over the past month. The project manager wants to support the engineer back to their usual standard. Which actions are appropriate parts of a supportive, well-reasoned response? (Select two.)

## Options

- **A**: Hold a private, non-judgemental conversation to understand what has changed for the engineer.
- **B**: Compare the engineer's output to that of their peers in the conversation to make the gap concrete.
- **C**: Together, identify any obstacles, workload pressures, or resource gaps and agree how to address them.
- **D**: Set a probationary review period to formalise the expectation of improvement.
- **E**: Reassign the engineer's most demanding tasks to a stronger performer until the dip passes.

## Answer

A, C

## Explanation

A sudden drop in a strong performer signals a change in circumstances, so the supportive response is to understand the cause without judgement (A) and then jointly tackle whatever obstacles, workload, or resource issues emerge (C). Comparing the engineer to peers (B) is demotivating and shifts a developmental conversation into a defensive one. Imposing a probationary review (D) escalates to a formal performance process before the cause is even known, which is disproportionate and damaging to trust for a recent, single dip. Reassigning the demanding work (E) removes the very tasks the engineer is capable of and treats a temporary dip as a permanent capability problem, denying them support and growth. Diagnose with the person, then remove what is in their way.
