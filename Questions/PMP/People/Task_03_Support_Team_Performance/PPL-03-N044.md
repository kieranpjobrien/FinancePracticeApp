# Question

A junior analyst solved a thorny data-reconciliation problem that had stalled a deliverable for a week, working through the detail carefully and documenting the fix so others could reuse it. The analyst is reserved and has previously seemed uncomfortable being singled out in front of the group. You want to sustain this kind of contribution from them. What is the most effective action?

## Options

- **A**: Acknowledge the work to them directly and specifically, and with their agreement share the reusable fix with the team as a useful contribution rather than a personal spotlight.
- **B**: Call out the analyst's achievement by name in the next team meeting so the whole team sees strong work being recognised publicly.
- **C**: Keep the recognition private to respect their reserve, telling them one-to-one exactly what they did well and the difference it made.
- **D**: Give the analyst the next comparably hard problem, framing the assignment itself as the recognition and a vote of confidence in their ability.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The aim is recognition that reinforces the behaviour without backfiring on a reserved person, so (A) combines specific personal acknowledgement (which lands for them) with sharing the *work product* to the team — spreading the value and modelling the behaviour while sidestepping the personal spotlight they dislike. (C) is genuinely respectful and specific, but purely private recognition forgoes the team-wide reinforcement and reuse that the documented fix makes possible; (A) achieves both. (B) maximises visibility but ignores a known signal that public singling-out makes this individual uncomfortable, risking that the "reward" discourages them. (D) loading more hard work onto a strong contributor is a common trap that can read as being penalised for competence, and assumes a stretch assignment substitutes for actual acknowledgement. The margin is between recognising the person in the way that works *for them* while still spreading the benefit (A) and the narrower or riskier alternatives (B public-but-uncomfortable, C private-but-contained, D more-load-as-praise).
