# Question

A delivery manager on a telecommunications rollout is supporting four team members, each at a different point in their development on a new network-automation toolset. For each person, choose the leadership style that best supports their performance and growth right now. Each style is used at most once.

## Items

- **1**: A new graduate who is enthusiastic and willing but has never used the toolset and does not yet know what good output looks like.
- **2**: A developer who has learned the basics, is past their initial enthusiasm, and is becoming frustrated and discouraged as the work gets harder.
- **3**: A capable engineer who can do the work well but is hesitant and unsure of their own judgement, occasionally seeking reassurance before committing.
- **4**: A senior specialist who is both highly skilled with the toolset and confident, fully able to work independently.

## Matches

- **A**: Provide clear direction and close instruction, specifying what to do and how, with frequent checking.
- **B**: Coach actively — keep giving direction but add heavy two-way support, encouragement, and explanation of the "why" to rebuild confidence and commitment.
- **C**: Support and facilitate — step back on direction, listen, and help them trust their own decisions.
- **D**: Delegate — hand over responsibility and let them run with it, staying available if needed.

## Answer

1-A, 2-B, 3-C, 4-D

## Explanation

This maps each person to the situational-leadership style matched to their development level, where the right style depends on the blend of competence and commitment, not on seniority. The willing-but-unskilled graduate needs directing (A); without clear instruction their enthusiasm produces unreliable work. The disillusioned learner needs coaching (B) — still direction, but with strong support to push through the dip in confidence that comes as difficulty rises. The skilled-but-hesitant engineer needs supporting (C); they have the ability, so the leader's job is to build their self-trust rather than tell them how. The expert who is competent and confident is best served by delegation (D); directing or coaching them would be intrusive and demotivating. Matching style to readiness is what makes the support effective rather than generic.
