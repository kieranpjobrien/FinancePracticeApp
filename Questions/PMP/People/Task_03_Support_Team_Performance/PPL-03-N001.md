# Question

Three sprints into a mobile banking project, your team's velocity has dropped from 42 to 28 story points while the burndown still shows the release scope being met on schedule. The product owner is happy with the delivered features, but the development manager has seen the velocity chart, is alarmed by the downward trend, and asks you in a corridor conversation what you are going to do about it. What should you do first?

## Options

- **A**: Tell the development manager the release is still tracking to scope and date, and that you will keep watching the velocity figure while letting the team continue.
- **B**: Explore with the team in the retrospective what changed across the three sprints before deciding whether the drop signals a problem at all.
- **C**: Re-estimate the remaining backlog with the team so the forecast reflects the lower velocity, then take that updated forecast back to the development manager.
- **D**: Agree a velocity recovery target with the team in the next planning session so the development manager has a concrete trajectory back toward the original figure.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

A single trend line tells you nothing about cause — the drop could be team members on leave, a hard integration story, a deliberate re-estimation, or a real slowdown — so the first move is to find out what actually changed before any other response is justified. (B) does that in the team's own forum and keeps the door open to every cause. (A) is reassuring and may even be true, but it brushes off a stakeholder's legitimate signal and commits only to passive watching, learning nothing about why velocity moved. (C) jumps to adjusting the forecast on the assumption the lower velocity is the new normal, which pre-judges the cause and could just as easily be hiding a fixable impediment. (D) converts a planning metric into a target the team must hit, which predictably drives point inflation and treats a symptom whose cause is still unknown. The discriminator is acting to understand before acting to reassure, re-forecast, or push.
