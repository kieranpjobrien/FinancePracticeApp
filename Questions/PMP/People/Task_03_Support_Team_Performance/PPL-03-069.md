# Question

Your single agile team has grown from 8 to 17 engineers over six months on a fast-growing logistics platform. Stand-ups are 45 minutes, refinements are chaotic, and the team lead has told you the group "no longer feels like one team." What should the PM do?

## Options

- **A**: Keep the team as one group but introduce sub-chapter sessions for each technical area so that detailed technical discussions can happen without taking over the whole team's calendar.
- **B**: Shorten stand-ups to 15 minutes strictly and restructure refinements into smaller rotating groups so that the existing team structure can continue to function at the new size.
- **C**: Split the team into two smaller teams aligned to distinct product areas, agree interfaces and shared rituals, and coach both new teams through their forming stage deliberately.
- **D**: Leave the structure as it is and trust that the team will naturally evolve its own working practices now that its size has increased, since reorganising may disrupt current delivery momentum.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

(C) is the PMI scaling pattern: above roughly 8-10 people, a single team's coordination cost exceeds its collaboration benefit, and splitting with clear interfaces preserves both flow and team identity. (A) delays the structural decision and piles more meetings onto an already overloaded calendar. (B) shortens the symptoms without addressing the underlying team-size problem that is driving them. (D) ignores a clear signal from the team lead and lets the group continue to function below the level its people are capable of.
