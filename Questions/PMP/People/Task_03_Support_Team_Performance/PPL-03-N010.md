# Question

To support performance on a large predictive construction-software project, your PMO mandates that each team's defect-closure rate be published weekly on a leaderboard. Within a month, closure rates across all the teams look excellent, but the number of defects reopened by QA has quietly tripled. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Take the reopen-rate spike to the PMO, show how ranking on closure rate alone is driving premature closures, and propose pairing closure rate with reopen rate so the measure reflects real quality.
- **B**: Have a direct conversation with your own team leads about the rise in reopened defects and agree they will only close a defect once it genuinely meets the exit criteria.
- **C**: Add a reopen-rate column to your team's own tracking and review it with your leads each week, so your team manages quality properly even while the leaderboard stands.
- **D**: Accept the leaderboard as PMO policy and focus your team on genuinely improving quality so their closure rate holds up legitimately over time.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A single metric tied to a public ranking has produced the same distortion across every team at once — that pattern points to the measurement design, not to any one team's discipline, so the highest-leverage first move is to fix the measure with the body that owns it. (A) does that and proposes a balanced pair of metrics that removes the incentive to close prematurely for all teams. (B) tackles your own leads directly, which is reasonable, but it treats a system-wide incentive problem as a local discipline issue and leaves the flawed leaderboard driving the same behaviour everywhere else (and back on your team the moment pressure rises). (C) improves your own team's visibility but similarly accepts the broken measure and addresses only your slice. (D) goes further still, accepting a metric that is actively degrading quality across the whole programme and asking your team to swim against it. The discriminator is whether you correct the flawed system at its source or settle for managing your own team around it.
