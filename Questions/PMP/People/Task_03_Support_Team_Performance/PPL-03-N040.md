# Question

A team lead reporting to you on a predictive telecoms rollout reviews every minor decision his five engineers make. The engineers now wait for his sign-off before acting, throughput has slowed, and two have privately said they feel untrusted. The lead genuinely believes his close oversight is what is keeping quality high, and points to a recent defect that slipped through when he was on leave as proof. What should the project manager do first?

## Options

- **A**: Coach the lead to identify which decisions are low-risk and reversible and can be delegated, and agree with him a way to safeguard quality on those without his sign-off, so he can see autonomy and quality are not in tension.
- **B**: Agree a decision-rights matrix with the lead and his engineers that defines which decisions need his sign-off and which the engineers own, removing the blanket approval bottleneck.
- **C**: Put lightweight quality controls on the engineers' routine decisions — peer review and a definition-of-done checklist — so the lead can safely release sign-off because quality no longer rests on his personal review.
- **D**: Reframe the lead's role around the higher-value work only he can do — risk, stakeholders, and integration — so he naturally steps back from routine sign-offs as his focus shifts.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The lead's behaviour is driven by a genuine belief, reinforced by the defect during his leave, that his oversight is the only thing protecting quality — so any first step that does not change that belief will be resisted or quietly reversed. (A) targets the belief directly: coaching him to see which decisions are safe to delegate *and* agreeing how quality is protected without him dissolves the false trade-off in his head, which is what actually frees the engineers. (B) a decision-rights matrix is the right artefact and may well result from the coaching, but imposing it while he still believes his review is what guarantees quality treats the structure as the fix and risks his compliance-without-buy-in, sliding back the moment another defect appears. (C) adding peer review and a checklist is a strong supporting move that makes delegation safe, but on its own it does not address his conviction that *his* eyes are required; he may keep reviewing anyway "to be sure". (D) redirecting him to higher-value work is a good developmental frame, yet it sidesteps rather than resolves his quality fear and assumes he will let go of sign-offs once busier, which a worried micromanager often will not. The margin is between changing the lead's underlying belief that oversight equals quality (A) and introducing a structure, controls, or a role reframe that only work once that belief has shifted (B, C, D).
