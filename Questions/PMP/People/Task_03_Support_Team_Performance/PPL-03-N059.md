# Question

A Kanban delivery lead wants to act on three performance concerns the team has raised. For each concern, choose the single metric whose movement would most directly tell the lead whether an intervention is working. Each metric is used at most once; one metric does not match any concern.

## Items

- **1**: The product owner can commit to a release date with confidence, but only if the team can promise that whatever is started will actually finish within a predictable window rather than occasionally dragging on for weeks.
- **2**: Leadership wants assurance the team can sustain its output as two members rotate off and two new members join over the next two months, without the monthly volume of completed work quietly eroding.
- **3**: Developers report they rarely finish a day without being pulled onto something new, and the lead suspects too much is being worked at once rather than carried to completion.

## Matches

- **A**: Cycle time, including its variability (e.g. the 85th-percentile spread, not just the average)
- **B**: Throughput (items completed per period) tracked across the transition months
- **C**: Work-in-progress (WIP) against agreed limits
- **D**: Average story-point estimate per item entering the board

## Answer

1-A, 2-B, 3-C

## Explanation

Each concern is framed by its decision, not by a metric's definition, and one option (D) is a plausible-sounding distractor that fits none. Concern 1 is about predictability of completion once work starts, so the lead needs cycle time and especially its variability (A) — average alone would not reveal the occasional weeks-long drag the PO is worried about. Concern 2 is about whether completion volume holds up through a team change, which is throughput over the transition (B); cycle time could look fine while throughput quietly falls. Concern 3 is about too much started at once, the direct signal of which is WIP against limits (C). Average estimate per item (D) speaks to story sizing, not to any of these three concerns, so it matches none. The skill is choosing the metric tied to the specific decision rather than the one that merely sounds related.
