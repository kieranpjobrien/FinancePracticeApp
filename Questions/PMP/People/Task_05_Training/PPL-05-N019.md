# Question

Your team spans Manila, Berlin and São Paulo and must adopt a new infrastructure-as-code toolchain. Each location has a senior engineer who is already fluent in the toolchain and respected locally. Live cross-region sessions are impractical — hours barely overlap and one site's bandwidth will not hold a long call. The phase that depends on the toolchain starts in four weeks, and every engineer must reach a dependable working standard, not just be introduced to it. Which approach should you take?

## Options

- **A**: Have each site's fluent engineer run hands-on local sessions for their colleagues from a shared lesson plan, and confirm every engineer meets a common practical standard before the phase starts.
- **B**: Have each site's fluent engineer run hands-on local sessions from a shared lesson plan, trusting that putting a local expert in the room will bring colleagues to the needed level.
- **C**: Issue self-paced modules and recorded walk-throughs everyone completes to a deadline, with a question channel for help when people get stuck.
- **D**: Run one live region-spanning session at a compromise time, recorded for those who cannot attend, so everyone receives the same instruction from one source.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The constraints rule out live cross-region teaching, but each site has a credible local expert — so a per-site cascade is the natural fit. What separates the options is whether the approach proves each engineer actually reaches the standard. (A) uses the local experts for hands-on teaching *and* verifies every engineer against one common practical bar, so an uneven cascade is caught before the phase depends on it. (B) is the same delivery without the check, and "a local expert in the room" does not guarantee each colleague becomes competent — gaps surface only once work starts. (C) self-paced material suits the time-zone and bandwidth limits but is passive, wastes the available local experts, and on its own confirms nothing about real proficiency. (D) a single compromise-time call collides with the very time-zone and bandwidth constraints stated and still verifies nothing. The margin is (A) vs (B): both teach hands-on through the local leads, but only (A) confirms the standard is met rather than assuming the cascade worked.
