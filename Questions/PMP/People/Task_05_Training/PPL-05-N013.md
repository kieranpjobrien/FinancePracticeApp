# Question

Three sprints into a new agile product, the team delivers working increments, but stories keep arriving with acceptance criteria too vague to build against, refinement is skipped, and the review runs as a status meeting. All of this is the product owner's accountability, and the team's flow is now visibly suffering. The product owner is a busy businessperson who has not done the role before. As Scrum Master, which action gives the team the most durable improvement?

## Options

- **A**: Establish a team-enforced "definition of ready" so any story without clear acceptance criteria is held out of the sprint until it meets the bar.
- **B**: Have the team's senior developer draft the acceptance criteria each sprint so stories are buildable, leaving the product owner to handle stakeholders.
- **C**: Sit with the product owner on the next backlog and review cycles, working through real stories with them so they learn to specify and order the work the team depends on.
- **D**: Raise with the sponsor that the product owner is not performing the role and ask the business to supply someone with agile product-ownership experience.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The root cause is a fixable capability gap in an accountable role-holder, so the most durable fix builds that capability rather than routing around it. (C) develops the product owner on their own real work, so the specifying and ordering the team depends on improves at source and keeps the accountability where it belongs. (A) is a sound protective practice and worth doing, but on its own it only blocks bad stories — it changes the symptom without making the product owner better at the role, so the gap persists. (B) makes stories buildable this sprint but moves backlog accountability onto a developer, hollowing out the role and entrenching the dependency. (D) discards a fixable gap and a key relationship before any development is attempted, and a replacement may not be available. The margin is (C) vs (A): both relieve the team, but only (C) closes the gap durably; (A) is best used to hold the line *while* (C) takes effect.
