# Question

Across several agile teams in your programme, members keep solving the same problems with a shared technology in isolation, and good techniques are not spreading between teams. The teams are delivering and value their autonomy. As the programme's agile lead, what is the most sustainable, low-overhead way to raise the collective skill level over time?

## Options

- **A**: Invite interested practitioners from each team to a regular, optional community-of-practice session where they share techniques, problems, and learning.
- **B**: Set up a shared knowledge base where teams are asked to document solutions and techniques for others to find and reuse.
- **C**: Rotate engineers between teams periodically so techniques travel with the people who move.
- **D**: Have each team nominate a representative who must attend a programme forum and is responsible for relaying techniques back to their team.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The aim is durable, cross-team learning that respects team autonomy without adding heavy process. (A) is best: a voluntary community of practice is a lightweight, ongoing structure where practitioners learn directly from one another and carry insights back, spreading techniques organically while leaving teams self-governing. (B) a knowledge base is a useful complement, but documentation is passive, often goes stale, and rarely transfers the tacit "how" the way peer exchange does. (C) rotating engineers does move knowledge but is disruptive to delivery and team cohesion — far from low-overhead. (D) a mandated representative forum can work, yet compelled attendance and single-point relaying make it more bureaucratic and brittle than direct practitioner exchange. A practitioner-driven community of practice builds capability across teams most sustainably.
