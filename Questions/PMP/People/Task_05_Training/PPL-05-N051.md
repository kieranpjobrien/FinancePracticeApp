# Question

A SaaS company has just adopted Scrum across several teams. One team has been through the standard two-day Scrum training and can recite the events and roles, yet in practice their retrospectives produce no real change, their backlog refinement is shallow, and they revert to old command-and-control habits under pressure. The training content was correct and complete. As the team's agile lead, what will most improve their actual ways of working?

## Options

- **A**: Work with the team over time as a coach across their real ceremonies and impediments, building the behaviours rather than the knowledge they already hold.
- **B**: Embed an experienced practitioner from a mature team to work within this team for a few sprints and model the behaviours day to day.
- **C**: Facilitate their retrospectives yourself for a while, since fixing the retro is what unlocks a team's ability to improve everything else.
- **D**: Surface the under-pressure reversion to command-and-control with their manager, whose behaviour may be cueing the team to fall back.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The team already knows the framework; what they lack is embedded behaviour, and shifting ingrained ways of working is developed through sustained coaching that works with the team across their real ceremonies and impediments over time (A). (B) embedding an experienced practitioner is a genuinely strong intervention and overlaps with coaching, but modelling alone, without deliberately coaching the team to own the behaviours, risks improvement that leaves when the practitioner does. (C) facilitating retrospectives addresses one ceremony, and while a better retro helps, it is narrower than the behavioural maturity the team needs across refinement and delivery too. (D) the manager's behaviour under pressure may well be a factor worth raising, but it is one possible contributor, not the broad development the team requires to change how they work. Behavioural maturity in agile is coached over time, not fixed by a single lever.
