# Question

On a public-sector benefits-system replacement, four different stakeholder groups each need to be brought up to speed for the change, and the right approach differs for each. Match each group's situation (left) to the most appropriate development approach (right). Each approach is used once.

## Items

- **1**: Eight hundred frontline caseworkers across many offices must reach a consistent baseline ability to process a claim in the new system before launch, and you need to know each has completed it.
- **2**: A small group of departmental managers needs to understand, at a working level, how the change affects their service so they can answer their teams' questions, but they will not operate the system themselves.
- **3**: A senior policy lead is being prepared to take long-term ownership of the system's future direction and needs to grow that strategic understanding gradually over the coming year.
- **4**: A handful of newly hired support analysts must become able to diagnose live faults, drawing on knowledge the current support staff hold mostly from experience.

## Matches

- **A**: A sustained mentoring relationship over time
- **B**: Self-paced e-learning rolled out at scale with completion tracking
- **C**: On-the-job pairing with experienced support staff
- **D**: A focused briefing or workshop tailored to the group's role

## Answer

1-B, 2-D, 3-A, 4-C

## Explanation

Match the approach to the group's scale, depth, and purpose. (1→B) A consistent baseline for hundreds of staff across sites with proof of completion is exactly what scalable, tracked e-learning delivers; nothing else covers that volume uniformly and evidentially. (2→D) Managers who need working awareness for their role, not hands-on operation, are best served by a focused, role-tailored briefing — short and fit to purpose. (3→A) Growing strategic ownership over a year is developmental and relational, so a sustained mentoring relationship fits where a single course would be too shallow. (4→C) Diagnosing live faults using largely tacit, experience-held knowledge transfers fastest by pairing with the current support staff on the real work. The near-misses are deliberate: e-learning could brief managers but is heavier than needed, and a course could not convey the tacit troubleshooting knowledge that pairing transfers.
