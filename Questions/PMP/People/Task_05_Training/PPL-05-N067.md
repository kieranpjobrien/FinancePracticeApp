# Question

On a national broadcast-playout migration you must build capability in four different situations. More than one method could plausibly help in each, but one fits best given the specific constraint described. Match each situation to the most appropriate development method.

## Items

- **1**: A single technician must operate a newly purchased, highly specialised playout-automation appliance for which the vendor is the only credible source of instruction, and a recognised certificate is contractually required.
- **2**: Several engineers each grasp the new workflow in theory after reading the runbook, but none has yet performed a live channel cutover and they need confidence under real conditions before go-live.
- **3**: A new graduate is technically able but unfamiliar with the team's conventions, tools, and unwritten ways of working, and needs steady guidance over her first weeks.
- **4**: Good techniques for the new scheduling tool are emerging unevenly across five regional teams, and the aim is to spread what works so the whole programme levels up.

## Matches

- **A**: Vendor-led formal certification course
- **B**: Supervised hands-on practice / simulated dry-run
- **C**: One-to-one mentoring
- **D**: Community of practice / cross-team knowledge sharing

## Answer

1-A, 2-B, 3-C, 4-D

## Explanation

Each situation has a distinguishing constraint that points to one method. (1) needs a contractually required certificate from the sole credible source, which only the vendor-led certification course (A) provides. (2) is a theory-to-practice gap where the missing element is performing the task under realistic conditions, best closed by supervised hands-on practice or a simulated dry-run (B) rather than more instruction. (3) is an individual needing sustained, relationship-based guidance on conventions and tacit norms — the classic remit of one-to-one mentoring (C). (4) is about diffusing emerging good practice across multiple teams, which a community of practice or cross-team knowledge sharing (D) is designed to do; a course or mentor would not spread peer-developed techniques at scale. The plausible-but-wrong pairings (e.g. a course for the cutover confidence gap, or mentoring to spread techniques across five teams) miss the specific constraint that makes each preferred method fit.
