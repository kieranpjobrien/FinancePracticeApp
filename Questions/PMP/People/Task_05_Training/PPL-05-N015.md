# Question

An external auditor on your pharmaceutical-packaging line project asks for evidence that operators were trained on the validated cleaning procedure before they began production runs. You delivered hands-on training and the operators are demonstrably competent on the line, but you kept no record of who was trained, on which procedure version, or when. The auditor needs to close this point. What should you do?

## Options

- **A**: Arrange for each operator to be formally re-assessed against the current cleaning procedure and record those dated assessments as the evidence of competence.
- **B**: Reconstruct the training history now from what you can verify — trainers, attendees, dates, and procedure version — record it, and put a standing process in place to capture it for all future training.
- **C**: Provide the auditor with the approved training plan and procedure showing the cleaning training was scheduled and resourced for this stage of the project.
- **D**: Have the line supervisor confirm in writing that all current operators were trained on the cleaning procedure before they started production runs.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

In a regulated setting, "adequately trained" includes auditable evidence linking each person to the procedure version and date the training occurred *before* the work — the gap is the missing historical record, so the fix must restore that record and stop it recurring. (B) does exactly that: it reconstructs the contemporaneous history from verifiable sources and institutes capture going forward (corrective and preventive action). (A) re-assessment shows competence *today* but generates no record that operators were trained before the original runs, which is what the auditor asked for. (C) a plan evidences intent and scheduling, not that the training actually happened or who received it. (D) a supervisor's after-the-fact attestation is weak, undated evidence that typically will not satisfy an audit trail. The margin is (B) vs (A): both create dated records, but only (B) addresses the actual finding — the absent record of training already given — rather than substituting a fresh competence check.
