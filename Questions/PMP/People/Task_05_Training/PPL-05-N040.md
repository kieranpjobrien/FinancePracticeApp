# Question

Your project is two weeks from deploying a new patient-scheduling system to clinical staff across several wards. Training has been running, and you want solid assurance that staff are genuinely ready to use the system on day one — that they can do the work and that everyone who needs to has been reached. Which TWO actions best give you that assurance? (Choose two.)

## Options

- **A**: Have staff complete hands-on competency checks on a representative set of their real workflows in a test environment, and record who passes.
- **B**: Reconcile the list of staff who have completed training against the full roster of who must use the system, and chase any gaps before go-live.
- **C**: Give trainees a short knowledge quiz at the end of each session and record the scores as evidence of learning.
- **D**: Run a supervised dry-run on one ward where staff process a day's scheduling in the new system, and review how they cope.
- **E**: Have each ward's super-user confirm that, in their judgement, their colleagues are ready for go-live.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

Real day-one readiness has two parts: staff can actually do the work, and no required user has been missed. (A) and (B) provide exactly those two assurances with objective evidence. (A) competency checks on real workflows demonstrate ability to perform, the strongest evidence an individual is ready, with a record of who has and has not passed. (B) reconciling completion against the full roster guarantees coverage so no critical staff slip through untrained. (C) a knowledge quiz tests recall, not performance, and passing it does not prove someone can operate the system under real conditions. (D) a single-ward dry-run is a valuable rehearsal but samples one location and does not by itself confirm each individual's competence or full coverage. (E) super-user judgement is a useful sense-check but subjective and unverified, weaker than demonstrated competence and a reconciled roster. Demonstrated competence plus confirmed coverage are what assure readiness.
