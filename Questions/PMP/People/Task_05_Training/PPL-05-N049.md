# Question

On an aircraft maintenance (MRO) project, you discover during planning that two of the four licensed engineers assigned to certify a critical avionics modification will have their type-certification authorisations lapse three weeks before the work that requires them. Recertification courses run monthly but fill quickly, and the modification work cannot be signed off by anyone without current authorisation. The schedule currently assumes all four are available throughout. What should you do first?

## Options

- **A**: Confirm places on the next recertification course for the two engineers now and add the courses to the schedule with the right lead time, so the authorisations are current when the work needs them.
- **B**: Re-sequence the avionics work so the certification-dependent tasks fall before the two authorisations lapse, keeping all four engineers available without recertification in this window.
- **C**: Replan the certification work around the two engineers whose authorisations remain current, and adjust the schedule for the reduced sign-off capacity.
- **D**: Log the lapse on the risk register with the recertification lead time as the trigger, and book the course if re-sequencing later proves unworkable.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

A known, dated competency lapse against legally required sign-off is a planned need with a hard deadline and a scarce, monthly course that fills quickly, so the first action is to secure it: confirm the places now and build the recertification into the schedule (A). That guarantees the capability is in place regardless of how the rest of the plan moves. (B) re-sequencing is attractive because it could avoid recertification entirely, but as a first move it gambles on the dependencies permitting the re-order; if they do not, the scarce course places may be gone and the lapse stands. It is worth checking, but after the need is secured, not instead of it. (C) discarding half the certifying capacity over a gap that a booked course closes creates an avoidable bottleneck for the whole project. (D) treating a known, dated requirement as a risk to monitor — with the lead time itself as the trigger — is how such needs get missed, since by the time the trigger fires the course may be full. Lock in the dated, scarce resource first, then optimise around it.
