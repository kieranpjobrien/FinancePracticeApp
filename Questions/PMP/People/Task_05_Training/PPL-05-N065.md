# Question

On a water utility's GIS modernisation, one analyst is the only person who can build and validate the spatial-join models that several upcoming features need. She is increasingly a bottleneck — work queues behind her, and a planned three-week absence is approaching that would stall the modelling entirely. The team raises this in their retrospective. The analyst is willing to help others learn but is already fully committed to her own sprint deliverables, so any time she spends developing colleagues comes out of throughput now in exchange for resilience later. As the project manager, what is the best way to build this capability across the team before her absence?

## Options

- **A**: Have the analyst spend her limited spare effort writing a thorough, worked how-to guide for the spatial-join models, so the knowledge is captured durably and several colleagues can draw on it during and after her absence.
- **B**: Bring in an experienced contract GIS analyst to cover the spatial-join modelling through the absence window, removing the immediate delivery risk without drawing down her capacity.
- **C**: Allocate sprint capacity for two analysts to pair with her on the real spatial-join work over the coming sprints, accepting reduced throughput now so they build the skill by doing it alongside her before she leaves.
- **D**: Send two other analysts on an intensive GIS spatial-modelling course so they return with the modelling skills the team needs and can take on the work.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The discriminator is *durable, distributed competence in the team's own people* versus options that protect short-term delivery but leave the single point of failure intact. The capability is tacit and hands-on, so it transfers fastest by doing — and the project manager's real lever is protecting the capacity for that, since the analyst is otherwise fully loaded. (C) accepts a near-term throughput cost to build genuine skill in two colleagues and break the dependency for good. (A) is a serious contender — documentation is cheap on her time and outlasts her — but a written guide rarely conveys the validation judgement in model-building, so readers who have never built one struggle at the edge cases; it complements pairing rather than replacing it. (B) removes the immediate risk neatly but buys coverage, not capability — when the contractor leaves, the team is exactly as exposed next time. (D) builds generic spatial-modelling knowledge, not the project's specific spatial-join technique and conventions, so the bottleneck persists. Only (C) actually spreads the scarce skill; the others manage around it.
