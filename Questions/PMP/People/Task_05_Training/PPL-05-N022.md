# Question

A cross-functional agile team has decided in its retrospective to spread automated-test scripting beyond the single member who currently does it all, so finished stories stop queuing behind that person each sprint. The team asked you, their coach, to help them make the cross-skilling actually happen. The lone tester is willing to teach but is also the busiest person on the team. What is the most effective way to help the team build this capability?

## Options

- **A**: Facilitate a hands-on workshop where the tester teaches the automation framework to the whole team in a focused session before the next sprint.
- **B**: Suggest the tester record annotated walk-throughs of the scripting workflow so any member can learn from them when they pick up a test-heavy story.
- **C**: Help the team lower the tester's story load next sprint so they have dedicated time to coach colleagues one to one.
- **D**: Help the team agree to pull one or two test-heavy stories per sprint where a learner does the scripting paired with the tester, and make that an explicit, sized item in the sprint plan.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

The team has already committed to cross-skilling, so the coach's job is to help them build the skill in a way that survives delivery pressure. (D) is best: pairing a learner with the tester on real, planned stories transfers a tacit hands-on skill in context and, by sizing it into the sprint, protects the learning against being squeezed out — the most reliable route to durable capability. (A) a single workshop conveys the framework but rarely transfers applied scripting skill without subsequent practice on real work. (B) recorded walk-throughs are a useful aid but passive; alone they seldom build genuine competence and depend on someone choosing to act. (C) freeing the tester's time enables coaching but does not by itself put learners' hands on the work, and unfocused "coaching time" easily evaporates. Deliberate practice on real stories, made visible in the plan, is what embeds the skill.
