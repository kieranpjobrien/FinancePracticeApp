# Question

A building-management-system commissioning project is in execution against a fixed handover date the client has tied to their tenant move-in. A defect review shows a steady stream of mis-configured BACnet controllers; the cause is confirmed — the technicians were signed off on generic controls work but never on this specific controller family, and about a third of the units installed so far are affected. The vendor can run a half-day competency session on the controller family, but only in four days' time. Work on the remaining controllers is continuing now. How should you proceed?

## Options

- **A**: Let configuration continue with an experienced controls engineer reviewing each controller before sign-off, and schedule the vendor competency session for the technicians as soon as it is available.
- **B**: Pause new controller configuration, hold the line until the vendor session in four days has brought the technicians to competence, and use the gap to start reworking the affected units.
- **C**: Keep the technicians configuring controllers and book the vendor session, but treat the existing sign-off as the governing competency record and address the gap as a lessons-learned item at closure.
- **D**: Bring in a contractor already competent on the controller family to take over all remaining configuration, leaving the technicians on other commissioning work until they are trained.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The cause is already confirmed and the defect is recurring, so further unsupervised configuration by technicians who are not competent on this controller family keeps manufacturing rework against a fixed window — the gap must be closed at source, and the four-day wait is short enough to absorb productively. (B) stops the bleed, repurposes the wait to rework known-bad units, and brings the technicians to genuine competence before they resume — adequate training delivered before the work continues. (A) keeps producing defects and leans on an inspection crutch (review-after-build) that catches errors without closing the competency gap, so the engineer becomes a permanent bottleneck and rework still accrues. (D) protects output but treats a closeable, short-duration training gap as a staffing problem, incurring contractor cost and abandoning the chance to make the existing team competent on a controller family they will keep commissioning. (C) is the trap: it papers over an evidenced competency gap by hiding behind a sign-off that demonstrably does not cover this controller family, and defers the real issue to closure while defects mount. The fine margin is between (A) and (B): both keep moving, but only (B) actually stops creating the defect rather than inspecting around it.
