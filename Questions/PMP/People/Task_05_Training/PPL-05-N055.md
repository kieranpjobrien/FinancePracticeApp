# Question

A defence-systems integrator's contract specifies that your software team will receive a named vendor's two-week secure-coding certification course. Since the contract was signed, the project has pivoted to a different secure-development toolchain, and the certified course now covers a technology the team will barely use; their real gap is in the new toolchain, for which no formal certification exists. The customer's contract manager still expects the named course to be delivered as written. What is the best course of action?

## Options

- **A**: Deliver the named certification course as contracted, since the contract is explicit and delivering it keeps you compliant with the customer's expectation.
- **B**: Redirect the budgeted training to the new toolchain to meet the team's real competency need, and inform the customer of the substitution at the next progress review.
- **C**: Raise the mismatch with the customer's contract manager now, evidence the team's actual gap, and seek agreement to redirect the training to the new toolchain, formalising any change.
- **D**: Deliver both the named course and additional toolchain training, absorbing the extra cost and time to satisfy the contract and the real need at once.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

There is a genuine conflict between a contractual training obligation and the team's actual competency need, and the professional path is to surface the mismatch with evidence and seek agreement before acting, formalising the change so the contract and the work stay aligned (C). (A) honours the letter of the contract while knowingly delivering training of little value to the project, spending two weeks on the wrong skill. (B) reaches the right competency outcome and is not done in secret, but it changes a contracted deliverable on your own authority and tells the customer only after the fact; a named certification in a defence contract is a commitment to agree before substituting, not to notify afterwards. (D) satisfies both but consumes extra cost and a fortnight of the team's time on a course they will scarcely use, which is poor stewardship when the contract can simply be amended. Agreeing the change before redirecting resolves the conflict without breaching the contract, wasting effort, or pre-empting the customer's decision.
