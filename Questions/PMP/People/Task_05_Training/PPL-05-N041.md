# Question

You are planning the build phase of a coastal flood-defence project. The design team has just confirmed that the works will use a new proprietary sheet-piling system that none of the current site engineers have installed before, while everything else on the project uses methods the team knows well. The vendor offers a standard induction course, your senior engineer has used a similar system elsewhere, and the schedule has the piling starting in eight weeks. You need to put a training plan together. What should you do first?

## Options

- **A**: Book the vendor's standard induction for every site engineer now, so places are secured before the course fills and no one is left short.
- **B**: Have the senior engineer who has used a similar system draft the training plan, since they best understand what the work will demand.
- **C**: Map the competencies the piling work will require against what each engineer can already do, then build the plan around the gaps that remain.
- **D**: Survey the site engineers on which aspects of the new system they feel least confident about, and shape the plan around their responses.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

A training plan should be built from the gap between what the work demands and what the team can already do, so mapping required competencies against current capability (C) is the step that tells you who needs what before any course is booked. (A) commits budget and the whole team's time to a single course before knowing who actually needs it or whether the induction covers the real gap. (B) draws on relevant experience but a similar system is not the same system, and one person's view is a thin basis for a plan that the comparison in (C) would ground in evidence. (D) gathers self-reported confidence, which is a useful input but can miss gaps people do not know they have and over-weight ones they happen to feel; it informs the plan rather than defining it. Characterising the gap comes before choosing how to close it.
