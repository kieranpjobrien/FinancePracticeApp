# Question

Six weeks ago your telecom field-operations team completed a well-reviewed two-day course on a new fibre-splicing diagnostic tool, and every technician passed the end-of-course assessment. In the field, however, most are still falling back to the old manual method and avoiding the new tool, so the expected fault-resolution improvement has not materialised. The tool itself works and the trainers were rated highly. What is the most effective action?

## Options

- **A**: Talk to the technicians and watch the tool in use on real jobs to find out what is stopping them applying it, then address that cause.
- **B**: Put an experienced super-user alongside the crews in the field for a fortnight to coach them through using the tool on live faults.
- **C**: Build a short refresher focused on the trickier diagnostic steps and run it as hands-on practice rather than another classroom session.
- **D**: Make use of the new tool the expected method and have supervisors check during job sign-off that it was used, raising any avoidance with the technician.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

Passing an assessment but not applying the skill on the job is a training-transfer failure, and the leverage is in diagnosing why transfer is not happening before choosing a fix, so talking to the technicians and observing real jobs (A) finds the actual barrier — workflow fit, missing support, confidence, or something else — and lets you target it. (B) field coaching is a strong intervention, but committing a super-user for a fortnight presumes the barrier is lack of on-the-job support; if the real issue is that the tool does not fit the workflow, coaching will not fix it. (C) a hands-on refresher assumes a skill gap, yet they already passed the assessment, so more practice may address a cause that is not present. (D) mandating use and checking at sign-off may be warranted later, but applied before you understand the avoidance it can entrench resistance and override a legitimate reason the tool is being skipped. Find the transfer barrier first, then act on it.
