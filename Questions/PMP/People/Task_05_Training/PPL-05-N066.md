# Question

You inherit a stalled automotive EV-battery-line commissioning project six weeks before a fixed commissioning milestone. The previous manager has already booked the whole maintenance team onto an expensive week-long vendor course on the new cell-assembly control system, citing "general team upskilling," and the slot is held for next week. Budget is tight. From earlier projects you know some of these technicians have commissioned near-identical control systems on other lines, while others are new to them. The vendor needs your confirmation by Friday to hold the booking. How should you handle the training?

## Options

- **A**: Keep the booking and send the whole team, since the milestone is close, a confirmed slot is in hand, and full coverage is the safest way to be sure no required skill is missing at commissioning.
- **B**: Run a short, targeted competency check this week against the specific skills commissioning will demand, then commit the spend only to the people and gaps it reveals — releasing the booking if the vendor course is not what those gaps need.
- **C**: Send only the technicians new to this class of system and stand the experienced ones down from the course, using their known background to focus the budget where it is most likely needed.
- **D**: Release the vendor booking to protect the budget and have the experienced technicians coach the newer ones on the line as commissioning proceeds.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The discriminator is whether training spend is committed against a *verified* gap or an *assumed* one, under real time and budget pressure that tempts you to skip the verification. (B) does the analysis without derailing the milestone — a short, focused competency check is days, not weeks, and it tells you who needs what and whether the booked course even fits before money is spent. (A) is the path of least resistance the deadline pushes you toward, but it pays the most for the least precision: it funds training experienced technicians may not need and still never confirms the genuine gaps are covered. (C) is the strongest distractor because it *looks* like targeting — but it targets on a proxy (prior exposure you recall) rather than assessed competence on this specific system, and "experienced elsewhere" does not guarantee current capability on this control set. (D) saves cash but also skips the analysis and gambles the fixed milestone on ad-hoc coaching that may not match the real gaps. Verify the gap first, even briefly, then spend to it.
