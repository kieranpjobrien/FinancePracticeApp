# Question

On a payments platform, the team repeatedly cannot demo back-end stories because only one developer understands the fraud-scoring service, and she is frequently on leave or pulled into incidents. The product owner is frustrated that the increment looks thin whenever she is away. Several upcoming epics depend on the same service. As Scrum Master, how should you tackle the underlying problem?

## Options

- **A**: Have the team mob on the next two fraud-scoring stories so the whole team learns the service together, even though it noticeably slows those two stories.
- **B**: Make spreading the fraud-scoring knowledge an explicit team goal: have the developer pair with a different teammate on each upcoming fraud-scoring story, and track coverage on the team's skills matrix until at least three people are competent.
- **C**: Ask the developer to run a hands-on workshop walking the team through the fraud-scoring service end to end, with a recorded session for later reference.
- **D**: Add a "two people per service" working agreement and let pairs self-organise to cover fraud-scoring whenever those stories come up.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

All four are legitimate ways an agile team grows shared skill — the question is which most reliably closes a critical single-point-of-knowledge gap that several epics depend on. (B) is deliberate and measurable: rotating the pairing partner spreads tacit knowledge across multiple people through real delivery, and the skills matrix makes the target (three-deep coverage) explicit and trackable. (A) mob-programming transfers knowledge but front-loads a heavy velocity hit on two stories and gives broad exposure rather than the depth of repeated hands-on work; it is a reasonable tactic, not the durable structural fix. (C) a workshop plus recording builds awareness but, like documentation, rarely produces working competence in a complex service. (D) sets the right intention but leaves coverage to chance with no mechanism to confirm the gap actually closes, so the bottleneck can persist.
