# Question

Your project requires team members to pass a regulator-recognised induction, but the accrediting body has now ruled that only trainers certified at Level 4 can deliver the course. Your two in-house trainers hold Level 3. Induction is needed in six weeks. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Let the Level 3 trainers deliver the course as planned and submit a retrospective exception request to the accrediting body once the induction is complete and recorded.
- **B**: Postpone the induction indefinitely until one of the in-house trainers upgrades their credential through the full Level 4 certification pathway the accrediting body now requires.
- **C**: Ask the team members themselves to self-study the course materials and sign a personal attestation that they have covered the full induction content to the required standard.
- **D**: Engage an accredited Level 4 trainer now while sponsoring the in-house trainers to upgrade, so that the six-week deadline is met and capability is rebuilt internally over time.

## Answer

D

## Explanation

PMI expects compliance with the accreditation while protecting long-term internal capability through parallel action. (D) satisfies the immediate requirement and invests in the in-house trainers. (A) delivers non-compliant training and uses retrospective exceptions as cover, which is an ethics issue. (B) ignores the six-week deadline and halts a project-critical activity. (C) substitutes self-study for an accredited induction, which does not meet the regulator's standard.
