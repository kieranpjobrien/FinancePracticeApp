# Question

Two weeks after every operator on a brewery automation line completed a full-day course on the new SCADA interface, line supervisors report that operators are still calling the integrator for routine tasks the course covered. The vendor's attendance log and end-of-course quiz scores were excellent, and the contract entitles you to a remediation session if objectives are not met. The integrator's on-site cover ends on Friday. A second cohort is due to take the same course next month. What should you do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Book the contractual remediation session for the current operators now, so the gap is being closed while you look into it and you do not lose the vendor's obligation by delaying.
- **B**: Spend time on the line watching operators attempt the tasks they are phoning about, to establish what specifically defeats them before choosing a response.
- **C**: Extend the integrator's on-site cover past Friday first, so operators keep a working fallback and the line is not exposed while you decide what the durable fix is.
- **D**: Gather the supervisors' call logs and the quiz results to pinpoint which tasks and which operators are generating the calls, then target action at that pattern.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

All four are reasonable next moves by a PM who takes the report seriously, so this is not "spot the diagnose option" — each rival rests on a fair reading of the situation. The deciding point is that excellent attendance and quiz scores rule out *ignorance of the material* as the explanation, which means the cause lies in how the course transfers to the live line — confidence, an interface difference from the training environment, or a plant-floor blocker — and only direct observation reveals which. (B) gets that first-hand evidence. (A) commits the contractual remedy to repeating the same intervention whose on-the-job failure is not yet understood; if the block is environmental, a re-run teaches the same thing again. (C) is a sound protective step and may well be needed, but securing a fallback is not the same as learning why competence is not transferring, and on its own it just funds the dependency. (D) is the closest rival and genuinely useful — call logs and scores narrow *where* to look — but they are second-hand proxies that show the symptom's distribution, not what physically stops an operator at the panel; they sharpen the observation rather than replace it. The finest margin is B over D: both seek the cause, but D infers it from records while B sees it happen.
