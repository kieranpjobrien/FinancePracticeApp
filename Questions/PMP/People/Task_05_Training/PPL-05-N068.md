# Question

A capable developer joins your hybrid e-commerce replatforming project in its third week. He is strong in the languages in use but new to the team's deployment pipeline, branching conventions, and the codebase's structure. His first assigned stories touch all three, and the team is delivering on a tight cadence with little slack. You want him contributing to real delivery quickly while learning the team-specific context, and without pulling a senior off the critical path for longer than necessary. How should you bring him up to speed?

## Options

- **A**: Have him work his assigned stories independently using the wiki and code as reference, with a senior reviewing each pull request closely and giving feedback through review comments.
- **B**: Pair him directly with an experienced team member on his first few stories, working through the pipeline, conventions, and codebase together on real tasks before he takes stories solo.
- **C**: Give him a short, isolated spike off the critical path — a small self-contained change exercising the pipeline and conventions end to end — before assigning him any delivery stories.
- **D**: Have him shadow the team's ceremonies and walk the documented architecture and runbooks for his first few days, then assign his stories once he has the overall picture.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The discriminator is the *fastest reliable* path to team-specific, tacit competence while still feeding delivery — and the genuine contest is between guided pairing and review-based independence, both real practices. (B) wins for this profile: pipeline, branching conventions, and codebase structure are unwritten know-how learned quickest by doing them beside someone who holds them, and pairing on actual stories produces shippable work *and* learning at once. (A) is the strongest alternative — PR review does transfer convention and keeps the senior off the critical path more of the time — but for a brand-new joiner whose stories touch all three unfamiliar areas, review catches mistakes only after they are made, costing rework cycles the tight cadence cannot absorb. (C) is safe but buys learning at the price of throughput: a senior must still scope and review the spike, and the output is throwaway rather than delivery. (D) front-loads passive orientation that conveys the big picture but not the hands-on judgement, delaying real contribution. Guided pairing converts his evident ability into both competence and delivered work soonest.
