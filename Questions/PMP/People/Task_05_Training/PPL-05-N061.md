# Question

An agile delivery team is releasing a new self-service expenses app to roughly 1,400 staff across eleven branch offices over the next two months. The product is stable and the help content is written. The three-person delivery team has the bandwidth to prepare materials and coach a handful of people thoroughly, but not to personally instruct 1,400. Each branch is led by an operations supervisor. You need branch staff competent on the app by the end of the rollout and able to get help once the delivery team disbands. How should you structure the training?

## Options

- **A**: Have the three-person team run two intensive central workshops covering every branch's staff in cohorts, so the people who built the app deliver the training directly and answer questions authoritatively on the spot.
- **B**: Equip and coach each branch's operations supervisor to run a short hands-on session for their own staff and to remain the local point of contact for questions afterwards.
- **C**: Have the team build a structured self-paced module with built-in exercises and a competency check each user must pass, plus a monitored channel for questions during the rollout.
- **D**: Schedule the three team members to visit each of the eleven branches in turn over the two months, delivering a hands-on session in person at each location.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

The discriminator is durable local capability after a tiny team disbands across a large, dispersed audience — train-the-trainer is what scales delivery *and* leaves competent support embedded in each branch. (B) reaches everyone through hands-on sessions and leaves a trusted local contact who persists after the delivery team is gone. (A) is genuinely tempting — direct delivery by the builders is high-fidelity — but central cohorts pull 1,400 people away from eleven sites, give no local follow-up, and concentrate all expertise in three people who then vanish. (C) scales the content and even verifies competence, but a self-paced module strips out the hands-on coaching branch staff need and still leaves no human support locally once the channel closes. (D) does provide hands-on practice, but eleven sequential visits consume the small team for the full window and still leave each branch with no one to ask afterwards. Only (B) solves both the scaling constraint and the lasting-support constraint together.
