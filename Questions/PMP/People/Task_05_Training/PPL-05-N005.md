# Question

A regional logistics firm is six weeks from cutting over to a new transport-management system. The project team is well prepared, but the 200 depot staff across several sites who will use the system daily have had no exposure to it, and their roles and shift patterns differ. The depot operations manager, who owns those staff, assumes "the project team will just train them at go-live." How should you respond?

## Options

- **A**: Reinforce the go-live floor-support plan and add a short pre-cutover hands-on session per site, on the basis that operational staff learn the system best by using it close to the moment they need it.
- **B**: Work with the operations manager to map which depot roles need which competencies by cutover, then design the format, sequence and timing around that and the shift patterns before committing to any single delivery method.
- **C**: Commission the vendor to run a structured webinar series for all 200 staff in the fortnight before cutover, so everyone across the sites receives the same instruction within the time left.
- **D**: Have super-users at each depot trained first, then cascade to their colleagues on site, so the knowledge is delivered locally and the depots own their own readiness.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Each option is a credible way to attack a real six-week readiness gap, so the call is not "pick the do-a-plan option" — it is which move a competent PM makes before the others can be sound. (A), (C) and (D) each pre-select a *delivery model* — late hands-on practice, a uniform webinar, or a super-user cascade — any of which could turn out right, but all assume an answer to questions not yet asked: which roles need what, how the differing shift patterns and sites constrain timing, and who must be competent by cutover versus supported through it. (B) settles those first, with the manager who owns the staff, and a plan built on that can legitimately *land on* floor support, a webinar, or a cascade — but as a chosen fit, not a guess. (A) is the closest rival because moment-of-need practice is genuinely effective, yet betting solely on a late session for 200 first-time users across several sites risks a thin, rushed cutover if the needs turn out to vary by role. The point of the task is ensuring stakeholders are *adequately* trained, and adequacy is defined against role-specific competencies, which (B) establishes and the others presume. The finest margin is B over A.
