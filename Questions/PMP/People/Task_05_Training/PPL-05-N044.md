# Question

A new developer joins your eight-person agile team mid-iteration on a fintech lending product. She is technically strong but unfamiliar with the team's regulated deployment pipeline, its testing conventions, and the lending domain. The Product Owner is keen for her to start taking story points to hit the release. You want her contributing as soon as is sensible without putting quality on the regulated code at risk. What is the best approach?

## Options

- **A**: Pair her with an experienced team member on real stories from the regulated codebase, so she picks up the pipeline, conventions, and domain in context while a competent pair stays on the code.
- **B**: Give her a small, self-contained story off the critical path to complete herself, with a teammate available for questions, so she learns the pipeline by working through it.
- **C**: Have her start on the team's lower-risk, non-regulated stories first to build velocity, and bring her onto the regulated code once she has the conventions down.
- **D**: Set her up with a focused walkthrough of the pipeline and testing conventions from a senior developer, then have her take her own stories from the next day.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

The knowledge she lacks — a regulated pipeline, the team's conventions, the domain — is largely tacit and transfers fastest by doing the real work beside someone who holds it, so pairing on actual regulated stories (A) ramps her up quickly while keeping a competent pair on code where mistakes are costly. (B) a small story with help on call is reasonable, but learning undocumented conventions largely alone is slower and still puts her solo on regulated work before she knows it. (C) starting on non-regulated stories protects the regulated code but parks a strong developer on lower-value work and defers the very capability the release needs; it trades the wrong thing for safety. (D) a walkthrough conveys structure but not working fluency, so sending her solo onto her own stories the next day on regulated code overestimates how much a briefing transfers. Pairing best balances rapid ramp-up against the quality the domain demands.
