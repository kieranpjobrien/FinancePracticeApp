# Question

While planning a predictive project, you determine that several team members will need formal training on two new technologies, each event tied to a point in the work, carrying a cost and a few days away from delivery. On past projects training like this was agreed in principle but then quietly dropped when delivery pressure rose, because nothing held it in place. You have already documented the needs in the resource management plan. What should you do next so the training is most likely to actually happen as planned?

## Options

- **A**: Log each training need in the risk register as a threat to capability, so it is reviewed and chased at every risk meeting until delivered.
- **B**: Capture each event as a milestone with an owner in the project, and report its status to the sponsor alongside other key milestones.
- **C**: Add the training events as planned activities in the schedule with durations and predecessors, and their costs to the cost baseline, so they are tracked and funded like any other work.
- **D**: Build the events into the resource management plan's calendar and confirm at each phase gate that each one still applies before it is booked.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The failure mode is training being dropped under delivery pressure because it sat outside the mechanisms that actually govern the work, so the fix is to place it inside them. (C) is best: as scheduled activities with dependencies and a line in the cost baseline, the training is monitored, resourced, and funded exactly like delivery work, so dropping it would show up as a schedule and budget variance rather than passing unnoticed. (A) the risk register would keep it visible and chased, but a known, planned need is something to schedule and fund, not an uncertainty to monitor; visibility there still does not reserve the time or the money. (B) milestones with an owner and sponsor reporting create accountability and are a reasonable complement, yet a milestone marks completion without committing the duration, the capacity, or the cost the event consumes. (D) a calendar plus phase-gate confirmation keeps the events under periodic review but, absent from the schedule and budget, they remain outside the day-to-day control that resists the very pressure that sank them before. Embedding training in the schedule and cost baseline is what holds it in place.
