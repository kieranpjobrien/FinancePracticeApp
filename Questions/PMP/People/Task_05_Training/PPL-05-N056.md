# Question

You are running the rollout of new infection-control procedures across a hospital trust. Clinical staff are being trained ward by ward as the procedures take effect, and the procedures are revised periodically as guidance changes. The trust is subject to unannounced inspection, and the governance team has asked you to make sure the training side of the rollout will stand up to scrutiny. Training is being delivered well. What must you ensure is in place alongside the delivery?

## Options

- **A**: A maintained record linking each named individual to the specific procedure version and date they were assessed as competent on it.
- **B**: A trust-wide compliance report showing the percentage of clinical staff trained, broken down by ward and updated weekly.
- **C**: Signed attendance registers for every training session, filed and retrievable, confirming who attended and when.
- **D**: Completed competency-assessment results for the cohort showing the proportion who passed on the current procedures.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

What an inspector tests is whether a particular clinician working under the procedure on a given day had been assessed as competent on the version in force at that time, so the evidence that holds up is a record linking each named individual to the specific procedure version and the date they were signed off (A). (B) a ward-level percentage, however current, shows coverage in aggregate but cannot prove that the specific person an inspector points to was trained on the current version. (C) attendance registers prove presence, not competence, and a register of a session does not by itself establish which version it covered or that the individual can apply it. (D) pass rates for the cohort demonstrate that assessment happened and the materials were current, but a proportion is still aggregate and does not tie a named individual to their qualification on a dated version. In a regulated clinical setting, per-person, version-specific competency records are part of being adequately trained, not an afterthought.
