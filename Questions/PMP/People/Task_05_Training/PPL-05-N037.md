# Question

You have arranged required training on a new collaboration platform that replaces the tools your team uses today and is mandatory for the next phase. Two experienced, high-performing members tell you bluntly they do not need it and would rather keep working. What is the best first response?

## Options

- **A**: Offer them a condensed, advanced session pitched at experienced users so the training respects their existing skill and does not waste their time.
- **B**: Ask them to attempt a representative task on the new platform so they discover for themselves which parts they cannot yet do.
- **C**: Sit down with them to understand why they feel the training is unnecessary, and talk through how the next phase will change their specific workflows where their current tools no longer apply.
- **D**: Have a respected peer who has adopted the platform show them how it improved their work, letting credibility rather than instruction win them over.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Resistance from capable people usually comes from not seeing why the change applies to them, so the best first move surfaces and addresses that specific objection. (C) is best: understanding their reasoning and connecting the training to the concrete ways their own work changes next phase tackles the real cause and is more durable than any workaround. (A) a condensed advanced session is a sensible accommodation, but it presumes the objection is about level when it may be about relevance, and it offers a fix before the cause is understood. (B) a hands-on reality check can be persuasive, yet springing it on experienced people before any conversation can feel like a trap and harden resistance. (D) peer influence is powerful and may follow, but leading with it outsources a conversation you should first have directly with them. Understanding and addressing the relevance objection is what converts resistance into genuine engagement.
