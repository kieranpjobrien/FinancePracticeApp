# Question

Your project deploys a new claims-processing system to a 90-person insurance operations department in three weeks. The planned end-user training is scheduled and resourced. At a readiness review the operations supervisor says a group of long-serving staff are anxious and "still have no idea how the new screens work," even though they attended the awareness briefings. The training itself has not yet started. What should you do first?

## Options

- **A**: Add an extended hypercare period after go-live with floorwalkers so anxious staff have support as they learn the system in live use.
- **B**: Confirm the scheduled training is designed around the operations roles' actual day-one tasks and includes hands-on practice, adjusting it if it is mainly demonstration.
- **C**: Arrange targeted one-to-one sessions for the specific staff the supervisor flagged so their anxiety is addressed before launch.
- **D**: Propose a short go-live delay to the sponsor so the most anxious staff have more time to build confidence with the new screens.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

Training is already planned, so the priority is making sure that training will actually produce competence before staff must use the system live. (B) is best: the readiness signal suggests the sessions may not yet equip staff for their real tasks, and verifying the training targets day-one workflows with practice — strengthening it if not — closes the gap at its root for all 90 staff, not just those who spoke up. (A) hypercare is a valuable safety net but supports a trained user base; relying on it to do the teaching risks an overwhelmed floor on day one. (C) individual sessions help the flagged few but may treat anxiety while leaving a wider design gap, and do not scale to the department. (D) slipping go-live is a heavier step that should follow only if effective training genuinely cannot be delivered in the window. Ensuring the training will build real capability comes first.
