# Question

Four weeks ago your cross-functional team completed a course on a new container-orchestration platform that the next phase depends on. The feedback forms were positive and everyone passed the end-of-course quiz. You now want genuine evidence that the training is translating into capable, independent work — not just that it was delivered and enjoyed. Which TWO observations would give you the strongest such evidence? (Choose two.)

## Options

- **A**: Team members are now configuring and troubleshooting deployments on the platform themselves, with the volume of help-desk tickets and expert escalations on it steadily falling.
- **B**: In the last two sprints the team has delivered platform-dependent stories at its normal pace, with defect and rework rates on that work in line with their other output.
- **C**: Average scores on the end-of-course quiz were high and every participant met the pass mark.
- **D**: Attendance was full and the post-course satisfaction rating averaged 4.6 out of 5.
- **E**: Every team member has a printed certificate of completion from the platform vendor on file.

## Answer

A, B

## Explanation

Effective training is proven by changed behaviour and outcomes on real work, not by reactions or recall measured at the point of delivery. (A) shows transfer to the job — people doing the work unaided with falling reliance on experts — which is the clearest sign competence is real. (B) shows the capability holding up under delivery conditions: normal throughput and quality on the very work the training was meant to enable. (C) measures recall in the classroom, which routinely overstates on-the-job ability and says nothing about application weeks later. (D) measures satisfaction — whether people liked the course — which is unrelated to whether they can now do the work. (E) records attendance and completion, an administrative fact, not evidence of competence. The two behavioural, outcome-based signals are the ones that tell you the training actually worked.
