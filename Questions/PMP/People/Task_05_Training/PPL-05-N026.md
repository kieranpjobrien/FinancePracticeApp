# Question

A technically strong analyst joins your six-month project in week three. She is new to the project's domain, its toolset, and its processes, and the team is under delivery pressure. You want her contributing reliably as soon as possible without her early work creating rework for others. Which approach best achieves that?

## Options

- **A**: Give her a structured first-week plan of self-study through the project documentation and recorded sessions, then move her onto tasks once she has covered the material.
- **B**: Pair her with an experienced team member on real upcoming tasks, with the peer walking her through the domain, tools, and process as they apply to that work.
- **C**: Assign her well-scoped, low-risk tasks immediately and have a peer review her output closely, coaching on whatever gaps the reviews reveal.
- **D**: Book her onto the next scheduled vendor course for the project's primary tool and bridge the gap with documentation until it runs.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

All four build capability; the question is which gets reliable contribution fastest while protecting others from rework, given she is strong technically but lacks project context. (B) is best: pairing on real work transfers tacit domain, tool, and process knowledge in the moment it is needed and catches mistakes before they become rework — the quickest route to dependable output for a capable newcomer. (A) front-loaded self-study delays contribution and leaves exactly the contextual gaps that documents convey poorly. (C) putting her on tasks immediately with heavy review can work, but learning by being corrected after the fact is slower and more error-prone than guided work, especially across an unfamiliar domain. (D) a generic course is point-in-time, may not run soon, and addresses only the tool, not the domain and process. Guided pairing on actual work is the fastest reliable ramp-up.
