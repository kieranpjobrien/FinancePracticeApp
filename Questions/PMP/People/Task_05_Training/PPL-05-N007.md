# Question

A business analyst experienced in claims but new to your organisation joins your insurance-claims modernisation project in week 10 of 30. Her upcoming tasks depend heavily on the bespoke rules engine, which is sparsely documented and understood mainly by two senior analysts who are already fully loaded delivering this quarter's high-priority rule changes. She needs to be writing rules-engine work independently within about three weeks. How should you bring her up to speed?

## Options

- **A**: Schedule her to pair with one of the two senior analysts on the live rule-change work for the next three weeks so she learns the engine in the context of real, current tasks.
- **B**: Ask the senior analysts to spend two days producing a structured walkthrough of the rules engine for her, then have her work through a representative back-catalogue of past rule changes, raising questions to them as they arise.
- **C**: Give her the lower-risk, well-bounded rules-engine tasks from the upcoming backlog first, with one senior analyst reviewing her output, escalating to deeper involvement only where she struggles.
- **D**: Have her shadow both senior analysts in their meetings and reviews for the three weeks so she absorbs how the engine is used before being assigned her own rules-engine work.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

The gap is understood and the constraint is that the only two experts are fully committed to priority delivery, so the best approach builds her real competence while drawing on their scarce time most efficiently. (C) does this: she does genuine rules-engine work from the start on bounded, lower-risk tasks, a senior analyst reviews rather than co-works (a far smaller time demand), and deeper help is pulled only where needed — competence grows through doing, with expert load kept proportionate. (A) is strong skill-building but commits a fully loaded senior analyst to sustained pairing for three weeks, directly competing with the quarter's priority rule changes. (B) front-loads a structured walkthrough but back-catalogue study on a sparsely documented engine without doing current work is slower to produce independent competence and still interrupts the experts ad hoc. (D) is the weakest active option: shadowing meetings is passive, parks her real contribution for three weeks, and rarely produces the hands-on configuration skill her tasks require. The fine margin is between (A) and (C): both develop her on real work, but (C) reaches independent competence within the deadline while protecting the experts' priority commitments.
