# Question

Your training budget for a hospital electronic-records rollout has been cut by 60% after a wider organisational squeeze. The original plan funded three things: vendor-led certification for clinicians, super-user workshops, and a floor-walking support team during the go-live fortnight. A safety-risk assessment of the rollout already exists, the certification is named in the vendor contract, and the clinical directors have told you they consider the workshops the part that will win round the sceptical consultants. You can fund roughly one and a half of the three at the reduced level. How should you decide what to retain?

## Options

- **A**: Retain the floor-walking support and a reduced certification, because hands-on help at the moment clinicians first use the live system is where errors that reach patients are caught.
- **B**: Retain the items the clinical directors back, because in a hospital adoption depends on clinical leaders carrying their peers, and training no one will use protects nothing.
- **C**: Use the existing safety-risk assessment to rank the rollout's competency gaps by potential for patient harm, then fund downward from the highest-risk gap until the budget is exhausted.
- **D**: Retain the contractually named certification in full and stage the workshops and floor-walking into a later, separately funded phase, keeping the rollout on contract and deferring rather than cutting the rest.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

Every option here is a position a capable PM could argue, which is what makes the call fine-margined rather than a matter of spotting the "risk-based" choice. (A), (B) and (D) each fix on one *answer* — a particular intervention to save — before establishing what the safest allocation actually is. (A) backs a defensible candidate (go-live support often is where harm is prevented) but pre-judges that floor-walking outranks certification and workshops without testing it against the others. (B) is right that clinical-leader buy-in matters, yet lets the directors' preference, not patient-safety exposure, set the priority. (D) keeps the project on contract and frames deferral as softer than cutting, but "later, separately funded" is not assured under an organisational squeeze, so it may quietly drop the items that close the highest-risk gaps. (C) is strongest because it decides on the basis that governs a constrained *safety-critical* training spend — which competency gaps carry the greatest potential for patient harm — and a risk-ranked allocation can still land on go-live support or certification, but only after weighing them against each other rather than assuming the winner. The finest margin is C over A: both may end up funding floor-walking, but A asserts it as the priority while C derives the priority from the harm exposure.
