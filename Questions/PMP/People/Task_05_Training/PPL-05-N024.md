# Question

Two weeks ago your team completed a vendor-delivered course on a new data-pipeline tool and rated it highly on the feedback forms. This week you observe that pipeline configuration defects have not decreased and team members are still phoning the vendor's support line with basic questions. The same course is offered again next month, and the vendor also sells hands-on mentoring and an advanced module. What should you do next?

## Options

- **A**: Book the team onto the vendor's advanced module, since the introductory course evidently was not deep enough to prevent the defects.
- **B**: Engage the vendor's mentoring service to sit with the team on live configurations until the defect rate comes down.
- **C**: Observe the team applying the tool to pinpoint exactly which tasks they still cannot perform, then arrange targeted reinforcement aimed at those specific gaps.
- **D**: Have the team member who scored strongest in the course coach the others on the areas where defects are occurring.

## Answer

C

## Explanation

High reaction scores measure satisfaction, not capability, and the defects show a behaviour-level gap whose precise cause is not yet known. (C) is best because you must first identify which specific tasks are failing and why before committing to any remedy; the right reinforcement cannot be chosen until the gap is characterised. (A) an advanced module assumes the problem is depth of content, but the issue may be application of the basics, so this could spend budget on the wrong fix. (B) vendor mentoring is a credible remedy and may follow, but engaging it before diagnosing the gap risks paying to coach tasks the team can already do while missing the real failures. (D) peer coaching is sensible only once you know which tasks and people need it; launching it blind may spread an incomplete understanding. Diagnosing the behaviour-level gap first is what makes any subsequent reinforcement land.
