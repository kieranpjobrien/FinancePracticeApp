# Question

During a move to a new project-controls platform, three of your most experienced planners decline the scheduled training, saying they have used scheduling tools for 20 years and will pick the new one up as they go. Their schedules feed the programme's critical-path reporting. What should you do FIRST?

## Options

- **A**: Make the training mandatory for all planners and inform the three that attendance is a condition of remaining on the controls function.
- **B**: Talk with the three planners to understand their objection and confirm whether their "pick it up" approach will actually meet the platform-specific competencies the reporting depends on.
- **C**: Accept their decision but increase quality checks on their schedules during the first two reporting cycles to catch errors.
- **D**: Arrange a tailored advanced session that skips the basics and focuses only on what differs from tools they already know.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

(B) opens with understanding the resistance and testing the real risk — experienced planners may genuinely self-learn, or may miss platform-specific behaviours that corrupt critical-path reporting — before choosing a response. (A) escalates to a mandate and ultimatum before the conversation, likely hardening resistance from senior staff. (C) accepts the gap and adds downstream inspection rather than addressing capability. (D) is a sensible *option* but pre-selects a solution before confirming the objection and the actual gap; it may be the right follow-up once (B) reveals what they truly need.
