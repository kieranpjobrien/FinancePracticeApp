# Question

Three months after delivering training on a hybrid programme, your sponsor questions whether it was worth the spend. You decide to assemble evidence at several levels. Match each thing you want to find out (left) to the method that most directly answers it (right). Each method is used once.

## Items

- **1**: Whether to run the same course for the next cohort, or rework it because participants found it a poor use of their time.
- **2**: Whether the people who attended can actually carry out the specific procedures the course set out to teach.
- **3**: Whether, back at their desks, people are doing the work the new way rather than reverting to old habits.
- **4**: Whether the capability the training built has shown up in the delivery result it was commissioned to improve.

## Matches

- **A**: A post-training assessment or test of the specific knowledge and skills taught
- **B**: Tracking the relevant performance or delivery metric the training was intended to improve
- **C**: An end-of-session reaction survey capturing participants' satisfaction and perceived relevance
- **D**: On-the-job observation of whether the new behaviours are being used in practice

## Answer

1-C, 2-A, 3-D, 4-B

## Explanation

Each question sits at a different level and is answered by a different kind of evidence. (1→C) Deciding whether to re-run or rework the course for the next cohort turns on how the participants experienced it, which a reaction survey on satisfaction and perceived relevance captures directly. (2→A) Whether attendees can actually perform the procedures is measured by assessing the specific knowledge and skills against what was taught, not by how they felt about it. (3→D) Whether they have changed how they work is a behaviour question, answerable only by observing the real job, since a good test score does not guarantee on-the-job use. (4→B) Whether the capability produced the intended result is shown by the performance or delivery metric the training targeted, the ultimate point of the investment. The traps are the adjacent levels: a reaction survey (C) cannot prove learning, and a test (A) cannot prove on-the-job application — which is why each level needs its own evidence.
