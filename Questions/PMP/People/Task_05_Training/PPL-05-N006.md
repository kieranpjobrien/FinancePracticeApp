# Question

A regulated medical-device firm is moving its embedded team to a new safety-critical RTOS. Compliance requires every developer to hold the vendor's certification before committing production code. The certification course runs only once a month and takes a week. The team is mid-flight on a fixed-date submission and the product owner wants to keep velocity up by having uncertified developers "start now and certify later." What is the best course of action?

## Options

- **A**: Allow uncertified developers to work on non-production spikes and test scaffolding now, while sequencing certification so each is certified before touching production code.
- **B**: Hold all RTOS production work until the whole team is certified, protecting compliance even though it stalls the current sprint.
- **C**: Agree with the product owner that developers begin production work immediately and complete certification before the submission date.
- **D**: Request a compliance waiver allowing provisional commits by uncertified developers under peer review until certification is scheduled.

## Answer

A

## Explanation

(A) preserves the hard compliance gate — no uncertified developer touches production code — while keeping the team productively engaged on legitimately permitted work, then phases certification to unblock production just in time. (C) breaches the requirement on the optimistic assumption that "later" arrives before any production commit, which the agile pull of an active sprint makes unlikely. (D) tries to dilute a safety-critical regulatory control through a waiver, which is rarely available and undermines the purpose of the gate. (B) is compliant but needlessly idles the team when valuable non-production work exists; it sacrifices flow without a compliance benefit over (A).
