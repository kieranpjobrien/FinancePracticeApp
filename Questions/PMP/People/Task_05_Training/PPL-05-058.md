# Question

Your organisation has rolled out a paid AI coding assistant to all developers with a two-hour introductory course. Three months in, half your team rarely uses it, cites distrust of its output, and continues to code the way they did before. What should the project manager do?

## Options

- **A**: Mandate that all team members use the AI assistant for a minimum percentage of pull requests so the investment begins to show measurable usage and value.
- **B**: Run applied coaching sessions on the team's actual codebase that show effective prompting, review practices, and failure modes, and measure meaningful usage not just seat activation.
- **C**: Allow the team to continue as they prefer since AI tooling is optional and individual productivity preferences should not be overridden by management directives.
- **D**: Request that the organisation claw back the AI licences from your team and redirect the funds towards traditional developer tooling the team already trusts.

## Answer

B

## Explanation

PMI reads low adoption after introductory training as a classroom-to-practice gap that is closed by contextual coaching and honest measurement, not by mandates or by withdrawal. (B) grounds learning in the team's real work and uses a meaningful metric. (A) forces usage without building capability or trust and distorts behaviour. (C) leaves a known capability gap unaddressed when a tool is already paid for. (D) gives up on a capability rather than developing it, which is rarely the servant-leadership response.
