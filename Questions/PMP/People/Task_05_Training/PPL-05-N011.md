# Question

On a multi-disciplinary smart-grid programme you must close four competency gaps. For each gap, the constraints differ — how many people, how fast, whether the result must be provable, and whether the knowledge is written down or held in experts' heads. Match each gap (left) to the development approach that best fits its constraints (right). Each approach is used once.

## Items

- **1**: A graduate engineer is expected to grow into the programme's go-to person for grid-protection design over the next two years. The know-how is held by a handful of senior engineers and is more about reading situations than following a procedure.
- **2**: Before go-live, sixty field technicians must each perform a switchgear isolation procedure, and the regulator may later ask you to show, per person, that they were assessed against the current procedure.
- **3**: One DevOps engineer needs a specific cloud-deployment competency within a fortnight; an experienced colleague is already doing this work daily and has spare capacity to take the engineer onto live tasks.
- **4**: A new asset-data standard changes how every discipline records work; the whole delivery team needs to start applying it the same way from the same date.

## Matches

- **A**: Formal certified course with per-person assessment and records
- **B**: Pairing on live work with an experienced colleague
- **C**: Sustained mentoring relationship over time
- **D**: Group workshop establishing a common baseline

## Answer

1-C, 2-A, 3-B, 4-D

## Explanation

Read each gap by its constraints, not by a label. (1) one person, a two-year horizon, tacit expert judgement held by seniors — only a sustained mentoring relationship (C) develops that kind of know-how over time. (2) many people and a per-person, provable standard a regulator may inspect — that auditability is exactly what a formal certified course with assessment records (A) provides; no other option produces the evidence. (3) one person, a tight deadline, a concrete skill, and an expert already on the live work with capacity — pairing on real tasks (B) is fastest and lowest-overhead. (4) everyone must adopt one shared way of working from one date — a single group workshop (D) sets that common baseline efficiently. The traps are the near-fits: a course could *touch* grid-protection design but cannot grow judgement (so not 1-A), and mentoring could in principle cover the cloud skill but is far slower than pairing for a two-week need (so not 3-C).
